Paul Horsnell is head of commodities research at [[Standard Chartered]] and one of the most cited energy economists in the oil markets. Previously spent over a decade at [[Oxford Institute for Energy Studies]] and served as head of commodities research at [[Barclays]]. His 2000 paper "The Strategic Petroleum Blunder?" critiqued the Clinton administration's SPR exchange mechanism as structurally flawed, arguing the swap mechanism created a hidden forward liability rather than genuine price relief. Known for rigorous empirical work on oil market dynamics, OPEC behavior, and price formation.

## Iran war stockpile arithmetic — minimum operating levels (FT, May 17)

Quoted in the FT's May 17 peak-summer-crunch piece (identified by his [[Oxford Institute for Energy Studies]] affiliation rather than [[Standard Chartered]], suggesting he is now back at or maintaining a visiting role at OIES) on why headline reserve numbers overstate available supply:

- "The minimum operating level depends by country and by product." Pipelines need minimum pressure to maintain pressure; refineries need continuous supplies; storage tanks cannot be fully drained without risking damage. Markets seize up well before inventories hit zero.
- On the $105 Brent price not yet being demand-curbing: "This is well short of the all-time high above $140 a barrel that was set 18 years ago. Not very long ago, we thought $90 a barrel was a normal price."

The framing is the cleanest available rebuttal to readers anchored to the ~3 bn-barrel headline inventory figure as a buffer — most of that volume is system inventory (pipeline-fill, refinery feed, tank minimums), not freely available reserve. The relevant horizon for stockpile arithmetic is the [[JPMorgan Chase|JPMorgan]] "early June operational stress" estimate, not literal depletion. See [[Iran conflict economic disruption#May 17 — peak-summer crunch in view (FT)|economic disruption May 17]].

*Source: FT, "Iran energy crisis enters new phase as peak summer season approaches," May 17, 2026.*
