# Toby Nangle

Toby Nangle is a macro and fixed-income strategist turned journalist -- a [[Financial Times]] contributing editor and FT Alphaville reporter who writes on bonds, rates, inflation, and central banking. Before journalism he spent about 25 years in asset management, most recently as Global Head of Asset Allocation at [[Columbia Threadneedle Investments]], where he ran the asset-allocation team (about $160bn AuM) until leaving in 2022. He became an FT contributing editor in 2023 and later joined FT Alphaville full-time. He holds a master's from the University of Cambridge and writes as a self-described financial historian.

## One-line read

Nangle is useful as a yield-decomposition translator: he splits a nominal bond move into expected policy path, expected inflation, and [[Term premium\|term premium]], then asks which component is actually driving a given catalyst. When he flags a divergence between inflation expectations and bond yields, the signal is that the market has re-priced the central-bank reaction function, not the inflation outlook.

## Jun 18 2026 -- "The war is over; why aren't bond yields lower?"

Writing the day after [[Kevin Warsh\|Warsh]]'s first [[FOMC]] as [[Federal Reserve\|Fed]] chair, and as the [[US-Iran framework deal June 2026\|US-Iran deal]] reopened the [[Strait of Hormuz]] and sent [[Brent crude\|Brent]] down about a third from its peak, Nangle posed the puzzle in the title. Three dual-axis charts carried the argument:

| Chart | Series | Reading |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 1 | [[Brent crude\|Brent]] (Aug-26) vs 1yr and 2yr US inflation swaps | Inflation expectations rose with oil into the April peak (1yr swap about 3.5%), then fell back to about 2.45% by mid-June -- more sharply than oil itself |
| 2 | 2yr UST yield vs 1yr inflation swap | The two moved in near-lockstep until mid-May, then decoupled -- the yield held near 4.1-4.2% while the swap fell |
| 3 | Dec-26 fed funds future vs 1yr inflation swap | The implied policy rate drifted up (toward about 3.9%) as the inflation hedge fell -- "linked, until they weren't" |

His answer has two parts. The first is credibility: inflation expectations may have fallen "precisely because the market has understood that a new Warsh-led Fed is no pushover," a reading he says is supported by the post-FOMC tape in which the dollar rose against stocks, bonds, gold, crypto, and most currencies. The second, which he favors on the evidence, is the data flow -- hawkish April [[FOMC]] minutes, hotter-than-expected PCE deflator and [[Inflation\|CPI]] prints, strong consumer-spending numbers, and a bumper [[Rate expectations#Jun. 5 2026 payrolls / AI-duration shock\|payrolls report]] -- which together began unwinding the Trump-headline "everything trade" weeks before the deal was signed. Either way the unwind stranded bond yields higher and left the Fed leaning into a new tightening cycle, an outcome awkward for [[Trump II\|Trump]]'s well-known low-rates preference and for debtors generally. His closing line -- "good luck explaining this to your new boss Kevin" -- is the [[Kevin Warsh\|Warsh]]-era framing in miniature.

Vault read-through: the piece is the cleanest dated statement of the rate / inflation-expectations decoupling. It updates [[Inflation expectations#June 2026 — the expectations collapse, and the decoupling from rates\|Inflation expectations]] (the June collapse), [[Rate expectations#Jun 18 2026 — the rate / inflation-expectations link breaks (Nangle)\|Rate expectations]] (the broken link), and [[Term premium#June 2026 — falling breakevens, rising nominal\|Term premium]] (falling breakevens plus rising nominal yields = a real-rate/policy move, the cleaner cousin of April's stable-breakeven term-premium case).

## What to watch

Decoupling persistence -- If Nangle keeps flagging inflation expectations falling while yields hold, treat it as confirmation the market is pricing the [[Kevin Warsh\|Warsh]] Fed's tightening bias rather than a growth scare.

Everything-trade unwind -- Watch for him tying cross-asset moves back to the unwinding of Trump-pronouncement correlation; that is his tell for a positioning regime change.

Real-rate framing -- If he emphasizes real yields and term premium over inflation expectations, the implication is that the duration problem is policy- and fiscal-driven, not a one-off CPI scare.
