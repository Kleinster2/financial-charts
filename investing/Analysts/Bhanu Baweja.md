## One-line read

Baweja is useful as a high-beta macro risk translator: when he turns defensive, the signal is usually not "sell everything," but that the market is underpricing a second-round channel from a visible shock into earnings, rates, currencies, or sector rotation.

## May 20, 2026 - Bloomberg Hormuz / rates read

On Bloomberg's "The Pulse" May 20 segment, Baweja separated the financial-market oil signal from the physical-market signal. His read was that implied oil volatility had compressed and markets were behaving as though the conflict was becoming rearview-mirror risk, while physical indicators such as jet fuel were still firming. That matched the vault's [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] frame: headline diplomacy can move futures faster than shipping, insurance, refined products, and inventory buffers can normalize.

He also linked the rates move to real yields and nominal US growth rather than just inflation expectations. On the equity side, his concern was that the market was still focused on the AI capex / hyperscaler earnings channel while consumer and financial-sector second-round effects from higher energy and tighter rates were still early.

### Rates view

Baweja's May 20 rates view had four parts:

| Point | Read-through |
|-------|--------------|
| Back-end selloff | The rise in 5Y / 10Y / 30Y yields was mostly real yields and nominal-growth repricing, especially in the US, not just higher inflation expectations |
| Front end | Markets had stripped out 2027 cuts too aggressively; if investors are worried about inflation today, they should also worry about growth tomorrow |
| Trade expression | He said the front end was where to be receiving rates, with value also emerging in steep yield curves such as [[JGB|Japan]] and the [[Bank of England|UK]] |
| Central-bank asymmetry | He pushed against a 2022-style synchronized-hiking playbook: the [[Federal Reserve]], [[ECB]], and [[Bank of England]] should respond differently because the shock hits each economy differently |

The portfolio implication is counter-consensus duration, but specifically at the front end rather than a blanket long-bond call. He is not saying inflation is irrelevant. He is saying the market is over-extrapolating the first-round inflation shock and underpricing the second-round growth slowdown. For the [[Bank of England]], he explicitly said UBS was not looking for hikes this year and expected cuts starting in 2027.

Bloomberg Linea's Apr 7 recap is the cleaner earlier statement of the same framework: bond traders risked misusing the 2022 playbook by pricing major central banks together, while Baweja expected a more asymmetric response and saw value in short-dated government debt, especially in the US and UK.

Vault check: the FRED split now lives in [[Treasuries]] under the real-yield decomposition tracker. For May 13-19 2026, the 5Y nominal move was +20 bp versus +24 bp in real yields, the 10Y move was +21 bp versus +19 bp real, and the 30Y move was +15 bp versus +10 bp real. That makes his "mostly real rates" description mechanically right for the recent U.S. belly/back-end selloff, while the growth-vs-term-premium cause remains an interpretation.

## What to watch

Physical vs paper divergence -- If Baweja keeps emphasizing jet fuel, physical crude, refined products, or insurance while futures volatility compresses, treat that as confirmation that the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] is still not fully priced.

Earnings transmission math -- Watch for UBS updates that translate GDP, tariffs, or energy prices into S&P 500 EPS changes. His value is often in the bridge from macro shock to earnings estimates.

Rates leadership -- If he emphasizes real yields and nominal growth rather than inflation expectations alone, the implication is that the equity multiple problem is broader than a one-off CPI scare.

Rotation calls -- Defensive equity calls, fixed-income value, or reversals in Europe-vs-US / value-vs-growth rotations are the practical portfolio outputs of his framework.
