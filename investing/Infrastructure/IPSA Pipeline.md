**IPSA Pipeline** — defunct 1,600 km crude oil pipeline from Iraq to the Red Sea port of Mu'ajjiz (south of [[Yanbu]]) in Saudi Arabia. Capacity: 1.65 mb/d. Built during the Iran-Iraq War (1985-1989) to give Iraq an export route independent of Iranian threats to the Strait of Hormuz and Turkey's Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline. Shut down in 1990 after Iraq invaded Kuwait and never reopened.

## History

Iraq built IPSA during the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988) as a third export route — alongside the Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline through Turkey and Gulf terminals. The pipeline ran parallel to the Saudi [[East-West Pipeline]] for much of its route, terminating at Mu'ajjiz on the Red Sea just south of Yanbu.

IPSA operated for only about one year. When Iraq invaded Kuwait in August 1990, Saudi Arabia shut down the pipeline as part of the broader coalition response. It has remained inactive ever since — through the Gulf War, UN sanctions, the 2003 invasion, and the post-2003 period. Saudi Arabia has periodically discussed allowing Iraq to reuse it, including during a 2017 rapprochement, but no agreement has been reached.

After 36 years of inactivity, the pipeline would require significant rehabilitation to become operational. The Mu'ajjiz terminal infrastructure has also degraded. Some analysts have proposed incorporating IPSA into a revived bypass network, but the political relationship between Iraq and Saudi Arabia and the pipeline's physical condition make this unlikely in the near term.

---

## Why it matters

IPSA represents 1.65 mb/d of latent Hormuz bypass capacity that remains stranded by politics. If rehabilitated and combined with the [[East-West Pipeline]] (~3 mb/d usable) and [[Habshan-Fujairah Pipeline]] (1.5 mb/d), total bypass capacity could theoretically reach ~6+ mb/d — still less than half of normal Hormuz flows, but materially better than the current ~4.5 mb/d.
