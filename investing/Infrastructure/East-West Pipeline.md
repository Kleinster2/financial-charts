**East-West Pipeline (Petroline)** — Saudi Arabia's 1,200 km crude oil pipeline running from the Abqaiq oil field in the Eastern Province to the Red Sea port of [[Yanbu]]. The most viable bypass route for the [[Strait of Hormuz]], with 7 mb/d pipeline capacity (permanent since 2024 expansion). Port loading at [[Yanbu]] was estimated at ~3 mb/d but has exceeded 4.65 mb/d during the March 2026 crisis. Operated by [[Saudi Aramco]].

## Construction history

Built during the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988) as a strategic bypass when Hormuz transit was threatened. The original 48-inch pipeline was commissioned in 1981 with ~1.85 mb/d capacity. Saudi Aramco expanded it with a parallel 56-inch line in 1987, boosting capacity to 3.2 mb/d. A 1992 pumping system expansion pushed capacity to 4.5-5.0 mb/d.

The pipeline connects to the massive Abqaiq processing facility, which handles most of Saudi Arabia's crude before export. From Abqaiq, crude flows west across the Arabian Peninsula through pump stations to Yanbu.

An Iraqi crude spur was added in the mid-1980s to carry Iraqi oil west, but was decommissioned after Iraq's invasion of [[Kuwait]] in 1990. [[Saudi Aramco]] absorbed the infrastructure, using the older segment for gas liquids transport. In the 1990s, the crude pipeline was expanded to ~5 mb/d. Following the September 2019 [[Abqaiq]] and Khurais drone/missile attacks — which knocked out half of Aramco's output — the company temporarily pushed throughput to 7 mb/d. A single line in Aramco's 2024 earnings presentation revealed the permanent expansion to 7 mb/d had been completed — a disclosure that attracted little attention at the time but proved strategically decisive. The pipeline traverses deserts from near sea level at [[Abqaiq]] to elevations exceeding 1,000 meters crossing the Hijaz mountains before descending to [[Yanbu]] on the Red Sea coast.

## Bottleneck: Yanbu port loading

The pipeline can deliver 5-7 mb/d to Yanbu, but the port's loading infrastructure (berths, storage, tanker handling) can only load ~3 mb/d onto ships. As of March 2026, actual throughput was ~1.9 mb/d — so there's ~1 mb/d of additional port capacity, not 3-5 mb/d of pipeline spare capacity.

Historically, Yanbu exports have consisted mainly of Arab Light crude. Asian buyers prefer heavier Arab grades, but the pipeline can handle heavier crudes. During the March 2026 Hormuz closure, [[Saudi Aramco]] offered Yanbu loading to Asian buyers as an alternative (per Argus reporting).

By mid-March 2026, [[Yanbu]] was loading more than 4 million barrels of oil per day on several occasions — exceeding the ~3 mb/d estimate that analysts had cited as the binding constraint. [[Saudi Aramco]] either had more loading capacity than assessed or improvised with temporary infrastructure. [[Bahri]], Saudi Arabia's state tanker company, was paying rates of $450,000/day and above to amass a fleet of supertankers at [[Yanbu]]. By March 10, at least 25 supertankers were heading to the port. One of the first signs the bypass was working came within days of the ramp-up beginning (March 4), when a major [[India]]n refiner purchased cargoes from [[Yanbu]].

## Strategic significance

The Petroline is Saudi Arabia's insurance policy against Hormuz closure — but it can't replace Hormuz. Even at maximum port throughput (~3 mb/d), it covers only a fraction of the ~15+ mb/d of crude that normally transits the strait. Combined with the [[Habshan-Fujairah Pipeline]]'s 1.5 mb/d, total bypass capacity is ~4.5 mb/d — less than a third of normal Hormuz flows.

An additional complication: crude shipped from Yanbu exits via the Red Sea, requiring transit through the Bab el-Mandeb strait — which Houthi forces have attacked since 2023. A second chokepoint on the bypass route.

## MEI panel assessment (March 20, 2026 — Connelly/Mulroy)

[[Colby Connelly]] ([[Middle East Institute|MEI]] Senior Fellow, energy expert) confirmed the East-West Pipeline is actively rerouting exports, with ~7 million bbl/d capacity (including surge capability) and lots of exports now flowing through. The pipeline is under some Iranian attack or disruption attempts — [[Iran]] is "very aware" of bypass routes and "sees them as leverage points."

Both Connelly and [[Mick Mulroy]] discussed pipeline alternatives as the structural post-war response:

| Pipeline | Status |
|----------|--------|
| East-West Pipeline expansion | Likely |
| [[Habshan-Fujairah Pipeline|ADCO pipeline]] expansion | Likely |
| Iraq-Turkey pipeline improvements | Possible |
| Iraq-Aqaba ([[Jordan]]) pipeline | Possible — would allow Red Sea + northbound Suez Canal exports |

But [[Iraq]] isn't exporting much oil right now (force majeure on all foreign-operated oilfields). Pipelines are expensive to build, take time, and still need assurance of protection. Longer term, expect more pipeline construction to get around the strait.

Source: MEI panel, March 20 2026 — Connelly

## Stellen Capital skeptical assessment — 2.5-3M bpd sustainable (March 23)

[[Nadia Martin Wiggen]] ([[Stellen Capital]], commodities/shipping hedge fund) offered a significantly lower sustainable throughput estimate than the 4.65M bpd peak achieved in mid-March.

Starting point: [[Saudi Aramco]] announced 7M bpd capacity through the pipeline. But Wiggen deducts ~1M bpd already committed to local Red Sea coast refineries and ~500K bpd flowing through the LPG pipeline — leaving ~5M bpd of crude export capacity. The binding constraint is port loading at [[Yanbu]]: it takes approximately 36 hours to load a VLCC. At 5M bpd, that requires loading 2.5 VLCCs per day, which Stellen does not believe is physically achievable on a sustained basis. Ships also must sail during daytime only through Red Sea waters due to potential [[Houthis|Houthi]] attacks.

Stellen's sustainable estimate: 2.5-3M bpd. Blue sky scenario (everything goes perfectly for a couple of days): 4M bpd.

This conflicts with the 4.65M bpd peak reported by Aramco in mid-March — either that peak was a burst achievable for days but not weeks, or Wiggen's loading time estimate is conservative. The truth likely sits between: the 4.65M peak was a surge effort (possibly using temporary infrastructure or [[Bahri]] supertanker queuing) while 2.5-3M bpd reflects what can be maintained without extraordinary measures.

*Source: Nadia Martin Wiggen (Stellen Capital), Other People's Money / Monetary Matters Network, March 23, 2026*

## Expert assessments (March 2026)

[[Jim Krane]] (Wallace S. Wilson Fellow for Energy Studies, [[Rice University]]): "The East-West pipeline is looking like a strategic masterstroke right now. The entire global economy is better off with the line in operation." Krane also warned that [[Houthis]] "now have a veto on Saudi oil exports via the [[Bab el-Mandeb]]" — if they resume attacks on Red Sea shipping, "oil markets will gyrate even more wildly." (Bloomberg, March 22, 2026)

[[Carole Nakhle]] (CEO, Crystol Energy): "The mere existence of an alternative route helps calm markets by reassuring buyers that not all the region's exports are trapped." But warned the route is "not a risk-free alternative" — if [[Yanbu]] and the pipeline came under sustained pressure, "that would mark a serious escalation." (Bloomberg, March 22, 2026)

[[Karen Young]] (Senior Research Scholar, [[Columbia University]] Center on Global Energy Policy): "It's a demonstration of energy security, planning and investment for a crisis like this. If the East-West pipeline can carry 7 million barrels a day, that would be a major relief valve. The issue is loading capacity and continuing port security." (Bloomberg, March 22, 2026)

[[Amin Nasser]] ([[Saudi Aramco]] CEO, March 10 conference call): "While we have faced disruptions in the past, this one by far is the biggest crisis the region's oil and gas industry has faced." Confirmed ~2 mb/d coming through the pipeline feeds domestic refineries on the Red Sea coast, which were still exporting refined products like diesel.

A December 1980 story in the Mideast Report — a newsletter specializing in the region — heralded the planned pipeline, expected to cost $495 million, saying it provided an alternative to the "strategic yet vulnerable [[Strait of Hormuz]], which could eventually come under Iranian guns." Forty-five years later, that scenario materialized.

Source: Bloomberg (Di Paola, Longley, Prem, El Wardany), March 22, 2026
