#event #legal #socialmedia

**2026 social media liability crisis** — two jury verdicts in one week found [[Meta]] and [[Alphabet|Google]] liable for harms to minors, while the EU opened its first [[Digital Services Act]] investigation into [[Snap|Snapchat]]. Senator [[Dick Durbin]]: "Big Tech has become Big Tobacco." [[Timothy Edgar]] (Harvard Law): "a major watershed event" representing "a big shift in how Americans are viewing Big Tech." Market impact: Meta -7.96%, Alphabet -3.45%, Snap -10.69% on March 26. More than 2,400 similar cases consolidated in California federal court, with a school-district trial expected later in 2026.

## New Mexico verdict (March 24)

State of New Mexico v. Meta, Santa Fe state court. AG [[Raúl Torrez]] sued Meta in 2023 after an undercover operation created a fake profile of a 13-year-old girl that Torrez said was "simply inundated with images and targeted solicitations" from child abusers.

Jury found Meta willfully violated the state's Unfair Practices Act. Damages: $375M (state attorneys sought $2B+). Meta will appeal.

Key evidence: internal Meta messages showed that [[Mark Zuckerberg]]'s 2019 decision to make [[Facebook Messenger]] end-to-end encrypted would impact the company's ability to disclose ~7.5M child sexual abuse material (CSAM) reports to law enforcement.

Phase 2 (bench trial, May 4): judge determines whether Meta created a public nuisance and must fund remedial programs. State seeking design changes: effective age verification, predator removal, protections in encrypted communications.

Torrez overcame [[Section 230]] motions in both the Meta and [[Snap]] cases — using a legal strategy focused on app design rather than user content, bypassing the traditional Section 230 defense. Torrez's separate child-exploitation suit against Snap (filed 2024) is in discovery.

*Source: [CNBC, Mar 24](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/24/jury-reaches-verdict-in-meta-child-safety-trial-in-new-mexico.html)*

## Los Angeles verdict (March 26)

Personal injury trial, LA Superior Court. Plaintiff identified as K.G.M. (Kaley) alleged she became addicted to [[Instagram]] and [[YouTube]] when underage, causing mental health harms (depression).

Jury found Meta and Google's YouTube liable — their negligence was a "substantial factor" in causing harm. Combined damages: $6M (Meta 70%, Google 30%). Both companies will appeal. Meta pointed out the jury awarded less than 0.5% of what plaintiff's attorneys requested.

This is a bellwether case for the federal multidistrict litigation (MDL) in the Northern District of California expected to commence later in 2026, where multiple school districts and parents allege social media caused negative mental health harms to children.

*Source: [CNBC, Mar 26](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/26/meta-lawsuit-decision-zuckerberg-woes.html)*

## EU DSA investigation into Snapchat (March 26)

The European Commission opened a formal [[Digital Services Act]] investigation into [[Snap|Snapchat]] — the first DSA probe of the platform. Five focus areas:

| Area | Concern |
|------|---------|
| Age assurances | Self-disclosure of being over 13 is "insufficient" |
| Grooming | Recruitment of minors for criminal activities |
| Default settings | Account settings that undermine minor safety |
| Illegal products | Access to info on buying drugs and age-restricted products |
| Reporting | Inadequate illegal content reporting mechanisms |

Investigation followed a review of Snapchat's risk assessments from 2023-2025. Potential penalties: up to 6% of global turnover or a ban for serious/repeated violations.

*Source: [Euronews, Mar 26](https://www.euronews.com/next/2026/03/26/european-commission-opens-investigation-into-snapchats-child-safety-protections)*

## Section 230 implications

Both NM and LA cases used a legal strategy targeting app *design* rather than user *content* — sidestepping the traditional [[Section 230]] shield. Senator [[Dick Durbin]]: "it's time for Congress to sunset Section 230 once and for all."

[[Timothy Edgar]] (Harvard Law) noted the verdicts are "consistent with the overall backlash against Big Tech" but warned of "unintended consequences" — the internet could become "tamed and regulated" by fear of liability. Cases could reach the Supreme Court on free speech grounds.
