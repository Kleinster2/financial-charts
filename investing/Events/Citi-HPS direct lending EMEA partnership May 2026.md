# Citi-HPS direct lending EMEA partnership May 2026

On May 18, 2026, [[Citigroup]] and [[HPS Investment Partners]] — now part of [[BlackRock]] following the July 2024 acquisition — announced a €15B ($17.48B) private capital program to expand direct lending to corporate and sponsor-owned borrowers across EMEA over an initial five-year term. [[Citigroup|Citi]] originates the deals through its Investment, Corporate, and Commercial Bank franchise; [[HPS Investment Partners|HPS]] supplies the capital from its $381B Private Financing Solutions platform (AUM as of March 31, 2026). The structure is a direct extension of the September 2024 [[Citigroup]]-[[Apollo]] $25B North American program, applied to a different geography with a different private-credit partner.

---

## Synthesis

The deal is most cleanly read on three levels. *For Citi*, it completes the geographic build-out of a strategic positioning that started with Apollo in 2024: Citi has now established itself as the dominant *bank-side origination partner* for major private credit firms across two regions, with the Americas going to Apollo (plus Mubadala and Athene) and EMEA going to HPS/BlackRock. The fee-for-origination economics fit precisely with [[Jane Fraser]]'s turnaround thesis — Citi monetizes the deepest part of its franchise (corporate client relationships across 90+ countries) without consuming balance sheet, while building a recurring fee stream that the market currently does not credit in its turnaround-rather-than-finished-franchise pricing.

*For HPS / BlackRock*, the deal validates the July 2024 acquisition. HPS's Private Financing Solutions platform AUM has scaled from roughly $100B at acquisition to $381B in March 2026 — a 3.8x growth in 21 months that the vault had not previously captured. The Citi-HPS program plugs HPS into a deal-origination pipeline that no private-credit firm could replicate organically. For [[Larry Fink]] specifically, this is the canonical proof-point that BlackRock's $28B 2024 alternatives-acquisition spree (HPS plus GIP plus Preqin) is delivering on the strategic logic — pairing scale capital with origination and distribution.

*For the [[Private Credit|private credit sector]]*, the deal is the European leg of a structural pattern now visible globally: major banks and major private-credit firms forming origination-and-capital partnerships that replace the traditional syndicated leveraged-loan model. The cleanest read is in the new [[Bank-private credit partnerships]] concept note. The structural consequence is that the sub-investment-grade financing market is increasingly intermediated by these joint ventures rather than by bank balance sheets — which has implications for credit cycle dynamics, recovery rates, and the [[AI infrastructure financing risk|circular-financing risk]] surfaced elsewhere in the vault.

## Comparison to Citi-Apollo (Sep 2024)

The Citi-HPS program is the EMEA mirror of the Citi-Apollo $25B North American program announced September 26, 2024. Both deals share the same structural skeleton; the differences are in geography, capital partner, and scale.

| Dimension | Citi-Apollo (Sep 2024) | Citi-HPS (May 2026) |
|---|---|---|
| Size | $25B | €15B (~$17.48B) |
| Geographic scope | Initially North America, expansion potential | EMEA (Continental Europe + UK + Middle East eventually) |
| Term | 5 years | 5 years initial |
| Citi role | Source deals, earn origination fee | Source deals, earn origination fee |
| Capital partner | [[Apollo]] + [[Mubadala]] + [[Athene]] | [[HPS Investment Partners|HPS]] (part of [[BlackRock]]) |
| Capital partner AUM at signing | Apollo ~$700B (Sep 2024) | HPS PFS $381B (Mar 2026) |
| Exclusivity | "Exclusive agreement" — Citi-Apollo language | Strategic collaboration — language doesn't claim exclusivity |
| Borrower type | Corporate + sponsor clients | Sub-IG corporate + sponsor clients |
| Compatibility | The Apollo exclusivity is geographic (North America). The HPS deal in EMEA does not conflict | — |

The two programs together total $42.5B of committed bank-private-credit capital with Citi as the origination engine. If a third partnership emerges for Asia / Pacific, Citi will have effectively built a globally-distributed private-credit origination utility — the most ambitious such positioning by any global bank to date.

## Strategic read-through

### For [[Citigroup]]

The deal extends [[Jane Fraser]]'s turnaround thesis in the cleanest possible way. Citi's most valuable franchise feature — its global corporate banking network — gets monetized through origination fees rather than balance-sheet lending, which means the revenue translates directly into return-on-tangible-equity expansion. Combined with the Apollo program, Citi is positioning itself as the *globally-dominant* bank-side originator for major private-credit programs. The market currently prices Citi as a turnaround-in-progress; if this revenue line scales as expected, it is the kind of recurring fee stream that justifies a multiple expansion.

### For [[HPS Investment Partners]] / [[BlackRock]]

The deal is the highest-profile validation yet of the July 2024 HPS acquisition. AUM scaling from ~$100B to $381B in 21 months substantially exceeds the integration playbook BlackRock has executed on prior alternative-asset acquisitions. Pairing BlackRock-scale capital with Citi-scale origination is the structural combination that no organic private credit firm can match. For [[Larry Fink]]'s thesis that BlackRock's future growth is in alternatives + technology platforms (Aladdin), this is the canonical Exhibit A on the alternatives leg.

### For [[Apollo]]

The Citi-Apollo $25B program established the format; the Citi-HPS program is the second instance and the European leg. The cleanest read is that Apollo's exclusivity was geographic (North America) by design, and the EMEA program does not conflict. But the precedent matters: Citi has demonstrated it will use multiple private-credit partners depending on region, which weakens informal expectations of broader exclusivity arrangements with any single firm. [[Marc Rowan]]'s positioning of [[Apollo]] as the dominant private credit firm globally is now structurally challenged by a BlackRock-aligned competitor with bank distribution.

### For the [[Private Credit|private credit sector]]

This is the second major instance of the bank-private-credit partnership structure, on top of an already-established pattern (JPM-Cliffwater, [[Goldman Sachs]]-Mubadala etc.). The structural reading: the sub-IG corporate financing market is transitioning from "banks underwrite + syndicate" to "banks originate + private credit holds." That has implications for credit cycle dynamics — private credit holders intervene earlier on covenant breaches than syndicate loan investors do — and for recovery rates in a downturn. See [[Bank-private credit partnerships]] for the full structural treatment.

### For [[BlackRock]]'s acquisition strategy

The 21-month HPS integration is now the proof-point for [[Larry Fink]]'s alternatives strategy. The $28B 2024 spree (HPS + [[GIP]] + Preqin) was a bet that BlackRock could integrate alternatives at scale; the Citi-HPS deal is the first major commercial validation of that bet. If the HPS PFS platform reaches $500B AUM in 2026-27 (a plausible trajectory), the acquisition will read as one of the best capital-allocation decisions in BlackRock's history.
