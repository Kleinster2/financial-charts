# Nexstar-Tegna Merger

**March 19, 2026** — The [[FCC]] approved [[Nexstar Media Group]]'s $3.54B acquisition of [[Tegna]], creating the largest local TV operator in the U.S. with 265 stations across 44 states and D.C. The deal faces dual legal challenges from eight Democratic-led states and [[DirecTV]].

## FCC waiver

The [[FCC]] waived its longstanding rule capping any single owner from broadcasting to more than 39% of U.S. households. Without this waiver, the combined Nexstar-Tegna entity would have exceeded the cap. Nexstar agreed to divest six stations as a condition of approval.

## Legal challenges

Both lawsuits were filed on March 18, 2026 — one day before the [[FCC]] approved the deal.

| Plaintiff | Court | Claim |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| 8 Democratic-led states | U.S. District Court, Sacramento | Seeking to block merger |
| [[DirecTV]] | Separate federal lawsuit | Seeking to block merger |

The state attorneys general argue the merger would harm competition in local broadcasting. [[DirecTV]]'s challenge likely centers on retransmission fee leverage — a combined Nexstar-Tegna would negotiate from a stronger position against pay-TV distributors.

## Deal closure and political context

Nexstar closed the acquisition the same day the FCC approved it — unusually fast execution. [[DOJ]] had also approved. CEO Perry Sook: "This transaction is essential to sustaining strong local journalism in the communities we serve."

Trump publicly supported the deal in February. FCC Chair [[Brendan Carr]]'s framing is explicitly political: he wants to empower local affiliates to push back against national networks ([[NBC]], [[ABC]]) and even preempt their programming. The FCC order stated the deal "will help preserve Nexstar's ability to influence network programming through collective negotiation and to preempt network programming in favor of programming that better serves the local community."

Context: Carr has repeatedly threatened network licenses over editorial content. In September, he praised Nexstar for briefly not airing "Jimmy Kimmel Live!" on its ABC affiliates after Kimmel made comments about the assassination of conservative activist Charlie Kirk. Hours before ABC suspended the show, Carr warned local broadcasters who aired Kimmel could face fines or license revocation.

Democratic FCC Commissioner Anna Gomez: the deal "concentrates broadcast power in fewer corporate hands, shrinking independent editorial voices, and prioritizing national business interests over local needs."

## Why it matters

This is the largest local TV consolidation deal in U.S. history. The [[FCC]]'s willingness to waive the 39% ownership cap signals a permissive regulatory stance toward broadcast consolidation under Carr — but more importantly, it's a tool in the administration's pressure campaign against networks that air unfavorable content. The combined entity reaches ~80% of US TV households.

The dual lawsuits (eight states + [[DirecTV]]) create uncertainty, but the deal has already closed — plaintiffs would need to unwind it.

For [[DirecTV]] and other pay-TV distributors, the combined entity's 265-station footprint gives [[Nexstar Media Group|Nexstar]] significant leverage in retransmission consent negotiations. Stations carrying [[ABC]], [[CBS]], [[Fox Corporation|Fox]], and [[NBC]] affiliates are must-have content for any distributor.
