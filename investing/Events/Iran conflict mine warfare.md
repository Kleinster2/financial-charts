# Iran conflict mine warfare

The mine warfare dimension of the [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]] — the single most consequential threat to reopening the [[Strait of Hormuz]]. Part of [[Iran conflict economic disruption]].

---

## The mine threat — Mulroy assessment (March 20, 2026)

[[Mick Mulroy]] (fmr Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Middle East, ex-CIA officer, ex-Marine, [[Middle East Institute|MEI]] Distinguished Military Fellow) provided the most operationally detailed public assessment of the mine warfare challenge:

### Scale and deployment speed

[[Iran]] has an estimated 5,000 naval mines available. The physical geography makes deployment trivially easy: the [[Strait of Hormuz]] is 21 miles wide, with only 2 miles navigable. Sea mining takes only a few hours with small speedboats. Even after clearing, Iran can reseed mines in hours — the US will never eliminate 100% of their military capacity. Iran is 4x the size of [[Iraq]], 2x the population, and extraordinarily mountainous.

### US mine countermeasures capability — the critical gap

The US decommissioned the Avenger-class minesweepers — the most critical and effective ships for this mission. The Navy is now relying on [[Littoral Combat Ship|Littoral Combat Ships (LCS)]] with drones for mine countermeasures — untested in this mission. Mulroy: "unfortunately" the US will find out whether this was a good decision in real-time. The Avengers used traditional sweep methods; the LCS uses drone-based detection and clearing, which has never been tested in contested waters against a large-scale mine deployment.

### Commercial trust gap — beyond the military problem

Even if the US Navy clears mines, commercial shipping companies and insurers won't trust it. "It isn't just a military issue." The trust gap is the binding constraint: a tanker owner won't risk a $100M asset with $200M of cargo and crew lives based on military assurances that mines have been cleared. We also need to convince shipping companies it's safe beyond mines — coastal missiles, drones, and suicide boats all pose additional threats.

### Iran's 1,000-mile coastline

Mulroy put the scale in perspective: [[Iran]]'s coastline is roughly equivalent to the entire US coast. Even the most significant military in the world cannot cover a 1,000-mile coastline. Iran can reseed mines, launch drone boats, and fire coastal missiles from any point along this coastline. The asymmetry is permanent.

### No allies

The US has no allies helping with mine clearance or Hormuz operations. "We didn't engage them before the war. I can understand why." This compounds every operational challenge — from mine clearance to sustained escort operations.

## Phased military operations

Mulroy outlined the US response as phased:

Phase 1: A-10 Thunderbolts taking out small boats — related to mine-laying and suicide drone boats. Suppressing the deployment capability before clearing can begin.

Phase 2: The 31st MEU (Marine Expeditionary Unit) being redirected from the Indo-Pacific to the Middle East. If this was a planned mission, it would already be there — the redeployment indicates an added contingency plan. "You would not be pulling a national asset from the Far East." The 31st MEU is "special operations capable" with two possible missions: secure islands around the strait, or seize [[Kharg Island]].

## Stellen Capital — shipping perspective on mine warfare (March 23)

[[Nadia Martin Wiggen]] ([[Stellen Capital]]) reinforced the mine assessment from a commercial/shipping perspective. The mines are "extremely difficult" — not because of the mines themselves but because "thousands of little fishing boats" can deploy them. This makes suppression a near-impossible game of whack-a-mole along [[Iran]]'s coastline.

The critical strategic dimension Wiggen adds: Iran can go quiet for two weeks, then strike something, and "that completely changes that feeling in the commercial shipping fleet." A single incident after an apparent all-clear resets the entire risk calculation — insurers reprice, crews refuse to sail, shipping companies pull back. The go-quiet-then-strike strategy makes mine warfare a permanent commercial weapon, not just a military tactic. Even if the US clears 100% of deployed mines, the threat of reseeding means the risk premium never returns to pre-war levels.

*Source: Nadia Martin Wiggen (Stellen Capital), Other People's Money / Monetary Matters Network, March 23, 2026*
