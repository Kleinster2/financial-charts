#event #bonds #japan #liquidity #volatility

**JGB crash January 2026** — $280 million of trading triggered a $41 billion wipeout across Japan's government bond curve on January 21, 2026. Ultra-long yields surged to record highs. [[Scott Bessent]] called it a "six-standard-deviation move."

---

## What happened

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Date | January 21, 2026 |
| Trigger volume | **$280M** combined (30Y + 40Y) |
| Market cap wipeout | **$41B** across JGB curve |
| 30Y yield move | +25+ bps intraday |
| 40Y yield move | +25+ bps intraday |
| 40Y yield (post-rout) | **>4%** — first JGB maturity above 4% in 30+ years |
| 30Y yield (post-rout) | ~3.66% (record high) |

For comparison: $41B traded in 10Y JGB futures the same day. The cash market for ultra-longs is structurally illiquid.

---

## Market structure problem

**Why $280M moved a $7.2T market:**

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| [[BOJ]] holdings | 50%+ of JGBs — supply absorbed |
| BOJ QT | Cutting purchases ¥400B/month → slowing to ¥200B/quarter from April |
| Life insurer pullback | Traditional long-end buyers stepped back |
| Life insurer losses | Big 4 reported **$60B combined unrealized losses** on domestic bonds, 4x prior year |
| Foreign share | 65% of monthly cash transactions (up from 12% in 2009) |
| Dealer constraints | Balance sheets limited, thin depth |
| Price discovery | Marginal trade sets price, not volume-weighted |

Mizuho's Shoki Omori: "This is not a paradox: it is exactly what you expect in a market where depth is thin, dealer balance sheets are constrained, and prices are set by the marginal trade."

## Catalysts

| Catalyst | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Weak 20Y auction** | Tepid response earlier in day — proximate trigger |
| **Inflation adjustment** | Japan normalizing after decades of deflation |
| **BOJ tightening** | Rate at 0.5%, market pricing more |
| **Fiscal concerns** | PM [[Sanae Takaichi]]'s sales tax pause on food — **¥5T/year** (~$31.6B) |
| **Nov stimulus** | ¥21.3T ($137B) package approved Nov 21 — biggest since pandemic |
| **Life insurer retreat** | Traditional buyers stepping back |

**Fiscal backdrop:**
- Takaichi called snap election for **Feb 8, 2026** — promised 2-year food tax suspension
- Main opposition (Centrist Reform Alliance) pledged **permanent** food tax abolition
- Fiscal discipline concerns across entire political spectrum
- Government shifting issuance to short-term debt (boosting 2Y/5Y by ¥300B each) to avoid long-end pressure
- Takaichi: "impossible to isolate the impact of fiscal policy on yield moves"

BOJ Governor Ueda warned Dec 9 that yields were rising "at a somewhat rapid pace."

**Rumored sellers:** Primary dealers, hedge funds, life insurers. No confirmed culprit.

---

## Corporate bond spillover

Japan's credit market also hit — average yields on high-grade corporate bonds jumped to a record. At least one multi-million dollar deal was pulled when the prospective buyer got spooked by broader financing cost concerns.

---

## Contagion risk

**Citi estimate:** JGB turmoil risks up to **$130B Treasury selling** as Japanese institutions repatriate.

**Mechanism:**
1. JGB yields spike → domestic bonds more attractive
2. Japanese life insurers/pensions sell foreign bonds
3. Repatriation = sell Treasuries, buy yen
4. UST yields rise, JPY strengthens
5. Carry trade unwind accelerates

## May 16-17 update — record yields + repatriation bets (FT)

*Source: [FT, "Record high Japanese yields trigger bets on repatriation"](https://www.ft.com/content/6e363bc3-3114-4ddf-9f3b-67f031b3ef92), Kate Duguid + Harriet Clarfelt in New York + Leo Lewis in Tokyo, May 16, 2026.*

Four months after the January crash episode, JGB yields hit fresh structural highs and the institutional thesis has shifted from "JGB-specific stress" to "repatriation of Japanese capital home." This is the natural continuation of the January thread — same supply-demand dynamics that broke the market in January, now compounded by the [[2026 Iran conflict]] inflation channel.

### Record-yield prints (Friday May 16)

| Maturity | Yield | Previous record | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10Y | 2.73% | Since May 1997 | Friday close |
| 30Y | 4.00% | First time since 1999 issuance | Friday close |
| 20Y | Record (level not specified) | — | Earlier in the week |
| 5Y | Record (level not specified) | — | Earlier in the week |

The 30Y at exactly 4% is the structural-break number — the first time the bond has reached that level since it was originally issued. The January 40Y print at >4% was the first JGB maturity above 4% in 30+ years; the May 30Y reaching the same threshold confirms the structural-rate regime change rather than a single-tenor anomaly.

### BOJ rate path

[[BOJ]] raised policy rate to 0.75% in December (three-decade high). The market is now pricing a quarter-point hike to 1% at the June meeting, driven by:

- Persistent inflation above the BOJ's 2% target
- Additional price pressure from the [[2026 Iran conflict|Iran war]] energy shock
- [[Sanae Takaichi]]'s expansionist fiscal posture (post-Feb election victory promising higher government spending + petrol-price subsidies)
- Expected supplementary budget later in 2026

### Repatriation thesis — the $1tn question

Japanese investors hold approximately $1tn of US Treasury debt — the largest foreign-holder cohort. The article captures the asset-manager positioning around the question of whether this stock starts to move home.

| Voice | Position | Quote |
|---|---|---|
| Mark Dowding ([[BlueBay]] CIO; launched first Japanese bond fund in March 2026) | Domestic allocations crowd out new overseas flows | "The new money that's being put to work won't be put to work overseas. It won't be going into US corporate bonds. It won't be going into US Treasuries. It will be going into those domestic allocations." |
| Matt Smith ([[Ruffer]] fund manager) | Long yen as key hedge | "We're long yen. It is one of our key hedges. In a moment of market turmoil, especially if the turmoil is US-credit-market-centric, you will see the yen strengthen as Japanese investors bring capital home." |
| Smith ([[Ruffer]]) on the timing | Slow-then-fast | "Pressure is building — long-end domestic yields are rising. And the institutional framework is now 'please can you bring this money home'. We think yen strength will happen slowly, then quickly." |
| Abbas Keshvani ([[RBC Capital|RBC Capital Markets]] Asia macro strategist) | Caveat: stock hasn't moved yet | Japanese investors net buyers of $50bn foreign bonds over past 12 months despite higher JGB yields "offering ostensibly better compensation to investors." Reason: the supply-demand path is for higher yields still, so why buy now |

The two-track read: new flows are turning home (EPFR records); existing $1tn stock is not yet moving because the path is for prices to fall further before stabilising. The "slowly, then quickly" framing captures the non-linearity — until the cohort decides the JGB sell-off is exhausted, no one wants to be the early mover; once that view shifts, the move could be fast.

### Fund-flow evidence (EPFR)

| Period | Japanese sovereign bond fund inflows |
|---|---|
| March 2026 | ~$700mn (biggest monthly on record) |
| April 2026 | $86mn (in line with recent moves) |

The March print is the diagnostic — a single-month record-breaking inflow into a category that has been ignored for decades. April's normalisation says it was a March-specific surge tied to the JGB crash repricing, not a sustained shift yet. The next data point is May (June EPFR release), which will show whether the May yield prints triggered another inflow spike.

### Finance Minister Katayama framing

[[Satsuki Katayama]] (Friday May 16): yields on government debt rising across the world's biggest bond markets — "These developments are interacting with one another, and that is creating a compounding effect." The compounding-effect framing is the official Japanese government acknowledgment that this isn't a Japan-specific problem but a global synchronization of long-end pressure (US Treasury, UK gilts, French OATs, Bund all moving together — see [[Treasury safe asset erosion]]).

### What it means for the broader thesis

1. JGB-record-yields confirm the January crash was a structural-regime warning, not a one-off liquidity event. The supply-demand math (BOJ exit from QE/YCC + life insurer pullback + Takaichi fiscal expansion) is binding.
2. The repatriation thesis is now actionable for asset managers but the $1tn US Treasury stock is not yet moving — EPFR new-money flows are turning, but stock-level reallocation requires a confidence inflection that hasn't fired.
3. Yen strength is the cleanest expression of the thesis ([[Ruffer]] long-yen position). If a US-credit-market shock materialises, the yen-strengthening response would be amplified by Japanese repatriation in a way that wasn't operative in previous risk-off episodes.
4. The 3% 10Y JGB target later in 2026 (analyst consensus per the article) implies another ~30bp of selling pressure before stabilisation — keeps the "not yet" framing alive but defines a price level at which the repatriation thesis becomes substantive flow rather than positioning trade.
