**Valeant Philidor scandal** — In October 2015, short seller Andrew Left (Citron Research) revealed that [[Valeant Pharmaceuticals]] secretly controlled a specialty pharmacy called Philidor Rx Services, with Valeant employees working at Philidor under fake names. The revelation triggered a congressional investigation, financial restatements, the firing of CEO Mike Pearson, and a ~96% stock collapse. [[Bill Ackman]] / [[Pershing Square]] lost ~$4B on their 5.7% stake. Left compared Valeant to [[Enron]].

## What Philidor was

Philidor Rx Services was a specialty pharmacy based in Hatboro, Pennsylvania. On paper, it was an independent company. In practice, Valeant controlled it:

- Valeant held an option (signed December 2013) to acquire Philidor for $100 — a nominal price reflecting de facto control
- Valeant employees worked at Philidor under assumed names
- Philidor processed prescriptions almost exclusively for Valeant drugs
- When insurers rejected Philidor's claims, the pharmacy resubmitted under alternate names and pharmacy IDs (a practice potentially constituting insurance fraud)
- Valeant consolidated Philidor's revenue on its financial statements, booking sales that might not have reflected genuine end-user demand

The arrangement served Valeant's business model: bypass pharmacy benefit managers who were pushing back on Valeant's extreme price increases by controlling the distribution channel directly.

## The short sellers

Andrew Left of Citron Research was the primary investigator. His October 19, 2015 report alleged that Valeant was using Philidor as a "captive pharmacy" to manufacture revenue. His October 21 follow-up compared Valeant's accounting to [[Enron]], calling it "the pharmaceutical Enron."

Southern Investigative Reporting Foundation (SIRF) journalist Roddy Boyd had also been investigating Philidor's relationship with Valeant and published contemporaneous reporting.

The reports landed at a moment of maximum vulnerability — Valeant was already under political pressure over drug pricing and carrying ~$30B in acquisition debt.

---

## Congressional hearings

The Senate Special Committee on Aging (chaired by Susan Collins) and the House Committee on Oversight opened parallel investigations into Valeant's drug pricing and business practices.

The hearing on June 28, 2016 featured Mike Pearson and [[Martin Shkreli]] — Valeant's former CEO and Turing Pharmaceuticals' CEO, the two faces of the drug pricing crisis — testifying in the same session. Shkreli invoked the Fifth Amendment. Pearson defended the pricing strategy.

## Why it mattered

The Philidor scandal ended the "platform pharma" model — the idea that pharmaceutical companies could generate outsized returns by acquiring drugs, slashing R&D, and raising prices. Valeant was the largest and most celebrated practitioner; its implosion discredited the approach.

Combined with [[Martin Shkreli]]'s Daraprim price hike (from $13.50 to $750), the scandal triggered a lasting regulatory and political backlash against drug price increases. [[Hillary Clinton]]'s September 2015 tweet promising to combat drug price gouging wiped $15B off biotech stocks in a single session.

For [[Bill Ackman]], the loss was career-defining. Combined with the [[Ackman-Icahn Herbalife feud]] (-$1B), the 2014-2018 period produced cumulative losses of ~$5B on two trades. Pershing Square's AUM fell from ~$20B to ~$8B before the 2020 COVID hedges ($2.6B profit) began the recovery.
