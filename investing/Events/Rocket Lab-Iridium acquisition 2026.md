#event #space #satellite #mergers

On June 29 2026, [[Rocket Lab]] agreed to acquire [[Iridium Communications]] for $54.00 per share in cash and stock — an enterprise value of about $8.0 billion — folding an operating LEO satellite-communications constellation, globally licensed L-band spectrum, and ~2.55M subscribers into Rocket Lab's launch-and-systems platform.

---

## Synthesis

This is the move that converts [[Rocket Lab]] from a launch-and-build company into a vertically integrated space operator with its own recurring-revenue network and licensed spectrum. Until now Rocket Lab sold launch ([[Electron]], [[Neutron]]) and hardware (Space Systems); Iridium adds the third leg — owning and operating a constellation that throws off subscription cash flow — which is the [[SpaceX]] / [[Starlink]] model of controlling the full stack from rocket to subscriber. The strategic prize is less the satellites than the global L-band spectrum and the embedded government / IoT / maritime subscriber base: assets that are scarce, licensed, and hard to replicate. The tape read it as company-specific — the target re-rated to the offer, the acquirer rallied (a vote of confidence in the strategic logic), the space cohort moved in sympathy, but rival operator [[Globalstar]] did not — so this was priced as a Rocket Lab vertical-integration story, not a broad satellite-operator re-rating (see Market reaction).

The collar fixes the exchange ratio outside a $67.50-$112.50 RKLB band, so Iridium holders bear Rocket Lab price risk beyond those bounds. With RKLB at $97.72 on the announcement close, the deal sits inside the collar.

## Strategic rationale

Rocket Lab is buying three things, in ascending order of strategic value:
- The constellation — 66 operational LEO satellites providing truly global (pole-to-pole) L-band coverage, a network Rocket Lab would otherwise spend years and billions to build and launch.
- The subscriber base — ~2.55M subscribers across government, defense, maritime, aviation, and IoT, i.e. recurring service revenue that diversifies Rocket Lab away from lumpy launch and contract-milestone income.
- The spectrum — globally licensed L-band, the scarce asset. L-band is the band of choice for direct-to-device, IoT, and resilient government comms; owning licensed global L-band is a moat that [[AST SpaceMobile]] and [[Starlink]] direct-to-cell programs are spending heavily to approximate.

The deal extends the vertical-integration arc that the [[Motiv Space Systems]] (robotics), [[GEOST]] (payloads), and [[SolAero]] (solar) tuck-ins began — but at a different scale and on the operations / services side rather than manufacturing. It also hands [[Neutron]] a captive launch customer (constellation replenishment) and pushes Rocket Lab deeper into the government / defense comms layer that its [[Golden Dome]] / SBI work already opened.

## What to watch

- Regulatory path — L-band spectrum transfer plus government-customer continuity invite [[FCC]] and national-security review; the mid-2027 close leaves a long arbitrage window.
- Financing / dilution — funding of the cash portion (RKLB cash was $1.21B at Q1 2026) and the size of the stock component will shape the balance sheet; watch for debt issuance.
- Neutron linkage — whether constellation replenishment becomes a captive [[Neutron]] manifest.
- Cohort read-through — [[AST SpaceMobile]], [[EchoStar]], and other direct-to-device names as the scarcity-of-spectrum theme re-prices.
