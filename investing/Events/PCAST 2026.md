# PCAST 2026

President Trump appointed his Council of Advisers on Science and Technology, co-chaired by [[David Sacks]] and [[Michael Kratsios]] (OSTP director). The modern incarnation dates to George H.W. Bush in 1990, but this version is weighted toward builders and industry operators rather than pure academics — a deliberate departure. 15 of 24 slots filled; 9 remain.

## Scope

Sacks transitioned from Special Government Employee (AI/crypto czar, ~130 days) to PCAST co-chair, broadening his advisory role beyond AI and crypto to:

- AI policy and regulation
- Nuclear power
- Quantum computing
- Advanced semiconductors
- Biotech

---

## Context: the [[China]] race

[[David Friedberg]] framed the urgency: 10 years ago, [[China]] published 50% of the number of peer-reviewed scientific papers as the [[United States]]. Last year, China published 50% more. This inversion spans all disciplines — physics, materials science, chemistry, life sciences. China is no longer a copycat; in many subdomains it is becoming the scientific leader. Friedberg: *"There's this moment that we're in right now where both the world is being reinvented by AI, but there's this extraordinary race with China, not just in fundamental research and discovery, but in the industrialization of new discoveries and new technologies."*

The council's emphasis on "doers and builders" — people who have created products and companies — reflects a shift toward industrial competition, not just basic research. Sacks: *"If you're going to make recommendations about advanced semiconductors, don't you want to have someone who actually invented some of the key products in the space?"*

---

## Online child safety

The administration's new AI framework, which PCAST contributed to, includes support for age assurance technology and parental controls. Sacks distinguishes between social media (where harms are debated) and AI chat apps (where he sees net positive for children learning): *"In China, they're incorporating AI into K through 12 education. Are we going to ban it for our kids and teenagers? I think that'd be a terrible mistake."*
