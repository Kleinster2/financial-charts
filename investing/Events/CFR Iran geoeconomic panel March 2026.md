# CFR Iran geoeconomic panel March 2026

[[Council on Foreign Relations|CFR]] convened a 72-minute expert panel (March 17, 2026) — "The Geoeconomic Ripple Effects of the Iran War" — producing the most comprehensive structural framing of the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|2026 Iran conflict's]] economic consequences. Panelists: [[Megan O'Sullivan]] ([[Belfer Center]]/[[Harvard]], former Deputy NSA), [[Ed Morse]] ([[Hartree Partners]], commodity strategist), [[Brad Setser]] ([[Council on Foreign Relations|CFR]], former Treasury), [[Karen Young]] ([[Columbia Center on Global Energy Policy|Columbia CGEP]], Gulf political economy). Moderated by [[Eddie Fishman]] (CFR Greenberg Center director).

---

## Key findings

### 1. Iran went existential — and Washington didn't see it coming

O'Sullivan's core argument: the Trump administration's calculation was based on two prior precedents (Soleimani assassination, nuclear strikes) where Iran didn't escalate. But once the operation killed both the supreme leader *and* military leadership, it became an existential war. [[Iran]] reached for its ultimate leverage — [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure — in a way Washington apparently didn't anticipate. The overconfidence was the miscalculation.

### 2. There is no assured way to reopen the strait

Morse was blunt: military escorts, subsidized insurance, and SPR releases are all insufficient. The drone threat (proven effective by the Ukraine-Russia conflict) means that even degraded Iranian capabilities can still threaten tanker transit. The economics: a $100M tanker earning $20M per voyage won't risk destruction when the crew's lives and 2+ years of replacement time are at stake. Insurance pricing is irrelevant when the asset itself is irreplaceable. Only Iran deciding to stop — or being worn down sufficiently — reopens the strait.

### 3. The $30/bbl oil move understates the real risk

Setser's macro arithmetic: $10/bbl = ~10bp off US GDP. At $30 up, that's ~0.3pp — noticeable but manageable. But if 10M bbl/day is actually lost for sustained period, supply-demand elasticity math implies oil at $170+ — which would mean ~1pp+ hit to US GDP, probably more with non-linear effects. Current prices reflect a probability-weighted average between "quick resolution" and "multi-month blockade." If the latter materializes, prices haven't moved enough.

### 4. Gulf damage is radically uneven

[[Karen Young]]'s data from [[Goldman Sachs]] (assuming hostilities end by early May, gradual Hormuz resumption):

| Country | GDP contraction (2026E) | Driver |
|---------|------------------------|--------|
| [[Qatar]] | -14% | LNG exports 100% Hormuz-dependent |
| [[Kuwait]] | -14% | No pipeline bypass |
| [[UAE]] | -5% | Fujairah pipeline partially operational, but hit by drone debris; foreign investor outflows |
| [[Saudi Arabia]] | -3% | East-west pipeline to Red Sea functional; Aramco anchor |
| [[Oman]] | -1% | Outside Hormuz, but still targeted |
| [[Iraq]] | Severe (unquantified) | 90% of government revenue from oil; KRG pipeline dispute blocking alternative |

### 5. US energy independence is a myth — the US is "six countries"

Morse's framework: Alaska (exporter), Gulf Coast (exporter), East Coast (importer), West Coast (importer), Canadian border (two-way), Pacific NW interior (self-sufficient). The coasts experience global supply shocks like any net importing country regardless of aggregate US production. The [[Jones Act]] makes it worse — it has failed every time the government tried to lift it except small emergencies. See [[US Energy Independence Six Countries]].

### 6. Russia is the clear geopolitical winner

Setser: Russian Urals crude went from $50 to ~$100. Sanctions effectively lifted (two US licenses — first India-only, then universal). Russia's break-even is $30-40/bbl vs Saudi's ~$90. Financial pressure on Russia to limit Ukraine operations has disappeared. The panel saw no realistic scenario where the Trump administration reimposed sanctions.

### 7. China is relatively insulated but not immune

O'Sullivan: China has more tools — 1.2B barrel stockpile (~80-104 days), fuel switching ability (back to coal, freeing LNG cargoes for Asia), demand management capacity. But 70%+ of China's oil is imported, and Iran/Russia suppliers are both strategically compromised. Setser added: this "ratifies China's decision to stockpile oil rather than US Treasuries" and validates China's electrification strategy.

### 8. Gulf states will demand more from the US — or diversify away

O'Sullivan: Gulf states housed US bases without defense treaties. They'll now demand formal defense agreements or diversify weapons procurement. Young: expect more defense spending (taking away from Vision 2030 diversification), more supply chain diversification toward China (renewables manufacturing), and a fundamental reassessment of the US security umbrella that's been fraying since the 2019 [[Saudi Aramco|Abqaiq]] attack.

### 9. Even a short war leaves a permanent Hormuz risk premium

Morse explicitly stated the change won't be structural like 1973 (OPEC empowerment, nationalization of oil majors). But the demonstrated ability to close Hormuz with drones means oil, shipping, and insurance markets will price a structural risk premium indefinitely. Future crises will be priced differently. Bearish for any asset whose valuation assumes stable, cheap Middle Eastern energy supply.

### 10. The AI / data center Gulf nexus is at risk

Fishman raised the point that US AI leadership depends on Gulf data center buildout (access to cheap power). Young responded that Gulf sovereign funds will continue deploying capital into tech globally — but with "a lot more caution," and the physical buildout in-region faces obvious security concerns after drone strikes on UAE infrastructure.
