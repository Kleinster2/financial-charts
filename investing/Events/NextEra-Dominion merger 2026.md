# NextEra-Dominion merger 2026

## Synthesis

[[NextEra Energy]] and [[Dominion Energy]] announced on May 18, 2026 that they had entered into a definitive all-stock combination. The deal is the power-sector consolidation version of the AI infrastructure buildout: NextEra brings low-cost generation development, renewables, gas, storage, and utility capital-markets access; Dominion brings the regulated utility serving the northern Virginia data-center load center and a large [[PJM]] footprint.

---

## Deal Terms

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Announcement | May 18, 2026 |
| Structure | All-stock combination |
| Deal value | $66.8B equity value per [[Reuters]]; pro forma enterprise value about $420B and market capitalization about $249B |
| Exchange ratio | 0.8138 [[NextEra Energy]] shares for each [[Dominion Energy]] share |
| Ownership | NextEra holders about 74.5%; Dominion holders about 25.5% |
| Dominion cash/dividend | Dominion holders keep the current quarterly dividend through closing plus a one-time $360M cash payment at close |
| Customer credits | Proposed $2.25B in bill credits for Dominion customers in Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina over two years post-close |
| Closing target | 12-18 months / second half of 2027, subject to shareholder, antitrust, [[FERC]], nuclear-regulator, and state utility approvals |
| Name / ticker | Combined company to operate as NextEra Energy and trade as NEE |

## Combined Platform

| Metric | Pro forma figure |
|---|---|
| Utility customer accounts | About 10M across [[Florida]], Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina |
| Owned generation | 110 GW across a broad power mix |
| Large-load pipeline | More than 130 GW of large-load opportunities |
| Business mix | More than 80% regulated |
| Strategic geography | Florida growth utility + Dominion's Virginia data-center load + entry into [[PJM]] at scale |

The key asset is not only generation capacity. It is the combination of load rights, regulated rate-base growth, and project-development capability. Dominion had nearly 51 GW of contracted data-center capacity around Virginia, while NextEra had already been building a [[BYOP]] product around off-system gas, renewables, batteries, and nuclear restart capacity.

## Market Reaction

The first-day tape was not a clean endorsement from NextEra holders. Dominion traded like a target receiving a premium; NextEra traded like an acquirer taking on valuation, regulatory, financing, and execution risk.

| Date | NextEra / NEE | Dominion / D | Read |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| May 18, 2026 | Down about 5% | Up about 9-10% | Market rewarded Dominion's takeout premium but discounted the risk that NextEra is paying up for already-hot AI-power utility assets. |

The all-stock structure matters: Dominion holders receive 0.8138 NEE shares for each D share, so NEE's selloff immediately reduces the realized value of Dominion's consideration. The market reaction therefore says "strategically logical, financially and politically unproven," not "obvious win." The burden of proof sits with NextEra: closing approvals, ratepayer protections, data-center cost allocation, and whether the combined platform can earn regulated returns on the AI-power buildout without triggering political backlash.

### Implied completion risk

Using May 18, 2026 closes, the spread implies a large discount to completion, though not a literal probability because the deal has a 12-18 month path, dividends, hedging costs, and regulatory time value.

| Input | Value |
|---|---:|
| NEE close | $89.04 |
| D close | $67.56 |
| Exchange ratio | 0.8138 NEE per D |
| Pro-rata $360M cash payment | About $0.41 per D share |
| Implied current deal value | ~$72.87 per D share |
| Gross spread | ~$5.31 / ~7.9% |
| D pre-deal close / simple break value | $61.73 |
| Binary close probability using break value | ~52% close / ~48% fail |

The binary math is deliberately crude: `D price = P(close) * deal value + P(fail) * break value`. With D at $67.56, current deal value around $72.87, and a $61.73 break value, the implied close probability is roughly 52%. That should be read as "market is charging a material discount for approval and execution risk," not as a precise forecast that the deal is more likely than not to fail.

External corroboration is directional, not a published 52/48 probability. [[RBC Capital]] raised its Dominion price target to $72 after the announcement, kept a Sector Perform rating, and highlighted Virginia/South Carolina/North Carolina regulatory approval timelines plus potential NEPOOL divestiture requirements. That sits close to the $72.87 current implied deal value while still embedding a risk discount versus the announced implied value around $77. The spread interpretation follows standard [[Merger Arbitrage|merger arbitrage]] logic: the target's discount to consideration compensates for time-to-close and completion risk.

Other external risk markers point the same way, but none publish a precise close/fail probability:

## Why It Matters

This turns the AI power problem from a PPA/project-finance story into a regulated-utility consolidation story. The earlier AI infrastructure cycle was hyperscalers signing power contracts or building behind-the-meter generation. This deal says the load center itself is worth owning if the buyer also has enough development scale to serve it.

For [[NextEra Energy]], the deal adds the Virginia/North Carolina/South Carolina regulated platforms and a major [[PJM]] growth option to its existing Florida utility and national development platform. For [[Dominion Energy]], it monetizes the data-center load thesis before the full capital and regulatory burden of serving it lands on Dominion's standalone balance sheet.

The [[Financial Times|FT]] framing is the important second-order question: the merger may create the platform needed to build faster, but it also makes the fight over who pays for the AI boom more explicit. The proposed bill credits help sell the transaction; they do not by themselves answer how future generation, transmission, interconnection, and reliability costs get allocated between hyperscalers and ordinary ratepayers.

### Relation to the broader energy-security capex cycle

This deal is the canonical M&A expression of the US AI-power leg of the [[Energy security as investment thesis]] — the framework that organizes the 2026 renewable-equity rally as a sovereignty ([[Europe]]) plus AI-power (US) capital-flow story. Per the FT May 3 2026 piece on clean-energy fund flows (Emily Herbert and Ramsay Hodgson), [[Nordea Asset Management]] CIO Kasper Elmgreen described the US side as "all hands on deck . . . they need more generation and they need to invest in the grid." This merger consolidates exactly that: 110 GW of owned generation + more than 130 GW of large-load opportunities + [[PJM]] entry at scale.

The structural read connecting the two: where the FT article identified the *flows* into US AI-power names ([[GE Vernova]] +65% YTD, [[NextEra Energy]] +22% YTD), this deal is the *corporate response* — the largest of those names buying its way into the most concentrated US AI-load utility. The merger validates the thesis at the M&A layer while sharpening one of the thesis's stated risks (regulatory backlash on data-center cost allocation).

## Read-throughs

- [[NextEra Energy]]: shifts from winning hyperscaler power contracts to buying one of the most valuable regulated AI-load territories.
- [[Dominion Energy]]: becomes the target that proves Virginia data-center load is a strategic asset, not just a capex burden.
- [[PJM]]: gets a new utility-scale builder with generation, storage, and procurement muscle inside the region's resource-adequacy problem.
- [[BYOP]]: broadens from site-level generation to utility-platform strategy; the buyer can now pair large-load customers, regulated grid upgrades, and owned generation development.
- [[DC power prices]]: ratepayer protection becomes the central political test, especially in data-center-heavy states.
- [[M&A tracker]]: confirms power and AI infrastructure as a 2026 mega-deal category alongside [[Constellation Energy]]-[[Calpine]] and other power take-private activity.

---

## Watch For

- Same-day consumer-advocacy opposition already centers on whether temporary bill credits offset future rate-base increases tied to AI/data-center infrastructure. Track this inside the merger approval path for now rather than as a separate crisis note.
- Whether state utility regulators condition approval on data-center cost allocation, bill-credit mechanics, or ring-fencing of Dominion's regulated utilities.
- Whether [[FERC]] focuses on [[PJM]] market power, interconnection control, or generation concentration.
- Whether the deal accelerates more utility M&A around data-center load, especially in regions with constrained grid queues.
- Whether hyperscalers respond with more direct [[BYOP]] contracts to preserve leverage against a larger combined utility.
