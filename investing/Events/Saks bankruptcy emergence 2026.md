# Saks bankruptcy emergence 2026

**June 26, 2026** — [[Saks Global]] formally exited Chapter 11, rebranding the parent to Exemplar Luxury Group (ELG). Debt cut ~75%, $500M new financing closed, store footprint cut from 148 to 49. Closes the case begun by the [[Saks bankruptcy|January 14, 2026 filing]].

Brand portfolio survives; discount format does not. Saks Fifth Avenue, Neiman Marcus, and Bergdorf Goodman all retain their names and logos. The strategy pivot ends the Saks OFF 5th discount-era approach and abandons [[Richard Baker|Baker]]'s real-estate-as-leverage era — the company will focus on high-end department store shopping and white-glove service.

## Restructuring terms

The emergence ratifies the restructuring plan sketched in the original [[Saks bankruptcy]] filing:

| Term | Plan (Jan 2026) | Actual (Jun 2026) |
|------|-----------------|--------------------|
| Exit financing | $500M | $500M ✓ |
| Debt-for-equity swap | Creditors take ownership | Confirmed |
| Store closures | Mid-size cities / off-price | 148 → 49 stores total (brand split TBD); Feb 10 announcement specified 8 SFA + 1 NMG |
| Amazon equity | Wiped | Wiped |
| Bergdorf sale | Attracting buyers | Retained (no sale) |

The plan's predicted outcomes largely materialized, with one notable hold: Bergdorf Goodman was not sold and remains part of the portfolio.

## Why it matters

For luxury retail:
- [[Department store decline]] thesis gets a data point on survival — not just failure
- Three of the oldest US luxury emporiums preserved as going concerns
- Validates the conglomerate-of-luxury-brands model over discount-format diversification
- Vendor relationships ([[Chanel]], [[LVMH]], [[Kering]] — top unsecured creditors in filing) restart with cleaner balance sheet

For credit markets:
- Another private-credit LBO resolution: creditors took ownership, original equity wiped
- [[Amazon]]'s $475M strategic investment confirmed lost
- Bondholders who arranged the DIP (Pentwater, Bracebridge) converted to equity stakes

For the broader [[Saks bankruptcy]] story:
- Closes the loop on the [[Saks-Neiman merger]] disaster — combined entity proved non-viable in its original leveraged form
- The flagship [[611 Fifth Avenue]] survives as ongoing collateral, not a liquidation asset
- Real estate "hidden value" thesis ([[Retail real estate as hidden value]]) preserved but locked — not monetized via liquidation

---

## Market Reaction

No public-equity event tape applies: ELG is private and the emergence was a creditor-owned restructuring, not a listed-company M&A close. The relevant market read is credit recovery and vendor normalization, with [[Amazon]]'s $475M equity confirmed wiped and original bondholders converting into post-emergence ownership.
