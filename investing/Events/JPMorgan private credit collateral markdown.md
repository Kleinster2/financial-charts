#event #credit #banks #risk #systemic

**JPMorgan private credit collateral markdown** — On March 11, 2026, [[JPMorgan Chase|JPMorgan]]'s trading division marked down the value of software company loans used as collateral by private credit funds in its back-leverage financing business. The first major bank to remark private credit collateral values, shifting the [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]] from fund-level stress (redemptions, gates) to the bank-funding layer that underpins private credit's leverage model.

---

## What happened

JPMorgan's Wall Street trading division reduced the value of loans — mostly made to software companies — sitting within the financing portfolios of private credit clients. The markdowns apply to the bank's **back-leverage** business, where private credit funds borrow from JPM against their loan portfolios to amplify fund returns.

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Business unit | JPM trading division (financing/back-leverage) |
| Collateral marked down | Software company loans in private credit portfolios |
| Trigger | Pre-emptive — no margin calls, no actual loan losses |
| Precedent | JPM pulled back leverage during early COVID (2020) |
| Scale of markdowns | Not disclosed |
| First mover? | **Yes** — potentially first major bank to take this step (FT) |

**The mechanism:**

```
Private credit funds pledge loan portfolios as collateral to JPM
→ JPM provides back-leverage (borrowing facility) based on collateral value
→ JPM marks down software loan collateral
→ Funds can borrow less against those portfolios
→ Some funds may need to post additional collateral
→ Reduced leverage = reduced returns = more NAV pressure
```

JPMorgan characterized the move as financial discipline — "important to do when markets warrant it rather than waiting for a crisis to come along."

## Why this is a qualitative escalation

This is the first time the private credit crisis has reached the **bank-funding layer**. Every prior event was fund-level:

| Date | Event | Stress layer |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Feb 19 | [[Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate]] | Fund — retail investors |
| Mar 3 | Blackstone BCRED record 7.9% redemption | Fund — retail investors |
| Mar 6 | BlackRock/HPS gate at 5% | Fund — retail investors |
| Mar 9 | Goldman TRS to short software loans | Market — derivative pricing |
| **Mar 11** | **JPMorgan collateral markdown** | **Bank — funding infrastructure** |

**Back-leverage is how private credit amplifies returns.** It's leverage upon leverage — private credit funds lend to companies (layer 1), then borrow from banks against those loans (layer 2). CNBC described this as "relatively risky because it layers leverage upon leverage — amplifying losses when the underlying loans sour."

When the bank marks down the collateral, it's not just one fund's problem — it's a systemic tightening of the leverage available to the entire industry.

---

## The two-sided squeeze

Private credit funds now face pressure from both directions simultaneously:

| Pressure | Source | Mechanism |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| **Outflows** (demand side) | Retail investors | Redemption requests → gates or forced asset sales |
| **Leverage withdrawal** (supply side) | Banks (JPM first) | Collateral markdowns → reduced borrowing capacity |

This is structurally similar to what happened to mortgage-backed securities in 2007-2008: banks that provided repo financing to mortgage funds marked down collateral, reducing leverage, which forced funds to sell assets, which depressed prices, which triggered more markdowns — a reflexive deleveraging spiral.

The difference: private credit is smaller ($1.8T vs $10T+ mortgage market) and JPM is acting pre-emptively rather than reactively. But the mechanism is the same.

## Will other banks follow?

**Competitive dynamics say yes.** No bank wants to be the last one holding overvalued collateral. Once JPM moves:

- **Goldman Sachs** — already pitching TRS products to short software loans (Mar 9). Clearly bearish on the sector. Likely to follow on collateral marks
- **Morgan Stanley** — large prime brokerage and financing business. Will face client pressure to match JPM terms
- **Citi** — analysts already warned (Feb 24) about BDC→CLO leverage transmission. Institutional awareness is high
- **BofA** — called Blue Owl a buy (Feb 26) but that's the research side. The trading/financing side has different incentives

**The 2020 precedent:** During COVID, JPM pulled back leverage first. Others followed within weeks. The financing business is oligopolistic — banks watch each other closely.

## Crisis progression

Where JPMorgan's move sits in the broader arc of the [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]]:

1. ✅ **Equity repricing** (Jan-Feb) — SaaS stocks -25% to -50%
2. ✅ **Primary market freeze** (Feb-Mar) — no major software debt deal since [[Oracle]] $25B (Feb 2)
3. ✅ **Retail BDC gates** (Feb 19 →) — [[Blue Owl]], [[BlackRock]]/[[HPS Investment Partners|HPS]]
4. ✅ **Record redemptions** (Mar 3) — [[Blackstone]] BCRED 7.9%
5. ✅ **Derivative instruments** (Mar 9) — [[Goldman Sachs]] TRS to short software loans
6. ✅ **Bank collateral markdowns** (Mar 11) — JPMorgan restricts back-leverage ← **this event**
7. ⏳ **Other banks follow?** — competitive dynamics make it likely
8. ⏳ **March 31 quarter-end** — >$100B in funds face simultaneous redemption windows

Each stage brings more sophisticated participants and larger systemic implications. The progression follows a textbook credit cycle: equity sells off first (most liquid), then primary issuance freezes (originators pull back), then retail flees (redemptions/gates), then Wall Street builds tools to price the risk (derivatives), then banks tighten the plumbing (collateral markdowns).

## Insights

- **Bank collateral markdowns are a leading indicator, not a lagging one.** JPM moved before defaults spiked — the same pattern as COVID. Banks have better real-time data on borrower health than fund quarterly marks show. JPM is telling the market what it sees in its own loan data
- **Back-leverage is the hidden amplifier in private credit.** The industry's returns depend on borrowing from banks to lever up loan portfolios. When that leverage gets pulled, the math changes for every fund simultaneously — returns compress even without a single default
- **The crisis progression follows a textbook credit cycle:** equity repricing → primary market freeze → retail flight → derivative creation → bank leverage withdrawal. Each stage brings more sophisticated participants and larger systemic implications. JPM's move is stage 6 of what could be 8
