**Ackman-Icahn Herbalife feud** — The most public hedge fund battle in history. [[Bill Ackman]] shorted $1B of [[Herbalife]] in December 2012, calling it "the best-managed pyramid scheme in the history of the world." [[Carl Icahn]] took the opposite side — building a 16.5% long position explicitly to squeeze Ackman. [[Third Point]] (Dan Loeb) also went long. The war lasted six years, produced the most famous moment in financial television (CNBC, January 25, 2013), and ended with Ackman losing ~$1B and Icahn making ~$1.3B.

## Ackman's short thesis

Ackman's December 2012 presentation argued:
- [[Herbalife]]'s compensation plan rewarded distributor recruitment, not retail sales
- The bottom 90% of distributors lost money on average
- "Nutrition clubs" in Latino communities were recruiting centers, not genuine retail
- Revenue was driven by distributors buying inventory they couldn't sell (inventory loading)
- The company met all the criteria the FTC had used to shut down other pyramid schemes

He also spent ~$50M on a PR and lobbying campaign — hiring community organizers, funding academic research, and lobbying the FTC and state attorneys general directly.

Target price: $0 (total equity wipeout).

---

## Icahn's counter-thesis

Icahn's position was partly financial, partly personal. He believed Herbalife was a legitimate business with real demand for its products — but the real motivation was the Hallwood Realty grudge from 2003.

In a January 2013 letter, Icahn said he saw "enormous opportunity" in HLF at depressed prices. He built his stake to 16.5%, became the largest shareholder, and gained board representation. The position served a dual purpose: profit from a squeeze and publicly humiliate Ackman.

---

## The CNBC confrontation (January 25, 2013)

The most famous moment in financial television. Ackman was live on CNBC's "Halftime Report" discussing Herbalife when anchor Scott Wapner brought Icahn on by phone — unannounced.

For approximately 30 minutes, the two billionaires traded personal insults on live television. Icahn called Ackman "a liar" and "a crybaby in the schoolyard." Ackman responded that Icahn was "not an honest guy" and had "the worst reputation of any investor on Wall Street." Icahn brought up Hallwood Realty: "I wouldn't invest in any fund that this guy manages."

Trading on the NYSE floor reportedly stopped as traders watched the screens. The segment generated more social media traffic than any CNBC segment in the network's history. HLF rose ~5% that day.

## FTC settlement (July 15, 2016)

The FTC's $200M settlement was the turning point. The commission required "fundamental restructuring" of Herbalife's compensation and found that distributors "often incurred significant expenses pursuing the business opportunity" — but never used the word "pyramid scheme."

The market read this as Herbalife surviving. The stock, which had been in limbo for two years during the investigation, rallied. Ackman maintained his position for another 18 months, insisting the restructuring would destroy the business model. It didn't.

## Significance

The feud demonstrated several dynamics:

The short-selling problem: even when a short thesis has merit (the FTC did force restructuring), the market can remain irrational longer than a short seller can remain solvent. Ackman's thesis was partly right — Herbalife's compensation plan did exploit distributors — but "partly right" on a short is still a losing trade if the company survives.

The personal dimension: Icahn's entry was not pure alpha-seeking. He explicitly told CNBC he bought Herbalife to "teach Ackman a lesson." The Hallwood Realty grudge from 2003 — nearly a decade old — drove a billion-dollar position. Markets are not always rational actors.

The asymmetry: Ackman staked his reputation on a $0 price target. The only way to fully win a short-to-zero thesis is if the company actually goes to zero. Anything short of that — even an FTC settlement requiring fundamental restructuring — is a loss for the short seller.
