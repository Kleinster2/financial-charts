# Anthropic IPO 2026

[[Anthropic]] confidentially filed a draft registration statement on Form S-1 with the [[SEC]] on June 1, 2026, formally beginning the process toward a Wall Street listing that investors expect to value the [[Claude]] maker above $1tn. The filing converts the vault's prior "IPO discussion / readiness" framing into a concrete dated event and puts Anthropic ahead of [[OpenAI]], which the [[Financial Times]] says is preparing its own confidential filing imminently. It also makes Anthropic the second of the three trophy AI/space issuers to formally enter the public-market queue, after [[SpaceX IPO 2026]] (public S-1 May 20) and ahead of OpenAI.

The filing is confidential, so no financial statements, share count, or price range are public. Anthropic's own statement: the draft registration "gives us the option to go public after the SEC completes its review," and "the proposed initial public offering will depend on market conditions and other factors." A listing could follow "within a few months" of SEC review.

## OpenAI's on-stage reaction

The filing was broken to [[OpenAI]] CFO [[Sarah Friar]] live at [[Liquidity 2026]] hours after announcement. Her response (All-In, Jun 2 2026): "It does not mean anything yet... you have to run now the gauntlet of the SEC, and who knows how long that takes for anyone." On sequencing: "the market is a weighing machine, not a popularity machine. No one remembers who won first, Google or Yahoo, Lyft or Uber" — and she framed the trio as "potentially three IPOs at a scale that will be bigger even than 2001, 2000," with capital returned via buybacks and M&A leaving "a lot of money sitting on the sidelines" to absorb them.

## Why now — the valuation and revenue arc

Anthropic's private valuation has risen more than 15-fold in 15 months: a March 2025 round valued the company at about $60bn; the May 28 2026 Series H closed at $965B post-money. The filing rides explosive revenue growth — annualized revenue (a run-rate estimate favored by fast-growing startups) has increased fivefold since the start of 2026, passing $47bn last month, up from roughly $10bn of annual revenue last year.

| Metric | Value | Note |
|--------|-------|------|
| March 2025 round | ~$60bn | Base for the 15-fold framing |
| May 28 2026 Series H | $65bn raised, $965B post-money | Co-led by [[Altimeter Capital]], [[Dragoneer]], [[Greenoaks]], [[Sequoia Capital]], [[Capital Group]], [[Coatue]], [[D1 Capital Partners]]; new public-market money from Capital Group and [[Baillie Gifford]] |
| 2026 run-rate revenue | >$47bn last month | 5x since start of 2026; up from ~$10bn annual last year |
| Expected IPO valuation | >$1tn | Investor anticipation; no target set |

Beyond the financial co-leads, the Series H pulled in strategic chip and memory partners — [[Samsung]], [[SK Hynix]], and [[Micron]] — alongside roughly $15bn of previously committed hyperscaler money, including $5bn from [[Amazon]] ([Anthropic, "Series H"](https://www.anthropic.com/news/series-h), May 28 2026). The memory-maker participation ties the raise to the compute-supply chain rather than to pure financial capital, and feeds reported expectations that [[Samsung]] could win AI-chip foundry orders from Anthropic — folding the listing into the same chip-supply story the vault tracks under [[Memory shortage 2025-2026]] and [[Foundry Wars]].

The growth engine is enterprise-first: the coding model [[Claude Code]] and the AI agent [[Claude Cowork]] are the named traction drivers. The cyber model [[Claude Mythos|Mythos]] — being rolled out to US allies including the EU — is the capability story that "spooked governments and financial institutions," sitting alongside the revenue narrative rather than driving near-term sales.

## The compute-obligation counterweight

The IPO is also a financing event against a heavy fixed-capacity ramp. Anthropic has struck compute deals with [[Google]], [[Broadcom]], and [[Amazon]] "potentially worth hundreds of billions" over coming years, and last month agreed to lease two data-center campuses from [[SpaceX]] worth $15bn a year to the rocket maker. The underwriting question for a listing is therefore not whether Anthropic is more capital-efficient than [[OpenAI]] — the private market already priced that relative advantage at $965B — but whether enterprise revenue scales fast enough to cover the absolute-dollar capacity obligations after the Series H. See the [[Anthropic#IPO-readiness stress test (May 2026)|actor-note stress test]] and [[AI infrastructure financing risk]].

## The three-issuer absorption test

The filing slots Anthropic into the combined public-market capacity test the vault tracks in [[2026 IPO pipeline]]. Together, [[SpaceX]], OpenAI, and Anthropic — three of the most valuable private companies in history — would present public markets with their largest listings of all time, comparable in scale to [[Saudi Aramco]]'s December 2019 IPO ($29bn raised at $1.7tn). The FT frames the trio as a single test of how much AI/space scarcity public buyers can absorb at once.

| Issuer | Status (June 1 2026) | Expected scale |
|--------|----------------------|----------------|
| [[SpaceX]] | Public S-1 filed May 20; pricing as early as June 11 | ~$1.75tn-$2tn target; up to $75B raise |
| Anthropic | Confidential draft S-1 filed June 1 | >$1tn expected; listing in coming months |
| [[OpenAI]] | Confidential draft S-1 filed June 8 (FT had flagged it "imminent" on June 1) | ~$852B recent private mark |

The [[2026 IPO pipeline#May 25 Reuters aftermarket screen|Reuters aftermarket screen]] sharpens the risk: even oversubscribed trophy IPOs have underperformed the [[S&P 500]] roughly three-quarters of the time over the past five years, because private investors capture most of the compounding before the public buyer arrives. Anthropic's near-$1tn private mark means the public buyer's margin of safety is already thin.

## Market signal

Anthropic is private, so there is no listed-equity reaction to measure directly from the filing. The relevant market signals are (1) the private-round valuation progression (March 2025 ~$60bn → May 28 2026 $965B) and the report that the Series H is likely the last private round, both of which the [[2026 IPO pipeline]] and [[Anthropic]] notes track; (2) prediction-market IPO-timing overlays ([[Kalshi]], [[Polymarket]]) maintained in [[2026 IPO pipeline]]; and (3) indirect listed read-through via Anthropic's public compute partners [[Amazon]], [[Alphabet]]/[[Google]], and [[Broadcom]], none of which is a clean same-day catalyst from a confidential filing. No `/event-tape` run applies — there is no tradeable primary actor.

## Synthesis

The filing converts a long-running "Anthropic is weighing an IPO" narrative into a dated, primary-sourced event, and the more important signal sits underneath the headline valuation. First, the sequencing: with [[SpaceX]] public-filed (May 20) and Anthropic now confidentially filed (June 1) ahead of [[OpenAI]], the [[2026 IPO pipeline|mega-IPO absorption test]] stops being a hypothetical and becomes a scheduling problem — public markets may be asked to digest two or three trillion-dollar-scale AI/space listings in a single window. Second, the "likely last private round" framing on the $65B Series H is the real tell: Anthropic is signalling that the next marginal dollar of equity comes from public investors, not private crossover funds, at exactly the point ($965B private mark, >$1tn anticipated) where the private market has already captured most of the compounding. The [[2026 IPO pipeline#May 25 Reuters aftermarket screen|Reuters base-rate warning]] applies with full force.

The non-obvious read is that the IPO is as much a financing event as a liquidity event. Anthropic's revenue arc is genuinely steep — run-rate fivefold to $47bn in five months — but the filing lands on top of [[Google]] / [[Broadcom]] / [[Amazon]] compute commitments "potentially worth hundreds of billions" and ~$15B/year of [[SpaceX]] data-center leases. The capital-efficiency story that distinguished Anthropic from OpenAI survives per-unit but erodes in absolute dollars; the public market will underwrite whether enterprise revenue ([[Claude Code]], [[Claude Cowork]]) outruns the capacity ramp. That places the durable analytical weight on [[AI infrastructure financing risk]], not on the trophy valuation itself.
