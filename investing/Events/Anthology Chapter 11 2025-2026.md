#event #bankruptcy #edtech #lms #restructuring

**Anthology Chapter 11 2025-2026** — [[Anthology]], parent of [[Blackboard]] and the largest legacy challenger in the US higher-education learning management system market, filed voluntary Chapter 11 petitions in the United States Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of Texas on September 29, 2025 and emerged February 27, 2026 with approximately $1.6 billion of funded debt eliminated. Under the plan, the Teaching & Learning business emerged debt-free as a standalone Blackboard Inc. owned by [[Oaktree Capital]] and Nexus Capital, while the Enterprise Operations business sold to [[Ellucian]] for ~$70 million and the Lifecycle Engagement & Student Success business sold to Encoura LE for ~$50 million. The event ends Anthology's 2020-2025 roll-up strategy as a coherent enterprise and reshapes the LMS competitive landscape in a single direction: away from Blackboard and toward [[Canvas LMS]] ([[Instructure]]) and [[D2L Brightspace]].

## Asset sales

The Chapter 11 carved Anthology into four pieces and sold three of them to credit-cycle buyers, leaving the LMS-only core to emerge.

| Buyer | Asset | Closed | Price |
|-------|-------|--------|-------|
| [[Ellucian]] | Enterprise Operations (SIS, finance, ERP for higher ed) | December 31, 2025 | ~$70M |
| Encoura LE LLC | Lifecycle Engagement & Student Success | January 31, 2026 | ~$50M |
| [[Oaktree Capital]] + Nexus Capital | Teaching & Learning (Blackboard core) | February 27, 2026 | Equitization (debt-for-equity) |

The Ellucian acquisition is meaningful for [[Ellucian]] — it consolidates the higher-ed SIS market further. The standalone Blackboard that emerged keeps the LMS product line but loses scale, cross-sell breadth, and a balance sheet to fund competitive deals.

## Background

Anthology was assembled by [[Veritas Capital]] beginning in July 2020, combining Campus Management, Campus Labs, and iModules into a single higher-ed software platform. In October 2021 — at the absolute peak of edtech multiples — Anthology acquired [[Blackboard]] for an enterprise value reported above $3 billion. The Blackboard deal added the LMS core but also added the legacy debt and turnaround complexity, since Blackboard had already been a sequential PE workout (Providence Equity Partners had taken it private in 2011 for $1.64B and held through multiple recap events before exiting).

The combined Anthology carried roughly $1.625 billion of merger debt against an integration thesis that never converged. Over 2024-2025 the company reported EBITDA collapsing from approximately $33 million to $4 million as customers continued to defect to [[Canvas LMS]] and [[D2L Brightspace]] and as the integration costs of the four-platform roll-up exceeded the cross-sell gains. The capital structure was unsustainable against that operating trajectory, and the September 2025 filing was the orderly recognition of that math.

## Customer migrations during the period

Several large institutional customers announced moves off Blackboard during 2025-2026 — visible signal that the bankruptcy was a lagging indicator of competitive loss, not a triggering event:

- [[University of North Carolina]] system Board of Governors mandated [[Canvas LMS]] across all 17 campuses
- University of Miami transitioned to Canvas
- Ohio University transitioned to Canvas
- North Greenville University transitioned to Canvas
- Several CUNY schools migrated off Blackboard

By Spring 2025 [[Edutechnica]] data, [[Canvas LMS]] held approximately 50% of North American higher-ed enrollment, [[D2L Brightspace]] held 20% and growing, and Blackboard had fallen to roughly 12%. For the first time, Canvas's enrollment share exceeded the next three competitors combined.

## Competitive consequence

The bankruptcy removes Blackboard as a credible enterprise sales threat for at least the next refresh cycle. The new debt-free Blackboard has scale to retain existing customers but limited firepower for greenfield deals against well-capitalized competitors. The two beneficiaries are clear:

- [[Instructure]] / [[Canvas LMS]] — the take-private by [[KKR]] in November 2024 closed eleven months before Anthology filed, meaning KKR's 4.8B underwriting did not assume this competitive windfall. The actual Canvas market position emerging in 2026 is materially stronger than the deal model implied.
- [[D2L Brightspace]] — picks up institutions defecting from Blackboard who reject the dominant-vendor lock-in case for Canvas. Brightspace's North American share has grown from below 15% to roughly 20% during the period.

## Synthesis

The bankruptcy is best understood as the end-state of a 2020-2021 PE roll-up thesis colliding with a 2024-2026 rate environment. [[Veritas Capital]] paid Blackboard valuations sized for COVID-era multiples and assumed integration synergies that never converged across four legacy product platforms. The capital structure was a margin trade on multiple expansion that the rate path then unwound.

Three structural takeaways follow:

The first is that competitive position in vertical SaaS compounds geometrically once a leader passes 50% share. Canvas's North American higher-ed enrollment share of approximately 50% by Spring 2025 is past the threshold where prospective customers default to it absent a specific reason to choose otherwise — a self-reinforcing dynamic that becomes nearly impossible for a debt-strained competitor to interrupt. Anthology's filing is the tail-end recognition that the run-rate of customer losses to Canvas exceeded what its capital structure could absorb.

The second is that PE-owned roll-ups in distressed-margin verticals are uniquely vulnerable when their cost-of-capital re-prices. The Blackboard-Anthology asset had been a sequential PE workout for 14 years (Providence Equity 2011, Veritas 2020-2021, now Oaktree-Nexus) without a single owner returning the asset to organic growth at scale. Each handoff added debt and complexity without resetting the underlying competitive trajectory.

The third is that the surviving Blackboard, debt-free under [[Oaktree Capital]] and Nexus Capital, is now a fundamentally different kind of competitor — smaller, focused, with a balance sheet that supports product reinvestment rather than debt service. That makes it a more interesting watch for the next 24-36 months than the over-leveraged predecessor, even if it cannot threaten Canvas at the high end of the market.

## Comparable cases

| Case | Lesson |
|------|--------|
| [[Cornerstone OnDemand]] | PE-owned learning software ([[Clearlake Capital]]) with senior debt at 78¢ in March 2026 — same playbook of overlevered roll-up but in adjacent corporate L&D segment |
| [[Pluralsight]] | [[Vista Equity Partners]] portfolio, edtech-adjacent, also restructured |
| [[Instructure]] FY2023 | The flip side: 40.4% adj EBITDA margin, $530M revenue, durable growth — the asset Anthology was supposed to compete with |

The pattern is consistent: PE roll-ups in edtech that funded entry valuations with cycle-peak debt did not survive the rate environment of 2024-2026, while the operating-leadership SaaS asset (Canvas) survived sequential PE owners by being a structurally better business.
