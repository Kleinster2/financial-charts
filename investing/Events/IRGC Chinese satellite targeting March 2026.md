# IRGC Chinese satellite targeting March 2026

Leaked [[Iran]]ian military documents reported by the [[Financial Times]] show the [[IRGC]] Aerospace Force acquired a [[China|Chinese]]-built high-resolution imaging satellite — [[TEE-01B]] — in late 2024 and tasked it during the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|March 2026 war]] to surveil at least 10 US military bases and multiple items of civilian Gulf infrastructure. Imagery timestamps line up with IRGC-claimed drone and missile strikes, including confirmed damage to five US Air Force refuelling planes at [[Prince Sultan Air Base]] in [[Saudi Arabia]] on March 14. The satellite was built by [[Earth Eye Co]] via its "in-orbit delivery" model and operated through [[Emposat]] ground infrastructure — a commercial arrangement that preserves Iranian operational capability even when domestic ground stations are struck by [[Israel]].

## The satellite and its provenance

[[TEE-01B]] captures imagery at ~0.5-metre resolution — roughly an order of magnitude better than Iran's domestic military satellites and comparable to high-end Western commercial imagery (able to identify aircraft type, vehicle movement, changes to infrastructure).

## Commercial architecture — in-orbit delivery

[[Earth Eye Co]] publicly describes an "in-orbit delivery" export model under which the company launches the satellite in China, verifies it on orbit, and then transfers operational control to the foreign customer. Earth Eye's own website says it has done one such transfer to an unnamed [[Belt and Road]] participant; [[Iran]] joined Belt and Road in 2021. The stated civilian use case on Earth Eye's website is agriculture, ocean monitoring, emergency management, natural resource supervision, and municipal transportation.

The ground-side counterparty is [[Emposat]] (Beijing), which provides software, telemetry, command uplink, and the global ground-station network. Emposat was founded by [[Richard Zhao]], previously 15 years at the [[China Academy of Space Technology]] (CAST). A report from the House China Select Committee flagged Emposat as closely tied to the [[People's Liberation Army]] Aerospace Force, including personnel linked to Chinese satellite-launch command centres. Several Earth Eye executives are alumni of the [[Seven sons of national defence]] Chinese universities known for [[PLA]] collaboration.

## The dispersion strategy

[[Jim Lamson]] ([[James Martin Center for Nonproliferation Studies]], former CIA Iran analyst) framed the operational value: Iran's own satellite ground stations — hit repeatedly by [[Israel]] in 2025 and 2026 — are physically vulnerable to strikes from a thousand miles away, but Chinese commercial ground stations located in third countries cannot be easily struck. The TEE-01B arrangement gives the IRGC ISR capability that is, in effect, survivable against counter-space strikes — exactly the problem [[Israel]]'s mid-March 2026 strike on the [[Iranian Space Agency]] research centre was designed to solve.

## The Chang Guang / Houthi precedent

This is the second documented case of a Chinese commercial satellite operator providing targeting-relevant imagery to a US-designated adversary. The FT reported in 2025 that [[Chang Guang Satellite Technology]] — another commercial group with ties to the Chinese military — provided satellite imagery to the [[Houthis]] for Red Sea targeting of US warships and international shipping. The pattern, per [[Aidan Powers-Riggs]] ([[CSIS]]): "Emposat is a rising star in China's commercial space sector, but it's still a product of the state and military establishment. It was founded by veterans of China's state-run space programme and bankrolled by investment from national military-civil fusion funds."

## China-Iran weapons arc

[[Dennis Wilder]] (former head of China analysis at [[CIA]]) placed the satellite deal within a historical pattern of Chinese arms transfers to Iran, including Silkworm anti-ship missiles that [[Iran]] previously used to threaten shipping in the [[Strait of Hormuz]]. Separately and during the current war, CNN reported (sourcing to a person familiar with US intelligence) that Washington had seen indications China was considering providing Iran with the shoulder-fired missiles of the type Iran recently used to shoot down a US F-15. The CIA declined to comment on the CNN report. A White House spokesperson referred to [[Trump II|Trump]]'s weekend warning that China would face "big problems" if it provided Iran with air defence systems.

## Methodology (FT)

FT analysed TEE-01B's orbit using public US Space Force tracking data and found the satellite's position consistent with the documents' timestamps, coordinates, and sensor angles. Government statements and open-source reporting confirm strikes in the days before or after the satellite's passes. Damage assessment used [[European Space Agency]] [[Sentinel-1]] radar imagery and [[Sentinel-2]] medium-resolution optical imagery.

## Why the article matters

- Quantifies the ISR capability Iran had during the war, beyond what its domestic programme could produce.
- Demonstrates the "dispersion" vulnerability Israel's counter-space campaign could not close.
- Documents a second instance (after [[Chang Guang Satellite Technology]]/[[Houthis]]) of Chinese commercial-space dual-use transfer to an adversary of the US.
- Frames the commercial vehicle (in-orbit delivery + third-country ground stations) as a transferable model — other [[Belt and Road]] members could purchase equivalent capability without the launch/operator signature that triggers US sanctions.
