# MEI Iran war economic panel March 2026

[[Middle East Institute|MEI]] panel (March 20, 2026) with [[Mick Mulroy]] (fmr Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Middle East, ex-CIA, ex-Marine, MEI Distinguished Military Fellow) and [[Colby Connelly]] (MEI Senior Fellow, energy expert) — the most comprehensive structural assessment of the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|2026 Iran conflict's]] economic trajectory.

---

## Pentagon $200B supplemental request

The Pentagon is requesting $200B more — roughly 1/4 of current defense budget, roughly 1/4 of entire [[Iraq]] war cost ($800B over 8 years). The war is not even a month old. Mulroy: this signals the Pentagon's own plan is NOT for 2-3 weeks. Bluster won't work — [[Iran]] understands asymmetric pain tolerance. Iran endured maximum pressure campaign for 6-7 years. Iran knows the US has a democracy — midterms coming. They don't.

## Sanctions paradox — unprecedented in wartime history

The US is discussing lifting sanctions on [[Russia]]n AND [[Iran]]ian oil mid-war. Neither Mulroy nor the moderator (Ken, ex-CIA) could think of another war in history where you lifted sanctions on the enemy during combat. 100-140 million barrels of Iranian oil in floating storage — waivers would apply to these. This emboldens hardliners who think they're winning. Russia benefits enormously: sanctions removed + oil prices soaring. Mulroy: "We should be going the other direction with sanctioning Russia." [[Ukraine]] is making military gains while world attention is diverted to [[Iran]]. See [[Iran conflict sanctions paradox]].

## Investor confidence collapse

Connelly: [[Kuwait]] was opening its oil sector to international investment for the first time — lost a major investor. [[Occidental Petroleum]] involved in [[UAE]] assets that were attacked. [[Exxon]]'s portfolio includes liquefaction capacity at [[Ras Laffan]] — enormous potential losses. Restoring confidence requires assurance "this doesn't happen again" — very far from that. The above-ground risk landscape has fundamentally changed.

## Gulf basing recalculation

Gulf countries hosted US bases believing it made them safer. Now reconsidering: hosting bases made them targets — not just military bases but civilian economy (hotels, highrises, energy infrastructure). Some may view US presence as liability. Mulroy: need to address why it's advantageous for them to house [[CENTCOM]] ([[Al Udeid]]), [[Fifth Fleet]] ([[Bahrain]]). Even if not saying it publicly, they're thinking: "If we didn't have US military assets here, would we be on the target list?" Since regime change won't happen, this might just be "a pause" before it happens again.

## Gulf tech/AI investment at risk

Connelly: the Gulf was attracting tech/AI investment because of cheap energy (natural gas for power, low-cost solar, wind). The war doesn't change the resource advantage — but the above-ground risk landscape affects investment decisions. The resource base that made the Gulf appealing for AI/data centers isn't going away, but the risk premium on physical presence has permanently increased.
