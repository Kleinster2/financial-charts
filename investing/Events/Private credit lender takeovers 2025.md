#event #credit #privateequity #risk

**Private credit lender takeovers 2025** — Lenders seized control of 45 companies in the first nine months of 2025, more than double the prior year, involving approximately $25 billion in pre-takeover principal (Lincoln International). This is the inverse of [[Private Credit]] LMEs — here, creditors take ownership because borrowers defaulted, rather than sponsors slashing creditor value while retaining control.

75% of these takeovers involved 2021-2022 vintage deals — the peak ZIRP lending era. The companies are overwhelmingly low-profile, PE-backed businesses that accessed far more credit than they would have a decade ago.

## Pattern analysis

### Who loses: the PE sponsors

| Sponsor | Company | Equity loss |
|---------|---------|------------|
| [[Platinum Equity]] | United Site Services | $1.4B (Fortress, Ares, Blackstone in continuation vehicle) |
| [[Platinum Equity]] | Aventiv/Securus | Full equity wipeout |
| [[BlackRock]] | Alacrity Solutions | ~$560M equity investment wiped |
| [[Vista Equity Partners]] | Pluralsight | $1.6B equity marked to zero |
| [[Blackstone]] | Fortrex/PSSI | Prepared to walk away from equity |
| Charlesbank / [[Oaktree]] | City Brewing | Equity wiped |
| Summit Partners | 48Forty Solutions | Equity wiped |

[[Platinum Equity]] appears twice — both United Site Services and Aventiv. The continuation vehicle structure (where a PE firm sells a portfolio company to itself at a new valuation with fresh debt) is a recurring feature.

### Who wins: the private credit lenders who become owners

[[Blue Owl]], [[KKR]], Antares Capital, and [[Oaktree]] appear as new equity owners across multiple deals. The same firms that made the original loans are now converting debt to equity — becoming de facto PE firms. This blurs the line between credit and equity investing.

### What kind of companies fail

- Low-profile, asset-light services: portable toilets, insurance claims, food sanitation, pallets, IT training
- Demand-sensitive: weather-dependent (Alacrity), construction-dependent (United Site), consumer-dependent (Hooters, On The Border)
- Regulatory risk: Aventiv/Securus (FCC rate caps), Fortrex (child labor scandal)
- Peak-vintage debt: most acquired in 2021-2022 at high valuations with floating-rate debt that repriced sharply when rates rose

### The 2021-2022 vintage problem

75% of takeovers involve deals from this era. The logic:
1. 2021-2022: Low rates → PE sponsors bid aggressively → loaded companies with cheap floating-rate debt
2. 2022-2024: Fed hikes 525 bps → interest expense doubles/triples
3. 2025: Companies that could barely service debt at 2% can't at 7% → covenant breach → lender seizure

This is the delayed mechanical consequence of the rate cycle, not an idiosyncratic failure.

## Why it matters

This is distinct from both the [[Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate]] (liquidity crisis) and the LME wave (Tropicana, Saks — sponsor keeps control, creditors take losses). Here, the credit worked as designed: lenders had sufficient protections to seize control when borrowers failed. The problem is the volume — 45 in 9 months signals that the 2021-2022 lending vintage is fundamentally impaired.

The companies are also a different risk profile than what the market is focused on. The headline fear is AI disruption of software borrowers ([[Private Credit Software Concentration]]). But the actual defaults are in prosaic, demand-sensitive services businesses — the kind of boring, steady-cash-flow companies that PE firms load with debt precisely because they're supposed to be safe.

If these "safe" borrowers are defaulting at 2x the prior rate while rates are still high and the economy is still growing, what happens in a recession?
