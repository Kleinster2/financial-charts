# 2025 Saudi-UAE Yemen rupture

In December 2025, [[Saudi Arabia]] launched airstrikes in southern Yemen against forces aligned with the [[UAE]]-backed [[Southern Transitional Council]] (STC), citing what Riyadh said were Iranian weapons shipments to STC fighters. The strikes were the first direct Saudi military action against a UAE-aligned force in the eight-year [[Yemen War|Yemen war]] and ended the working coalition under which the two Gulf powers had jointly fought the [[Houthis]] since 2015. The rupture preceded the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|February 2026 Iran war]] by two months and is the load-bearing political background for the [[2026 UAE OPEC exit]] announcement, where it is cited as evidence that the Saudi-UAE rivalry was paused — not resolved — by Iran's strikes on every Gulf state in late February 2026.

The event is sourced from the April 28, 2026 reporting wrap on the OPEC exit ([[Al Jazeera]], *Washington Post*) where Saudi-UAE political background was reconstructed as part of explaining the apolitical-framing skepticism around the cartel exit. The vault treats the rupture as the dated marker of when the Saudi-UAE Yemen partnership formally fractured, even though competition over [[Red Sea]] commercial influence, port investments, and post-war Iran preferences had been visible for years prior.

## Why it matters for the vault

Three through-lines.

First, OPEC exit context. The [[2026 UAE OPEC exit]] note's "what the framing leaves out" section uses the December 2025 rupture as the first of three contextual elements complicating Mazrouei's "no political linkage" denial. Without the rupture in the record, the OPEC exit reads as a market-management decision; with it, the exit reads as the first major unilateral structural decision the UAE has taken since the war-induced pause in Saudi-UAE rivalry began.

Second, Gulf cohesion under wartime stress. [[Anwar Gargash]]'s April 28, 2026 admission that "the Gulf Cooperation Council countries supported each other logistically, but politically and militarily, I think their position has been the weakest historically" reads more candidly when paired with the December rupture: the wartime cohesion he describes is operational, not political.

Third, post-war Iran preferences. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have divergent post-war preferences for Iran (Saudi prefers a stable Iran given its Eastern Province Shia exposure; UAE prefers a weakened Iran given its security entanglement with [[Israel]]). The December rupture is the first visible expression of that divergence affecting joint operations.

## Synthesis

The December 2025 rupture is best read as the first dated marker of a structural Gulf realignment that the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|February 2026 war]] briefly obscured. Two readings are available. The narrow reading is operational: a tactical disagreement over [[Houthis|Houthi]]-aligned Yemeni factions and Iranian arms flows, contained to the Yemen theater. The broader reading is doctrinal: the rupture surfaces what [[Marc Lynch]] had already documented as the pre-war Saudi-UAE divergence over post-Iran-conflict architecture, with Saudi Arabia preferring multilateral GCC-plus alignment (including Turkey, Qatar, Egypt, Pakistan) and the UAE pursuing bilateral US security anchoring with [[Israel]]-coordinated regional counter-Iran activity. The April 28, 2026 [[2026 UAE OPEC exit|OPEC exit]] is the test that distinguishes the two readings: a market-management technicality fits the narrow reading; a unilateral structural decision in the immediate post-war pause fits the broader one. The vault's working hypothesis — supported by the parallel [[Saudi PIF international retrenchment April 2026]] and the [[Currency swap lines|Bessent swap-line architecture]] — is that the broader reading is operative, with the December rupture as its first dated waypoint.
