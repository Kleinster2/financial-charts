#event #credit #privateequity #risk #systemic

**Private credit retail liquidity crisis (2026)** — The first systemic stress test of private credit's retail model. After 5 years of aggressively marketing semi-liquid BDCs and perpetual vehicles to retail investors as "bond alternatives with quarterly liquidity," the industry faces an accelerating wave of redemptions that the underlying illiquid loan portfolios cannot absorb. The [[Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate]] (Feb 19, 2026) was the first fund to break — but the $2.9B/quarter redemption surge is industry-wide.

## Liquidity, not defaults, as the first stress (Mar 2026)

Bloomberg Opinion (Mar 5, 2026): The first real problem hitting private credit isn't loan losses — it's **liquidity withdrawal**. Wealthy investors are pulling cash due to falling interest rates (lower floating-rate yields), AI/software fears, and war uncertainty.

**Key observations from the piece:**
- Some firms are using their own balance sheet money to meet redemption requests
- No firm wants to gate because of reputational damage to future fundraising
- Rival managers worry OBDC II's wind-down created an expectation that "illiquid doesn't really mean illiquid" — wealthy investors now expect to get cash back from vehicles holding 5-year term loans
- Inflows to unlisted BDCs have fallen "dramatically" alongside rising redemption requests

*Source: Bloomberg Opinion (Marcus Sherwood, Mar 5, 2026)*

## The fundamental contradiction

Private credit's retail push was built on an inherent mismatch:

| What was sold | What exists |
|---------------|-------------|
| "Bond-like yields (8-12%)" | Loans to levered middle-market companies |
| "Quarterly liquidity" | Illiquid loans that can't be sold quickly without losses |
| "Low volatility" | Opaque quarterly marks that hide real-time losses |
| "Diversified credit" | 14-16% software concentration at "all-time" levels (Deutsche Bank) |

**The math doesn't work under stress:** When everyone wants out at once, honoring early redemptions benefits those investors at everyone else's expense → classic bank run dynamics → gate or wind down.

## Instances

### [[Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate]] (Feb 19, 2026)
- **First retail BDC to permanently gate investors**
- $1.7B fund, sold $1.4B in loans at 99.7% par
- Quarterly 5% NAV redemption window — promised for 8 years — ended permanently
- Replaced by pro-rata distributions (30% of NAV by end of March)
- OWL -8.7%, alt manager contagion across sector

### Blackstone BCRED (watching)
- **$50B+** — largest non-traded private credit fund
- Survived 2022 redemption spike via quarterly caps
- Any sign of stress here = systemic escalation
- Quarterly 5% cap means it takes 5 years to fully exit

### BlackRock TCP — Infinite Commerce Holdings (Mar 5, 2026)
- **$25M loan marked from 100 to 0 in one quarter** — zero recovery
- Borrower is an "Amazon aggregator" (buys online marketplace sellers)
- Infinite Commerce merged with another BlackRock debtor, **Razor Group**, in August — creating new debt structure valued at par
- Previously, BlackRock had valued Razor loans at deeply distressed levels — the merger *reset marks to par*, then the combined entity immediately deteriorated
- **Victory Park** also fully wrote off its Infinite Commerce position — blamed poor demand + tariff-driven inventory costs
- BlackRock TCP also partially wrote down **SellerX** (another Amazon aggregator)
- **Second sudden wipeout** for BlackRock TCP after Renovo Home Partners (Nov 2025)
- **91% of valuation cuts** across the portfolio from 2021 or earlier vintage deals
- Fund cut dividend from 25¢ to 17¢ per share → shares tumbled

**Valuation sequence:** BlackRock held deeply distressed Razor Group loans → Razor merged with Infinite Commerce (Aug 2025) → new debt structure marked at par (100¢, Q3 2025) → written to zero (Q4 2025). The merger created a new debt structure that reset marks.

**Amazon aggregator context:** The sector boomed during COVID (Thrasio, Razor, Perch, SellerX) on a thesis of consolidating Amazon marketplace sellers. Victory Park blamed poor performance on depressed demand and higher inventory costs from tariffs. Most aggregators are now in restructuring or distress.

### Ares ARCC (watching)
- **$26B+** — largest publicly traded BDC
- Canary for broader BDC market health
- Next quarterly report is critical

### Blue Owl OBDC (watching)
- **$16B+** — Blue Owl's larger, publicly traded BDC
- Moody's upgraded to Baa2 (Jan 2026) — but OBDC II gate raises questions
- If redemption/sale pressure hits OBDC, it's 10x the scale of OBDC II

## Why this could be systemic

### 1. Liquidity mismatch at scale
Retail BDCs and perpetual vehicles hold **illiquid middle-market loans** but offer **quarterly redemption windows**. This works when redemptions are 2-3% of NAV. At $2.9B/quarter (and accelerating), it forces asset sales, gates, or both.

### 2. Software concentration as trigger
**14-16% of speculative-grade credit** is in software — an "all-time concentration" (Deutsche Bank). AI disruption fears are compressing software valuations → PE sponsors less willing to inject equity → floating-rate debt squeezes borrowers → defaults rise → BDC NAVs decline → more redemptions. A feedback loop.

See: [[Private Credit Software Concentration]]

### 3. Opacity masks losses
Private credit isn't marked to market. Quarterly valuations lag reality. By the time losses are recognized, they've compounded. The Blue Owl gate happened at **1.1% non-accruals** — imagine what happens at 4-5%.

### 4. Interconnection
Many lenders hold the same borrowers. A forced sale by one fund depresses marks for everyone holding the same loan → contagion across funds and managers.

### 5. Regulatory vacuum
No widespread stress testing, limited disclosure requirements. Sen. Warren's call for regulation (Feb 19) could change this — but if regulators force disclosure, the opacity that's been hiding problems gets blown open all at once.

## The 2007 parallel

Mohamed El-Erian: *"Is this a 'canary-in-the-coalmine' moment, similar to August 2007?"*

| August 2007 | February 2026 |
|-------------|---------------|
| Bear Stearns hedge funds gated | Blue Owl OBDC II gated |
| Subprime mortgage concentration | Software/tech loan concentration |
| CDOs with liquidity mismatch | Retail BDCs with liquidity mismatch |
| "Contained" narrative | "Finite-life wind-down" narrative |
| Banks had opaque off-balance-sheet exposure | PE firms have opaque cross-fund exposure |
| $10T+ mortgage market | $1.8T private credit market |
| Rating agencies asleep | Moody's *upgraded* OBDC in January |

**El-Erian's actual argument** isn't that 2008 is coming — it's that **systemic crises always begin the same way**: a small fund gates, management says it's orderly, everyone calls it "contained," and the real question isn't this fund but what it reveals about the funds behind it. Bear Stearns' first gated fund also had fine numbers on paper. The crisis wasn't in the credit metrics — it was in the **structure**: opaque assets, liquidity mismatch, sector concentration, and retail money that shouldn't have been there. By the time everyone agrees it's systemic, it's too late to act. OBDC II's non-accruals are 1.1% and loans sold at 99.7% par — but the gate happened anyway. That's the signal.

**Key difference:** Private credit ($1.8T) is much smaller and better capitalized than the 2007 mortgage market ($10T+). The systemic risk is real but bounded — unless contagion spreads to insurance companies and pension funds that are now the marginal buyers of private credit assets.

**Key similarity:** In both cases, the first gate was dismissed as idiosyncratic. Bear Stearns said the same things Blue Owl is saying now.

**Jamie Dimon (late 2025):** Private credit's *"cockroaches"* — stress in one borrower signals more hidden trouble.

**Marc Rowan (Apollo CEO, Mar 5):** Warned that **a shakeout is coming for private credit firms** — biggest name in the industry publicly acknowledging the reckoning.

**Mike Arougheti (Ares CEO, Mar 5):** Called UBS forecast of **15% private credit default rates** "absolutely wrong" — same day as Rowan's shakeout warning. The industry can't agree on whether this is fine or existential.

Acknowledged dispersion coming: *"There will be clear dispersion coming. It will be interesting to see."* Note: Apollo simultaneously warning of a shakeout (Rowan) while downplaying withdrawal concerns (Zito) — talking out of both sides.

**Mark Zandi (Moody's):** *"The private credit industry is probably currently able to absorb any losses reasonably well, this may not be the case a year from now."*
