[[India]] and [[Brazil]] signed a non-binding memorandum of understanding on critical minerals and rare earths on February 21, 2026, during [[Lula]]'s state visit to New Delhi. The deal establishes a cooperation framework covering reciprocal investment, exploration, mining, and AI applications — and was explicitly designed as a trial run before [[Brazil]] negotiates with the US, where the power asymmetry is greater.

## Why it matters

**For [[Brazil]]:** The MOU is a geopolitical positioning move, not primarily an economic one. [[Roberto Goulart Menezes]] (University of Brasilia): "For [[Brazil]], this agreement represents a kind of pilot before negotiating with a country with which [[Brazil]] has an asymmetrical relationship" — meaning the US. The 2025 US confrontation (50% tariff over [[Jair Bolsonaro|Bolsonaro]] trial) catalyzed [[Brazil]]'s recognition that rare earths are geopolitical leverage, not just exports.

**For [[India]]:** Access to [[Brazil]]'s world #2 rare earth reserves (~22M metric tons) at a moment when [[China]]'s export controls are disrupting global supply chains. [[India]] ranks #5 globally in reserves (~6.9M tons) but needs diversification.

**Strategic framing:** [[Oliver Stuenkel]] (Getulio Vargas Foundation) frames the deal as part of both countries' broader push for strategic autonomy from [[China]] *and* the US: "The reading is that, given how turbulent and unpredictable things have become, the more partners, the better."

## Timeline context

This MOU sits between the US-[[Brazil]] preliminary rare earth talks (Dec 2025/Jan 2026, FT) and the NYT report (Mar 20, 2026) that the US is pushing a deal on a "reluctant" [[Brazil]]. [[Lula]] is expected to visit Washington D.C. in "coming months" — the rare earths negotiation will likely be on the agenda.

The [[EU]] is also competing: [[Ursula von der Leyen]] confirmed discussions with Brasília on critical raw materials in March 2026.
