SEMI's flagship China conference, held March 25-27, 2026 in Shanghai. Notable for unusually candid admissions from Chinese industry leaders about the domestic semiconductor gap — and for framing equipment as the binding constraint on AI's next phase.

## Key statements

### David Wang, CEO of [[ACM Research]]

Future AI gains depend on semiconductor equipment that hasn't been invented yet. Next-generation manufacturing tools are still in development, and will define computing performance going forward. Sustained investment and market scale are critical to global competitiveness, with differentiated technologies forming the foundation.

### Lee Haiming, SVP of Chongqing Xinlian Microelectronics

China remains competitive in consumer chips but **lags 5-10 years behind in automotive and data center semiconductors**. AI adoption in manufacturing is one path to narrowing the gap. Talent retention and equipment utilization remain key constraints. Chongqing Xinlian is a state-owned specialty foundry backed by [[China semiconductor strategy|Big Fund Phase II]], building the first Chinese 12-inch wafer fab in Chongqing with 20,000 wafers/month initial capacity for automotive-grade chips.

### Wei Li, Standing VP of National Silicon Industry Group

Rising demand for memory, data center power management ICs, and optoelectronic technologies. Data transmission and 6G emerging as key focus areas.

### Daniel Yuan, EVP of Sino IC Leasing

Multilayer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs) facing shortages as data center construction accelerates. Sino IC Leasing is a state-backed financial leasing company focused exclusively on the IC industry — Yuan's comments reflect broader supply pressure across passive components underpinning AI server builds.

### Lily Feng, President of SEMI China / Benjamin Loh, Chairman of Comet AG

AI computing is pushing the semiconductor industry into a structural growth phase. Global semiconductor market projected to reach **$1.8 trillion by 2030**. China leads in capacity additions and equipment spending.

## Significance

Three threads converged at this event:

1. **Equipment as bottleneck.** Wang's argument — that future AI progress depends on tools not yet invented — is self-serving (ACM makes equipment) but tracks with the broader [[Long WFE]] thesis. Gate-all-around transistors, backside power delivery, high-NA EUV, and hybrid bonding all require new or significantly improved process tools.

2. **China's candid self-assessment.** A 5-10 year lag in data center chips, admitted at a domestic conference, is strategically notable. It validates the premise behind [[export controls]]: the restrictions are working to maintain the gap, even as China pours capital into catching up.

3. **Passive component strain.** MLCC shortages from AI datacenter builds signal that the AI infrastructure boom's bottlenecks extend well beyond GPUs and advanced chips — mundane components are also constrained.
