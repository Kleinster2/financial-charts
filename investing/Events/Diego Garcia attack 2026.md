**Diego Garcia attack 2026** -- on March 21, 2026, [[Iran]] fired two intermediate-range ballistic missiles at the joint [[United States|US]]-[[United Kingdom|UK]] military base on [[Diego Garcia]], approximately 3,700 km from Bandar Abbas. One missile failed mid-flight; the other was intercepted by a US Navy [[SM-3]] interceptor. Neither reached the target. The attack was the farthest-range strike Iran had ever attempted, roughly doubling its previously confirmed 2,000 km operational envelope, and marked the first time a state actor targeted [[Diego Garcia]] with ballistic missiles. [[Iran]] officially denied the attack, calling it a "false flag."

---

## What happened

Two missiles launched from southern [[Iran]], assessed by [[Israel]]'s Chief of Staff as "two-stage ballistic missiles with a range of 4,000 km." [[NATO]] said it "cannot confirm" the Israeli assessment. The missile type remains debated:

| Hypothesis | Proponent | Argument |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Modified [[Khorramshahr]] | US initial assessment | Known IRBM, stretched range with reduced payload |
| [[Qaem-100]] derivative | Tal Inbar (Israeli analyst) | Three-stage solid-fuel SLV, matches "two-stage" if one stage dropped. Single-stage [[Khorramshahr]] "cannot realistically achieve 4,000 km" |
| Further-modified [[Khorramshahr]] | The Conversation defense expert | Iran operating "at distances they are not reliably capable of" |

The first missile broke up mid-flight -- consistent with structural failure at extreme range, where aerodynamic and thermal stresses exceed design limits. The second was intercepted by a US Navy destroyer using an [[SM-3]] Standard Missile-3, the [[Aegis]] system's primary exo-atmospheric ballistic missile interceptor.

## Strategic significance

The attack demonstrated three things simultaneously:

1. [[Iran]] is attempting to break out of its ~2,000 km operational range ceiling, likely by weaponizing the [[Qaem-100]] space launch vehicle or heavily modifying the [[Khorramshahr]] with a minimal payload
2. The capability is unreliable at this range -- 0-for-2, with one airframe failure and one intercept
3. The [[range-payload tradeoff]] binds hard: the payloads light enough to reach [[Diego Garcia]] cannot accommodate a first-generation nuclear device (estimated 750-1,000 kg), meaning the nuclear threat to Diego Garcia is functionally zero with current Iranian technology

The attack also validated US Navy missile defense investment. The [[SM-3]] intercept at this range -- against a missile on a long-arc ballistic trajectory -- was well within the system's design envelope. Extended-range missiles follow predictable, high-apogee arcs that are easier to track and intercept than shorter-range missiles with depressed trajectories.

## Market context

The attack occurred during [[Operation Epic Fury]], with markets already in crisis mode from the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure and energy price spike. The Diego Garcia attack added a new dimension -- demonstrating Iran's willingness to target Western military assets far from the immediate theater -- but had limited incremental market impact because risk premiums were already extreme. [[Brent crude]] was trading above $108/bbl; the [[VIX]] was above 30; defense primes were already up 8-15% on the month.

The more durable market signal is for missile defense procurement. Every range extension by Iran's program triggers reassessment of the missile defense umbrella needed to protect forward-deployed US/UK assets. The [[SM-3]]'s successful intercept supports the investment case for [[RTX]] (SM-3 manufacturer) and [[Lockheed Martin]] ([[Aegis]] system integrator, [[THAAD]]).
