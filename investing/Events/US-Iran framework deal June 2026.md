# US-Iran framework deal June 2026

The framework agreement announced June 14, 2026 to end the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|2026 Iran war]] — the US-Israeli campaign that began February 28, 2026 with the killing of Supreme Leader [[Ali Khamenei]] and the closure of the [[Strait of Hormuz]]. The deal is the resolution the vault's [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium|risk-premium]], [[Iran conflict oil price timeline|oil-price]], and [[Iran conflict war duration|war-duration]] threads had treated as the single binding variable since March, and it consummates the "60-day extension plus Hormuz reopening" diplomacy the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact#June 1, 2026 -- inflation shock tracking well below the 2022 surge|market-impact hub was tracking as of June 1]]. Markets staged a peace-dividend rotation on June 15 — oil and the war-premium longs down, the risk complex up — though strategists framed it as a trading event rather than a confirmed all-clear.

This note is the resolution event; the full conflict and its market history live in the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact]] hub and its deep dives.

## The deal

President [[Donald Trump]] and Iranian officials confirmed an interim framework on June 14 to halt the war and reopen the [[Strait of Hormuz]]. The memorandum is to be signed June 19 in Switzerland, opening a 60-day window to negotiate the fate of Tehran's nuclear program. Reported terms: the [[Strait of Hormuz]] reopens within the week; the US lifts its naval blockade and the blockade on Iranian ports; and Iran dismantles its nuclear program in exchange for phased economic incentives conditional on compliance. Trump's framing — that Gulf oil shipments could "flow" again — is the operative market signal.

The agreement caps a campaign that began February 28 with joint US-Israeli strikes that killed [[Ali Khamenei]], triggered Iranian retaliation across the [[Persian Gulf]], and closed the strait through which more than 20M bbl/day of oil transits. The diplomacy ran through multiple false starts the hub documents — the April 8 ceasefire that broke within 72 hours, the April 12 US naval blockade, the May Strait fire exchanges — before the 60-day-extension architecture held into a signed framework.

## Market reaction — the peace-dividend rotation (June 15)

Oil led, and the cross-asset map inverted the war trades. Crude gave back the risk premium it had carried since February: WTI settled below $81, [[Brent crude]] fell as much as 5.7% to below $83 — both around two-month lows and roughly 30% below the war peak. Equities and bonds rallied together; havens and oil fell. The [[S&P 500]] rose 1.7%, the Nasdaq 100 3.1%, and the Dow and S&P Industrials indices set records, the latter erasing its entire war-era selloff.

Two second-order moves matter for the rates read. Gold miners ([[Agnico Eagle]], [[Kinross Gold]], [[Aura Minerals]], [[Newmont]], [[Barrick Gold]]) popped early on the disinflation/rate-cut revival, then faded intraday as semis took leadership — consistent with the war having been [[Gold|paradoxically bearish for gold]] by pricing out Fed cuts, an overhang the deal removes only partially. And regional banks sold off into the green tape ([[Truist]] −6.2% / −5.7σ, [[PNC Financial Services]], [[Fifth Third]], [[US Bancorp]]) — plausibly the war-driven rate-hike/NIM trade unwinding as peace pulls year-end hike bets lower, though a −5.7σ cluster move on [[Truist]] is unexplained by its same-day CEO-succession headline alone and is flagged for confirmation.

Strategists at KCM Trade, Pepperstone, and Stifel cautioned the move is more likely a short-term trading opportunity than the start of a longer-term rally, and a parallel "inflation worries keep markets wary" read persists into the [[FOMC]] decision June 16–17 (hold expected; the dot plot, not the held rate, is the test).

## What the deal inverts in the vault

The war generated a coherent set of trades the vault tracked for three months; the framework deal inverts most of them, and the open question is which inversions are durable:

- Oil longs / the supply-shock premium — the [[Iran conflict oil price timeline]] thesis (Brent $70 → $112) reverses toward [[Robin J. Brooks]]'s ~$85 normalization path; the [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]] concept's claim is that a residual premium survives the spot unwind even as the panic premium collapses.
- The [[Iran conflict long-short basket]] thesis — built long oil/defense, short airlines/travel — inverts: the peace-dividend tape is short the oil-and-defense leg and long travel ([[Booking Holdings]], [[Royal Caribbean]] rallied earlier in the session).
- The rates/stagflation read — the [[Steepener trade]] and the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact#May 15, 2026 — global long-end repricing on inflation shock|long-end repricing]] depended on oil-driven inflation keeping the Fed hawkish; peace pulls the inflation impulse, but the [[2026 recession risk|inflation overhang]] and an ~80%-priced year-end hike mean the cut revival is partial, not clean.
- Gold's failed-hedge story — gold fell during the supply shock; the disinflation relief is a marginal tailwind, visible in the miners' early pop and intraday fade.

## Synthesis

The framework deal resolves the single variable the vault treated as binding for the entire war: duration. From the February 28 strike onward, every cross-asset read — oil to $112, the failed gold hedge, the [[Steepener trade]] blow-up, the global long-end repricing — keyed off whether the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure was days or quarters. The answer is now "ended at ~3.5 months," and June 15 priced the inversion in one session: the war-premium longs (oil, refiners, services, defense, shipping, steel) down, the risk complex (semis/AI-infra, nuclear, quantum, crypto) up. The structurally interesting part is what does not cleanly invert. Oil unwinds the panic premium toward [[Robin J. Brooks]]'s ~$85 path, but the [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]] thesis says a residual premium survives the reopening. The rate-cut revival that lifted gold miners is only partial, because an ~80%-priced year-end hike and a still-elevated [[2026 recession risk|inflation]] base mean the [[FOMC]] can pull the oil-inflation tail without turning dovish — which is why the miners popped and faded and why regional banks sold off on a green day. The deal is the cleanest event yet for testing whether a supply-shock premium is fully reversible (the spot price) or partly structural (insurance, shipping, the risk premium) — and the April 8 ceasefire that broke in 72 hours is the standing reminder that the signing, not the announcement, is the confirmation.
