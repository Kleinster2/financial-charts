# Private Economy Promotion Law

The People's Republic of China's first dedicated statutory framework for protecting private-sector firms, passed by the [[National People's Congress]] Standing Committee on April 30, 2025 and effective May 20, 2025. The law codifies legal protections for private firms across property rights, fair competition, financing access, and treatment by regulatory and judicial bodies — explicitly addressing the trust deficit that opened during the 2021-2023 [[Common Prosperity]] enforcement phase. It is widely read as the formal closing of the crackdown cycle and the rhetorical anchor for the 2025-2026 private-sector rehabilitation arc.

The structural significance for the 2026 vault thread: the law is the legislative-level evidence that the 2021 enforcement-era version of Common Prosperity has been quietly retired, paired with the academic-level reframing in [[Li Shi]]'s May 2026 lecture (see [[Common Prosperity]]). Whether the law actually changes enforcement practice — or remains symbolic — is the open empirical question.

## Key provisions

The law's nine chapters cover (per the official Xinhua translation and analyst summaries):

| Chapter | Subject | Notable provision |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| 1 | General principles | Equal protection for private firms vs SOEs in all market activities |
| 2 | Fair competition | Anti-discrimination in market access, procurement, licensing |
| 3 | Investment and financing | Equal access to credit, bond markets, equity financing |
| 4 | Technology innovation | Inclusion in state R&D programmes on equal terms |
| 5 | Regulatory environment | Procedural protections against arbitrary enforcement |
| 6 | Service support | Government services for SMEs |
| 7 | Rights protection | Property rights, due process in commercial disputes |
| 8 | Legal liability | Penalties for officials violating the law's provisions |
| 9 | Supplementary | Implementation timeline |

The provisions that signal most clearly relative to the 2021-2023 enforcement era are chapter 5 (procedural protections against arbitrary regulatory action) and chapter 7 (property rights and due-process protections in commercial disputes). These directly address the most damaging perceived behaviour of the 2021 phase — sudden enforcement, asset seizure, founder detention — without explicitly admitting it.

## Context — the rehabilitation arc

The law sits at the legislative end of a multi-channel rehabilitation effort that began after the late-2024 macro deterioration:

| Date | Event | Channel |
|------|-------|---------|
| Late 2024 | Macro weakness intensified — private fixed-asset investment turning negative | Forcing function |
| January 2025 | [[DeepSeek-R1]] release | Symbolic — demonstrated private-sector innovation capacity |
| February 17, 2025 | [[Xi Jinping]] private-entrepreneur symposium | Political — Xi shook hands with [[Jack Ma]], [[Pony Ma]], [[Liang Wenfeng]], others |
| April 30, 2025 | Private Economy Promotion Law passed | Legislative |
| May 20, 2025 | Law effective | Operational |
| 2025-2026 | Various sector-specific easing measures | Implementation |

The law is the durable element — symposia and slogans can fade; statutory text persists. Whether it changes enforcement practice in the next downturn is the test that's still unfolding.

## Limits and open questions

Three structural caveats (inference):

1. The law's enforcement depends on the same regulatory and judicial bodies that conducted the 2021-2023 crackdowns. Statutory text doesn't override implicit political signals about which firms are "off limits" — the protections work for routine commercial disputes but not for cases involving political tension
2. The law does not explicitly repeal the regulatory frameworks (anti-monopoly, data-security, algorithmic regulation) that powered the 2021-2023 enforcement. Private firms still operate inside the same regulatory architecture; the law adds procedural protections within it
3. "Equal protection" with SOEs is the most ambitious provision and the hardest to verify. SOEs continue to access credit, government procurement, and regulatory deference on terms private firms cannot match — whether the law shifts this in practice is testable over multi-year windows only

---

## Investment-thread implications

Three reads (inference):

1. The law lowers the tail-risk premium on Chinese private-sector equity — not by enforcement-proof guarantee, but by raising the political cost of repeat enforcement waves. Multi-year mean reversion in private-firm multiples is consistent with the law as durable change
2. Sectors most directly hit in 2021-2023 (internet platforms, gaming, edtech, real estate) have asymmetric upside if the law's enforcement-restraint signal proves credible
3. Watch the first test case — the next administrative enforcement action against a major private firm will reveal whether the law's procedural protections are binding or symbolic
