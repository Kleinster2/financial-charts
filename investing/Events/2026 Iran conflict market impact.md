## Defense sector reaction (March 2)

### Key finding: hardware/kinetic names lead; IT services flat — but AVAV reversed on idiosyncratic risk

The market opened pricing munitions burn and hardware demand, not consulting or software. Drones and primes surged in premarket. Defense IT (LDOS, BAH, SAIC, CACI) and software-defined defense ([[Palantir]]) showed no Iran-driven move. But the session's defining story was [[AeroVironment]]'s intraday reversal — see below.

### Drones — divergent by close

| Company | Premarket | Close | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|-------|
| [[AeroVironment]] | **+20.1%** | **-17.4%** ($208.32) | ~40% intraday swing; SCAR recompetition + Raymond James triple-downgrade overwhelmed geopolitical bid |
| [[Kratos Defense]] | +10% | **+5.3%** ($90.72) | Held gains but faded from highs |

**AVAV reversal explained:** Mid-morning, Space News reported the [[US Space Force]] had reopened competitive bidding for the **$1.4B SCAR contract** (Satellite Communications Augmentation Resource) — AeroVironment's largest program (~50% of $2.8B backlog), inherited via its May 2025 [[BlueHalo]] acquisition. Raymond James analyst Brian Gesuale issued a triple-downgrade (Strong Buy to Underperform), removing his $348 target. The SCAR news transformed AVAV from the Iran conflict's biggest winner to its biggest loser in a single session. See [[AeroVironment]] for full details.

### Saudi Aramco — Ras Tanura attack (Mar 2)

Two Iranian drones intercepted near [[Saudi Aramco]]'s Ras Tanura refinery (~500K bbl/day capacity). Debris caused fire, no casualties. Saudi Defense Ministry confirmed. Follows earlier strikes on Riyadh, Qatar refineries, Dukhan, Jebel Ali. GCC foreign ministers issued joint statement asserting "right to take all necessary measures to protect security and stability."

### Strait of Hormuz scenario

IRGC declared Hormuz closed. ~150 ships stalled. As of March 2, traffic effectively halted except 1-2 Iranian/Chinese-flagged vessels going eastbound (likely under Iranian indemnity). [[Maersk]] blocked all vessels from transiting. ~40M barrels disrupted over first 2 days ([[FGE]] estimate).

[[FGE]] energy consultant Iman Nasseri (CNN, Mar 2) identified three supply risk layers:
1. Iranian production at risk (2.5-2.6M bbl/day) if regime change succeeds
2. [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure — 20M+ bbl/day of oil + 5M bbl of product (>20% of global supply)
3. Regional infrastructure attacks escalating — Ras Tanura, Qatar LNG facilities, Dukhan, Jebel Ali

If sustained:
- Barclays analyst: Brent could hit $100/bbl
- Most significant supply disruption since 1973 Arab oil embargo
- [[OPEC|OPEC+]] only unwinding 206K bbl/day — "insignificant" given scale ([[FGE]])
- Market expecting IEA emergency stock release from OECD countries
- [[Japan]], [[South Korea]], Thailand implementing export restrictions and inventory draws

### Beyond oil — LNG and petrochemicals

European gas +23% on March 2. ~25% of global [[LNG]] transits Hormuz. Qatar LNG production halted after direct Iranian strikes on facilities. ~4M tons/day of LPG and naphtha to Asian petrochemical sectors at risk. [[FGE]]: "Gas and LNG perhaps is not as easily replaceable if the situation prolongs." Asian petrochemical plants ([[China]], [[Japan]], [[South Korea]]) may shut in if supply disruption continues.

## Insurance / reinsurance

### Marine war risk — coverage withdrawal, not repricing

The defining insurance story is **coverage cancellation**, not premium increases. Major P&I clubs ([[Gard]], [[Skuld]], [[NorthStandard]], London P&I Club, American Club) announced cancellation of war risk cover for the Persian Gulf effective March 5. [[Japan]]'s MS&AD Insurance Group also suspended war risk underwriting for waters around Iran, Israel, and neighboring countries. The LMA Joint War Committee held an extraordinary meeting on March 2 to discuss the escalation.

This isn't a hard market repricing — it's underwriters choosing to **forgo premium income rather than hold the risk**. [[Lloyd's]] underwrites ~40% of global marine cargo, making concentrated exposure a systemic concern. War risk is a small premium pool (<$3B/year globally across marine and aviation) with little standalone disclosure.

[[Marsh McLennan|Marsh]] estimates marine hull insurance rates in the Gulf could rise **25-50%**. Shipping war risk costs from alternative providers have at least doubled. VLCC spot rates on the Middle East-to-China route hit ~W225 on Worldscale (~$12M), nearly tripling since start of 2026.

### Adjacent coverage gaps

- **Airlines**: operations disrupted for multiple days; revenue losses **not covered** by standard policies. War exclusions triggered.
- **Commercial property**: war-related damage "almost always" excluded — [[Dubai]] Palm Jumeirah properties potentially uncovered
- **Travel insurance**: many policies exclude military action benefits

**European/specialty insurance (March 2):**

Barclays flagged [[Hiscox]], [[Lancashire]], and [[Conduit Re]] as most exposed — marine, aviation, and political risk specialty premiums account for 5-15% of total premiums. Second tier: [[Swiss Re]], [[Munich Re]], [[Hannover Re]], [[SCOR]]. **All seven names traded lower on Monday.** War risk exposure is "critical and also intransparent" (Barclays).

[[Beazley]] dipped -0.2% to 1,241.85p but is dominated by [[Zurich Insurance]]'s £8B takeover bid (1,335p/share, March 4 deadline).

**Sector-level read:** Insurance is not getting a war premium in equities. The market is pricing the **convex risk profile** — premiums go up linearly but losses can be catastrophic and correlated across marine, aviation, property, and specialty lines simultaneously. Coverage withdrawal signals uncertainty too deep to price. Franklin Templeton named insurance among favored sectors, but the tape disagrees so far.

Taiwan's largest life insurers conducting internal reviews of Middle East exposure. Hedge funds rushing to size up aggregation risk.
