# US Section 301 investigation of Germany drug pricing (2026)

On June 18, 2026, [[USTR]] [[Jamieson Greer|Greer]] self-initiated a [[Section 301]] investigation into [[Germany]]'s pricing of innovative pharmaceutical products — the first US trade-law challenge targeting an ally's domestic drug-reimbursement regime and the test case for using trade enforcement to force [[Pharma R&D burden-sharing|global R&D burden-sharing]]. The investigation could result in tariffs on German goods; the US is simultaneously offering the [[US-UK pharma pricing deal (2026)|UK bilateral-deal template]] as the negotiated exit.

---

## Synthesis

The investigation is the operational expression of the [[Pharma R&D burden-sharing|burden-sharing]] doctrine: a foreign government's underpayment for patented drugs is an "unreasonable or discriminatory" trade practice that "burdens or restricts US commerce" by shifting R&D costs onto American patients. USTR's Federal Register notice cites a ~3.9× US-vs-Germany branded-drug price ratio as the core evidence and names two specific instruments — confidentiality rebates and mandatory variable-rate rebates — as the focus.

The discrete trigger is the [[Germany|German]] Ministry of Health's 2026 statutory-health overhaul, which adds a mandatory rebate on patented medicines and introduces a variable rebate that USTR estimates could reach ~20% by 2030. The probe opened the same week as a broader [[Trump II]] pharma-tariff stack: the April 2 [[Liberation Day tariffs|100% Section 232 pharma tariffs]] (effective Jul 31 for 17 majors, Sep 29 for others), the [[Most Favored Nation drug pricing|MFN]] voluntary-price-cut deals, and the [[US-UK pharma pricing deal (2026)|US-UK bilateral]].

Chancellor [[Friedrich Merz|Merz]]'s response is that drug reimbursement is a matter of national jurisdiction and that the US should operate under the existing EU trade agreement — i.e., Germany declines to treat domestic health policy as a trade-policy subject. The structural standoff is therefore both substantive (who pays for global drug R&D) and jurisdictional (whether trade law reaches domestic health systems).

---

## What triggered it — the German 2026 reform

The [[Germany|German]] Ministry of Health unveiled the overhaul in April 2026 to close a looming ~€20B ($23B) statutory-health funding gap. Pharma-relevant provisions (per the USTR Federal Register notice):

| Provision | Detail |
|---|---|
| Additional mandatory rebate, H1 2027 | 3.5% on patented medicines, on top of existing price cuts |
| Variable ("dynamic") rebate, H2 2027+ | Tied to actual drug spend vs target health-insurance outlays |
| Projected dynamic rebate by 2030 | ~20% (estimate cited in the notice) |
| Original proposal | Variable discounts — withdrawn after pharma-industry opposition, replaced by the rebate structure |

The reform sits inside Germany's [[AMNOG]] framework, the statutory reference-pricing regime that already produces the price gap USTR is challenging. See [[AMNOG]] for the mechanism.

| ~12 months out | Earliest determination (standard Section 301 timeline) → potential tariff action on German goods |

USTR [[Jamieson Greer|Greer]] called the German legislation "a serious step backwards." [[Robert F. Kennedy Jr.|HHS Secretary Kennedy]] endorsed the move: "Fighting the war against disease is a shared burden across wealthy nations." Both framed the US-UK deal as the template Germany should follow.

---

## The German response

Chancellor [[Friedrich Merz|Merz]] (Jun 19, EU Summit, Brussels): the US should abide by the existing EU trade agreement, and reimbursement by German health-insurance providers is "a matter of national jurisdiction." He offered to provide the US information but did not signal willingness to renegotiate the reimbursement regime. This is a harder line than the [[United Kingdom|UK]] took (the UK negotiated a bilateral pricing deal), and it routes the dispute toward the EU-trade-agreement frame rather than a Germany-specific bilateral.
