# Capesize

Capesize is the largest class of dry bulk carriers — vessels too large to transit the Panama or Suez Canals (hence the name, as they must round the Cape of Good Hope or Cape Horn) — typically 180,000 DWT and above. They dominate long-haul iron ore and coal trade, particularly from [[Brazil]] ([[Vale]] / Ponta da Madeira, Tubarão) and Australia (Port Hedland, Dampier) to [[China]] (Qingdao, Caofeidian, Rizhao). The [[Baltic Exchange]] BCI (Baltic Capesize Index) is the most-watched dry bulk benchmark, and Capesize freight futures are the largest component of the [[BDRY]] ETF (~50% weighting).

## Quick stats

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Size | 180,000 DWT and up (modern Newcastlemax: 210,000 DWT) |
| Typical cargo | Iron ore, coking/thermal coal |
| Dominant routes | Brazil → China (C3), Western Australia → China (C5) |
| Cape of Good Hope constraint | Cannot transit Suez/Panama at full load |
| 2021 BDI Capesize peak | ~$87,000/day (Oct 2021) |
| 2021 BDRY effect | Drove fund +390% Jan → Oct 2021 |
| Global fleet | ~1,800+ vessels |

## Economic leverage

Capesize rates are the single biggest swing factor in dry bulk. Because each voyage moves ~180,000+ tonnes of cargo, a $10,000/day rate move compounds into ~$500K per voyage. During the 2021 dry bulk boom — the precedent [[John Kartsonas]] regularly cites — the BCI peaked at ~$87,000/day after starting 2021 near $20,000/day. The move drove [[BDRY]] up nearly 390% before rates normalized through 2022.
