# Schumpeterian growth

A theory of economic growth in which growth is driven by innovation and "creative destruction" — new technologies, products, and firms continuously displacing incumbents. The name traces to Joseph Schumpeter (1883-1950); the modern formal version is the endogenous-growth model of Philippe Aghion and Peter Howitt (1992), which makes innovation an investment choice responding to competition, market size, and institutions.

## Why it matters here

Schumpeterian growth is the framework behind the [[Philippe Aghion]] side of the [[US-Europe productivity paradox]] debate: if growth comes from frontier innovation and creative destruction, then a region that innovates less (on this reading, Europe) genuinely falls behind, and the policy task is to lower barriers to entry, scale, and competition. [[Brad DeLong]]'s product-wage/user-wage rebuttal contests not the growth theory but the claim that the producer-side innovation gap maps onto a living-standards gap (see [[Technological surplus capture]]). The theory also underpins the innovation-and-competition case in [[Industrial policy]] and the EU agenda of the [[Mario Draghi report]].
