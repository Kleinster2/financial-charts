**Industrial commodities** are bulk chemicals, metals, and materials produced at scale for use as inputs across manufacturing, agriculture, mining, construction, and energy sectors. The category spans bulk chemicals ([[Sulfuric Acid]], [[Caustic Soda]], [[Ammonia]], ethylene, propylene), industrial gases ([[Air Liquide]], [[Linde]], [[Air Products]]), industrial metals ([[Copper]], [[Aluminum]], steel), and construction materials (cement, [[Soda Ash]]). Collectively valued at over $700 billion annually, their aggregate demand is one of the most reliable macro indicators of global industrial activity.

---

## Synthesis

The industrial commodities complex in early 2026 reads as a split signal. [[China]]'s manufacturing PMI divergence -- the official NBS reading at 49.0 (contraction) vs. the private RatingDog survey at 52.1 (strongest since December 2020) -- captures the tension. Export-oriented manufacturing (autos, solar, electronics) is running hot, but the domestic property downturn continues to weigh on steel, cement, and copper consumption. Industrial profits grew 0.6% in 2025, enough to provide a floor but insufficient to drive a broad-based commodity cycle. GDP growth projections of 4.8% for 2026 suggest the floor holds but the ceiling is capped.

The more structural story is the energy transition pulling industrial commodity demand in new directions. [[Copper]] is entering structural deficit starting 2026 as electrification, data centers, and grid buildout outstrip new mine supply -- S&P Global projects demand rising from 34.5 Mt in 2025 to 42 Mt by 2030. [[Sulfuric Acid]] demand from lithium spodumene processing (~7-8 tonnes H2SO4 per tonne LCE) and nickel HPAL could rival fertilizer demand growth within a decade. [[Ammonia]]'s potential as a zero-carbon shipping fuel could create 150-200 Mt of incremental demand by 2050 -- doubling total current production. [[Aluminum]] is pricing in persistent deficits through 2026-2027 as EV lightweighting and solar frame demand compound against [[China]]'s 45 Mt capacity cap.

The counterweight is overcapacity in bulk petrochemicals. Ethylene capacity additions in [[China]] and the US Gulf have outpaced demand, with China's excess capacity hitting 11.5 Mt in 2025 (a record). Caustic soda and [[Chlorine]] markets are mired in a low-profitability cycle from weak PVC demand. European chemical producers ([[BASF]], [[Dow]]) face structural energy cost disadvantages that have already driven capacity closures. The bulk chemicals market ($715 billion in 2025) is growing at 3.6% CAGR -- fine for a mature industry, but the growth is concentrated in Asia while margins compress in the West.

The macro read: industrial commodities are not signaling recession (volumes are growing, trade flows are shifting, not shrinking), but they are not signaling a new supercycle either. The commodity complex is bifurcating -- energy transition metals (copper, aluminum, lithium-adjacent chemicals) are in structural demand expansion, while traditional petrochemicals (ethylene, propylene, PVC chain) face overcapacity. The investment signal is in the divergence, not the aggregate.

## Taxonomy

Industrial commodities group into five categories, each with distinct supply-demand dynamics, pricing mechanisms, and macro sensitivities.

### Bulk chemicals

The highest-volume manufactured chemicals. Produced in hundreds of millions of tonnes annually, typically low unit value ($30-500/t), high capital intensity, and long asset cycles. Demand is diversified across agriculture, mining, manufacturing, and construction.

| Chemical | Global production (~2024) | Market value | Key demand | China share |
|----------|--------------------------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| [[Sulfuric Acid]] | ~261 Mt | ~$15-17B | Phosphate fertilizers (55%), mining, energy transition | ~46% |
| [[Caustic Soda]] | ~86 Mt | ~$46B | Alumina refining, pulp/paper, chemicals, water treatment | ~45% |
| [[Chlorine]] | ~76 Mt | ~$35-40B | PVC (34%), water treatment (18%), isocyanates | ~40% |
| [[Ammonia]] | ~150 Mt | ~$80-160B | Nitrogen fertilizers (70%), explosives, emerging fuel | ~29% |
| Ethylene | ~177 Mt | ~$163B | Polyethylene (57%), packaging (52% end-use) | ~22% |
| Propylene | ~137 Mt | ~$100B+ | Polypropylene, acrylonitrile, propylene oxide | ~30% |
| [[Soda Ash]] | ~60 Mt | ~$18-20B | Glass (50%), detergents, lithium carbonate | ~48% |
| [[Phosphoric Acid]] | ~50 Mt | ~$30B+ | Fertilizers (85%), food-grade, batteries (LFP) | ~40% |

Ethylene is the single largest-volume organic chemical, often called the "king of petrochemicals" because its derivatives (polyethylene, ethylene oxide, ethylene glycol, vinyl chloride, styrene) touch virtually every manufactured product. Asia Pacific accounts for ~43% of ethylene revenue, with the polyethylene segment taking 57% of output.

The chlor-alkali co-product lock ([[Caustic Soda]] + [[Chlorine]]) is the most important structural dynamic in bulk chemicals. Electrolysis of brine produces both in a fixed ~1:0.89 stoichiometric ratio -- you cannot make one without the other. Demand for the two products is driven by entirely different end markets (caustic tracks alumina/manufacturing, chlorine tracks PVC/construction), creating a permanent pricing see-saw. See the dedicated product notes for the full mechanics.

### Industrial gases

High-purity gases for manufacturing, healthcare, food processing, electronics, and welding. Unlike bulk chemicals, industrial gases are produced on-site or via pipeline under long-term contracts, creating recurring revenue with inflation-linked escalators. The sector is an oligopoly: five companies control ~80-84% of the global market.

| Company | Ticker | Revenue (FY2024) | Market cap (~2026) | HQ |
|---------|--------|-----------------|-------------------|-----|
| [[Linde]] | LIN | ~$34B | ~$210B | UK/US |
| [[Air Liquide]] | AI.PA | EUR25.3B | ~$100B+ | [[France]] |
| [[Air Products]] | APD | $12.6B | ~$65B | US |
| Messer | Private | ~$4B+ | -- | [[Germany]] |
| [[Taiyo Nippon Sanso]] | 4091.T | ~$8B | -- | [[Japan]] |

The global industrial gases market was $119 billion in 2025. Asia Pacific led at 36.4% share, driven by industrialization, healthcare expansion, and semiconductor fabrication. The sector's appeal is defensive: long-term take-or-pay contracts, on-site production tied to customer facilities, and low customer switching costs. Growth is tied to hydrogen ([[Green hydrogen]] economy), semiconductor fabs (high-purity nitrogen, argon, specialty gases), and healthcare (medical oxygen, anesthesia). [[Linde]] alone has a larger market cap than [[BASF]] and [[Dow]] combined -- the market prices the recurring-revenue model at a premium to the cyclical chemical model.

### Industrial metals

Metals consumed at scale in construction, manufacturing, electrical systems, and infrastructure. Traded on organized exchanges (LME, COMEX, SHFE) with transparent price discovery. Demand is a direct function of industrial activity, capital investment, and construction cycles.

| Metal | Global production (~2025) | Price (~early 2026) | Key demand | China share of consumption |
|-------|--------------------------|---------------------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Steel | ~1,900 Mt | ~$700-800/t (HRC) | Construction (52%), autos, machinery | ~54% |
| [[Aluminum]] | ~74.5 Mt | ~$3,296/t (LME) | Transport, packaging, construction, power | ~60% |
| [[Copper]] | ~26 Mt (refined) | ~$6.00/lb / ~$13,200/t | Power (28%), construction (26%), electronics, EVs | ~54% |
| Zinc | ~14 Mt | ~$2,800/t | Galvanizing (60%), alloys, chemicals | ~48% |

Steel is the largest industrial metal by volume but lacks a single global benchmark -- pricing varies by product (HRC, CRC, rebar), grade, and region. The ~1,900 Mt global market is dominated by [[China]] (~54% of production and consumption), with the property sector downturn the single largest drag on demand growth. See [[Iron ore]] for the upstream dynamics.

[[Copper]] has earned the moniker "Dr. Copper" for its macro sensitivity, but in 2025-2026 the rally is driven more by AI/data center demand and supply constraints than by traditional PMI signaling. Global copper demand is projected to grow 40% by decade-end, with energy transition technologies driving 120% of incremental demand. See the [[Copper]] concept note for full analysis.

[[Aluminum]] is pricing in structural deficits through 2026-2027 as EV lightweighting demand, solar panel framing, and grid infrastructure compound against [[China]]'s hard capacity cap at 45 Mt. LME aluminum at $3,296/t in early 2026 is the highest since April 2022.

### Construction materials

Cement, aggregite, glass, and structural steel consumed by the building sector. Demand tracks housing starts, infrastructure spending, and urbanization. Heavy and low-value-to-weight, making these highly regional markets. [[China]] produces ~55% of global cement. Not covered in detail here -- see sector-specific notes.

### Fertilizer chemicals

Chemicals whose primary end market is agriculture. [[Ammonia]], [[Sulfuric Acid]], and [[Phosphoric Acid]] are all bulk chemicals that happen to serve fertilizer as their largest demand segment, but they cross-cut into industrial, mining, and energy transition applications. Key producers:

## Macro signal value

Industrial commodities are among the oldest and most reliable coincident indicators of global economic activity. The logic is straightforward: when factories produce more goods, they consume more chemicals, metals, and energy. When construction accelerates, steel, cement, copper, and caustic soda volumes rise. When agriculture expands, fertilizer feedstock (ammonia, sulfuric acid, phosphoric acid) consumption increases.

### Correlation with economic indicators

| Signal | Commodity proxy | Mechanism |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Global manufacturing PMI | Ethylene, copper, caustic soda | Packaging, wiring, and chemical intermediates track factory output |
| [[China]] industrial production | Steel, aluminum, sulfuric acid | China consumes 40-60% of most industrial commodities |
| US housing starts | Lumber, copper, PVC (chlorine), steel | Residential construction pulls on multiple commodity chains |
| Agricultural expansion | [[Ammonia]], [[Sulfuric Acid]], [[Phosphoric Acid]] | Fertilizer demand is a function of planted acreage and crop prices |
| Energy transition capex | [[Copper]], [[Aluminum]], [[Sulfuric Acid]], [[Ammonia]] | Electrification, EVs, grid, hydrogen economy |
| Data center buildout | [[Copper]], industrial gases (nitrogen cooling), steel | Power infrastructure, cabling, structural steel |

The American Chemistry Council publishes a Chemical Activity Barometer (CAB) that aggregates production, inventory, and equity data across the US chemical sector. It has led the US business cycle by an average of six months over its history. The US PPI for sulfuric acid (FRED: WPU0613020T1) is a useful narrow-spectrum proxy.

### Limitations as leading indicators

Industrial commodity prices are noisy macro signals because supply shocks can dominate demand signals. A copper price spike from a mine disruption in [[Chile]] does not signal economic expansion. Chinese smelter overcapacity flooding the sulfuric acid market does not signal industrial contraction. European caustic soda prices spiking on energy costs reflects European competitiveness, not global demand. Volume data (production, shipments, capacity utilization) is a cleaner signal than price data, but it lags by weeks to months.

## Current dynamics (2025-2026)

### China: the floor and the ceiling

[[China]] produces 40-60% of most industrial commodities and consumes a comparable share. The macro read depends on which China you are looking at:

- Manufacturing PMI (official NBS): 49.0 in February 2026, second straight contraction month. Property-driven demand for steel, cement, and copper continues to weaken.
- Manufacturing PMI (private RatingDog): 52.1 in February 2026, highest since December 2020. Export-oriented manufacturing in autos, solar, and electronics is expanding.
- Industrial profits: +0.6% in 2025, a stabilization after the 2023 decline, but not strong enough for a broad commodity re-rating.
- Chemical production: grew ~6.8% in 2024 vs. 1.1% for the rest of the world, according to [[BASF]]'s annual report. China is pulling away from the global chemical industry in both scale and growth.

The net read: China is providing a volume floor for industrial commodities through export-oriented manufacturing, but the property downturn caps upside for construction-linked materials. The two Chinas (export factory vs. domestic consumer) are running at different speeds, and the commodity signal depends on which China your commodity serves.

### Energy transition demand pull

The structural demand shift from energy transition is the single most important long-term force in industrial commodities:

- [[Copper]]: structural deficit expected from 2026. S&P Global projects demand rising from 34.5 Mt (2025) to 42 Mt (2030) to 70+ Mt (2050). EVs use 2.9x more copper than conventional vehicles. AI data centers are an additive demand source that was not in any forecast before 2023.
- [[Aluminum]]: deficit expected 2026-2027. EV lightweighting, solar panel framing, and power cable demand compound against China's 45 Mt capacity cap. Global production at ~74.5 Mt, growing 2% YoY.
- [[Sulfuric Acid]]: lithium spodumene processing (~7-8 tonnes acid per tonne LCE), nickel HPAL, and rare earth extraction are creating structurally new demand. Each 100 kt of new spodumene-route LCE capacity requires ~700-800 kt of H2SO4. See the [[Sulfuric Acid]] product note for the full thesis.
- [[Ammonia]]: the IMO's net-zero 2050 target for shipping could create 150-200 Mt of incremental ammonia demand if it captures 35-50% of the marine fuel mix. Green ammonia costs 3-4x grey today ($885-1,050/t vs. $250-400/t); cost parity optimistically 2028-2030 in favorable regions. The [[NEOM]] Green Hydrogen Complex ($8.4B, [[Saudi Arabia]]) targeting 1.2 Mt/year of green ammonia by ~2027. See [[Green hydrogen]].
- Industrial gases: hydrogen production and distribution is the growth thesis for [[Linde]], [[Air Liquide]], and [[Air Products]]. Semiconductor fab expansion in the US, [[Japan]], and Europe drives demand for high-purity nitrogen, argon, and specialty gases.

### Petrochemical overcapacity

The flip side of energy transition demand is structural oversupply in traditional petrochemicals:

- Ethylene: capacity additions in [[China]] and the US Gulf have outpaced demand. China's excess ethylene capacity hit 11.5 Mt in 2025 (record), up 6.3 Mt from 2024. Global production ~177 Mt in 2024 against a market valued at ~$163 billion.
- Propylene: global production ~137 Mt in 2025, with 36% capacity growth expected through 2030. Asia Pacific holds ~55% of global polypropylene volume.
- Chlor-alkali: global capacity at ~287 Mt in 2025, growing 3.1% annually. But the industry is in a low-profitability cycle from weak PVC demand and persistent oversupply. China plans 2.19 Mt of new caustic soda capacity in 2026.
- The European chemical industry faces a structural competitiveness crisis. High energy costs drove European sulfuric acid production down 4.3% in 2023 and forced fertilizer plant closures in [[Germany]] and the [[Netherlands]]. [[BASF]]'s Ludwigshafen complex -- the world's largest integrated chemical site -- is undergoing restructuring. European chlor-alkali producers are evaluating capacity rationalization.

### Reshoring and infrastructure spending

US reshoring of manufacturing and the Inflation Reduction Act / CHIPS Act capital deployment are creating incremental demand for copper, steel, industrial gases, and construction materials. The buildout of semiconductor fabs, battery plants, and clean energy manufacturing in the US requires industrial commodities at scale -- but this demand is still small relative to China's consumption. Electric utility capacity additions to support reshored manufacturing are driving copper wire and transformer demand.

## Structural dynamics to watch

1. The China bifurcation -- export-oriented manufacturing expanding while property-linked demand contracts. Which force dominates determines the direction for steel, cement, copper, and base chemicals.
2. Energy transition demand vs. petrochemical overcapacity -- copper, aluminum, and sulfuric acid are structurally short; ethylene, propylene, and PVC feedstock are structurally long. The commodity complex is splitting.
3. European chemical deindustrialization -- energy costs are driving permanent capacity closures in chlor-alkali, fertilizers, and basic chemicals. Production is migrating to the US Gulf (cheap gas), Middle East (cheap energy), and China (scale).
4. The sulfur supply question -- if oil refining declines over decades, sulfur (a refinery byproduct) becomes scarce, and sulfuric acid transitions from involuntary oversupply to primary-production deficit. See [[Sulfuric Acid]] for the long-arc thesis.
5. Ammonia as energy carrier -- if green ammonia costs reach parity with grey by 2030, the demand implications dwarf the current fertilizer-dominated market. See [[Ammonia]] and [[Green hydrogen]].
6. Industrial gas oligopoly durability -- [[Linde]], [[Air Liquide]], and [[Air Products]] control 80%+ of the market with long-term contracts and on-site production. Semiconductor expansion and hydrogen infrastructure are additive demand vectors. The question is whether new entrants (China, Middle East) can disrupt the oligopoly in their home markets.
