#concept #longevity #biotech #healthcare #regulatory

**TAME** (Targeting Aging with Metformin) is a proposed large clinical trial designed not to prove that metformin works, but to establish that biological aging itself can be treated as a regulatory endpoint — the missing piece of the [[Longevity]] thesis. It is the field's flagship attempt to get the [[#Why it matters|FDA to recognize "aging" as something a drug can target]].

## Design

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Drug | Metformin, 1,500 mg/day vs placebo |
| Population | ~3,000 non-diabetic adults aged 65–79 |
| Duration | ~4 years |
| Endpoint | Composite of age-related outcomes (cardiovascular events, cancer, dementia, mortality) — not a single disease |
| Sponsor | American Federation for Aging Research (AFAR) |
| FDA status | Trial design agreed with the FDA; funding remains only partial |

Chart data not applicable: TAME is a clinical trial, not a tradeable entity — no price or market series exists to plot.

## Why it matters

Regulators approve drugs for specific diseases anchored to the WHO International Classification of Diseases, and aging is not on that list — so an anti-aging drug has no indication to be approved *for*. TAME's purpose is to create a precedent: if the FDA accepts a composite "diseases of aging" endpoint, anyone with the capital could pursue the same path for other gerotherapeutics. It is the regulatory-pathway counterpart to the [[Longevity|disease-of-aging proxy]] and [[Loyal|animal-first]] workarounds — three different routes around the same missing indication. The chronic underfunding of TAME is itself a signal: there is no obvious commercial sponsor for a generic drug (metformin is off-patent), which is exactly why the proof-of-concept burden has fallen to a nonprofit.
