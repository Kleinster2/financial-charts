# Listing rule modernisation

Listing rule modernisation — the [[Financial Conduct Authority|FCA]]'s overhaul of the [[UK]] Listing Rules, the most significant reform of the London listing regime in over a decade. Final rules were published 11 July 2024 and took effect 29 July 2024. The reform's flagship change replaced the two-tier premium/standard listing structure with a single "Equity Shares in Commercial Companies" (ESCC) category, and stripped out several requirements — mandatory shareholder votes on significant and related-party transactions, the three-year financial track-record requirement, the "clean" working-capital statement — that issuers and advisers had cited as deterrents to a London listing.

The reform is the supply-side thread of the broader [[UK]] capital-markets reform package — it makes listing in London easier and less procedurally burdensome, where [[Pension reform]] addresses the demand side (deepening the domestic institutional bid). Both trace to the same diagnosis: London has lost relative scale and listing flow to [[NYSE]] and Nasdaq over two decades, with high-profile defections (Arm's 2023 [[Nasdaq]] listing being the totemic example) and a steady decline in [[LSE]] main-market company count. The political driver under [[Rachel Reeves]] is to make London a venue that profitable private fintechs like [[Revolut]] and [[Monzo]] would rationally choose.

---

## Origin: the Hill Review

The reform traces to the UK Listing Review led by [[Jonathan Hill|Lord Hill]], commissioned by the Treasury and published Spring 2021. The Hill Review diagnosed the [[UK]] listing regime as too restrictive relative to international competitors and recommended a package of changes — dual-class share permissions, lower free-float thresholds, a rethink of the premium/standard split, and prospectus-regime reform. The [[Financial Conduct Authority|FCA]] ran multiple consultation rounds (most recently CP23/31) before the final 2024 rules. The arc from Hill Review (2021) to in-force rules (July 2024) spanned three years and crossed a change of government — the diagnosis and direction held across the [[Jeremy Hunt|Hunt]]→[[Rachel Reeves|Reeves]] transition.

## What changed (July 2024)

| Old regime | New regime |
|------------|-----------|
| Two tiers: premium + standard listing | Single ESCC category for commercial-company equity |
| Shareholder vote on significant transactions (Class 1) | No mandatory vote; disclosure-based regime |
| Shareholder vote on related-party transactions | No mandatory vote; board + sponsor fair-and-reasonable opinion |
| Three-year revenue / financial track record required | Requirement removed |
| "Clean" working-capital statement | Modified — qualified statements permitted |
| Restrictive dual-class share rules | Enhanced voting rights / dual-class structures permitted with conditions |

The shift in philosophy is from a prescriptive, vote-gated regime toward a disclosure-based one — moving the [[UK]] closer to the US model where investor protection rests on disclosure and litigation rather than mandatory shareholder approval of individual transactions. Critics (notably some UK institutional investors and governance advocates) argued the reform trades away shareholder-protection mechanisms; proponents argued those mechanisms were deterring listings without materially improving outcomes.

## PISCES

A parallel reform: the Private Intermittent Securities and Capital Exchange System. PISCES is a regulatory framework for platforms that allow trading of shares in private companies — companies not admitted to a public market — during intermittent, scheduled trading windows.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Purpose | Secondary liquidity for private-company shares |
| Not | A capital-raising venue — no new-share issuance |
| Trading model | Intermittent windows, not continuous |
| Operator model | Existing trading platforms can operate within the PISCES framework |
| Target users | Pre-IPO companies, employee shareholders, early investors seeking liquidity |

PISCES matters to the [[Revolut]] / [[Monzo]] question because it offers a middle path — private companies can give shareholders and employees periodic liquidity without the disclosure and scrutiny burden of a full public listing. For a profitable, well-capitalised private fintech "not in a hurry" to IPO, a PISCES-style window reduces the pressure to list at all, which cuts both ways for the London-listing pitch: it keeps the company in the UK ecosystem but may delay the full listing the [[HM Treasury|UK government]] wants.

## Implications for actors

### UK fintech / IPO candidates

[[Revolut]] (leaning [[NYSE]]) and [[Monzo]] ("not in a hurry") are the marquee [[Fintech]] test cases. The reform removes procedural friction but does not close the valuation gap — US listings still command higher multiples and deeper institutional coverage. Listing rule modernisation is necessary-but-not-sufficient for the London-listing pitch; it has to be paired with the demand-side [[Pension reform]] effect, which takes years to materialise.

### LSE / LSEG

[[LSE]] is the direct beneficiary if the reform reverses the listing decline. [[LSEG]] has lobbied for exactly this package. The risk: reform that makes listing easier without fixing the liquidity/valuation gap could attract listings that then underperform, reinforcing rather than reversing the London-discount narrative.

### Issuers already listed

Companies on the old premium segment were mapped into the ESCC category automatically. They gain flexibility (fewer mandatory votes) but their existing institutional shareholders lose some approval rights — a live source of governance friction.

## Synthesis

Listing rule modernisation is the cleanest, fastest-acting thread of the [[UK]] capital-markets reform package — a regulator can rewrite listing rules in a single rulebook change, where [[Pension reform]] needs a decade of allocation shifts to land. But "fastest to enact" is not "fastest to results." The reform removes procedural deterrents; it does not manufacture the institutional demand, analyst coverage, and valuation multiples that actually decide where a company lists.

That is the structural limit. A profitable fintech choosing between London and [[NYSE]] is not primarily weighing whether it must hold a shareholder vote on a Class 1 transaction — it is weighing the multiple, the liquidity, and the investor base. Listing rule modernisation addresses the procedural layer; the valuation layer depends on [[Pension reform]] succeeding in deepening the domestic bid. The two threads are complements, and the supply-side one alone is necessary-but-not-sufficient.

The PISCES wrinkle sharpens the irony: by giving private companies a sanctioned secondary-liquidity mechanism, the reform package may have reduced the very urgency to list that the listing-rule simplification was meant to capture. A reform designed to pull companies onto public markets has, in PISCES, also built them a credible reason to stay private longer. Whether [[Revolut]] and [[Monzo]] ultimately list in London is the real-world test of whether the whole package works — and the answer will say more about valuation and demand than about rulebooks.
