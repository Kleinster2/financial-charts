**INVEST Act** (Incentivizing New Ventures and Economic Strength Through Capital Formation Act of 2025) — package of 22 bills passed by the US House of Representatives on December 11, 2025 aimed at lowering regulatory requirements relating to securities and capital markets. As of May 2026 the package has not advanced through the Senate. The legislation's stated purpose is to reduce the cost and complexity of going public for emerging companies and to broaden the pathways into US capital markets.

## Synthesis

The INVEST Act is a piece of the broader 2025-2026 regulatory shift toward easier capital formation, alongside the SEC's proposed expansion of the JOBS Act IPO on-ramp, scaled disclosure requirements indexed to issuer size, and adjacent self-regulatory-organization rule changes (such as [[Nasdaq Enhanced SPAC IPO listing standards 2026|SR-NASDAQ-2026-033]] which moves in the opposite direction on SPAC IPO thresholds specifically). The pattern is consistent with a deregulatory phase intended to reverse the post-2020-2021 IPO drought that has left private companies sitting on the sidelines longer and concentrating exit value in late-stage private rounds rather than public-market liquidity events.

Specific provisions include:

- Allowing emerging growth companies to provide the previous two years of profit and loss statements when applying to list on a national securities exchange, instead of the current three years.
- Expanding the definition of "Emerging Growth Company" to include issuers up to $1.235 billion in annual gross revenues (already in effect under existing SEC rules but codified here).
- Various tax and reporting accommodations targeting the friction between private capital formation and public listing.

The legislative calculus is that lowering listing friction encourages more IPOs, which broadens retail access to growth-stage equity returns and reduces the late-stage private-market premium currently captured by venture and growth-equity firms. The counter-argument — that lower listing standards correlate with worse retail outcomes (see the SPAC boom-bust) — is implicit in the simultaneous Nasdaq SPAC tightening. The two reforms are operating at different points on the listing-quality curve: INVEST Act broadens entry for operating companies, the Nasdaq rule narrows entry for SPAC shells.
