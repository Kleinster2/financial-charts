# Fiscal Dominance

A regime in which [[Federal Reserve|central bank]] policy is subordinated to government financing needs. When sovereign debt grows large enough that the government cannot tolerate sustained high real interest rates, monetary policy effectively becomes a tool of fiscal management — the central bank must accommodate the Treasury regardless of inflation mandates.

---

## The mechanism

The classic framework: when debt-to-GDP exceeds a threshold (debated, but roughly 100%+), the government faces a structural constraint. Higher rates increase debt servicing costs, which widen deficits, which require more borrowing, which pushes rates higher — a doom loop that can only be broken by either fiscal consolidation (politically impossible in mature democracies) or monetary accommodation (the path of least resistance). The central bank may formally maintain independence, but its range of viable policy narrows to options compatible with Treasury solvency.

The US entered this territory gradually. Structural deficits driven by debt, demographics, and entitlements mean the government runs large deficits regardless of the economic cycle. The [[Federal Reserve]] held rates at 5.25-5.50% through 2023-2024 while the Treasury ran ~$2T annual deficits — the tension was masked by strong nominal GDP growth, but the debt servicing burden crossed $1T/yr annualized by late 2024.

[[Lyn Alden]]'s formulation is the clearest: the US faces both fiscal dominance and the [[Triffin Dilemma]] simultaneously. The "train" of fiscal spending is politically unstoppable regardless of party — the only variable is pace. Her "gradual print" thesis argues the [[Federal Reserve|Fed]] is already accommodating via balance sheet expansion (~$420B/yr annualized as of March 2026, ~6% of base), and the question is whether exogenous shocks (war, energy crisis, recession) force a shift to "big print" ($2T+). See [[Lyn Alden#Fiscal Dominance / "Nothing Stops This Train"|Lyn Alden § Fiscal Dominance]].

[[Zoltan Pozsar]] frames a complementary angle through the monetary plumbing: reserve scarcity, repo market stress, and the structural shift from market-based to policy-driven liquidity provision all point to a regime where the Fed's balance sheet becomes permanently larger — not as stimulus, but as infrastructure.

## Indicators

| Signal | What it means |
|--------|--------------|
| Fed balance sheet expansion during "tightening" | Accommodation despite stated policy |
| Treasury issuance shifting to bills (short duration) | Government avoiding long-term rate lock-in |
| Debt servicing > defense spending | Structural constraint on fiscal flexibility |
| Real rates persistently negative | Inflation tax as implicit debt reduction |
| Central bank buying government bonds at auction | Direct monetization (extreme case) |
