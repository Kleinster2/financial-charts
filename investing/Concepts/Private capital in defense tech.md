#concept #defense #private-markets #venture-capital

Private capital in defense tech — the story of venture, growth, and strategic capital mobilizing into software-defined defense since 2022. A sector that was taboo for top-tier US venture firms before the Russia-Ukraine war is now the default investment thesis of [[Founders Fund]], [[Andreessen Horowitz]], [[General Catalyst]], [[Sequoia Capital]], [[Lux Capital]], and sovereign-backed vehicles. The capital base is what makes companies like [[Anduril]], [[Shield AI]], [[Saronic]], [[Helsing]], and [[Stark]] possible — and it is what distinguishes this defense cycle from every prior one.

See [[Software-defined Defense]] for the company-level sector view and [[Private markets]] for the broader private-capital universe. Regional slice: [[Private capital in European defense tech]].

---

## The pre-2022 taboo

Before the Russia-Ukraine war, defense was largely off-limits for top-tier US venture capital:

- Universities and LP advisory boards flagged weapons-related investments
- [[Palantir]]'s 2003 founding by [[Peter Thiel]] and [[Alex Karp]] was an outlier — even Palantir struggled to raise for years before its 2020 direct listing
- [[Anduril]]'s 2017 founding by [[Palmer Luckey]] was the other early outlier, also backed by Thiel via [[Founders Fund]]
- Aerospace-prime partnerships dominated — startups that did raise typically went to [[Lockheed Martin]] / [[Raytheon]] / [[Boeing]] for joint ventures rather than to VC

The pattern: a small cluster of thesis-driven investors ([[Founders Fund]], [[Lux Capital]], [[8VC]]) carried the sector while mainstream VC stayed away.

---

## The 2022 unlock

The Russia-Ukraine war changed the funding landscape:

| Shift | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| LP tolerance | University and pension LPs softened objections once "defending democracy" became the frame |
| Proof of concept | Cheap Ukrainian drones destroying Russian armor validated the attritable-hardware thesis |
| Government urgency | US DoD Replicator Initiative (thousands of autonomous systems in 18 months) created visible demand |
| Commercial crossover | Starlink demonstrated commercial tech being decisive on the battlefield |
| Talent pipeline | AI/ML engineers previously uninterested in defense began accepting offers post-Ukraine |

[[Andreessen Horowitz]]'s [[American Dynamism]] framing (coined by Katherine Boyle around 2022-2023) named the mainstreaming. The frame: defense, aerospace, critical manufacturing, and national security were reclassified as a single investment area rather than separate silos.

## How private-capital financing differs from prime financing

Defense primes ([[Lockheed Martin]], [[Raytheon]], [[Boeing]]) operate on cost-plus contracts over 10+ year cycles with government as both customer and financier. Software-defined defense companies use a different capital structure:

- Product-first development requires years of burn before procurement revenue
- Talent compensation via equity requires mark-to-market valuations and secondary liquidity
- Iteration speed requires decoupling from annual government budget cycles
- Export and dual-use optionality requires patient, long-duration capital with cross-border flexibility

Return realizations are thin so far — [[Palantir]]'s 2020 direct listing is the only large-scale liquidity event. Secondary markets (Forge, EquityZen, Hiive) carry most of the mark-to-market for [[Anduril]] and peers. The IPO pipeline is forming but has not yet printed.

## Structural features

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Clearances | Security clearances are a throughput constraint on hiring; slower than standard VC-backed tech |
| Procurement cadence | Government contracting cycles run years, not quarters; capital covers long sales cycles |
| Exit history | Only large-scale US liquidity event is [[Palantir]]'s 2020 direct listing; secondary markets carry most mark-to-market |
| Primes involvement | [[Lockheed Martin]], [[Raytheon]], [[Boeing]] participate via acquisitions, JVs, and partnerships rather than as competitors |
| Policy context | Autonomous-weapon regulation, export controls, and cross-border sovereignty rules apply |
| Valuation basis | 2024-2025 marks reference government-contract scale-up assumptions; [[Anduril]] at ~$60B is the largest mark |

Cross-border holdings carry specific frictions — a US-backed company operating in a European market, for instance, triggers political review. See [[Private capital in European defense tech]] for that dimension.
