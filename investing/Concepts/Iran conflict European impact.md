## European electricity price divergence (March 19)

*Source: FT, "Spain is a role model in weathering Iranian oil shocks," March 19, 2026*

![[ft-gas-price-setting-hours-europe-2026.png]]
*Hours where the price of electricity was set by gas in 2026 (%). Source: [[Ember]] on Montel, GME, MIBGAS, ENTSO-E data. Data until March 10, 2026.*

![[ft-q2-electricity-prices-europe-2026.png]]
*Expected electricity prices for Q2 2026 (€/MWh). Source: FT.*

Gas prices have doubled since the start of 2026. But the impact on electricity prices is radically uneven across Europe — a function of energy mix, not geography.

| Country | Gas sets electricity price (% of hours, 2026 YTD) | Q2 2026 forecast (€/MWh) | Why |
|---------|---------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------|-----|
| [[Spain]] | 15% | ~€42 | Nuclear (20%), solar, wind, hydro — structural |
| [[France]] | Low (est. <15%) | ~€30 | Nuclear baseload dominant |
| [[Germany]] | ~40% | ~€90 | Post-nuclear phase-out; gas dependency |
| Netherlands | ~43% | — | Gas-heavy grid |
| [[Italy]] | 89% | ~€125 | No nuclear; gas-dependent |
| UK | ~66% | — | Intermittent renewables without nuclear baseload |

Spain's average electricity price for the remainder of 2026 forecast at ~€66/MWh — half Italy's level. [[Aurora Energy]] (consultancy): UK gas-fired plants set the electricity price ~2/3 of the time despite >50% of power from renewables and nuclear — illustrating that intermittent renewables alone don't decouple electricity from gas without nuclear or hydro baseload.

The political dimension: countries with lower electricity exposure face less pressure to engage militarily in [[Strait of Hormuz]] reopening. Spain's insulation bolsters its diplomatic independence — FT frames it as "one less reason to wade in."

## European refusal on Hormuz naval support (March 19)

*Source: Bloomberg TV, Will Kennedy (Bloomberg News Director for Energy), March 19, 2026*

Trump asked European allies for naval help in the [[Strait of Hormuz]]; most rebuffed. Kennedy's reporting from London and Paris on the European position:

1. Not consulted about the war starting
2. Dubious about its strategy and endgame
3. Dubious about what a military solution to Hormuz looks like — opening it to commercial shipping and keeping it open during active conflict would be "an enormous naval task" that "probably the US Navy, let alone smaller European navies, are not equipped for"
4. View: this might work after the war ends, but not while the conflict is ongoing — "turn your mind to finding a way to end the conflict"

European stance summarized: "we did not ask to be in this conflict and the way that you are asking us to be involved is not realistic."

[[Macron]] has spoken to the Iranian president — diplomatic channels are open. Gulf states maintaining conversations with European allies ([[UAE]]-[[UK]] channel active). But Europe's leverage is limited — only the antagonists can end it.

### Industrial passthrough — BASF 30% price hike (March 19)

[[BASF]] announced it will raise prices on some products by 30% at its Ludwigshafen Verbund — the world's largest integrated chemical complex. This is one of the first concrete examples of energy cost passthrough from the Hormuz crisis into European industrial output.

The passthrough chain: Hormuz closure → naphtha/feedstock shortage → petrochemical plant curtailment → price hikes to downstream customers. Kennedy: naphtha prices "moonshoting" — chemical plants "will slow down, if not close" as feedstock runs out.

### "Happening now, just not evenly distributed" (Kennedy, March 19)

Kennedy's core analytical frame: the market has been complacent, focused on flat crude ($108 Brent), but the real disruption is already unfolding in product markets:

| Market | Signal |
|--------|--------|
| US diesel | $5/gallon — trucking and agriculture hit |
| [[Vietnam]], [[Thailand]] | Fuel rationing underway |
| [[Japan]] | Refineries cutting runs |
| [[China]] | Keeping fuel domestic |
| Naphtha | Prices parabolic — petrochemical feedstock crisis |

The Israel/US strike on [[Iran]]'s gas field (domestic-use, not export-relevant) is the escalation signal: Iran reads it as "the energy system is fair game" — expect intensified attacks on refineries, oilfields, export terminals, and the Strait. [[Qatar]] called the gas field strike an "irresponsible step."

Kennedy: *"Don't concentrate just on the flat price of crude. Yes, 108 is a big number, but look at jet fuel markets, fuel oil markets, diesel markets."* The consumer impact is arriving via fuel costs → price passthrough, with a lag — same dynamic as the Ukraine war, but across more channels simultaneously.

## European gas surge (March 19)

Post-attack gas market reaction (CNBC, Mar 19):

| Benchmark | Move (Mar 19) | Level |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| Dutch TTF (front-month) | +21% (paring earlier gains) | €66.3/MWh ($75.9) |
| US Henry Hub | +4% | $3.19/MMBtu |
| Nymex RBOB gasoline (April) | +4.6% | $3.24/gal (near four-year high) |
| Brent (May delivery) | +8% | $116.2/bbl |
| WTI | +1.4% | $97.65 |

Earlier gas context (week of Mar 2-3): TTF +35% in one day, +76% on the week. [[Goldman Sachs]] estimated Qatar production pause would reduce near-term global LNG supply by ~19%. Goldman warned a monthlong halt to Hormuz flows risks driving TTF and JKM toward €74/MWh — the level that triggered demand destruction during the 2022 European energy crisis (which peaked at €345/MWh in August 2022).

Qatar context: world's second-largest LNG exporter after the US, accounting for ~20% of global shipments ([[Kpler]]). Qatar suspended LNG production Mar 2 after initial drone attacks; Mar 18 missile strikes inflicted "extensive damage" on Ras Laffan Industrial City, the world's largest LNG export facility.

[[Stifel]] analyst Chris Wheaton: "We are much more concerned about European gas prices than we are about oil prices." [[Omnis Investments]] CIO Patrick O'Donnell: LNG disruption "may have more negative implications for the European economy and the reindustrialization that the market has been hoping that we get to see."

Asia LNG exposure from Middle East ([[Invesco]] data):
- [[India]]: 58% of LNG imports from Middle East (~2% of primary energy)
- [[China]]: 26.6% of LNG imports from Middle East
- Singapore: 27% of LNG imports from Middle East
- Other Asia-Pacific: >37% of LNG from Middle East

[[BBH]] global head of markets strategy Elias Haddad: "A protracted conflict that leads to further disruption in energy production and shipping raises the risk of stagflation." Most vulnerable: [[Japan]], [[India]], South Africa, Turkey, Hungary, Malaysia. Least exposed: Norway, [[Canada]], Mexico.

*Source: CNBC, Mar 19 + Mar 3, 2026*

## EU lowers gas storage targets (March 21)

*Source: FT, Ian Johnston and Henry Foy, "EU member states urged to lower gas storage targets due to Iran war," March 21, 2026*

[[EU]] Energy Commissioner [[Dan Jørgensen]] instructed the bloc's energy ministers via letter (sent after markets closed Friday) to lower gas storage filling targets to 80% of capacity — 10 percentage points below the official 90% target introduced after [[Russia]]'s 2022 invasion of [[Ukraine]]. The move aims to reduce demand pressure at a time "where the supply is tense."

Key directives in the letter:
- Do not rush to replenish depleted reserves
- Fill gradually to avoid an "end-of-summer rush" that would pressure markets
- Wait until December 1 to meet storage targets — a month later than the November 1 deadline under existing legislation
- Member states have "flexibility" under existing EU law to fill to 80%, or even 75% if market conditions are unfavourable

Jørgensen: EU energy supply "remains relatively protected" but "recent developments indicate that it could take longer for Qatari [[LNG]] production to return to levels known before the crisis."

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| TTF weekly move (Mar 21 week) | +21.5% |
| TTF since start of Iran conflict | Doubled |
| Netherlands storage level | ~7% |
| EU storage share of winter gas needs | 25-30% |
| Original target (2022 legislation) | 90% by Nov 1 |
| New recommended target | 80% by Dec 1 |
| Floor under adverse conditions | 75% |

The risk: Netherlands at 7% storage heading into filling season means the gap to even 80% is enormous. Two EU officials called the demand-reduction move "low-hanging fruit," but acknowledged it could stoke concern that Europe will not be prepared for next winter's energy shocks.

European leaders at their Thursday summit (March 20) called on the Commission to develop "temporary and targeted measures" to tackle high energy prices caused by imported fossil fuels — this letter is the first policy response.
