#concept #energy #nuclear #fusion

**Nuclear fusion** — energy produced by forcing light atomic nuclei to combine, replicating the reaction that powers the Sun. The opposite of [[Nuclear primer|fission]] (splitting heavy atoms). No fusion reactor has ever delivered commercial electricity. As of 2026, the sector has attracted $7B+ in private capital and $10B+ in government spending across the US, [[China]], [[UK]], and the [[EU]], driven increasingly by [[Artificial intelligence|AI]]-related power demand expectations.

---

## Synthesis

Fusion is transitioning from a pure science story to a capital formation story — but the physics-to-engineering gap remains enormous. The Dec 2022 NIF ignition milestone proved the science works for ICF; HTS magnet breakthroughs have made compact tokamaks plausible. Yet no approach has demonstrated repeatable net energy gain at commercial scale. The capital pouring in ($1.6bn in US private funding in 2025 alone) is a bet on engineering timelines, not physics discovery.

The competitive structure is clarifying: the US leads in private venture funding (CFS, Helion, Inertia, Zap), [[China]] leads in state investment ($6.5-10bn since 2022), and the [[UK]] is now hybridizing with [[UK Fusion Energy]]'s shift from public lab to commercial entity. The key question isn't whether fusion works — it's whether any approach can be engineered to deliver dispatchable power before fission SMRs and renewables+storage fill the gap. For the [[Long nuclear]] thesis, fusion is the 2035+ option on the card; fission is the 2025-2035 play.

## How fusion works

Light nuclei (typically hydrogen isotopes) are heated to 100+ million degrees Celsius, forming a plasma where atoms move fast enough to overcome electrostatic repulsion and fuse. The mass difference between inputs and outputs is released as energy per E=mc².

| Fuel cycle | Reactants | Products | Status |
|------------|-----------|----------|--------|
| D-T (deuterium-tritium) | ²H + ³H | ⁴He + neutron + 17.6 MeV | Standard; easiest to ignite |
| D-D (deuterium-deuterium) | ²H + ²H | Various | Harder; unlimited fuel |
| D-He3 (deuterium-helium-3) | ²H + ³He | ⁴He + proton | Low neutrons; He-3 scarce |
| p-B11 (hydrogen-boron) | ¹H + ¹¹B | 3 ⁴He | Aneutronic; hardest to achieve |

D-T is the default because it has the lowest ignition temperature (~150M °C). Every approach beyond D-T is progressively harder — p-B11 ([[TAE Technologies]]' bet) requires roughly 10x higher temperatures.

## Confinement approaches

The core engineering challenge: how to contain plasma at fusion temperatures long enough for net energy gain.

| Approach | How it works | Key players | Status |
|----------|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Tokamak (magnetic) | Toroidal magnetic field confines plasma | [[Commonwealth Fusion Systems]], [[UK Fusion Energy]], [[Tokamak Energy]], ITER | Most mature; CFS demonstrated 20T HTS magnet (2021) |
| Spherical tokamak | Compact tokamak variant, tighter aspect ratio | [[UK Fusion Energy]] (STEP), [[Tokamak Energy]] | Smaller, potentially cheaper than conventional tokamak |
| Inertial confinement (ICF) | Lasers compress fuel pellets | [[Inertia Enterprises]], [[National Ignition Facility]] | Only approach to achieve scientific breakeven (NIF, Dec 2022) |
| Pulsed FRC | Magnetized plasma rings collide | [[Helion Energy]] | Direct electricity conversion claimed; no net energy yet |
| Field-reversed config | Sustained FRC plasma | [[TAE Technologies]] | Targeting aneutronic p-B11; highest technical risk |
| Z-pinch | Electrical current compresses plasma | [[Zap Energy]] | No magnets or lasers; simplest hardware |

## Key milestones

| Date | Milestone | Significance |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| 1958 | ZETA (UK) claims fusion | Retracted — actually thermonuclear neutrons, not sustained fusion |
| 1968 | Soviet T-3 tokamak | Proved tokamak concept; redirected global research |
| 1997 | JET achieves 16 MW | Record fusion power output for decades |
| 2006 | ITER construction begins (Cadarache, [[France]]) | $22B+ international megaproject; 35 nations |
| 2021 | CFS demonstrates 20T HTS magnet | Enables compact tokamak designs |
| 2022-02 | JET achieves 59 MJ (5-sec sustained) | Final record before decommissioning |
| 2022-12 | NIF achieves ignition | First-ever net energy gain from fusion target; 3.15 MJ out vs 2.05 MJ laser in |
| 2023-05 | [[Microsoft]]-[[Helion Energy]] PPA | First-ever commercial fusion electricity agreement (2028 target) |
| 2025-12 | [[TAE Technologies]]-TMTG merger announced | $6B deal; first publicly traded fusion entity |
| 2026-02 | [[Inertia Enterprises]] $450M Series A | Largest ICF-specific private raise; NIF technology commercialization |
| 2026-03 | [[UK Fusion Energy]] commercial restructuring | UK government opens flagship STEP project to private investment |

## Supply chain

Fusion creates demand for materials and components that don't yet exist at commercial scale.

| Input | Role | Key supplier |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Lithium-6 | Tritium breeding (D-T reactors need it) | [[EnergyX]] (NUKE-it platform) |
| HTS tape (REBCO) | High-field magnets for compact tokamaks | [[VEIR]], various |
| Niobium alloys (Nb3Sn) | Superconducting magnets (ITER-type) | [[CBMM]] |
| Deuterium | Fuel (extracted from seawater) | Abundant — not a constraint |
| Tritium | Fuel (must be bred from Li-6) | Scarce; ~20kg global inventory |
| Beryllium | Neutron multiplier in blankets | Limited suppliers |

Tritium scarcity is the binding constraint for D-T fusion at scale. Global tritium inventory is ~20kg, decaying at 5.5% per year. Commercial fusion reactors must breed their own tritium from lithium-6 — hence [[EnergyX]]'s strategic position.

## Fusion vs fission for AI power

| Dimension | Fusion | Fission |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Timeline | 2035+ (if ever) | Now (restarts) to 2030s (SMRs) |
| Technical risk | High — no commercial prototype exists | Low — 450+ reactors operating globally |
| Fuel | Abundant (deuterium from water, lithium) | [[Uranium]] supply chain constraints, HALEU |
| Waste | Minimal long-lived waste | Long-lived waste (political problem) |
| Public acceptance | Higher (no meltdown risk) | Lower (Fukushima, Chernobyl legacy) |
| AI relevance (2025-2030) | None | High ([[Constellation Energy]] TMI, [[Amazon]]-Talen deals) |
| AI relevance (2030+) | Possible option | Primary baseload source |

For the [[Long nuclear]] thesis: fission is the trade, fusion is the option. Fusion matters for positioning if timelines compress, but no portfolio should depend on it delivering this decade.
