# Ethane

Two-carbon hydrocarbon (C₂H₆) — the simplest feedstock for producing [[Ethylene|ethylene]] and, through it, [[Polyethylene]]. Recovered as a natural gas liquid (NGL) at gas processing plants, priced off natural gas at the upstream end and off ethylene at the downstream end. Almost nowhere to go outside chemical cracking: too light to be a fuel without processing, too heavy to leave in the pipeline gas stream once the calorific-value spec bites. That captive-supply structure is what gives the US its structural petrochemical cost advantage.

---

## Synopsis

Ethane is the pivotal commodity in global [[Polyethylene]] economics. Unlike [[Naphtha|naphtha]], which is a mid-distillate priced off crude oil and freely traded worldwide, ethane is a stranded NGL: it has no consumer-facing market, very limited cross-border trade, and its price is anchored to domestic gas-processing economics in a few plays. Roughly 70% of US ethane is produced in three plays — the [[Marcellus]]/Utica ([[Appalachia]]), the [[Permian Basin]], and the Eagle Ford — from "wet" gas where ethane is a co-product with methane, propane, and butane. When shale operators chase wet-gas economics, ethane supply runs ahead of domestic cracker demand, and the surplus either gets "rejected" back into the pipeline gas stream (where it's sold as Btu, not feedstock) or exported via a handful of US Gulf Coast terminals.

The 2026 Iran war exposed the reverse of the [[Fortress China]] story: while Asian and European naphtha crackers are hostage to [[Strait of Hormuz|Hormuz]] naphtha flows, US ethane crackers are running on a domestic feedstock whose price can't move with the war. That has widened the gap between US ethylene cash costs and global naphtha cash costs to multi-year highs, with margin transferring to [[LyondellBasell]], [[Dow]], Westlake, [[CPChem]], ExxonMobil, Formosa Plastics USA, and midstream enabler [[Enterprise Products]].

## What ethane is and is not

| Property | Value / Note |
|----------|--------------|
| Chemical formula | C₂H₆ (two carbons, six hydrogens) |
| Phase at pipeline conditions | Gas; liquefied (LPG-adjacent) for storage and marine transport |
| NGL "spec" position | Heaviest of the "dry NGLs"; between methane (C₁) and propane (C₃) |
| Calorific value | ~1,770 Btu/ft³ vs ~1,010 for methane — so leaving it in the gas stream enriches Btu content |
| Fuel use outside cracking | Minimal (direct combustion inefficient; rarely used as motor/heating fuel) |
| Primary commercial use | Steam cracking to make [[Ethylene|ethylene]] (~80% of global cracker feedstock in the US; ~15% globally) |

Ethane is not [[LPG]] (propane + butane), not [[Naphtha]] (C5-C10 refined from crude), and not [[Natural gas]] (primarily methane). It is the "bridge molecule" that lets shale gas production lean into petrochemicals rather than pure energy markets.

## Source and geography

### US shale plays (dominant)

| Play | Ethane output (mbpd, 2025 est.) | Notes |
|------|------------------------------|-------|
| [[Marcellus]]/Utica ([[Appalachia]]) | ~900 | Largest and richest wet-gas source; originally stranded, now exports via Mariner East pipeline |
| [[Permian Basin]] (West Texas, SE New Mexico) | ~700 | Growing fast with oil-led associated gas; exports via Mont Belvieu |
| Eagle Ford (South Texas) | ~250 | Mature, steady contributor; short haul to Gulf Coast crackers |
| SCOOP/STACK ([[Oklahoma]]) | ~150 | Secondary wet-gas play |

US total recoverable ethane production ~2.3-2.5 mbpd (2025). Domestic cracker demand ~1.8 mbpd. The surplus (~0.5-0.7 mbpd) is either exported or rejected into the gas stream.

### Outside the US

| Region | Source | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Middle East | [[Saudi Arabia]], [[Qatar]], [[UAE]] (associated gas) | Fed directly into local crackers ([[SABIC]], QAPCO, Borouge); almost no export |
| Canada | Alberta gas processing | Feeds Alberta crackers (NOVA Chemicals); some export to US |
| Norway / UK North Sea | Associated gas | Feeds [[Ineos]] Grangemouth and Rafnes; declining with field maturity |
| Russia | Associated gas from West Siberia | [[Gazprom]] / Sibur feedstock; sanction-constrained |
| Argentina (Vaca Muerta) | Emerging | Small export volumes to Chile / Brazil, not global-scale |
| China | Imported (not domestic production) | Buys US ethane for a handful of coastal VCM/PE crackers |

Outside the US, ethane is either fed directly into co-located state-linked petrochemical complexes (Middle East) or produced in declining volumes (North Sea). There is no liquid global ethane market the way there is for naphtha or LPG.

## The US/global price disconnect

Ethane trades at Mont Belvieu (Texas Gulf Coast) and Conway (Kansas) hubs, with basis differentials to Appalachia ethane via pipeline tariffs. Pricing ranges (2025 baseline):

| Price point | Typical range | Driver |
|-------------|---------------|--------|
| Mont Belvieu ethane | 18-28¢/gallon (~$95-150/tonne) | US cracker demand + export pull |
| "Rejection floor" | ~Btu-equivalent to [[Henry Hub]] natural gas | When ethane sells for less than its Btu value in gas, processors stop extracting it |
| Global ethane-equivalent (Middle East) | ~$100-150/tonne | Government-set; not a real market |
| Naphtha (Singapore / ARA) | ~$550-700/tonne | Tracks Brent crude closely |
| "Ethane advantage" vs naphtha | $400-600/tonne feedstock cost gap | Full-cycle, converts to ~$200-350/tonne ethylene cash cost advantage |

The reason the disconnect persists: US ethane can't get out fast enough. Export terminals are capacity-constrained (see below), Appalachian ethane struggles to reach Gulf Coast terminals via limited pipeline capacity, and when prices fall the rejection mechanism kicks in to support a floor — but on the upside, prices can't rally to global naphtha equivalence without export capacity that doesn't yet exist. The spread is a structural rent, not a price that will arbitrage away.

## Export infrastructure

| Terminal / pipeline | Operator | Capacity / role |
|----------------------|----------|-----------------|
| Morgan's Point Ethane Export (Houston) | [[Enterprise Products]] | ~275 kbpd ethane + ethylene; largest in the world; expansion to >400 kbpd underway |
| Marcus Hook (Philadelphia) | Energy Transfer | ~70-80 kbpd ethane export via Mariner East pipeline from Appalachia |
| Nederland (TX) | Energy Transfer | Ethane and ethylene export |
| Orbit (Beaumont, TX) | [[Enterprise Products]] / Navigator JV | ~150 kbpd ethane export to Asia (mainly China) |

Asia-bound ethane loads onto Very Large Ethane Carriers (VLECs) — refrigerated ships typically delivering to Chinese coastal crackers (Satellite Petrochemical, Wanhua Chemical) or Indian crackers (Reliance). These Chinese VLEC-fed crackers are exposed to US policy risk: tariff escalations and re-export restrictions (as telegraphed by US export control review bodies) could choke feedstock, partially neutralizing the [[Fortress China]] framework in petrochemicals.

## The cracker economics

### Ethane cracking yield

| Output | Share of cracker yield |
|--------|------------------------|
| [[Ethylene]] | ~80% |
| [[Propylene]] | ~2% |
| Mixed C4s (butadiene, butylenes) | ~2% |
| Aromatics (benzene, toluene) | ~1% |
| Hydrogen + fuel gas | ~15% |

### Naphtha cracking yield (for comparison)

| Output | Share of cracker yield |
|--------|------------------------|
| [[Ethylene]] | ~30% |
| [[Propylene]] | ~15% |
| Mixed C4s | ~10% |
| Aromatics ([[Petroleum benzene|benzene]], toluene, xylenes) | ~15% |
| Pyrolysis gasoline / fuel gas | ~30% |

Implication: the ethane advantage is really an ethylene / [[Polyethylene]] advantage. US crackers dominate global PE economics but are structurally short on [[Propylene]], butadiene, and aromatics. That's why [[LyondellBasell]] and [[Dow]] also run propane dehydrogenation (PDH) units for on-purpose propylene, and [[Covestro]]/[[BASF]] still import some US ethylene downstream but continue to run naphtha crackers for the aromatics slate.

## 2026 Hormuz crisis impact

| Date | Event / data point |
|------|---------------------|
| Feb 28 2026 | [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] begins; Middle East petchem exports disrupted |
| March 19 | US Gulf Coast ethylene spot at 30.25¢/lb (Mont Belvieu), +12% in two days ([[Enterprise Products]] data) |
| March 19 | North American PE producer operating rates climbing toward ~100%; US ethane cracker utilization low-90s (from ~85% pre-conflict) |
| March 2026 | US ethane price largely unmoved — remains disconnected from global naphtha rally |
| April 11 | Domestic China PE prices roughly doubled in some grades ([[Tidalwave Solutions]] via [[FT]]); naphtha parabolic; [[China PPI]] turns positive YoY first time since 2022 |

The headline: the US ethane-to-ethylene advantage, which had been compressing at ~$200/tonne through 2024-2025 as naphtha cracks were weak and [[Methanol]]/MTO capacity was absorbing Chinese demand, just blew back out to ~$400/tonne. The re-set transfers several billion dollars a year of economic rent from Asian/European naphtha crackers to US ethane crackers while the war persists. See [[Iran conflict supply chain cascade#US polyethylene/ethylene boom]] for the dedicated arbitrage section.

## Why US ethane stays advantaged structurally

1. No practical substitute demand. Ethane isn't a motor fuel, heating fuel, or power-gen feedstock at scale. The rejection floor to [[Henry Hub]] gas Btu value caps downside, but it doesn't create an alternative outlet.
2. Supply grows with oil, not demand. Permian ethane is a co-product of oil production — it shows up whether crackers want it or not. Marcellus wet-gas economics keep pulling ethane out as long as gas is economically extractable.
3. Export capacity is the binding constraint. Morgan's Point and Orbit are running near capacity; new terminal builds take 3-5 years and face permitting friction.
4. VLEC fleet is small. Only a few dozen purpose-built ethane carriers exist globally; fleet expansion is slow and tied to long-term off-take contracts (mostly Chinese and Indian).
5. Cracker builds lock in the advantage. Every new US ethane cracker (Shell Pennsylvania, Formosa Sunshine, potential LYB-Sasol rebuilds) further anchors domestic demand, narrowing the surplus available for export and making the ethane-vs-naphtha gap a persistent feature, not a cycle.
