#concept #commodity #agriculture #softs

Cocoa — tropical soft commodity, primary input to the global chocolate industry. World production ~5M tonnes/year of beans; world consumption of cocoa butter alone runs ~2M tonnes/year. Supply is geographically concentrated: West [[Africa]] produces ~70% of the global crop, with [[Ivory Coast]] and [[Ghana]] together accounting for roughly 60%. Traded on ICE futures (NY and London) and via ETN vehicles (NIB).

## Why prices surged (2024-2025)

A multi-year structural squeeze, not a single shock:

- Adverse weather across [[Ivory Coast]] and [[Ghana]] linked to [[Climate change]] — heavier rains during harvest, drier pod-setting periods, and the spread of swollen shoot virus and black pod disease
- Under-investment in replanting — trees aging past productive peak (~25 years); farmer margins too thin to fund renovation
- Regulated farmgate pricing in Ghana/Ivory Coast left growers exposed when input costs rose faster than state-set prices, discouraging expansion
- Smuggling and artisanal gold mining diverted acreage away from cocoa in both countries

Bean prices ran from under $3,000/tonne in early 2023 to a peak near $12,000/tonne in 2024, squeezing chocolate manufacturers' margins. Cocoa butter — the fat fraction used in premium chocolate — spiked even harder, topping €40,000/tonne in late 2024 before retracing to ~€5,000 as yields recovered and consumer demand softened in Europe and the US.

## Alternatives — disruption layer

The 2024-25 price shock accelerated funding and corporate partnerships in three parallel substitutes:

### Cell-cultured cocoa butter

Grows cocoa cells in bioreactors fed sugars and nutrients — the same fats and flavour compounds as a natural bean, without the tree.

- [[Celleste Bio]] ([[Israel]]) — Mondelez-backed; produced the first lab-grown chocolate bars at [[Cadbury]] Bournville in April 2026. Targeting US/Israel regulatory approval by end of 2027, 50,000 tonnes/year at $12,000-$15,000/tonne
- [[Kokomodo]] ([[Israel]], [[Pluri]] subsidiary) — [[Cargill]] collaboration across beverages, dairy, confectionery; FDA submission planned 2026
- [[Food Brewer]] ([[Switzerland]]) — [[Lindt Spruengli]]-backed

### Cocoa-free "chocolate"

Uses non-cocoa ingredients (grape seeds, sunflower protein, cereals) to recreate chocolate's sensory profile at far lower cost, usually outside the "chocolate" regulatory definition.

- [[Voyage Foods]] (US) — grape seed / sunflower protein formulation; [[Cargill]] distribution partner
- [[Win-Win]] (UK) — fermentation from cereals and legumes

### Economics vs. nature

Independent adviser Raphaël Felenbok (Win-Win board) frames lab-grown as a "moonshot" — high energy use, capital cost, and regulatory hurdles — while ingredient substitution can scale faster. Alternatives combined were under 1% of the chocolate market as of April 2026.

Celleste's target price of $12,000-$15,000/tonne sits well above the current conventional cocoa butter price of ~€5,000/tonne, but within the range that conventional cocoa butter traded at through much of 2024-early 2025. The pitch is structural supply security rather than day-one cost competitiveness.

---

## Reader's note on market size

With conventional cocoa butter consumption at ~2M tonnes/year and Celleste alone targeting 50,000 tonnes/year at full scale, the entire first generation of cell-cultured cocoa capacity — even if every announced project delivers — represents low-single-digit share of global demand. The disruption potential sits in the tail: who controls supply when the next climate-driven yield shock hits West [[Africa]], and whether chocolate manufacturers diversify their cocoa butter sourcing the way food companies diversified away from single-source ingredients after the 2010s supply shocks.
