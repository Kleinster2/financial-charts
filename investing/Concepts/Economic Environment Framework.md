# Economic Environment Framework

The Economic Environment Framework is the macro foundation of [[Ray Dalio]]'s All Weather approach: an asset's return is driven primarily by two forces — economic growth and inflation, each either rising or falling — producing four environments. Because no one can reliably predict which comes next, the prescription is to balance risk across all four so the portfolio is never catastrophically exposed to any single one.

## The four quadrants

Growth (rising/falling) × inflation (rising/falling):

| | Growth rising | Growth falling |
|---|---|---|
| Inflation rising | Q2 — growth + inflation (late cycle, reflation) | Q4 — stagflation |
| Inflation falling | Q1 — goldilocks | Q3 — deflation / recession |

- Q1 goldilocks: equities and credit lead, bonds stable (1990s, 2017, much of the 2010s).
- Q2 reflation: commodities, TIPS, EM lead, equities hold early (2004–06, 2021).
- Q3 deflation/recession: long [[Treasuries]] and safe-haven currencies (USD/JPY/CHF) lead, equities and credit fall (2008, Mar 2020, 2001).
- Q4 stagflation: commodities, gold, TIPS lead, both legs of a 60/40 fall (1970s, partially 2022).

## Asset sensitivity

Each asset has a growth beta and an inflation beta; that pair places it in the grid:

| Asset | Growth sensitivity | Inflation sensitivity |
|-------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Equities | ++ | + |
| Nominal bonds | − | −− |
| [[TIPS]] | neutral | ++ |
| [[Commodities]] | + | ++ |
| Gold | neutral | + |
| Credit | ++ | − |
| Cash | − | − |

Some assets span quadrants: equities work in Q1+Q2 (growth up), TIPS in Q2+Q4 (inflation up), gold across Q2/Q4 and partially Q3 (inflation hedge and crisis hedge at once).

## Correlations shift by quadrant

The framework's sharpest insight, and the link to [[Stock-Bond Correlation Regime]]: the stock-bond correlation is not a constant — it is a function of which quadrant you are in.

| Quadrant | Stock-bond correlation | Consequence |
|----------|------------------------|-------------|
| Q1 goldilocks | Negative | Bonds hedge equities — 60/40 works |
| Q2 reflation | Positive | Both hurt by rate hikes |
| Q3 deflation | Negative | Flight to bonds — 60/40 works |
| Q4 stagflation | Positive | Both hurt by inflation |

The 60/40 works in Q1 and Q3 and fails in Q2 and Q4 — which is why the 2022 inflation shock (a Q4-lite regime) broke it, and why [[Risk Parity]] adds commodities and gold to cover the quadrants nominal bonds cannot.

## The All Weather principle

Allocate equal risk — not equal capital — to each quadrant, so no single environment can sink the book:

| Quadrant | Risk budget | Instruments |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Q1 | 25% | Equities + nominal bonds |
| Q2 | 25% | Equities + commodities + TIPS |
| Q3 | 25% | Long nominal bonds |
| Q4 | 25% | Commodities + TIPS + gold |

This requires [[Risk Budgeting]] and leverage: bonds are low-volatility, so to contribute an equal share of risk they must be sized above their capital weight (see [[Equal Risk Contribution]]). The framework does not predict regimes — it accepts underperformance in any single one in exchange for never being catastrophically wrong.

## Quadrants vs market regimes

The economic quadrant ("what to hold") is distinct from the market regime ("how much to hold"):

| | Quadrants | Market regimes |
|---|-----------|----------------|
| Basis | Growth × inflation fundamentals | Volatility, correlations |
| Observable in real time? | Hard | Easier (market data) |
| Timeframe | Months–years | Days–months |
| Use | Strategic asset selection | Tactical position sizing |

Quadrants are clear only in hindsight, so the discipline is to stay balanced (strategic) and let measured market regimes adjust leverage (tactical) — not to bet on the next quadrant.

## Synthesis

The framework's power is not prediction but humility: it concedes the next quadrant is unknowable and builds for all four. That converts the allocation question from "what will happen?" to "what am I unbalanced against?" — and for most portfolios the answer is Q4 stagflation, where nominal bonds and equities both lose. This is the macro case for [[Risk Parity]]: if you cannot know the quadrant, equal risk across quadrants dominates a capital-weighted bet that implicitly assumes Q1. The input it cannot escape is the correlation structure, which itself moves by quadrant ([[Stock-Bond Correlation Regime]]) — so the framework is robust to growth and inflation surprises but still exposed to the correlation regime shifting underneath it.
