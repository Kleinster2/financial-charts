The fertilizer industry generates ~250 million tonnes of phosphogypsum waste annually — a radioactive byproduct of phosphoric acid production that has accumulated to billions of tonnes in stacks worldwide. That waste contains trace rare earth elements at 0.4-0.5% concentration. [[Rainbow Rare Earths]] has developed a hydrometallurgical process to extract those REE without any hard rock mining, and the economics at the Uberaba project in [[Brazil]] (51% [[Mosaic]] / 49% [[Rainbow Rare Earths|Rainbow]]) are extraordinary: $916M post-tax NPV, 45% IRR, 1.7-year payback on $279M capex. If this scales, it turns the world's largest environmental waste stream into a non-[[China]] rare earth supply chain.

---

The story starts with a waste problem. For every tonne of [[Phosphoric Acid]] produced (measured as P2O5, the industry-standard unit) via the wet process — the standard route for ~90% of global production — roughly 5 tonnes of phosphogypsum (calcium sulfate) are generated. At ~50 Mt P2O5/year global production, that implies ~250 Mt of phosphogypsum per year, and the cumulative global inventory runs to billions of tonnes. Florida alone has 1+ billion tonnes stored in 25 stacks, sitting atop the Floridan Aquifer. The material is radioactive — radium-226, radon-222, polonium-210 from the original phosphate rock — and contaminated with heavy metals (cadmium, arsenic, lead). The EPA banned its use in road construction in 1992. It is, in regulatory and environmental terms, a liability.

But phosphogypsum also contains rare earth elements. The REE in the original phosphate ore concentrate in the phosphogypsum during acid digestion. Typical grades are 0.4-0.5% total rare earth oxide (TREO) — low by primary mining standards, but the feedstock is free (it already exists in stacks), requires no mining, crushing, or milling, and the stacks are often co-located with existing chemical processing infrastructure. The question has always been whether extraction could be made economic.

[[Rainbow Rare Earths]] answered that question at Phalaborwa in [[South Africa]], where phosphogypsum stacks from historic phosphate mining hold a JORC-compliant resource (the international standard for reporting mineral reserves) of 35.0 Mt at 0.44% TREO. Rainbow developed a process: mechanical reclamation of gypsum from the stacks (pumped, not mined), followed by a hydrometallurgical leach, continuous ion exchange (CIX), impurity removal, and solvent extraction (SX) separation. Through iterative optimization, they cut leach stages from three to two, residence time from 32 hours to eight, filters from 14 units at 180 m² to seven at 163 m², and added a calcium-depletion step that delivers 93%+ purity while reducing costs. The pilot plant launched in January 2026. First production at Phalaborwa is expected in 2028.

Rainbow then licensed this IP to the Uberaba project in Minas Gerais, [[Brazil]], a 51/49 joint venture with [[Mosaic]] (NYSE: MOS), the world's leading phosphate producer. The preliminary economic assessment, published March 11, 2026, delivered numbers that rewrite the rare earth investment case. On a throughput of ~2.7 million tonnes of phosphogypsum per year at 0.51% TREO, the project would produce 1,971 tonnes/year of separated neodymium-praseodymium (NdPr) oxide and 659 tonnes/year of a SEG+ product (a separated mix of samarium, europium, gadolinium, and heavier rare earths) containing dysprosium (60 tonnes), terbium (14 tonnes), and yttrium (188 tonnes), all at +99.5% purity. Post-tax net present value (NPV) at 10% discount: $916M. At 13.5%: $609M. IRR: 45%. Payback: 1.7 years on $279M capex. Annual revenue: $319M. Average EBITDA: $217M. Project life: 30 years with extension potential. Operating cost: $38/kg against a basket price of $128/kg. The JV is now proceeding to a pre-feasibility study (PFS), with a definitive feasibility study (DFS) targeted for later in 2026, construction start in 2027, and first production in 2030.

The reason these economics work is the absence of mining. Primary rare earth projects require hard rock mining, crushing, milling, and complex metallurgy to liberate REE from mineral matrices. That drives capex into the billions and timelines into decades (US average: 29 years from discovery to production). Phosphogypsum extraction skips all of that. The feedstock already exists, already sits above ground, already has infrastructure around it, and the owner (a fertilizer company) is motivated to reduce its waste liability. The byproduct after REE extraction is cleaner gypsum that can be repurposed. This is waste-to-value in the most literal sense.

The geopolitical implications are significant. [[Brazil]] holds ~22M metric tons of rare earth reserves (#2 globally) but produces almost nothing — the Uberaba project could make [[Mosaic]]'s fertilizer waste a faster path to Brazilian rare earth production than conventional mining. Florida's 25 phosphogypsum stacks contain an estimated 200,000 tonnes of trapped REE, and [[Mosaic]]'s three active Florida facilities generate ~660 tonnes of mixed rare earths annually in their phosphogypsum output. If the Phalaborwa/Uberaba process can be replicated on Florida feedstock, the US could develop a domestic rare earth supply chain from existing industrial waste — no new mines, no 29-year permitting cycles, no environmental opposition to greenfield projects. The DOE has funded research into this pathway, and academic groups at Case Western Reserve, Penn State, and Clemson are working on extraction processes.

This creates a strategic tension that neither [[Brazil]] nor the US has openly acknowledged. [[Brazil]]'s current rare earth leverage — the reason the US, [[EU]], and [[India]] are all courting Brasília — rests partly on the assumption that the US needs Brazilian cooperation to build a non-[[China]] supply chain. But if [[Mosaic]] can replicate the Uberaba process on its own Florida feedstock, the US strategic urgency for a [[Brazil]] deal decreases. The regulatory path is harder in Florida (EPA NORM rules, Floridan Aquifer proximity, the 1992 phosphogypsum reuse ban) and the process would need re-engineering for different phosphate rock chemistry. Uberaba is also 4+ years ahead of any hypothetical Florida project — no US company has a comparable pilot plant. But the direction is clear: [[Brazil]]'s leverage window has a shelf life. [[Roberto Goulart Menezes|Goulart Menezes]] calling the [[India]] MOU a "pilot before negotiating with the US" reads as [[Brazil]] extracting maximum concessions while the window is open. If Uberaba succeeds, it ironically accelerates the technology transfer that eventually makes [[Brazil]]'s cooperation less essential.

The challenges are real but bounded. Phosphogypsum REE grades (~0.5% TREO) are much lower than primary deposits, requiring high throughput to reach scale. The radioactive content (NORM) creates handling and regulatory complexity. The technology is proven at pilot scale but not yet at commercial scale. And the economics are sensitive to rare earth prices — the $128/kg basket price assumption reflects elevated prices from [[China]]'s 2024-2025 export controls, which could moderate if restrictions ease. The 1-year suspension of some controls (November 2025) is a reminder that the geopolitical premium is not permanent. But even at significantly lower prices, the combination of near-zero feedstock cost, minimal mining capex, and waste-liability offset keeps the project viable.
