# Chinese dual-use commercial satellite exports

A recurring architecture by which [[China|Chinese]] commercial satellite operators — ostensibly civilian under the state's [[Military-civil fusion|military-civil fusion]] doctrine — supply high-resolution imagery and operational satellite access to US-designated adversaries. Two public cases document the pattern: [[Chang Guang Satellite Technology]] providing targeting imagery to the [[Houthis]] (FT, 2025) and [[Earth Eye Co]] transferring [[TEE-01B]] satellite control to [[Iran]]'s [[IRGC]] Aerospace Force via an "in-orbit delivery" contract (FT, April 2026 — see [[IRGC Chinese satellite targeting March 2026]]). The commercial wrapping (private companies, stated civilian use cases, payment in yuan) gives Beijing operational distance, while the underlying capability — dispersed ground stations in third countries, PLA-adjacent engineering talent, [[Belt and Road]] customer relationships — makes the exports strategically durable against Western sanctions.

## What is "in-orbit delivery"

A little-known export model under which a Chinese company launches a satellite from Chinese territory, commissions and verifies it on orbit, and only then hands operational control to the foreign customer. The customer never takes physical possession. The foreign country signs a commercial agreement (Rmb250mn in the TEE-01B case) that covers the satellite, the launcher, integration, and a ground-side services contract — typically with [[Emposat]], which supplies commanding, telemetry, and imagery downlink through its global station network.

Key commercial-strategic properties:

| Property | Implication |
|---------|-------------|
| Satellite never leaves Chinese territory physically before handover | No hardware-export licence pathway |
| Ground infrastructure is Chinese-operated abroad | Foreign ground stations in third countries not targetable by Israeli/US strikes |
| Payment in renminbi | Bypasses dollar-clearing and [[Secondary sanctions]] chokepoints |
| Civilian cover story (agriculture, ocean monitoring, disaster) | Stated export purpose distinct from end use |
| Vendor firms nominally private | Distance between Chinese government and capability |

## Who builds and operates

[[Earth Eye Co]]: Chinese commercial satellite builder/operator. Publicly claims one prior in-orbit transfer to an unnamed [[Belt and Road]] country (Iran joined BRI in 2021). Executive and engineering staff disproportionately from the [[Seven sons of national defence]] — Chinese universities with deep [[PLA]] collaboration.

[[Emposat]]: Beijing-based ground-segment operator. Founded by [[Richard Zhao]], 15 years at the state-run [[China Academy of Space Technology]] (CAST). Flagged by the US House China Select Committee as closely tied to the [[People's Liberation Army]] Aerospace Force; funded partly by "national military-civil fusion funds" ([[Aidan Powers-Riggs]], [[CSIS]]). Operates commercial ground stations across Asia, Latin America, and other regions — this dispersion is the operational value proposition.

[[Chang Guang Satellite Technology]]: Commercial arm of the Jilin-1 constellation, another dual-use case. FT reported in 2025 that it supplied imagery to the Iran-backed [[Houthis]] for Red Sea targeting.

## Strategic rationale

China's official framing is commercial exports under the civilian space sector, consistent with BRI technology transfer and [[Chinese exports]] diversification. The strategic function per former Western intelligence and [[CIA]] China analysis:

- Provide adversaries ISR capability that the US and allies cannot easily degrade (dispersion across third-country ground stations).
- Build a customer base of sanctioned or politically-aligned states who pay in non-dollar instruments and owe commercial relationships to Chinese state-adjacent firms.
- Preserve plausible deniability via commercial form — Beijing can and does officially deny state involvement (Foreign Ministry statement April 2026: "relevant reports are untrue").
- Create operational facts on the ground in the [[GCC]]/Middle East/Africa theatres that complicate US dominance over adversary ISR, without direct state-to-state military transfer.

Dennis Wilder (former head of China analysis at CIA) placed satellite exports within a longer arc of Chinese weapons transfers to Iran going back to Silkworm anti-ship missiles — "a pragmatic strategy to influence the Islamic republic on other issues." A former senior Western intelligence official: "There is no way that any Chinese company could do something like launch a satellite without somebody in the administration giving it the go-ahead."

## The dispersion problem for counter-space

[[Israel]] hit multiple satellite-related targets inside Iran during the 2026 war, including the main research centre of the [[Iranian Space Agency]] in mid-March. Those strikes degrade Iranian domestic capability but do not touch Chinese commercial ground stations operated by [[Emposat]] from third countries. The result is an ISR capability that is, in [[Jim Lamson]]'s framing, survivable against the counter-space campaign that would otherwise blind Iran: "Iran's satellite ground stations, which were hit in 2025 and 2026, can be hit very easily by missiles from a thousand miles away. You can't just hit a Chinese ground station located in another country."

## Investment and policy implications

**Chinese commercial-space sector.** The model is replicable. Other [[Belt and Road]] states (for example, members with limited indigenous space capability and strained relations with the West) are plausible next customers. [[Earth Eye Co]] confirmed one prior BRI transfer; [[Emposat]]'s ground network supports global customer on-boarding. Incremental capacity lands in a sector that is largely private but domestically subsidised through [[Made in China 2025]] and military-civil fusion funds.

**US export-control exposure.** US policy tools calibrated to hardware export lose grip when the hardware never leaves China. Primary available levers: sanctions on Chinese firms (Emposat, Earth Eye, Chang Guang), secondary sanctions on counterparties (e.g., BRI states, Iranian entities), and action against Chinese banks processing payments for the contracts (see [[Operation Economic Fury]]). [[Scott Bessent]]'s April 14 announcement reaching to Chinese banks on secondary-sanctions exposure is the natural financial chokepoint.

**Insurance and Gulf infrastructure pricing.** That [[Khor Fakkan]], [[Qidfa]], and [[Alba]] were on an IRGC tasking list raises the question of whether Gulf war-risk and property insurance models capture the shift in ISR fidelity against civilian infrastructure. See [[Iran conflict Gulf infrastructure]] and [[Iran conflict insurance impact]].

**Commercial-space equity exposure.** Western EO names ([[Planet Labs]], [[Maxar]], [[BlackSky]]) sell commercial imagery into the same market segment. The dual-use flap arguably pulls Western commercial-imagery capacity into the defence supply chain faster (re-tasking, higher-priority contracts) and forces the sector into a sharper bifurcation between Western-aligned and Chinese-aligned providers. See [[Sectors/Space]] for market structure.

## Pattern questions for the next case

- Which BRI member is [[Earth Eye Co]]'s "unnamed" prior transfer recipient? Earth Eye itself has not named it; attribution would matter for sanctions policy.
- Does the commercial channel extend to launch services, not just imagery? [[Chang Guang Satellite Technology]] controls a larger constellation; a full-stack lease to a sanctioned state would be a further step.
- What is the financing structure? The RMB250mn IRGC contract is small relative to weapons-grade transfers — this is a programme that scales by unit count, not unit size.
- Is the pattern matched by Russia? [[Russia]] has launched several [[Iran]]ian satellites in recent years; the in-orbit delivery model is a Chinese innovation, but Russian cooperation fills a parallel gap.
