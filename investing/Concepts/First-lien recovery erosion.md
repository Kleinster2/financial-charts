#concept #credit #leveraged-finance

First-lien recovery erosion — the structural decline in recovery rates for senior secured leveraged loans, driven by the disappearance of junior debt cushions and the rise of creditor-on-creditor violence.

---

## Synthesis

First-lien senior debt used to mean something — a claim secured against assets, cushioned by junior junk bonds underneath. That cushion is gone. [[Moody's]] data shows junior debt in leveraged capital structures fell from 33% two decades ago to just 9% by 2024. Loan-only "unitranche" structures have replaced the traditional senior-plus-junior LBO stack, meaning losses that junior tranches once absorbed now hit senior lenders directly.

The result: first-lien recovery rates have fallen ~20 cents from their historical average of 75 cents per dollar, with a ~30% standard deviation — meaning near-total losses in some cases. The asset class marketed as safe (high single-digit returns, floating rate protection, attractive to insurers and pensions) is structurally riskier than its reputation suggests.

Compounding this, "creditor-on-creditor violence" exploits covenant-lite loan contracts. A troubled company pits rival senior creditors against each other for rescue financing: the winner gets upgraded to "super senior," the loser gets subordinated. The seniority that investors paid for can be stripped without their consent.

*Source: FT Lex, "Senior loans, like airport lounges, are losing their elite status" (Mar 12, 2026)*

## Mechanisms of erosion

### 1. Junior debt cushion disappears

Traditional LBO: senior secured loans at top, junior junk bonds below. The junior tranche absorbed first losses, protecting first-lien. With unitranche dominance, there is no junior layer — senior IS the only layer.

### 2. Creditor-on-creditor violence

When a company hits distress, it can exploit covenant-lite contracts to offer rescue financing that creates a new "super senior" tranche above existing first-lien. The rescue lender gets better collateral and priority; existing first-lien holders are effectively subordinated without agreeing to it.

Record pace: 33 US companies with $500M+ debt restructured via LMEs in 2024, 23 in 2025. See [[Private Credit]] sector note for case studies.

### 3. Covenant erosion

Cov-lite loans lack the protective triggers that would force early restructuring. Middle-market CLO cov-lite cap rose from 16% (2021) to 25% (2025 Q1) per [[S&P Global]]. Without covenants, problems compound before creditors can act.
