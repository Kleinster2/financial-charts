# Liability management exercises

Out-of-court restructuring of corporate debt — exchange offers, drop-down financings, uptiering transactions, priming amendments — that allow stressed but not bankrupt issuers to restructure leverage without filing Chapter 11. The dominant pre-bankruptcy distressed-credit playbook of the late 2010s and 2020s, and the practice area through which firms like [[Kirkland & Ellis]] (recipient of [[Josh Feltman]] from [[Wachtell Lipton]]) generate large M&A-adjacent restructuring fees.

## Common structures

- *Drop-down financings*: existing collateral transferred to a non-guarantor subsidiary that takes on new debt secured by that collateral. Net effect: senior creditors at the holdco are subordinated to the new lenders at the subsidiary.
- *Uptiering / priming amendments*: a subset of existing creditors receive an upgrade in lien priority in exchange for new money or extended maturities. Other creditors at the same tranche are subordinated.
- *Exchange offers*: existing debt swapped for new debt with revised covenants, lower coupons, longer maturities, or different security packages.
- *Distressed exchanges*: a category of LMEs where the new package is worth materially less than face value of the original; typically counted as default-equivalent by rating agencies.

## Why they matter for the vault

LMEs are highly fee-intensive and lawyer-intensive. The [[Big Law transformation|Kirklandisation]] of the legal industry was driven in significant part by [[Kirkland & Ellis]]'s dominance in this practice area — a recurring deal type with high partner-revenue density and PE-aligned client relationships ([[Apollo]], [[Ares Management]], [[BlackRock]]). The structural creditor-vs-creditor conflicts that LMEs generate (e.g., the [[Altice]] uptiering case that forced Kirkland to withdraw in 2025) are also at the heart of why the same firms dominating LMEs face structural conflict-of-interest constraints.
