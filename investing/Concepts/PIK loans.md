# PIK loans

Payment-in-kind (PIK) loans allow borrowers to defer cash interest payments by adding the interest to the principal balance instead — effectively an IOU on interest. Useful for fast-growing companies reinvesting every dollar of cash flow, corrosive when used to paper over deteriorating credit.

Now the dominant structure in [[Private Credit]] software-sector deals, and a central feature of [[Carson Block]]'s structural critique of the asset class.

---

## How it works

| Type | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| **True PIK** | 100% of interest accrues to principal; no cash interest paid during the loan |
| **PIK toggle** | Borrower can choose cash interest or PIK at each payment date (usually with a rate premium if toggled to PIK) |
| **Partial PIK** | Part cash, part PIK — e.g., 6% cash + 4% PIK on a 10% coupon |

At maturity (or refinancing) the borrower owes principal plus all accrued PIK interest. If the business can't refinance or generate cash at that point, the PIK balance has compounded the default risk, not deferred it.

---

## Why private credit likes PIK

- **Borrower appeal** — Preserves cash for growth investment or dividend recaps
- **Headline yield** — PIK accrual counts as income for the fund's reported NAV and returns, even without cash receipt
- **Flexibility** — PIK toggle lets sponsors manage liquidity through cycles
- **Software fit** — SaaS companies with high operating leverage and recurring revenue have historically used PIK aggressively in leveraged buyouts

PIK loans are the #1 sector category for software in private credit (PitchBook LCD).

---

## The structural problem

PIK creates an agency gap between lender manager and lender investor:

1. Manager clips management fee on AUM
2. Higher NAV (from PIK accrual) = higher fees
3. Markdown of a PIK loan = both a fee hit and a reputational hit
4. Incentive: avoid marking down even when cash interest is clearly at risk

[[Carson Block]] ([[Muddy Waters]], WSJ *Take On the Week*, April 2026): *"You have a misalignment of incentives because the managers of the private credit funds, their incentive is because they're getting paid on management fees, percentage of assets under management. So if you can keep the NAVs higher, you get to clip more fees. And also you're going to scare away fewer investors. So the incentives are all screwed up. Just like we saw going into global financial crisis, when you have a misalignment of incentives you get bad outcomes."*

This is the mechanism behind the S&P Global Ratings finding that private credit default rates look very different depending on whether "selective defaults" (PIK conversions, maturity extensions without adequate compensation) are included — headline ~1% narrow vs ~4.5% with selective defaults included (S&P, Mar 2026).

---

## Where PIK is biggest

- **Software buyouts** — PIK dominates the middle-market and upper-middle-market LBO pipeline
- **Healthcare services** — Second-largest PIK user
- **Consumer discretionary** — Common in PE-backed brands
- **Post-2022 BDC income** — PIK income has been rising as a share of total interest income across the public BDC universe

---

## Why this could get worse

- **Refinancing wall 2027-2028** — Many 2021-2022 vintage software deals have PIK-heavy structures maturing into a market that no longer supports the same leverage levels
- **AI revenue compression** — See [[Private Credit Software Concentration]]. If SaaS revenue compresses faster than anticipated, PIK-deferred interest can never be repaid
- **Fund-level transparency** — PIK accrual in semi-liquid retail BDCs means NAV marks can look stable right up until a forced restructuring
