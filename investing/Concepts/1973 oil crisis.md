The 1973 oil crisis was the first major weaponization of oil supply, when [[OAPEC]] members led by [[Saudi Arabia]] embargoed nations supporting [[Israel]] during the Yom Kippur War. Crude prices quadrupled from ~$2.90/bbl to ~$11.65/bbl between October 1973 and January 1974. The crisis reshaped Western energy policy, created the [[IEA]] and the [[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]], and established the template for oil-as-geopolitical-weapon that persists through the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]].

---

## Timeline

- Oct 6, 1973: [[Egypt]] and [[Syria]] launch surprise attack on [[Israel]] (Yom Kippur War / Ramadan War).
- Oct 16: [[OAPEC]] raises posted oil price 70%. Oil ministers agree to production cuts.
- Oct 19: [[Nixon]] requests $2.2B in emergency aid for Israel. OAPEC institutes embargo against the [[United States]].
- Oct 20: [[Saudi Arabia]] declares total embargo on US oil shipments.
- Late Oct: Embargo extended to [[Canada]], [[Japan]], [[Netherlands]], [[United Kingdom]].
- Nov 23: Embargo extended to [[Portugal]], Rhodesia, [[South Africa]].
- Nov 27: Nixon signs Emergency Petroleum Allocation Act authorizing price, production, allocation, and marketing controls.
- Dec 1973: [[OPEC]] raises posted price to $11.65/bbl (~300% increase from pre-crisis).
- Jan 18, 1974: [[Kissinger]] negotiates First Egyptian-Israeli Disengagement Agreement (Sinai I). Saudi King [[Faisal]] persuaded that conditions for embargo have ended.
- Mar 18, 1974: OAPEC lifts embargo.

## Consequences

The embargo triggered the first major global recession tied to energy prices. The [[United States]] experienced simultaneous inflation and stagnation ("stagflation"), which persisted through the decade and shaped [[Federal Reserve]] policy under [[Arthur Burns]] and later [[Paul Volcker]].

Institutional responses created during or because of the crisis:

- [[IEA]] (1974): proposed by [[Kissinger]] to coordinate Western energy policy and maintain emergency oil reserves. Irony: [[Paul Sankey]] began his career there in 1990.
- [[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]] (1975): authorized by the Energy Policy and Conservation Act, the US government's emergency crude stockpile.
- National 55 mph speed limit (1974-1995).
- Accelerated nuclear power programs in [[France]], [[Japan]], and others.
- Dawn of energy conservation as policy priority.

The crisis permanently shifted the global financial balance of power toward oil-producing states, triggering the recycling of petrodollars through Western banks (particularly [[City of London|London]]) and laying the groundwork for the [[Petrodollar]] system -- [[Saudi Arabia]] pricing oil in USD, purchasing US weapons and Treasuries.

## The missed warning

[[Kissinger]] declined to meet with four oil company CEOs who warned of King [[Faisal]]'s serious threats about an embargo in summer 1973. The warnings were assessed as inconsequential. The intelligence failure echoes in subsequent crises: pre-2026, the consensus was that Iran would never actually close the [[Strait of Hormuz]] because it would be "suicidal for the regime."

## As benchmark

The 1973 crisis is the standard reference point for severe oil supply shocks. Comparisons:

- [[Paul Sankey]] (March 2026): the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] is "possibly" the worst since 1973.
- [[Ed Morse]] (CFR panel, March 2026): the structural change won't be 1973-level (which enabled OPEC empowerment and nationalization of oil majors), but the demonstrated Hormuz capability is permanent.
- Scale comparison: 1973 removed ~4.4 mb/d from the market (OAPEC cuts); 2026 removed ~20 mb/d (strait closure). The 2026 disruption is ~4.5x larger in volume terms but affects a much larger global economy.
