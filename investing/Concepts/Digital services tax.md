# Digital services tax

A digital services tax (DST) is a levy on the gross local revenue large digital firms earn from users in a jurisdiction — search ads, online marketplaces, social platforms, data — rather than on booked profit. Because the largest in-scope firms are American ([[Alphabet]], [[Meta]], [[Amazon]], [[Apple]]), DSTs function in practice as targeted taxes on US tech, which is why Washington treats them as a trade matter, not just a tax one. Rates are small but apply to revenue: France 3%, the UK 2%, with versions in Italy, Spain, Austria, Turkey, India (equalisation levy), and Canada.

DSTs are the unilateral fallback to a stalled multilateral fix. The [[OECD]]/G20 two-pillar deal was meant to reallocate taxing rights ([[Pillar One]]) so market countries could tax big tech without unilateral DSTs, in exchange for countries repealing them. Pillar One never fully ratified — the US Senate never moved it — so the standstill that froze DSTs has frayed and countries are reactivating or threatening them.

## The US retaliation pattern

The US response has been consistent across administrations: treat a DST as a discriminatory trade barrier and answer with tariffs. USTR ran [[Section 301]] investigations into the French, UK, Italian, Spanish, Austrian, Indian, and Turkish DSTs (2019-2021), finding them discriminatory and authorizing retaliatory tariffs that were repeatedly suspended pending the OECD deal. With Pillar One stalled, the suspension logic is gone.

## June 2026 — the 100% threat

On June 26 2026, [[Donald Trump]] posted on Truth Social that any country imposing a digital services tax on US companies "will immediately be met with a 100% TARIFF on any and all Goods sent to the United States," adding that the measure would supersede any previously negotiated trade deal. The threat landed days before the July 4 deadline for implementing the US-[[EU]] framework that caps tariffs on most EU exports at 15% — digital taxes were never part of that deal and have remained the open sticking point. It extends a pattern: Trump issued a similar blanket DST-tariff threat in 2025. (Source: Truth Social via PBS, CBS, Euronews, Jun 26 2026; verify-grade — a threat, not an enacted measure.)

For markets the read-through is less the tariff arithmetic than the signal: the digital-tax fight is the cleanest live example of the [[Geoeconomic fragmentation|fragmentation]] dynamic where the failure of a multilateral rule (Pillar One) converts directly into bilateral coercion, and where the [[EU]] retaliation calculus (the Anti-Coercion Instrument, see [[US-EU trade]]) is most likely to be tested.
