#concept #accounting

**ASC 360** — FASB standard governing long-lived assets (PP&E, finite-lived intangibles, ROU lease assets). Covers impairment testing and asset disposal. The standard that forces companies to write down factories, equipment, and real estate when they're worth less than book value.

---

## Impairment testing

Unlike goodwill ([[ASC 350]]), long-lived asset impairment is not tested annually — only when a triggering event suggests the carrying amount may not be recoverable.

### Two-step test

| Step | Test | Method |
|------|------|--------|
| Recoverability | Are undiscounted future cash flows < carrying value? | If yes → impaired |
| Measurement | Fair value vs carrying value | Impairment = carrying value − fair value |

The undiscounted cash flow test in Step 1 is a low bar — it's easier to pass than a discounted test. This means US GAAP is slower to recognize long-lived asset impairments than IFRS (which uses discounted cash flows from the start under IAS 36).

### Asset grouping and the [[CODM]] link

Assets are grouped at the lowest level for which identifiable cash flows are largely independent. This grouping can follow the [[CODM]]'s review structure — how management monitors performance determines how assets cluster for impairment testing.

A broad grouping (entire division) can mask impaired assets behind healthy ones. A narrow grouping (individual plant) triggers impairment sooner. Companies have discretion here, and auditors scrutinize the grouping rationale.

## Triggering events

| Event | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Significant decrease in market price | Stock drops 50%, implying assets overvalued |
| Adverse change in business climate | New regulation, loss of key contract |
| Costs significantly exceed plan | Factory overruns, project delays |
| Operating losses or negative cash flow | Sustained unprofitability of asset group |
| Expectation of disposal | Plan to sell or abandon asset |

## IFRS vs US GAAP

| Feature | ASC 360 (US GAAP) | IAS 36 (IFRS) |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Recoverability test | Undiscounted cash flows | Discounted cash flows |
| Impairment reversal | Never allowed | Allowed (except goodwill) |
| Held-for-sale | Fair value less costs to sell | Same |
| Result | GAAP recognizes impairments later; IFRS catches them earlier but can reverse |

The impairment reversal difference is significant: an IFRS company that wrote down a factory in a downturn can reverse the charge when conditions improve, boosting earnings. A US GAAP company cannot — the impairment is permanent.

## Why it matters for investors

- Asset-heavy sectors (industrials, energy, real estate, airlines) carry the most ASC 360 risk
- Impairment charges are non-cash but reduce book value and can breach covenants
- Watch for companies avoiding impairment by using aggressive cash flow assumptions or broad asset groupings
- Restructuring waves often trigger cascading impairments (e.g., retail store closures, oil field write-downs)
