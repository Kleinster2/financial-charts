#concept #regulation #macro #banking

**Competitive deregulation** — The dynamic where one jurisdiction loosening rules forces others to follow or risk losing capital, institutions, and market share. Distinct from [[Financial Center Competition]] (which covers talent/capital migration broadly) — this is specifically about the ratchet mechanism in prudential and financial regulation.

---

## Synthesis

The Basel III Endgame (Mar 2026) is the clearest live example: the US loosened bank capital requirements, the [[Bank of England]] and [[EU]] had deliberately delayed their own Basel reforms to see what Washington did, and the lenient US outcome now creates irresistible pressure for European banks to demand matching relief. The mechanism is self-reinforcing — each concession becomes the new baseline that the next jurisdiction must match or beat. The end state is a collective erosion of safety margins that no single regulator chose but all enabled.

The pattern is not new. Pre-2008, London and New York competed for financial services business through progressively lighter regulation — London's "light touch" under the FSA attracted derivatives activity away from the US, which responded with the Commodity Futures Modernization Act (2000) exempting OTC derivatives from regulation. Both jurisdictions got what they competed for: market share and a financial crisis.

The critical insight from the [[Financial Center Competition]] data is that this framing may be partly wrong — the GFCI 38 survey found regulatory *predictability* rated more important than regulatory *lightness*. Jurisdictions winning long-term (Singapore, Abu Dhabi) prioritize clarity over laxity. The race-to-the-bottom frame captures the short-term political dynamic accurately but may overstate the equilibrium outcome.

---

## The mechanism

1. Jurisdiction A loosens rules (responding to domestic lobbying, competitiveness fears, or ideological preference)
2. Capital and activity shift toward A (or threaten to)
3. Jurisdiction B's domestic industry demands matching relief ("level playing field")
4. B loosens rules — the new baseline resets downward
5. Repeat until crisis forces re-regulation

The ratchet works faster downward than upward: loosening is popular (industry applauds, consumers see cheaper credit), tightening is painful (lobbying, adjustment costs, political resistance). This asymmetry means the system drifts toward fragility between crises. See [[Minsky cycle]].

## Case studies

### Basel III Endgame (2023-2026)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2023 | Fed proposes 19% capital increase for big US banks |
| 2023-2024 | Bank lobbying blitz (Super Bowl ads, Congressional pressure) |
| 2024 | Fed agrees to dilute proposals |
| Mar 12, 2026 | [[Michelle Bowman]] announces final framework — net *decrease* in capital requirements |
| Pending | [[Bank of England]], [[EU]] face pressure to match US leniency |

Bowman explicitly framed deregulation as correcting rules that "push activity into the less-regulated nonbank sector" — acknowledging that [[Basel III]] constraints drove banks out of lending markets that [[Private credit]] captured. The policy goal is to re-arm banks to compete. Source: FT (Mar 12, 2026)

### Pre-2008 London vs New York

| Period | Dynamic |
|--------|---------|
| 1986 | UK "Big Bang" deregulates London markets |
| 1990s | London's "light touch" FSA attracts derivatives activity |
| 1999 | US responds: Glass-Steagall repealed (Gramm-Leach-Bliley) |
| 2000 | Commodity Futures Modernization Act exempts OTC derivatives |
| 2008 | Both jurisdictions' banking systems collapse |
| 2010-2012 | Dodd-Frank, Basel III — re-regulation |

The 22-year deregulation arc (1986-2008) ended with the largest financial crisis since the Depression. Both jurisdictions "won" market share in the interim.

### Digital assets / crypto (2020s)

Jurisdictions competing for crypto firms via regulatory clarity or laxity:
- Abu Dhabi ([[ADGM]]): strict but clear frameworks
- Dubai (VARA/[[DIFC]]): permissive licensing
- Singapore: balanced regime
- US: regulatory uncertainty under Gensler → permissive shift under Trump II
- [[EU]]: MiCA framework (comprehensive but compliance-heavy)

Per GFCI 38 data, predictability matters more than permissiveness — Abu Dhabi and Singapore gaining despite stricter rules than Dubai.

## Why it matters for positioning

The competitive deregulation dynamic is structurally bullish for bank equities in the short/medium term (more lending capacity, more capital return) but bearish for systemic stability on a longer horizon. For the current cycle:

- US [[G-SIB]]s ([[JPMorgan]], [[Goldman Sachs]], [[Morgan Stanley]]) benefit most directly from Basel III Endgame
- European banks will lobby for matching relief — watch [[Bank of England]] and ECB responses
- [[Private credit]] faces headwinds as banks re-enter lending markets with lower capital constraints
- The cycle resets when the next crisis reveals the buffers were too thin — timing unknowable, but the direction of travel is clear
