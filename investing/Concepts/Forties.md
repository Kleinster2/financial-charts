# Forties

Forties is a North Sea crude oil blend and the most-supplied grade in the BFOET basket ([[Brent crude|Brent]]-Forties-Oseberg-Ekofisk-Troll + [[WTI]] Midland) that underpins the [[Brent crude|Dated Brent]] benchmark. Because it is the most abundantly available grade in the basket, Forties typically sets the Dated Brent price as the marginal barrel — making it one of the most important physical crude streams in global oil pricing.

---

## Synthesis

Forties plays a role in oil markets that is both modest and foundational. It is not a headline commodity — production runs ~216,000 bbl/day, a fraction of the 100M bbl/day global market. But as the cheapest deliverable grade into the Brent benchmark that prices two-thirds of the world's internationally traded crude, Forties is the physical bottom of the global pricing stack. When [[S&P Global|Platts]] publishes Dated Brent at 16:30 London time, it is usually assessing a Forties cargo.

The blend changed character when the [[Buzzard]] field came online in 2007. Buzzard is the single largest contributor (~28% of throughput) and is heavier (32.6 API) and sourer (1.44% sulfur) than the legacy Forties blend, dragging the composite quality from ~40 API / 0.2% S toward ~37-40 API / 0.35% S depending on Buzzard's share. Platts applies a sulfur de-escalator to discount Forties cargoes when sulfur rises above threshold, which is why Forties — originally lighter and sweeter than Brent Blend itself — now tends to trade at a discount within the BFOET basket.

During the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis|2026 Hormuz crisis]], Forties physical cargoes traded $34-35 above Brent futures on April 7 — a spread [[Rystad Energy]] called "fairly unnatural" for the time of year. The premium reflected [[Atlantic basin]] barrels being bid up as they rerouted to [[Asia]] to replace lost Persian Gulf crude.

## History

The Forties field was discovered by [[BP]] on October 7, 1970, 110 miles east-northeast of Aberdeen in 350 feet of water. First oil flowed in September 1975 — inaugurated by Queen Elizabeth II on November 3, 1975. Production peaked at ~500,000 bbl/day in 1978-79, held through 1981, then declined to ~77,000 bbl/day by 1999. The decline was offset by connecting dozens of satellite fields to the Forties Pipeline System, which now aggregates production from ~85 fields across the UK and Norwegian continental shelves.

---

## Forties Pipeline System

The FPS is a 235-mile system: a 105-mile offshore pipeline from the Forties Unity platform to Cruden Bay on the Aberdeenshire coast, then 130 miles onshore to the Kinneil processing plant near Grangemouth. From Kinneil, stabilized crude goes to the Dalmeny storage terminal and the Hound Point loading jetty for export, or directly to the Grangemouth refinery.

[[INEOS]] acquired the FPS from [[BP]] in October 2017 for up to $250M: $125M cash at completion plus an earn-out of up to $125M over seven years. The deal included Kinneil, Dalmeny, the Forties Unity Platform, and Aberdeen facilities. INEOS publishes the Forties blend quality monthly to inform traders of grade characteristics.

## Role in Brent pricing

The BFOET basket determines Dated Brent, the world's most important physical oil benchmark. Platts assesses Dated Brent as the price of the most competitive (cheapest) grade in the basket for cargoes loading 10-25 days forward, assessed daily at 16:30 London in the Market on Close (MOC) window. See [[Physical Oil Trading]] for the full pricing stack.

Forties sets Dated Brent more often than any other BFOET grade because:

1. It is the most abundantly produced grade in the basket (~216K bbl/day vs declining Oseberg/Ekofisk/Brent Blend)
2. Post-Buzzard, it is typically the lowest quality — heavier, sourer — trading at a discount
3. Its large cargo availability means sellers are more likely to offer Forties in the MOC window

When Buzzard throughput drops (maintenance, weather), the blend lightens and sweetens, narrowing the Forties discount and potentially shifting the cheapest-to-deliver grade to another BFOET component.
