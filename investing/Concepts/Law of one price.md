# Law of one price

The proposition that, after adjusting for transport costs, taxes, and tariffs, identical tradeable goods should sell for the same price across markets denominated in a common currency. The intuition: if not, traders buy in the cheap market and sell in the expensive one until prices converge. The law underpins purchasing power parity ([[PPP]]), most general-equilibrium trade models, and the cross-asset arbitrage logic used by commodity desks, hedge funds, and equity-pair traders.

The law is empirically a *limit case*, not a daily reality. The academic literature has documented persistent violations for over a century. The 2024-2026 period has produced an unusually broad set of violations across commodities, technology, and capital-flow chokepoints — the central observation in [[Patrick Foulis]]'s April 24 2026 [[Financial Times|FT]] essay framing the era as a "golden age of arbitrage."

---

## Synthesis

Three intellectual lineages frame the concept and its limits.

The classical international-trade lineage runs Gustav Cassel (1916, PPP) → Balassa-Samuelson (1964, non-tradeables drift) → Engel-Rogers (1996, "How wide is the border?", which quantified the surprising distance-equivalent of the US-Canada border for retail prices). The empirical answer this lineage gives is that the law holds *less well than theory suggests*: distance, currency boundaries, and product differentiation create persistent wedges even in friction-light goods.

The financial-markets-anomaly lineage runs Lamont-Thaler (2003, *Anomalies: The Law of One Price in Financial Markets*, AEJ Perspectives) and adjacent work by De Long, Shleifer, Summers, Waldmann. It documents violations in closed-end country funds, twin shares (Royal Dutch / Shell, Unilever NV / PLC), dual-class shares, and corporate spinoffs (3Com / Palm). The takeaway: even in highly liquid securities markets where the "same cash flow, two prices" arbitrage is mechanical, limits-to-arbitrage (capital constraints, short-sale frictions, noise-trader risk, holding-period uncertainty) hold the wedges open for years.

The institutional-trade lineage (PIIE's *The Law of One Price and Its Limits* chapter, more recent IMF and BIS working papers) treats the law as a continuum: how close any given product gets to one price depends on transport elasticity, exchange-rate pass-through, vertical integration, retail markup heterogeneity, and — newly load-bearing in the 2020s — sanctions architecture and export-control regimes. This lineage is the one Foulis activates for the 2026 fragmentation regime.

The 2026 question is not whether the law fails — it has always failed at the margins — but whether the *magnitude and breadth* of failures has shifted regime. The evidence below suggests yes, in three areas: physical commodities, China-US tech products, and capital flows through chokepoint geographies.

## Classical violations (financial markets)

| Anomaly | Mechanism | Limit-to-arbitrage |
|---|---|---|
| Closed-end country funds | Fund NAV ≠ market price for years | Holding-period risk; redemption gates |
| Twin shares ([[Shell|Royal Dutch / Shell]], [[Unilever]] NV/PLC) | Same cash-flow claim, persistent percent gap | Short-rebate cost; tax/jurisdictional friction; index inclusion |
| [[Dual-class shares]] | Same economic interest, different vote, persistent discount | Index-rebalance dynamics; lockups |
| Corporate spinoffs (3Com / Palm, 2000) | Stub trades at negative implied value | Short squeeze; locate scarcity |
| ADR / underlying mispricings | Same security, two listings | Settlement, FX, and cross-listing flows |

The unifying point is that even when the arbitrage is *mechanical* (same cash flow), capital constraints and idiosyncratic frictions hold the wedges open. The 2026 fragmentation argument extends this from financial markets to physical and technology markets, where the frictions are sanctions, export controls, and geopolitical chokepoints.

## 2026 dispersion regime — Foulis evidence (April 24 2026)

Foulis's central empirical claim is that the law of one price has retreated across three product classes in 2024-2026:

### Physical commodities

| Asset | Dispersion observation | Cross-reference |
|---|---|---|
| Crude oil grades | Recent prices for similar barrels in [[WTI\|Texas]], [[Guyana]], [[Brent crude\|North Sea]], and [[Russia]] ranged $97-$147 — among the biggest gaps ever recorded | [[Brent-WTI spread]] has its own three-tier read on this |
| [[Gold]] | EU vs New York price divorced in 2025 | "ultimate fungible asset" framing |
| [[Copper]], silver, nickel | "Similar dislocations" | [[Commodity trading houses]] earnings beneficiary |

### China-US tech products

| Asset | Foulis-cited gap | Independent verification |
|---|---|---|
| AI inference tokens | ~80% cheaper in China than California | [[DeepSeek]] V4 Flash $0.14/$0.28 per M tokens vs [[Claude Sonnet]] $3/$15 — actual gap is closer to 10-100× depending on model tier; Foulis's 80% is conservative |
| [[NVIDIA]] [[B200]] chips | ~50% more expensive in China | Anhui-based "Gate of the Era" grey-market operator confirmed at this premium per Tom's Hardware / Digitimes; over $1B of B200 reached China in three months post-controls |
| [[EU-China EV tariffs\|Chinese EVs]] (Dalian vs Detroit) | ≥30% cheaper in Dalian | Trade in cars between US and China is "minuscule" (de facto autarky) |
| China biotech drugs | "Cheap breakthrough drugs" emerging | Pre-IPO and licensing-stage pipeline visible in deal flow |

### Capital-flow chokepoint exposure

Foulis cites that *~50% of global trade and capital flows pass through places or sea lanes with high military tensions* (Baltic Sea to [[Taiwan]]). This is an estimate, not an officially measured statistic, but it is consistent with [[Strait of Hormuz]] (~20% of seaborne crude), [[Strait of Malacca|Malacca]] (East Asia trade), [[Suez Canal]] / [[Bab el-Mandeb|Red Sea]] (~12% world trade), Taiwan Strait / South China Sea, and the Baltic. The frame links directly to [[Weaponized interdependence]].

## Why the law is in retreat (Foulis's three drivers)

| Driver | Mechanism | Vault link |
|---|---|---|
| Less reliable America | More wars and crises that disrupt trade routes | [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] |
| Sanctions and economic nationalism | Markets segment along policy lines | [[US Sanctions]], [[Secondary sanctions]] |
| Industrial policy expansion | Subsidised firms protected from foreign low-cost competition | [[Inflation Reduction Act]], [[CHIPS Act]], EU subsidies |

The structural read: ~50% of trade/capital flow exposure to chokepoint geographies is the hard ceiling. Even if any single crisis is "transitory," the *probability distribution* of severe events is no longer cold-war-low.

## Consequences if dispersion persists

### 1. Arbitrage as an extracted tax

Foulis's framing: arbitrageur profits are the tax the world pays to incentivise traders to bridge fractured markets. Recipients ranked by 2024-2026 returns:

- [[Commodity trading houses]] — combined with Wall Street trading desks earning ~$140bn/yr per Foulis (2× 2019 level). The commodity-only [[Oliver Wyman]] 2024 figure was ~$95bn baseline; the rest is the bank-trading-desk share.
- [[JPMorgan Chase]] markets arm — record Q1 2026 markets revenue of $11.6B, +20% YoY, "best result in history" for the bank's markets franchise (verified).
- [[Buffett Japan sogo shosha trade|Japanese trading houses]] — 19th-century business model "on a tear." [[Mitsubishi]], [[Mitsui]], [[Itochu]], [[Marubeni]], [[Sumitomo Corp]] — Buffett's $24B paper gain through Feb 2026 is one signal.
- Macro hedge funds — rush of new fund openings, 14 years after [[Soros Fund Management|Soros]] returned outside capital and the strategy was widely written off as obsolete.
- [[Neocloud financing|Neoclouds]] — digital arbitrage venue, renting AI chip access cross-border "without too many questions asked."

### 2. Inflation and rate divergence

Cumulative five-year inflation has split sharply:

| Country | Foulis 5yr cumulative | Cross-check |
|---|---|---|
| [[United States]] | +25% | [[BLS]] CPI shows ~22% (Mar 2021 → Mar 2026); Foulis figure is at the high end but directionally correct |
| [[Russia]] | +51% | Rosstat consistent with high-single-digit annual rates compounding from 2022 onward |
| [[China]] | +4% | [[NBS]] shows near-zero average annual inflation 2020-2025; Mar 2026 annual 1.0% |

The downstream chain Foulis flags: bigger inflation gaps → eventually bigger interest-rate gaps → bigger exchange-rate gaps → bigger asset-valuation gaps. Higher-price geographies could see weaker currencies and lower asset valuations over the medium term — the opposite of the post-1990 convergence trade.

### 3. Innovation catalyst

The 1970s analog: [[1973 oil crisis|Middle East oil crises]] → North Sea deepwater drilling, Trans-Alaska Pipeline (1977), VLCC/ULCC tanker generation, US strategic petroleum reserve. The barriers created the engineering response.

The 2020s candidate response set:
- Energy and materials science — alternatives to chokepoint-dependent inputs
- Manufacturing automation — lowering the offshoring incentive that made [[China]] industrial capacity load-bearing
- AI agents masking user nationality — "running rings around the digital barriers governments erect"
- Synthetic biology and domestic-pathway pharmaceuticals — bypassing China API dependence

The vault treatment: each candidate response has its own concept note where the technology and capital-flow dynamics are tracked. The law-of-one-price retreat is the *demand-side* signal that makes those candidate responses commercially viable.

## Limits of the framing

The Foulis frame is one of three live readings of fragmentation:

| Frame | Lens | Vault link |
|---|---|---|
| Foulis (this note) | Price dispersion / arbitrage opportunity / market structure | [[Patrick Foulis]] |
| [[Weaponized interdependence]] (Farrell-Newman) | Network topology / chokepoint coercion | [[Henry Farrell]], [[Abraham Newman]] |
| [[Second China shock]] (Autor-Hanson school) | Aggregate flows / labour markets / industrial policy | [[Michael Pettis]] adjacent |

All three can be true simultaneously. Foulis's frame is the most market-facing and the most quickly tradable; weaponized interdependence is the institutional-policy frame; second China shock is the political-economy frame. For a portfolio decision, Foulis's frame yields the most direct positioning suggestions.
