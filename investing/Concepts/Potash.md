# Potash

Potassium fertilizer — the "K" in NPK. The product sold as potash is almost entirely potassium chloride (KCl, muriate of potash or MOP), mined from deep underground deposits or extracted from salt lakes. About 80% of global supply comes from conventional underground mines. Potash strengthens roots, helps plants retain water, and builds resistance to heat and disease. It is the smallest of the three primary nutrient markets at ~35M tonnes annual consumption (~18% of global fertilizer nutrient demand), the most geographically concentrated on the supply side, and the least directly exposed to the 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] disruption — but structurally tight on a multi-year horizon.

---

## Synopsis

The 2022 price spike is over, but potash has settled into a higher range. After a decade of trading between $200 and $400/t, prices surged above $1,100/t in 2022 when sanctions on [[Russia]] and [[Belarus]] removed ~40% of global export capacity. That shock has passed and prices are back in the $350-400/t range, but they have not returned to the $200 floor seen in 2019-2020 and are unlikely to. Industry-wide production costs have moved up and the cheapest mines are running at full speed. Delivered cash cost to [[Brazil]] (the best global price proxy) rose from $215/t in 2020 to $270/t in 2025 and is projected at $315/t by 2030 on a short-run basis; long-run marginal cost (the price needed to justify a new mine) has climbed from $395/t to $480/t over the same period and is heading toward $545/t by 2030. Current spot is above short-run cost — incumbent producers are earning healthy margins — but below long-run, so nobody has a financial reason to build new capacity. That gap can persist; eventually demand forces the issue.

Capacity looks comfortable on paper and tighter in practice. Global capacity has grown from ~80M tonnes in 2017 to an estimated 100M tonnes by 2028. Utilization peaked near 88% in 2018, fell to 75% in 2022 as sanctions and buyer pushback collided, and has recovered into the low 80s where forecasts keep it through 2028. In a commodity with concentrated supply and long lead times for new projects, an 80% utilization rate leaves less cushion than it suggests — a single major producer pulling back can move the market. Production is dominated by [[Canada]] (~25M mt, 33% of global), [[Russia]] (~9M mt), and [[Belarus]] (7M mt in 2024, +56% YoY as sanctions leaked), with [[China]] (~6M mt) and Germany (~5M mt) rounding out the top five.

Demand is structurally growing, doubling since 2000 and projected to reach 80-85M tonnes by 2030. [[China]] and [[Brazil]] each consume close to 20M tonnes annually and together drive the bulk of global trade. North America and Other Asia are flat. [[India]] is the emerging demand story — imports have nearly doubled since 2021 on agricultural intensification, potassium-depleted soils, and government policy pushing higher crop yields. The NPK blend market — which carries ~36% of global potash consumption, 17% of nitrogen, and 26% of phosphate — means no nutrient segment is truly insulated: disruptions in nitrogen or phosphate supply ripple into potash-containing blends.

The Iran conflict exposure is indirect. Potash production is concentrated in [[Canada]], [[Russia]], [[Belarus]], and [[China]] — none routing through [[Strait of Hormuz]]. But the 2026 crisis puts NPK blenders in a bind: nitrogen and phosphate inputs are disrupted, so blend economics deteriorate even though the potash leg is physically available. The larger multi-year risk remains [[Russia]]/[[Belarus]] supply. Those two countries caused the 2022 shock, their continued access to global markets is uncertain, and any renewed disruption would tighten the market fast — with less backup capacity this time because global utilization has already crept back into the low 80s.

## India — the emerging buyer

![[india-potash-imports-aurelion.png]]
*[[India]] potash imports have nearly doubled since 2021. Drivers: agricultural intensification, potassium-depleted soils, government crop-yield policy. Source: Aurelion Research.*

[[India]] is the structural growth story. Soils across much of the Indian subcontinent are potassium-depleted after decades of imbalanced fertilizer use (nitrogen-heavy, underapplied P and K). Government subsidy reforms and crop-yield targets have pushed potash uptake higher. India has nearly doubled its imports since 2021 and is now a major marginal buyer — influential enough to move prices at tender time. India imports virtually all its potash, with no domestic production.

## Brazil cost curve — the pricing anchor

![[brazil-potash-cost-curve-aurelion.png]]
*Delivered cash cost of potash to [[Brazil]], short-run (incumbent producers) and long-run (price needed to justify new mine). Short-run cost rose from $215/t (2020) to $270/t (2025) to a projected $315/t (2030F). Long-run cost rose from $395 to $480 to $545 over the same period. Source: CRU, Nutrien via Aurelion Research.*

[[Brazil]] imports nearly all its potash and buys more than any other single country, which makes cfr Brazil the best global price indicator. Two cost curves matter:

| Cost type | 2020 | 2025 | 2030F | What it represents |
|-----------|------|------|-------|--------------------|
| Short-run (marginal cash) | $215/t | $270/t | $315/t | Ongoing operating cost of existing mines |
| Long-run (marginal greenfield) | $395/t | $480/t | $545/t | Price needed to justify a new mine |

Spot has traded above the short-run line since 2021 — incumbent producers are profitable. But spot has not sustained above the long-run line since 2022, so no one is sanctioning greenfield capacity. Unless prices rise to meet long-run cost or demand creeps above the existing supply ceiling, the market keeps tightening until something breaks.

## Supply concentration

Production is more concentrated than nitrogen or phosphate. Five countries account for ~70% of global output:

| Country | 2024 production (M mt) | Share | Main operator |
|---------|------------------------|-------|---------------|
| [[Canada]] | ~25 | 33% | [[Nutrien]] (dominant), Mosaic (Esterhazy, Colonsay) |
| [[Russia]] | ~9 | 12% | Uralkali, EuroChem |
| [[Belarus]] | 7 | 9% | Belaruskali |
| [[China]] | ~6 | 8% | Qinghai Salt Lake, SDIC Mining |
| Germany | ~5 | 7% | K+S |
| [[Israel]] / [[Jordan]] | ~5 combined | 7% | [[ICL Group]] (Dead Sea) |

Because production is so concentrated, the potash market is more like copper or iron ore than nitrogen — moves by any of the top three producers are felt globally within weeks.

## Russia / Belarus risk

Sanctions on [[Russia]] and [[Belarus]] removed ~40% of global export capacity in 2022, driving the price spike above $1,100/t. Both have since recovered significant volume — Belarusian potash output rose to 7M mt in 2024, up 56% from 4.5M mt in 2023, as sanctions leaked and volumes rerouted through non-sanctioned channels. Uralkali's sales rose 28% in 2024. This recovery is what pulled prices back from the 2022 highs, but it also means the scarcity premium has mostly unwound — the market is now fully exposed to any renewed sanctions tightening or voluntary supply discipline from these producers.

## Potash and the 2026 Hormuz crisis

Unlike [[urea]] and [[Phosphate|DAP]], potash does not ship through the [[Strait of Hormuz]]. Production is in [[Canada]], [[Russia]], [[Belarus]], and [[China]] — all routing via Vancouver, Baltic, Pacific, and inland rail. Potash producers have been called relative beneficiaries of the [[Nitrogen Trap]] crisis because their physical supply is intact while competitor nutrients are constrained.

The indirect pressures matter more than the direct ones:

- NPK blends: 36% of potash consumption goes into NPK products, which require functioning nitrogen and phosphate inputs. Constrained N/P supply compresses blend volumes and forces straight-MOP substitution for farmers who can't access NPK.
- Demand destruction risk: severely elevated nitrogen/phosphate prices + low crop prices compress farmer economics, which can reduce total fertilizer application and pull potash demand down with the complex.
- [[Brazil]] dependency: [[Brazil]] covers a large share of its potash needs from [[Russia]] and [[Canada]]. Any sanctions escalation would remove a meaningful share of its supply overnight.
