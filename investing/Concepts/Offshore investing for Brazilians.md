# Offshore investing for Brazilians

Offshore investing is the route by which a Brazil resident holds foreign assets directly — in dollars, in an account outside Brazil — as opposed to the onshore [[Brazilian Depositary Receipts|BDR]] route, which keeps everything in reais on [[B3]]. A wave of digital platforms turned what was once a private-banking service into a retail product: a Brazilian can now open a US brokerage or bank account from an app, convert reais to dollars, and buy US stocks, ETFs, Treasuries, and the shares behind any US-listed name. The same total-return position can be expressed either way; the choice between offshore and BDR turns on currency, liquidity, convenience, and — most of all — [[#Taxation|tax]], which the 2023 "Lei das Offshores" rewrote.

## The two routes

| Dimension | Offshore account | Onshore BDR (B3) |
|---|---|---|
| Where | US broker/bank, abroad | [[B3]], in Brazil |
| Currency | USD | BRL (embeds USD/BRL) |
| Ownership | The actual foreign share | A depositary receipt |
| FX | You convert (câmbio + IOF) | Inside the BDR price |
| Reporting | CBE filing if assets ≥ $1M; offshore income on the annual return | The Brazilian broker handles it |
| Tax | 15% on offshore financial investments (Lei 14.754) | 15% on gains, no R$20k exemption |

## Mechanics

Moving money offshore runs through three steps the BDR route avoids:

- Câmbio — the BRL→USD conversion, where the platform earns a spread and the IOF on FX applies (a rate the government has changed repeatedly, including increases in 2025).
- Custody abroad — the assets sit in a US broker (FINRA/SIPC) or bank (FDIC), outside the Brazilian system.
- Declaration — a resident with offshore assets of $1M or more must file the annual Declaração de Capitais Brasileiros no Exterior (CBE) with the [[Banco Central do Brasil]], and all offshore income is reported on the annual income-tax return.

## Taxation

The 2023 "Lei das Offshores" (Lei 14.754/2023, effective Jan 1 2024; regulated by IN RFB 2.180/2024) reshaped the economics of the offshore route:

- A single 15% rate on offshore financial investments held by individuals, settled annually in the Declaração de Ajuste Anual.
- Controlled offshore entities (offshores) — especially in tax havens or earning under 60% active income — are taxed at 15% annually on profits regardless of distribution, ending the deferral that made offshore holding companies attractive.
- FX variation on offshore-acquired assets is no longer exempt; it now enters the capital-gains base.

The net effect narrows the old tax advantage of going offshore through a holding structure, so the offshore-versus-BDR decision turns more on currency exposure, liquidity, and convenience than on a tax-deferral edge.

## Why it grew

- Rate and currency. A persistent interest-rate differential and recurring BRL depreciation made dollar assets both a return and a hedge.
- Diversification. Concentrated [[B3]] exposure pushed investors toward the deeper US market and instruments with no clean Brazilian equivalent.
- Access. Digital onboarding, Portuguese-language apps, and built-in câmbio collapsed the friction that once limited offshore investing to private-banking clients.

## Synthesis

Offshore investing and the [[Brazilian Depositary Receipts|BDR]] are two wrappers on the same underlying foreign asset, and for a long time the offshore route carried a structural tax-deferral advantage through holding companies. Lei 14.754 closed most of that gap, which reframes the choice as a clean trade-off: the offshore account gives direct ownership, voting where it exists, deeper liquidity, and a USD balance, at the cost of câmbio, custody abroad, and CBE/annual reporting; the BDR keeps everything in reais inside the Brazilian system at the cost of a depositary layer, thinner local liquidity, and the missing R$20k exemption. The platform cohort — [[Avenue Securities|Avenue]], [[Nomad]], [[Banco Inter|Inter]] — is the distribution layer that made the offshore side a retail product, and the [[Foreign private issuer]] lens explains the other end: many of the US names a Brazilian buys this way are themselves foreign issuers, so the chain is cross-border on both sides.
