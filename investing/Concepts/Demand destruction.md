# Demand destruction

Demand destruction is the commodity-market phenomenon in which sustained high prices reduce consumption — through behavioral substitution, industrial input substitution, output cuts, or business closures — until the market clears at lower volumes rather than higher prices. In oil and gas markets, the term is used to describe the asymmetric way the supply-demand balance closes when supply shocks persist past inventory drawdown: physical scarcity does not produce indefinite price escalation because at some price level, marginal demand exits the market. The term is most often invoked in vault analysis of the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] and the [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]] — where the question is not whether prices rise but at what level demand begins to shrink permanently.

The concept is the demand-side counterpart to [[Shock absorber|cartel shock-absorber]] dynamics on the supply side: both are mechanisms through which the oil market reaches equilibrium when normal balance is impossible. The shock absorber moderates supply; demand destruction moderates demand. The current Hormuz episode is a stress test for both at once.

## Channels of demand destruction in oil

Demand destruction is not a single mechanism but a sequence of substitutions and cuts that activate at different price thresholds:

1. Behavioral consumer substitution — at moderate price increases (gasoline at +20-30%), discretionary driving falls; transportation share shifts to public transit or remote work; air-travel discretionary trips compress. Cyclical and reversible.
2. Industrial input substitution — at sustained high prices, industries with substitutable feedstocks switch (e.g., natural gas vs heavy fuel oil for power, biodiesel blending up where mandated). Slower than behavioral but largely reversible.
3. Output cuts in price-taking industries — petrochemical complexes facing high feedstock costs (notably naphtha and LPG, both downstream of crude) cut production rather than absorb margin compression. Plastics, packaging, fertilizers, and some pharmaceutical inputs all move up in price as a result. Begins to convert to permanent destruction if the cuts last long enough to break commercial relationships.
4. Business closures and capital reallocation — at extended high prices, energy-intensive businesses close or relocate. This is the irreversible tail of demand destruction; the demand does not return when prices fall.

Per [[Karen Young]]'s framing in the [[2026 UAE OPEC exit|April 28, 2026 PBS NewsHour interview]], the current Hormuz episode is now far enough along that channels 3 and 4 are activating: petrochemical feedstock prices have produced visible cost increases in plastics and shipping; transportation fuels (gasoline, jet, marine, diesel) have hit four-year highs; restart timing for shut-in production once Hormuz reopens means demand destruction continues to compound through the recovery period.

## Why the term matters in vault analysis

Three threads.

First, demand destruction places a floor on how high oil prices can go without triggering a regime shift. If Brent is at $108 and Hormuz remains closed, the question is not "how high can it go" but "at what level does demand structurally exit." The latter determines the recovery trajectory more than the supply side does. The 2008 spike to $147 ended primarily through demand destruction (the Great Financial Crisis did the rest); the 2022 spike from the [[Russia-Ukraine war]] eased partly through European industrial-output cuts.

Second, demand destruction is asymmetric — recoveries are slower than collapses. Industrial capacity that exits at high prices does not return immediately when prices fall; the capital reallocation has a hysteresis. This is the mechanism by which a supply shock leaves a permanent footprint on the demand curve, captured in the vault's [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]] concept.

Third, refinery and petrochemical destruction is concentrated in specific regions. Europe and Northeast Asia bear the largest share of vulnerability because their refining and petchem complexes run on imported Middle East crude. US refining is partially insulated by domestic [[US Shale]] supply. This regional asymmetry interacts with the [[Refining slate rigidity]] dynamic in unfavorable ways: the regions facing input shocks are the same ones whose refineries are slate-locked into specific crude grades.
