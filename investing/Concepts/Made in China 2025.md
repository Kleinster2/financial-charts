# Made in China 2025

"Made in China 2025" is the Chinese state industrial-policy program announced in May 2015 by [[Li Keqiang]]'s [[State Council]] and modeled partly on [[Germany]]'s Industrie 4.0 framework. It set 10 priority sectors where [[China]] aimed to achieve global technological leadership by 2025 — and, implicitly, self-sufficiency in supply chains the US and Europe had previously controlled. Though Beijing formally stopped using the phrase in English-language communications after 2018 ([[Xi Jinping]]'s lieutenants decided the rhetoric was inflaming US backlash), the underlying program was accelerated, not abandoned, and is the direct antecedent of the [[Second China shock]] now hitting Western industrial sectors.

---

## The original 10 priority sectors

| Sector | Strategic goal |
|---|---|
| Next-generation information technology | Semiconductors, 5G, cloud, AI foundations |
| High-end numerical control machinery and robotics | Industrial automation parity with [[Germany]] and [[Japan]] |
| Aerospace equipment | Commercial aircraft ([[COMAC]]), satellites, space |
| Ocean engineering and high-tech ships | Shipbuilding leadership (achieved) |
| Advanced rail equipment | High-speed rail export capacity ([[CRRC]]) |
| New-energy vehicles and equipment | EV and battery leadership (achieved) |
| Power equipment | Grid, turbines, renewables manufacturing (largely achieved) |
| Agricultural machinery | Domestic food-security support |
| New materials | Composites, specialty chemicals, rare-earth processing |
| Biopharma and high-performance medical devices | [[Biotech]] ambitions |

The explicit quantitative targets were for domestic content shares: 40% of core components domestic by 2020, 70% by 2025.

## Why it matters to the vault

Made in China 2025 is the policy document that the [[Second China shock]] frame implicitly references. The sectors hitting European industrial employment in the 2020s are almost exactly the ten priorities of the 2015 plan — EVs, batteries, solar, wind, shipbuilding, rail equipment, industrial robots, advanced materials. That is not coincidence; the plan worked in roughly its stated direction, with massive subsidy, procurement preference, and intellectual-property development support behind it.

For vault analysis, MIC 2025 is the bridge between:
- [[China]]'s strategic planning documents (what Beijing said it would do)
- [[China trade]] surplus composition (what Beijing actually produced)
- [[Second China shock]] (what that production did to Western competitors)
- [[US-China chip export controls]] (US response targeting the semiconductor pillar specifically)

## The Washington reaction

Made in China 2025 was the proximate trigger for the Trump 1.0 Section 301 tariffs (announced March 2018). The USTR Section 301 investigation report explicitly named the plan as evidence of Chinese forced technology transfer and state-directed IP acquisition. From 2018 onward, "Made in China 2025" became a tell for hawkish Washington framing — which is why Beijing stopped using the phrase in English-language communications while continuing to execute the substance.

The Biden administration's response architecture ([[CHIPS Act]], [[Inflation Reduction Act|IRA]], export controls, outbound investment screening) can be read as a point-by-point counter to the specific sectoral targets of MIC 2025, especially on semiconductors, EVs, batteries, and clean-tech manufacturing.

---

## Successor programs

MIC 2025 was followed and in part absorbed by:

- **"Dual circulation" strategy** (2020) — explicit policy of building domestic-demand self-sufficiency while maintaining selective export orientation
- **"New productive forces"** (新质生产力, Xi Jinping, 2023-onward) — the current umbrella rhetoric for advanced-manufacturing industrial policy, framed explicitly around AI, biotech, quantum, new energy
- **14th Five-Year Plan (2021-2025)** and 15th Five-Year Plan (2026-2030, announced 2025) — state-investment sequencing
- **"Whole-of-nation system"** (举国体制) mobilization framework for chokepoint technologies (especially semiconductors post-2019 Entity List)

Each successor program retains the sectoral targeting of MIC 2025 while adjusting the rhetoric and broadening the policy stack.

## Assessment by sector (as of 2026)

| Sector | MIC 2025 goal status |
|---|---|
| High-speed rail, shipbuilding, power equipment | Achieved — dominant global position |
| EVs, batteries, solar, wind turbines | Achieved — leading or co-leading global position |
| Industrial robotics | Mid-tier — competitive but not yet leading [[Japan]]/[[Germany]] |
| Advanced semiconductors (sub-7nm) | Not achieved — blocked by export controls; domestic progress slow but real |
| Commercial aircraft (COMAC C919) | Entry-level achieved, widebody far behind |
| Biopharma / medical devices | Improving; not yet leadership |
| AI foundation models | Competitive with US labs (Qwen, DeepSeek, ByteDance Doubao) |

Net: most non-semiconductor pillars of MIC 2025 are substantially achieved by 2026. The semiconductor pillar is the one where US [[weaponized interdependence]]-style chokepoint intervention has meaningfully slowed China's progress.
