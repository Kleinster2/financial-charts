## April 2026, AI-led EM rerating

[[Ruchir Sharma]]'s FT column, "The return of the e-merging markets" (Apr 20, 2026), sharpened a point that had already been showing up in flow and market reporting earlier in the quarter: the current EM comeback is being led less by generic commodity beta or carry and more by the part of EM embedded in the AI hardware stack. His clean formulation was that [[South Korea]] and [[Taiwan]] are the "biggest beneficiaries of the AI wave". That matters because it reframes the asset class. EM is no longer only a weaker-dollar or domestic-reform trade. In this cycle it also offers listed exposure to memory, foundry capacity, and advanced manufacturing without paying full US megacap multiples.

[[Reuters]] had already shown the flow backdrop. On Feb 2 it reported year-to-date inflows above $39 billion into EM equities after one of the strongest January starts in more than 20 years, helped by a softer dollar and improving earnings expectations. By Feb 20, [[Reuters]] reported that US-domiciled investors had put about $26 billion into EM equities year to date, with [[South Korea]] the largest single-country destination at $2.8 billion and [[Brazil]] next at $1.2 billion. The readthrough is that the EM bid was not just theoretical, allocators were actively rotating capital out of the crowded US trade.

The April 2026 [[Iran]]-war aftershock also showed the limits of the rally. EM equities could recover on AI optimism even while EM currencies weakened on higher oil and a firmer dollar. That split matters. The current rerating is real, but it is narrow and equity-led. It helps the parts of EM tied to the AI supply chain first, not every oil-importing or rate-sensitive market at the same time.
