# US-Europe productivity paradox

A methodological framework articulated by [[Paul Krugman]] in his May 16-18, 2026 Substack pair ("Challenging the Narrative of European Decline, Continued" + "Europe Versus America: A Wonkish Data Follow-up") that argues the standard productivity-growth comparison between the United States and Europe is misleading because it depends on a pricing methodology choice that flips the conclusion. Under constant-price measurement, Europe shows clear relative decline. Under current-price (PPP — Purchasing Power Parity) measurement, there is no meaningful trend between the regions. The implication: the consensus narrative of European technological backwardness "rests on bad data analysis" (per Krugman, Substack May 18, 2026).

The framework is structurally important for the vault thread because it directly affects the analytical foundation for [[Industrial policy]] EU regime evaluation, [[De-equitisation]] EU market commentary, and any cross-jurisdictional comparison that relies on productivity-growth data. If the constant-price-vs-PPP distinction is load-bearing, then a substantial portion of the conventional EU-decline narrative used by Western analysts and policymakers is methodologically unstable.

---

## The paradox

The core empirical claim: US-vs-Europe productivity comparisons produce *opposite* conclusions depending on the pricing methodology used (per Krugman, May 18 2026):

| Methodology | Result | Interpretation |
|-------------|--------|----------------|
| Constant prices (the standard productivity-growth comparison) | Europe in relative decline vs US since ~2000 | The dominant narrative — Europe is falling behind technologically |
| Current prices (PPP) | No meaningful trend between EU and US | No relative decline; the levels move together |

Krugman's framing of the puzzle in his own words: "European relative decline if you use constant prices, no trend using current prices" — same data, different methodology, opposite conclusions (per Krugman, May 18 2026).

---

## Why the methodologies diverge

The mechanism Krugman identifies (per Krugman, May 18 2026): America's faster constant-price productivity growth reflects dominance in a narrow IT sector where rapid quality improvements drive constant-price measures up faster than current-price measures. The constant-price framework treats faster IT-sector improvement as productivity gain accruing to the US economy in aggregate. The PPP framework treats the same underlying activity as cheaper IT services flowing to consumers globally, with no aggregate productivity differential.

The puzzle is real because both methodologies are legitimate measurements of different things:

- Constant prices measure aggregate output volume holding the price index fixed — useful for "how much real output is the economy producing"
- Current prices (PPP) measure relative purchasing power across jurisdictions — useful for "what does the economy actually buy"

When the price index for the sector driving productivity gains is falling rapidly (as in IT through 2000-2025), the two methodologies diverge sharply. The constant-price measure attributes the price drop to the US economy's productivity; the PPP measure recognises that the cheaper IT is available to European consumers and firms too, and that European productivity should be measured against the cheaper-IT-adjusted output, not the constant-price-of-IT-from-2000 output.

## What Krugman is *not* claiming (the careful framing)

The framework explicitly disclaims the strong version of the EU-rehabilitation argument. Krugman is "not saying that all is well with Europe" (per Krugman, May 18 2026). The narrower claim:

- The constant-price-vs-PPP divergence undermines the *productivity-growth* narrative specifically
- It does not address other components of the EU vs US comparison (capital formation, labour force participation, demographics, innovation outputs, financial market depth, energy costs, regulatory burden)
- It does not claim European outcomes are equivalent — only that the productivity-growth metric used to argue otherwise is methodologically unstable
- The "Europe is technologically backward" common characterisation "rests on bad data analysis" — but other criticisms of European economic performance may stand on different grounds

The careful framing matters for the vault thread because it limits how much the paradox can be used to support specific investing or policy positions (inference — Krugman doesn't stake out the limits explicitly but his hedged language implies them).

## Data sources Krugman cites

Three institutions provide the data underlying the analysis (per Krugman, May 18 2026):

| Source | Role |
|--------|------|
| World Bank | Primary data |
| IMF | Slightly different estimates of the same comparison |
| OECD Data Explorer | Confirms World Bank findings with euro area vs US, 2000-2023 |

All three converge on the constant-price-vs-PPP divergence, which strengthens the methodological claim (per Krugman). The convergence across data sources means the paradox is robust to source-selection rather than being a single-dataset artefact.

## Limits and open questions

(All marked as inference.)

1. Causal direction: the framework identifies that the methodologies diverge but doesn't fully resolve which is "correct" for the underlying economic question being asked. Different policy questions plausibly require different methodologies
2. IT-sector specificity: the divergence is driven primarily by IT-sector quality adjustment. Whether the same pattern operates in other rapid-improvement sectors (biotech, AI services, financial services) is empirically unclear
3. Statistical agency methodology: the constant-price calculations depend on hedonic adjustments by statistical agencies (BLS in the US, Eurostat in the EU). Different agency conventions could explain part of the divergence — Krugman doesn't address agency-level methodology differences explicitly
4. Time dependence: the paradox may operate primarily during periods of rapid IT-sector improvement. In a future period of IT-sector stagnation, the two methodologies might converge — making the paradox a time-specific phenomenon rather than a permanent feature
5. PPP measurement is itself contested: cross-jurisdictional purchasing-power comparisons rely on identifying comparable consumption baskets, which is methodologically difficult. The PPP measure may be more accurate than constant-price for cross-country comparison but less accurate than constant-price for within-country time-series
