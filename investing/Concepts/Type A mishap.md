#concept #space #policy

**Type A mishap** — [[NASA]]'s most severe accident classification under its mishap-reporting framework. Reserved for incidents involving fatalities, permanent total disability, total damage to a vehicle, or damages exceeding established financial thresholds. Historical Type A precedents are rare and consequential: [[Apollo 1]] fire (1967, 3 crew lost), [[Challenger]] disaster (1986, 7 crew lost), [[Columbia]] disaster (2003, 7 crew lost). [[Boeing Starliner]]'s 2024 crewed flight test was reclassified as Type A on February 19, 2026 by administrator [[Jared Isaacman]] — the first Type A reclassification of a commercial-crew incident.

---

## Why Type A matters

The classification is regulatory signaling, not just bookkeeping. A Type A incident triggers:
- Independent mishap investigation board with broad authority
- Comprehensive root-cause analysis
- Mandatory implementation of recommendations before next flight
- Significant additional certification cost and timeline
- Permanent reputational mark on the program and contractor

For commercial-crew incidents specifically, Type A reclassification can reset certification timelines by years and impose process burdens that change the economic case for continued program participation.

## Type A precedents

| Year | Mishap | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| 1967 | [[Apollo 1]] fire | 3 crew lost on launch pad |
| 1986 | [[Challenger]] disaster | 7 crew lost on ascent; STS grounded ~2.5 years |
| 2003 | [[Columbia]] disaster | 7 crew lost on reentry; STS retired ~2011 |
| 2024 (reclassified Feb 2026) | [[Boeing Starliner]] CFT | Crew safely returned via [[SpaceX]] [[Crew Dragon]] 9 months later; Starliner certification path significantly steeper |

## Boeing Starliner reclassification (Feb 19, 2026)

[[Jared Isaacman]] reclassified Boeing's 2024 [[Boeing Starliner|Starliner]] crewed flight test as Type A on Feb 19, 2026. Reasoning: loss of maneuverability approaching ISS, near-loss of crew (Wilmore and Williams), financial damages from the 9-month uncrewed deorbit and crew rotation backfill via [[SpaceX]] [[Crew Dragon]]. Isaacman publicly criticized the prior administration: "Concern for the Starliner program's reputation influenced that decision" not to immediately classify Type A.

The reclassification matters for [[Boeing]]'s defense/space segment beyond Starliner itself — it sets a regulatory tone that legacy fixed-price prime contractor programs operating with cost overruns and quality issues can be retroactively reclassified. See [[Boeing Starliner]] and [[NASA]].
