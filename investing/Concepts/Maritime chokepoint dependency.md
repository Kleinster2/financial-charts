# Maritime chokepoint dependency

Maritime chokepoint dependency is the vulnerability created when a country, sector, or commodity supply chain relies on one or two narrow sea routes with limited rerouting options. It is the trade-flow version of single-supplier risk: the economic impact depends less on the physical width of the waterway than on the share of trade forced through it, the availability of substitutes, and whether shippers and insurers believe passage is safe.

---

## Why it matters now

The May 24, 2026 [[Financial Times]] visual story, "The power struggle in the world's narrow seas," reframed the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] as the live stress test for a wider chokepoint regime. [[Strait of Hormuz]] is the acute case because much Gulf oil, LNG, fertilizer, and petrochemical trade has no true seaborne alternative. Other chokepoints have alternates, but those alternates convert low-cost just-in-time logistics into longer voyages, higher insurance, inventory delays, and geopolitical bargaining.

The investment implication is that "route exists" is not the same as "capacity exists." The [[Cape of Good Hope]] can absorb some [[Suez Canal]] / [[Bab el-Mandeb]] rerouting. Trucks through [[Saudi Arabia]] and [[Turkey]] can move selected cargoes. Pipelines can bypass part of the Gulf. None of those substitutions recreate the capacity, timing, or insurance profile of open sea lanes.

---

## Structural framework

[[Jasper Verschuur]], [[Johannes Lumma]], and [[Jim W Hall]] estimate expected chokepoint-disrupted trade by combining route exposure, disruption probability, duration, severity, and country-level trade flow. Their 2025 [[Nature Communications]] paper estimates about $191.5B of global trade value exposed to chokepoint disruption each year and separates chokepoints by rerouting feasibility.

| Class | Meaning | Examples | Investing read |
|---|---|---|---|
| No rerouting | Cargo is delayed or stranded because the route is enclosed or destination-specific | [[Strait of Hormuz]], [[Turkish Straits]], [[Oresund Strait]] | Highest supply-chain convexity; price impact comes from physical shortage, not only freight cost |
| Long rerouting | Ships can sail around, but extra distance exceeds 5,000 km | [[Suez Canal]], [[Bab el-Mandeb]], [[Panama Canal]], [[Strait of Gibraltar]] | Freight rates, schedule reliability, working capital, and inventory buffers absorb the shock first |
| Short rerouting | Detour exists near the original path | [[Taiwan Strait]], [[Korea Strait]], parts of the South China Sea routing complex | Direct sailing cost may be modest, but conflict implications can dominate the navigational distance |

The study's highest-risk combinations line up with the live market map: geopolitical tension at [[Taiwan Strait]], [[Strait of Hormuz]], and [[Bab el-Mandeb]]; drought at [[Panama Canal]]; and piracy risk around [[Strait of Malacca]].

## Market channels

| Channel | Transmission |
|---|---|
| Energy and fuels | No-reroute energy chokepoints move crude, LNG, jet fuel, diesel, bunker fuel, and fertilizer faster than general freight indices |
| Freight and schedules | Long reroutes absorb vessel capacity, raise charter rates, and create port bunching when routes normalize |
| Insurance | Even an open waterway can become commercially closed if war-risk cover is repriced or withdrawn |
| Working capital | Longer routes tie up cargo and inventory, turning low-margin goods into financing problems |
| Strategic control | Port concessions, naval exercises, escort regimes, and inspection campaigns become market variables |
| Substitution | Overland trucking, pipelines, and Arctic routes offer redundancy but create new chokepoints of their own |
