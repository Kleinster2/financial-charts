#concept #longevity #biotech #healthcare

**Longevity** — the investment and research thesis that biological aging can be slowed, targeted, or treated as a regulable medical condition, extending healthy lifespan (healthspan) rather than just treating individual age-related diseases.

## The investment problem

Longevity is a category that does not legally exist yet. The field spans geroscience research, drug development against aging pathways, and the venture and public capital underwriting it — but its central obstacle is regulatory: the [[#The regulatory crux|FDA does not recognize aging as an indication]], so a drug that slows aging has nothing to be approved *for*. Every serious program therefore routes around the gap — through a [[#The regulatory crux|specific disease of aging]], through an [[Loyal|animal-first lifespan claim]], or through a [[#The regulatory crux|metformin-style proof of concept]] that aging itself is treatable. The investable surface is almost entirely private (see [[#Why it is hard to own]]), so the public-market expression is indirect and the real signal is in who is underwriting which [[#Mechanisms — the science map|mechanism]].

## Why it is hard to own

There is no clean listed pure-play longevity company and no representative index. The investable universe is dominated by private names — [[Altos Labs]], [[Retro Biosciences]], [[Life Biosciences]], [[Calico Life Sciences]], [[Loyal]] — none of which trades. The cautionary public precedent is [[Unity Biotechnology]] (UBX), an early senolytics pioneer that ceased operations in 2025 after clinical failures. The closest current listed proxy is [[BioAge Labs]] (NASDAQ: BIOA), an AI-enabled metabolic-aging company that raised ~$132M in a January 2026 follow-on — but a single beaten-down clinical-stage stock is not a longevity basket. Global longevity financing reached ~$8.49B across 325 deals in 2024; the money is real, the listed exposure is not.

## Mechanisms — the science map

The field is organized less by company than by mechanism, and the mechanism determines the regulatory path and the risk.

- Partial cellular reprogramming — apply a subset of Yamanaka factors (OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, sometimes c-Myc) to push aged cells toward a younger epigenetic state without erasing cell identity. The buzziest and best-funded approach as of 2026. Players: [[Altos Labs]] (most capital, least disclosed), [[Retro Biosciences]] ([[Sam Altman]]-backed, AI-designed reprogramming factors via [[OpenAI]] GPT-4b), [[Life Biosciences]] (first to a human trial). Risk: reprogramming too far risks loss of cell identity and tumorigenesis — hence "partial."
- Senolytics — selectively clear senescent ("zombie") cells that accumulate with age and drive inflammation. The approach [[Unity Biotechnology]] pioneered and that has so far disappointed in the clinic; still pursued in metabolic-aging programs at [[BioAge Labs]].
- Metabolic and growth-factor modulation — repurpose or design drugs (metformin, rapamycin/mTOR, GLP-1 adjacencies) that tune the nutrient-sensing and growth pathways tied to aging. This is the lane closest to a regulatory proof of concept via the [[#The regulatory crux|TAME trial]].
- Animal-first lifespan extension — get a regulator to accept "extend healthy lifespan" as an approvable claim in animals before humans. [[Loyal]]'s canine program is the leading example; dogs age several times faster than people, so a result reads out in years and de-risks the human-geroscience translation.

## The regulatory crux

This is what makes longevity a category play rather than a drug bet. Regulators approve treatments for specific diseases, anchored to the WHO International Classification of Diseases; aging is not on that list, so "anti-aging" has no approvable indication. The workarounds:

- The [[TAME trial]] (Targeting Aging with Metformin) — a ~3,000-person, 4-year, placebo-controlled study in non-diabetics aged 65–79, designed not to prove metformin works but to establish that *aging itself can be treated* as a regulatory endpoint. The FDA agreed to the trial design, which in principle opens the same path to anyone with the capital to run it. It remains only partially funded.
- Disease-of-aging proxies — pick a specific age-related disease the FDA already recognizes while the underlying mechanism is general. [[Life Biosciences]] chose optic neuropathy (glaucoma/NAION) for its ER-100 reprogramming therapy; [[Retro Biosciences]] chose Alzheimer's for its lead pill. Approval comes through the disease; the platform is the prize.
- Animal-first — [[Loyal]] is pursuing FDA acceptance of a canine lifespan-extension claim (LOY-002), a category that does not exist for humans, as the wedge.

## What to watch

- [[Retro Biosciences]] Alzheimer's-pill data, expected ~August 2026 — the field's nearest binary clinical catalyst.
- [[Life Biosciences]] ER-100 Phase 1 (optic neuropathy) readout — first human partial-reprogramming safety signal.
- [[Loyal]] LOY-002 — manufacturing (CMC) submission, the last of three FDA sections before a conditional approval reported for late 2026 / 2027.
- [[TAME trial]] funding status — full funding would formalize "aging is treatable" as a regulatory endpoint.
- Any longevity IPO that creates a real listed pure-play (would enable a basket chart and cluster work).
