# Industrial policy

Government intervention designed to shape the sectoral composition of the economy — directing capital, regulatory protection, subsidies, procurement, and technical standards toward favoured industries on the basis that the market alone will underproduce strategically important capabilities. Industrial policy ranges from narrow vertical interventions (supporting a specific industry like semiconductors) to broad horizontal policies (R&D tax credits, infrastructure, workforce training). After a decades-long market-fundamentalist retreat (~1980-2010), industrial policy has returned to the centre of policymaking in both democratic and authoritarian capital allocators — most visibly through the US's [[CHIPS Act]] (2022), [[Inflation Reduction Act]] (2022), and the EU's response, plus China's continuous and deepening industrial planning framework (see [[Made in China 2025]], [[Common Prosperity]], [[Dual circulation]], [[Fortress China]]).

This is a hub concept. Specific national applications are tracked in dedicated notes ([[CHIPS Act]] for US semiconductors, [[Made in China 2025]] for China's high-tech push, [[Inflation Reduction Act]] for US clean energy, etc.). This note covers the general framework, the historical lineage, the theoretical arguments, and the cross-jurisdictional comparison.

## Synopsis

Industrial policy is one of the oldest tools of statecraft — older than central banking or modern fiscal policy — and one of the most contested. Its historical lineage runs from 17th-century mercantilism through Alexander Hamilton's 1791 *Report on Manufactures* and Friedrich List's 1841 *National System of Political Economy*, into the post-WWII East Asian developmental state models (Japan MITI, Korea HCI, Taiwan ITRI), the European indicative-planning traditions, and the 2020s rediscovery of "industrial strategy" by the major Western democracies (per general policy history).

The market-fundamentalist retreat (~1980-2010) — Reagan-Thatcher deregulation, the Washington Consensus, the WTO accession of [[China]] in 2001, and the broad academic consensus against picking winners — narrowed industrial policy to a few narrow categories (basic R&D, defense procurement, agricultural subsidies). The retreat reversed sharply after the 2008 financial crisis (industrial-policy-as-crisis-response in the auto rescue and bank recapitalisations) and definitively in the 2017-2025 period under both [[Donald Trump]] (Trade Act Section 301 tariffs on China, [[Defense Production Act]] expansion, CHIPS Act precursors) and [[Joe Biden]] (CHIPS Act, IRA, Bipartisan Infrastructure Law). The bipartisan continuity of the policy turn is more structurally important than the partisan rhetoric (inference — most analysts focus on the partisan branding rather than the substantive convergence).

The 2020s revival has four mutually reinforcing drivers: (1) geopolitical fragmentation that makes globalised supply chains a strategic liability ([[Weaponized interdependence]], [[Economic latency]]); (2) climate transition requiring directed investment at scales the private market won't deliver alone; (3) the visible success of Chinese industrial planning in semiconductors, EVs, batteries, and renewable manufacturing; (4) the political economy of populist coalitions in both US parties requiring an answer to the manufacturing-employment collapse of 2000-2015 ([[Second China shock]]). The convergence of these four drivers is unusual historically and is what makes the 2020s industrial-policy revival potentially durable rather than cyclical (inference).

## Definition and typology

Industrial policy is not a single tool but a family of instruments. The main typological distinctions:

| Axis | Categories | Examples |
|------|------------|----------|
| Sectoral specificity | Vertical (targeted) vs Horizontal (broad-based) | [[CHIPS Act]] (vertical, semiconductors) vs R&D tax credit (horizontal) |
| Intent | Promotional (build capability) vs Defensive (preserve capability) vs Strategic (deny adversary capability) | [[Made in China 2025]] (promotional) vs [[Economic latency]] (defensive) vs [[Export controls]] (strategic) |
| Time horizon | Cyclical / crisis (auto rescue 2009) vs Structural (multi-decade reshoring) | TARP auto rescue vs the 2022-onward US semiconductor reshoring |
| Instrument type | Subsidy / tax credit / tariff / procurement / regulation / R&D / standards | [[CHIPS Act]] $52bn direct subsidy vs IRA production tax credit vs Section 301 tariff |
| Ownership posture | Market-shaping (subsidise private firms) vs State-equity (direct stakes) vs State-owned (SOEs) | Tesla EV subsidies vs France's stake in Renault vs China's SOE semi fabs |
| Domain | Manufacturing vs Services vs Critical minerals vs Knowledge / IP vs Workforce | CHIPS Act (manufacturing) vs Service-sector productivity programmes (services) |

The two most useful distinctions for the 2020s analysis are vertical-vs-horizontal and promotional-vs-defensive-vs-strategic. The Biden-Sullivan-Brainard "new Washington consensus" articulation (per Jake Sullivan's April 2023 Brookings speech) explicitly embraces vertical + promotional/defensive instruments that the prior Washington Consensus would have rejected.

## Historical lineage

### 1. Mercantilism (17th-18th c.)

The original industrial-policy framework — accumulate trade surpluses, protect domestic manufacturing via tariffs, restrict raw-material exports to preserve domestic value-add. Mercantilist policy underpinned the rise of the Dutch, British, and French economies and was the explicit target of Adam Smith's 1776 critique in *The Wealth of Nations*. Modern industrial policy inherits the strategic-sector logic of mercantilism while shedding most of the bullion-accumulation framing.

### 2. Hamiltonian American System (1791-)

Alexander Hamilton's *Report on Manufactures* (1791) to the US Congress argued for tariffs, subsidies, and infrastructure investment to nurture infant American manufacturing against British competition. Hamilton's framework — infant industry protection + internal improvements + sound money + national bank — became the "American System" championed by Henry Clay in the 19th century and is the canonical English-language industrial-policy doctrine.

The Hamiltonian tradition is the source of the modern "infant industry" argument: industries with significant learning-curve effects or coordination requirements can be permanently uncompetitive without temporary protection, even if they would be globally competitive once mature.

Chartbook's May 27 link to the New York Almanac is a useful historical corrective to a purely tariff/subsidy version of the story. Early U.S. industrialization also depended on the transfer of British machinery designs, skilled-worker know-how, and production routines that Britain tried to restrict. For modern investing, that makes export controls and industrial policy partly a tacit-knowledge problem: legal controls can slow technology leakage, but catch-up happens through people, tools, memorized process knowledge, and organizational replication, not only through formal trade in machines.

Source: [[Adam Tooze]], Chartbook Top Links 1114, May 27 2026; New York Almanac item on early U.S. industrial technology transfer via Chartbook.

### 3. The Listian tradition (1841-)

Friedrich List's *National System of Political Economy* (1841) gave the Hamiltonian framework its theoretical foundation, arguing that classical economics' free-trade-as-universal-good prescription applied only to economies at the same stage of development. For catching-up economies, protective industrial policy was the necessary path to industrialisation. List's framework directly influenced 19th-century German industrialisation, late-19th-century Japanese Meiji-era reforms, and the post-WWII East Asian developmental state model.

### 4. Post-WWII variants (1945-1980)

The post-WWII period was a golden age of industrial policy across the non-communist developed world (per Robert Wade, *Governing the Market*, 1990 and broader literature):

| Country | Framework | Outcome |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Japan | [[MITI]] guidance (then METI) — sector selection, "administrative guidance" to keiretsu | Post-war economic miracle through ~1980s; later structural stagnation |
| South Korea | Heavy and Chemical Industries (HCI) drive (1973-79); chaebol nurturing | Semis ([[Samsung]], SK Hynix), shipbuilding, autos — successful catch-up |
| Taiwan | ITRI + [[TSMC]] spinoff (1987) | The cleanest single-policy-bet success of the 20th century |
| France | Indicative planning + national champions ([[Airbus]], [[EDF]], Thomson) | Mixed — strong on aerospace/nuclear, mixed elsewhere |
| Germany | Ordoliberalism + sector-specific R&D + apprenticeship | Sustained manufacturing competitiveness; less explicitly "industrial policy" branded |
| Italy | IRI (state holding) + Mediobanca + family-business clusters | Mixed |
| Sweden | Saltsjöbaden corporatist model + industrial banks | Strong through 1980s |
| US | Defense-procurement-as-industrial-policy ([[DARPA]], military R&D), agricultural subsidies | The "hidden developmental state" — significant but unbranded |

The pattern: explicit industrial policy was the norm, not the exception, across the developed world for the first three decades after WWII. The retrospective view that "market economies don't do industrial policy" is a 1980s-onward rewriting of the actual history.

### 5. Market-fundamentalist retreat (1980-2010)

The Reagan-Thatcher revolution, the Mont Pelerin / Chicago intellectual ascendancy, the fall of the Soviet bloc, and the WTO/Washington Consensus framework collectively narrowed industrial policy to a small set of acceptable horizontal interventions (basic research, infrastructure, education). The retreat had several components:

- Academic consensus against "picking winners" (Anne Krueger on rent-seeking; later Paul Krugman's critique of strategic trade theory in policy)
- WTO rules constraining subsidies and tariffs (TRIMS, SCM Agreement)
- IMF/World Bank conditionality enforcing Washington Consensus on developing countries
- US/UK privatisation of state assets ([[British Telecom]], etc.)
- The collapse of Japanese MITI's reputation after the 1990s stagnation

What remained: defense procurement, agricultural subsidies, narrow R&D programmes ([[DARPA]], NIH), antitrust as competition-policy-substitute. The 1990s-2000s was the historical low-watermark of explicit industrial policy in the developed world.

### 6. The 2020s revival

The 2017-onward turn from market-fundamentalism back to explicit industrial policy is one of the largest reversals of bipartisan policy consensus in modern US history. Key catalysts:

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 2008-09 | Auto rescue + bank recapitalisation | Crisis-mode industrial policy; broke the 1990s consensus on its terms |
| 2015 | [[Made in China 2025]] launched | Chinese ambition was visible at a scale that forced Western response |
| 2017 | [[Donald Trump]] Section 301 tariffs on China | First major US tariff regime since the 1980s; bipartisan support in Congress |
| 2018-19 | Huawei / ZTE / Entity List crisis | [[Weaponized interdependence]] becomes operationally visible |
| 2020 | Covid supply shock | Made strategic dependencies in pharma, PPE, electronics undeniable |
| 2021 | Biden EO on supply chains; Bipartisan Infrastructure Law | Explicit "industrial strategy" branding adopted by US government |
| 2022 (Aug) | [[CHIPS Act]] ($52B direct + 25% investment tax credit) | Largest single industrial-policy bill in US since 1950s |
| 2022 (Aug) | [[Inflation Reduction Act]] (~$370B clean-energy provisions) | Largest climate-tied industrial-policy bill globally |
| 2023 (Apr) | Jake Sullivan Brookings "modern American industrial strategy" speech | Doctrinal articulation of the "new Washington consensus" |
| 2025-26 | [[Trump II]] reshoring + tariff regime continuation | Bipartisan continuity confirmed |

The bipartisan continuity is the single most important structural fact. [[Joe Biden]] and [[Donald Trump]] disagree on everything except industrial policy — the CHIPS Act is the most prominent example of a bill that both administrations have continued to implement and expand. This is the source of the durability claim: industrial policy in the US is not a partisan project that flips with the next administration; it is a settled bipartisan consensus that survives political turnover (inference — this is the most structurally important claim about the durability of the 2020s revival).

## Theoretical foundations

The economic arguments for industrial policy fall into several distinct families. Each addresses a different type of market failure.

### Infant industry argument

Industries with steep learning curves or economies of scale cannot compete with mature foreign producers in the short run, but could become globally competitive if given temporary protection while building scale and capability. The argument dates to Hamilton (1791) and List (1841); modern formalisation by [[Paul Krugman]] in the 1980s. The empirical record is mixed: Korean semiconductors and Taiwanese foundries are clear successes; Brazilian computers in the 1980s and many ISI-era Latin American attempts were failures. The differentiator is whether the protected industry was ever subjected to export discipline (Korea/Taiwan) or remained domestically protected forever (Latin America).

### Strategic trade theory (Krugman-Brander-Spencer)

When global markets are imperfectly competitive with rents to incumbents, government subsidy can shift the rent distribution toward domestic firms. The 1980s [[Boeing]]-[[Airbus]] subsidy competition is the canonical case. Strategic trade theory was the academic high-water mark for industrial-policy reasoning in the 1980s; [[Paul Krugman]]'s own subsequent reconsideration (the political-economy difficulty of implementation) is also part of the story.

The 2026 update of this lineage comes from [[Chad Bown]] and [[Soumaya Keynes]] (*How to Win a Trade War*), discussed on Krugman's May 30 2026 podcast. Their argument: the post-WWII rules-based system has collapsed under [[Second China shock|the China shock]], Russia's war, and asymmetric-interdependence strategies ([[Made in China 2025]], [[Dual circulation]]), so the relevant policy question shifts from "is free trade optimal?" to "how does a state play a strategic, defensive trade game?" Their prescriptions — stockpiling, selective industrial policy, preserving critical manufacturing capacity, and coordinating with allies against China rather than fighting intra-Western tariff battles — are an industrial-policy reading of trade strategy. The sharpest concrete example is China inverting the West's forced-technology-transfer model by licensing the outbound flow of its own battery/EV technology (see [[China retaliatory toolkit]]).

*Source: [Paul Krugman, "How to Win a Trade War" (podcast with [[Chad Bown]] and [[Soumaya Keynes]]), May 30 2026](https://paulkrugman.substack.com/p/how-to-win-a-trade-war).*

### Learning by doing + path dependence

Industries with significant tacit knowledge, "learning by doing" effects, or network externalities can be permanently stuck in a low-equilibrium without coordinated intervention. The semiconductor industry is the canonical case — fab construction costs grow with each node and tacit process knowledge is non-transferable. The TSMC-MIT-DARPA collaboration in the 1980s-90s is the kind of intervention that builds permanent capability.

### Coordination failures

Some industries require simultaneous investments across multiple firms (supplier networks, workforce training, R&D infrastructure) that no single private actor will provide. Industrial policy can solve the coordination problem. The German Industrie 4.0 framework + apprenticeship system is the cleanest contemporary example.

### National security / supply-chain resilience (2010s-2020s addition)

The economic-statecraft turn added a new argument: even where private markets allocate efficiently, certain capabilities must be domestically available for national-security reasons. This is the rationale behind US Defense Production Act invocations, [[CHIPS Act]] sovereign-fab requirements, [[Critical minerals]] domestic-processing investment, and the proposed [[Economic latency]] fund. The argument doesn't depend on traditional market-failure logic — it depends on coercion-vulnerability logic.

## The arguments against industrial policy

The classical critiques remain analytically powerful:

### Knowledge problem (Hayek)

Centralised planners cannot know which industries to favour, because market prices and dispersed knowledge are the only mechanism for revealing comparative advantage. Industrial policy must guess; markets discover. The critique is most powerful against narrow vertical interventions in fast-changing industries (digital, biotech).

### Rent-seeking (Krueger, Stigler)

Once industrial policy is on the table, firms will lobby for subsidies and protection regardless of whether they actually need them. The political economy of selection becomes dominated by rent-seeking rather than economic-merit. The critique is most powerful against subsidy regimes that lack strict performance metrics.

### Regulatory capture

The agencies administering industrial policy are vulnerable to capture by the industries they regulate. The US transportation sector after the 1970s deregulation, the financial sector post-2008, and various national-champion programmes globally have all exhibited this dynamic.

### Fiscal cost + opportunity cost

Direct industrial subsidies are expensive, and the alternative uses of the same fiscal capacity (income transfers, public goods, debt reduction) are not always inferior. The CHIPS Act + IRA combined cost is ~$420B over a decade — comparable to the entire Medicaid expansion in scale.

### Adverse selection on losing industries

Firms that need subsidy are often the ones that lack competitive prospects. The strongest firms don't need help. This means industrial-policy spending naturally accrues to politically connected but economically weak sectors.

### Trade-war risk

Aggressive industrial policy invites retaliation. The 2025-2026 escalation of US-China-EU tariff dynamics is partly an industrial-policy-on-industrial-policy escalation cycle.

## Measuring economic power — the production-vs-profit debate

[[Chris Miller]]'s May 16, 2026 Substack pair ("What is economic power?" + "How should we measure economic power?") articulates the live methodological debate that the 2020s industrial-policy revival has surfaced: how should economic power actually be quantified? Two competing schools (per Chris Miller, May 16 2026):

| School | Metric | Argument | Implication |
|--------|--------|----------|-------------|
| Production-based | Manufacturing value-added share, industrial output | "Victory has repeatedly gone to the side with a flourishing productive base" ([[Paul Kennedy]], *Rise and Fall of the Great Powers*); historical track record from Britain → Germany → US → Japan | China's ~40% share of global manufacturing value-added ≈ 3x US share → China is the rising power |
| Profit-based | Sectoral profit margins, particularly in high-tech | "Profit results from power imbalances, within and between societies" — [[Brooks]] + [[Vagle]] framework; US-and-allied firms dominate profit margins, "particularly pronounced" in high-tech | US-allied firms dominate the value-extraction layer despite manufacturing migration → US is still the dominant economic power |

Miller "settles in the messy middle," acknowledging both schools contain validity without endorsing a single metric (per Chris Miller, May 16 2026). The debate matters operationally because the two frameworks imply very different industrial-policy responses:

- The production school justifies aggressive reshoring and manufacturing capacity-rebuilding (CHIPS Act maximalist read)
- The profit school justifies preserving the profit-margin position via IP protection + financial-system control + standards-setting (the [[Weaponized interdependence]] read)

Specific cases Miller cites (per Chris Miller, May 16 2026):

- [[CATL]] (~$300B) — scale generates technological moats; production-school evidence
- [[Apple]] supply chain — profitable US firm reshapes production networks but "factory locations are disproportionately in China"; profit-school anomaly
- Ukraine drone ecosystem — hybrid dependency: Chinese manufacturing components + Ukrainian coordination software; suggests neither school alone captures the modern war economy

The Miller framework is structurally important for the vault thread because it provides the analytical vocabulary for the policy-implications question that the [[CHIPS Act]] / [[Inflation Reduction Act]] / [[Made in China 2025]] / [[Common Prosperity]] / [[Economic latency]] notes all gesture at but don't directly address: which kind of economic power is being protected or built, and is the chosen instrument matched to the kind being targeted? (inference — this is the framework-to-implementation gap Miller's pair surfaces but doesn't fully resolve.)

## Major contemporary industrial-policy regimes (2020s)

### United States

The 2022 [[CHIPS Act]] + [[Inflation Reduction Act]] + [[Defense Production Act]] expansion + Section 301 tariffs represent the largest US industrial-policy programme since the 1950s. Key features:

- Bipartisan support (CHIPS passed 64-33 in Senate, 243-187 in House)
- Mixed instruments: direct subsidy ($52B CHIPS), production tax credits (IRA), tariffs (Section 301)
- Defensive + promotional split: CHIPS Act is mostly defensive (preserve semiconductor capability); IRA is promotional (build clean-energy industry)
- Explicit doctrinal articulation: Jake Sullivan April 2023 Brookings speech as the "new Washington consensus"
- Implementation pace: $50B+ of CHIPS Act commitments by mid-2024; IRA tax-credit utilisation expanding through 2026

Visible beneficiaries: [[TSMC]] Arizona, [[Intel]] Ohio, [[Micron]] Idaho/New York, [[Samsung]] Texas, [[GlobalFoundries]] New York; on the IRA side, EV battery firms ([[LG Energy Solution]], [[CATL]] via Ford partnership), solar manufacturers, hydrogen-hub partnerships.

### Biden clean-energy standards vs administrative capacity

The May 2026 Volts / Roosevelt Institute debate usefully separates two questions that are often collapsed in the "everything bagel" critique of Biden-era industrial policy: whether labor/community standards themselves slow deployment, and whether the administrative system implementing them has enough capacity to process large flows of money quickly. Betony Jones, who ran labor and jobs policy at the [[Department of Energy]] (DOE) under [[Joe Biden]], argues from interviews with more than a dozen clean-energy funding recipients that the main friction points were not prevailing-wage, apprenticeship, or community-benefit standards. Recipients more often cited permitting delays, US supply-chain constraints, steep learning curves, and government contracting timelines.

The Roosevelt report's quantitative frame is material: DOE tied 74% of major IIJA/IRA clean-energy project funding, or $91B across 110 major projects, to formal labor or community agreements. Clean-energy manufacturing investment rose from less than $5B in 2019 to nearly $60B in 2023, while clean-energy manufacturing employment grew 9.5% versus 1.8% for broader manufacturing. The report's claim is not that standards remove trade-offs; it is that community benefits plans and labor partnerships can turn workforce/community risk into a project-development discipline. That matters for industrial-policy design because it points to administrative capacity and policy stability, not necessarily deregulation, as the binding implementation variable.

For the investing lens, this slightly changes the clean-energy industrial-policy read-through. If the constraint is weak execution capacity, permitting, contracting, and supply-chain depth, then the beneficiaries are not only manufacturers receiving credits; they include firms and localities that can navigate federal compliance, secure labor supply, pre-negotiate community support, and keep projects bankable through policy volatility. [[One Big Beautiful Bill Act]] rollbacks still hurt the IRA demand signal, but the operational lesson survives: standards can either be bureaucratic drag or execution-risk management depending on administrative design and project sponsor capability.

*Sources: [Volts, "A limited defense of Biden's everything-bagel industrial policy"](https://www.volts.wtf/p/a-limited-defense-of-bidens-everything), interview with Betony Jones, May 27 2026; [Roosevelt Institute, "The Receipts: The Untold and Underappreciated Outcomes of Biden's Clean Energy Strategy"](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/publications/the-receipts-the-untold-and-underappreciated-outcomes-of-bidens-clean-energy-strategy/), Betony Jones and Joe Peck, Apr 16 2026.*

### Arizona abundance playbook

[[ChinaTalk]]'s May 28 2026 interview with Ian O'Grady, senior policy adviser to Arizona Governor Katie Hobbs, turns the CHIPS Act from a federal subsidy story into a state-capacity story. The useful frame is mundane execution: permits, power, people, construction workforce, apprenticeships, local schools, water allocation, and mediation among companies, contractors, unions, community colleges, utilities, and federal agencies.

Arizona's edge is not only that [[TSMC]], [[Intel]], and [[LG Energy Solution]] chose the state. It is that the state has built a quasi-public [[Arizona Commerce Authority]] as a quarterbacking institution, kept a long-serving economic-development leadership layer, and cultivated an engineering-first culture around aerospace, semiconductors, defense, water management, and growth politics. O'Grady's argument is effectively that abundance is an institutional operating model: fewer veto points, more technical coordination, and a local political story that makes industrial construction socially legible.

The official numbers support the scale of the claim. Arizona Commerce Authority says the state has won more than 60 semiconductor expansions since 2020, representing more than $210B in capital investment. NIST's CHIPS Program Office describes [[TSMC Arizona expansion|TSMC Arizona]] as a $65B-plus three-fab leading-edge cluster with up to $6.6B in direct federal funding, $5B in available loans, about 6,000 direct manufacturing jobs, and over 20,000 accumulated construction jobs. That scale means the bottleneck shifts from "can Washington allocate money?" to "can a state assemble workforce, permitting, infrastructure, housing, water and political consent fast enough for federal industrial policy to become real capacity?"

The market read-through is specific. If industrial policy is capacity policy, the advantaged geographies are not just low-tax states. They are places with permitting competence, utility coordination, workforce institutions, trade-school credibility, regional suppliers, and a narrative that lets communities accept construction externalities. Arizona's water politics are the live stress test: O'Grady frames Colorado River allocation in national-priority terms, arguing that no basin state converts water into advanced chips, guided missiles, and leafy greens as efficiently as Arizona. That is an industrial-policy claim disguised as a water-rights claim.

*Sources: [[ChinaTalk]], "Arizona's Abundance Playbook," May 28 2026; Arizona Commerce Authority, "Semiconductor Advantages," accessed May 30 2026; NIST / CHIPS for America, "TSMC Arizona," accessed May 30 2026.*

### Policy tooling and the cost of policy entrepreneurship

[[ChinaTalk]]'s May 29 2026 "Adventures in Vibecoding Policy" is important less for any individual proposal than for what it reveals about the falling cost of policy entrepreneurship. Schneider used [[Claude Code]] and other coding agents to turn policy kernels around green-card procedure, Chinese biotech licensing, Chinese EV border enforcement, and a revived [[Office of Technology Assessment]] into microsites, draft arguments, rough legislative/regulatory language, and devil's-advocate pages.

The narrow lesson is that AI agents can now compress the first-pass policy-research stack: issue framing, stakeholder-specific messaging, web presentation, counterargument generation, and rough legal-text ideation. The broader industrial-policy lesson is more uncomfortable: if cheap agents make policy advocacy cheaper, the scarce asset moves from memo production to taste, fact-checking, human networks, and in-person persuasion. That favours operators with domain judgment and political access, while also lowering the barrier for sloppy or manipulative policy campaigns.

For this note, the connection is direct. Industrial policy is no longer only about factories and subsidies; it is also about the policy-production function that selects, advocates, defends, and iterates those interventions. AI can make the policy pipeline faster, but it does not solve the two hardest problems: whether the mechanism actually works in the real economy, and whether the political coalition can carry it through implementation.

*Source: [[ChinaTalk]], "Adventures in Vibecoding Policy," May 29 2026.*

### China

China's industrial policy is the most ambitious in absolute scale and most integrated across instruments:

| Framework | Year | Focus |
|-----------|------|-------|
| [[Made in China 2025]] | 2015 | 10 high-tech industries; 70% domestic content target |
| 14th Five Year Plan | 2021-2025 | Tech self-sufficiency, decarbonisation, dual circulation |
| [[Common Prosperity]] | 2021- | Inequality + redistribution; reframed 2026 |
| [[Dual circulation]] | 2020- | Domestic-engine + selective external openness |
| [[Fortress China]] | 2021- (descriptive) | Defensive economic security across energy + minerals + tech |
| [[China semiconductor strategy]] | 2014- | Big Fund I-III, ~$100B+ committed |
| Military-civil fusion | 2015- | Defense + dual-use technology development |

China's industrial-policy budget and execution scale dwarf any Western counterpart. The Chinese Big Fund III alone (~$47B, 2024) is comparable to the entire US CHIPS Act subsidy pot. The integration is also tighter: China's SOE network, state-directed banks, and party-controlled allocation surface allow a level of coordination that no Western system can match.

### European Union

The EU response has been slower and more fragmented but is now substantial:

- Green Deal Industrial Plan (Feb 2023) — EU response to IRA
- Net-Zero Industry Act (March 2024) — 40% domestic clean-tech capacity target
- Critical Raw Materials Act (March 2024)
- European Chips Act (Sep 2023) — €43B target
- Sovereign technology investments by individual member states (France's [[BPI France]], Germany's KfW)

The EU's challenge is fiscal capacity: EU-level industrial policy is constrained by treaty rules on state aid, and member-state fiscal capacity is highly heterogeneous.

The urgency framing behind EU industrial policy depends partly on the perceived US-vs-EU productivity-growth gap; [[Paul Krugman]]'s May 2026 [[US-Europe productivity paradox]] framework argues that this productivity-growth comparison is methodologically unstable (constant-price methodology shows EU decline; PPP shows no trend). If the methodology challenge holds, the urgency case for matching US scale weakens — though the technology-substitution and strategic-autonomy cases remain (inference — Krugman's framework is methodological, not a defence of EU industrial-policy approach).

## Synthesis

The single most useful structural read of the 2020s industrial-policy revival is its bipartisan and trans-Atlantic durability (inference). The CHIPS Act has survived three changes of administration (Trump I → Biden → Trump II) and continues to expand. The EU has converged toward US-style sectoral support. India, Japan, Korea, and the UK have all implemented analogous frameworks. The pattern is consistent enough that "industrial policy is back" should be treated as a structural condition of the 2020s policy environment, not a cyclical phase that will reverse with the next election.

The second structural read is the convergence on a specific *kind* of industrial policy: sector-targeted (vertical) interventions in domains classified as strategic (semiconductors, critical minerals, clean energy, defense supply chains, AI infrastructure), administered through a mix of direct subsidy + production tax credit + procurement + tariff + R&D, with explicit national-security justification. This convergence is consistent across democratic and authoritarian regimes — China, the US, the EU, Korea, Japan, and India have all adopted variants of the same basic recipe.

The May 2026 Substack Theme 4 synthesis sharpens the implementation question: the binding asset is no longer the policy idea, but the operating institution that turns the idea into capacity. Arizona's abundance playbook, AI-generated policy microsites, Noah Smith's liberal-nationalism frame, and China's global-governance caucus all point to the same layer. Ideas are cheap; execution institutions are the asset. For investors, that means the investable edge sits in places, companies, agencies, and coalitions that can assemble permits, power, labor, supplier depth, political legitimacy, technical review, and rule-writing forums faster than peers.

The third structural read is that the convergence creates a coordination problem that no individual country can solve alone (inference — the analysis of this is mine, not yet articulated in mainstream commentary I've found). If every major economy subsidises semiconductors, the result is overcapacity and rent-extraction by the firms receiving the subsidies. If every major economy subsidises clean-energy manufacturing, the result is overcapacity that already manifested in 2024-2026 in solar and EV battery pricing. The coordination problem is structurally similar to the 1930s competitive devaluations — and the 1944 Bretton Woods response was an explicit attempt to solve a coordination problem of similar shape. No comparable contemporary framework exists for industrial-policy coordination. This is a structurally significant gap.

The fourth structural read is the fiscal-political ceiling. Industrial policy is fiscally expensive — the US has committed ~$420B across CHIPS + IRA + DPA expansions, China's Big Fund III adds ~$47B, the EU's announced programmes commit hundreds of billions, India's PLI tranches add ~$24B. Whether the political coalitions sustaining industrial policy survive a fiscal-stress scenario (sovereign debt crisis, rates shock, recession) is the durability test that hasn't been run yet (inference).

| Clean energy manufacturing | Mixed | IRA tax credits create demand; Chinese overcapacity compresses pricing |
| EV manufacturing | Mixed | IRA + tariffs vs Chinese EV competitiveness; sector economics still developing |
| Defense primes | Beneficiary | DPA expansion + budget priorities; [[Lockheed Martin]], [[RTX]], [[Northrop Grumman]] |
| Industrial automation + robotics | Beneficiary | Reshoring demand creates capex; [[ABB]], [[Rockwell Automation]], Japanese automation cohort |
| Steel, aluminum (basic metals) | Beneficiary | Tariffs preserve domestic capacity; less margin upside |
| Globalised-supply-chain firms | Risk | Reshoring increases costs; firms that built China-centric supply chains face structural margin pressure |
| Low-wage offshoring beneficiaries | Risk | Mexico (USMCA-favoured) + Vietnam less hit than China + originally-China supply chains |
| Sovereign bonds (US) | Risk | Industrial-policy fiscal cost adds to deficit; rates sensitivity |
| Domestic banks (regional, project-finance) | Beneficiary | Industrial-policy projects generate financing demand |

The two-track risk for foreign-policy reasons: (1) industrial policy invites retaliation (China-EU brandy case 2024-2025; China-EU EV tariff case 2024); (2) the subsidies themselves create overcapacity in subsidised sectors that compresses margins for all participants. Solar manufacturing is the canonical 2024-2026 case of subsidy-driven overcapacity destroying sector economics.

## Limits and open questions

(All marked as inference except where sourced.)

1. Coordination failure risk: If every major economy subsidises the same strategic sectors, the result is overcapacity. The 2024-2026 solar + EV battery + recently semiconductor memory pricing dynamics illustrate the risk. No coordination framework exists
2. Fiscal-political durability: The bipartisan coalition sustaining US industrial policy has not yet faced a fiscal-stress test. A sovereign-debt or rate-shock scenario would test whether the policy survives or gets cut
3. China response asymmetry: Chinese industrial-policy execution is faster and more integrated than the Western counterpart. A "race" framing implicitly assumes Western policy can match Chinese execution speed, which is unproven
4. Selection mechanism: The US industrial-policy programmes have not yet developed strong performance-metric enforcement on subsidised firms (vs the East Asian developmental-state model of export discipline). Without performance metrics, rent-seeking dominates
5. WTO/trade-architecture incompatibility: The WTO rules constrain explicit industrial subsidies. Either WTO becomes irrelevant, or industrial policy operates in legal grey zones, or the rules get rewritten. Each is structurally significant
6. Climate vs strategic tension: Some climate-policy goals (allowing cheap Chinese solar imports for fastest decarbonisation) conflict with industrial-policy goals (build domestic clean-energy manufacturing). The trade-off is unresolved
7. AI-infrastructure as next domain: The 2026 [[AI infrastructure financing risk|AI capex cycle]] is arguably the next major industrial-policy domain. The [[CHIPS Act]] indirectly funds AI (via semiconductor supply); whether a dedicated AI-industrial-policy bill emerges is an open question for 2026-2028
