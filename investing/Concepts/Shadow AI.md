# Shadow AI

Shadow AI is employee or business-unit use of AI tools, [[AI agents]], or AI-enabled [[SaaS]] outside formal enterprise approval, monitoring, identity control, or data-governance policy. It is the AI-era version of shadow IT, but the blast radius is larger because tools can ingest confidential data, receive credentials, connect to [[SaaS]] systems, and act across workflows.

---

## Synthesis

The May 2026 [[Okta]] survey turns shadow AI from a policy nuisance into a security and identity-management investment theme. Executives reported high confidence in visibility and responsible use, while knowledge workers admitted broad use of unapproved tools and routine sharing of sensitive data. The gap is not primarily malicious behavior. Workers use the tools because personal accounts are easier, team norms normalize them, approval processes are slow, and approved tools often do not meet the task.

That matters because enterprise AI adoption is moving faster than enterprise AI governance. If workers and teams already rely on unsanctioned tools, the control surface shifts from "which AI vendor wins?" to "who discovers, governs, and secures the AI identities and data flows already in use?" This is favorable to identity vendors such as [[Okta]], security-control vendors, browser/data-loss-prevention tools, and platforms that make the approved path easier than the shadow path.

The key read-through for the vault is not "ban AI." Strict bans can push usage further underground. The investable problem is discovery plus friction removal: organizations need inventories of AI tools and agents, identity controls for non-human actors, data-loss controls at the browser/app layer, and sanctioned AI workflows that are usable enough to displace personal accounts.

## Okta May 2026 survey

| Finding | Data point |
|---------|------------|
| Executive confidence | 90% confident in visibility into AI tools; 95% confident employees use AI responsibly |
| Shadow usage | 52% of employees use AI tools without approval |
| Incidents | 58% of executives reported an AI-related security incident or close call in the prior year |
| Data sharing | Among workers using unapproved tools: 54% share internal messages/emails; 45% HR-related information; 39% confidential company documents |
| Credentials | More than 20% of unapproved-tool users share login credentials/passwords; 28% share banking/payment information |
| Policy gap | 65% of executives say AI policies are very clear; only 43% of knowledge workers agree |
| Controls gap | Only 34% of organizations always apply the same controls to AI agents/digital labor as to human workers |
| Geography | US workers had the highest shadow-AI usage at 67%; [[Australia]] 60%; [[UK]] 55%; [[Canada]] 50% |
