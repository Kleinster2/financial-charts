# US Energy Independence Six Countries

**US Energy Independence Six Countries** is a framework articulated by [[Ed Morse]] ([[Hartree Partners]]) during a [[Council on Foreign Relations|CFR]] panel on March 17, 2026, arguing that the slogan "energy independence" obscures the fact that the United States functions as **six distinct energy economies**, not one integrated system.

## Synopsis

The phrase "energy independence" has been central to US political rhetoric since the [[shale revolution]] made America the world's largest oil producer and a net petroleum exporter. But the 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure exposed a structural reality: the US is not one energy market. It's six. Alaska exports. The Gulf Coast exports. The East Coast and West Coast import — and even lifting the [[Jones Act]] wouldn't change the economics, because it has failed every time the government has tried it except for small emergencies. The Canadian border is a two-way energy corridor. And a small landlocked part of the Pacific Northwest is genuinely self-sufficient. This means that when a global supply shock hits, the coasts experience it like any other net importing region — regardless of what's happening in the Permian Basin. The framework demolishes the assumption that "largest producer" = "immune to shocks" and explains why US gas prices, jet fuel costs, and diesel spreads respond to Middle Eastern disruptions even when aggregate national production exceeds consumption.

## The six economies

| Region | Energy role | Global integration |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Alaska** | Exporter | Sells crude to Asia-Pacific markets |
| **US Gulf Coast** | Oil & gas exporter | LNG terminals, refinery complex, exports to global market |
| **East Coast** | Net importer | Depends on waterborne crude/product imports |
| **West Coast** | Net importer | Same — cannot be economically served by Gulf Coast even without Jones Act |
| **Canadian border** | Two-way flow | US exports gas north; Canada exports heavy crude south (Alberta → Midwest refineries) |
| **Pacific Northwest interior** | Self-sufficient | Small, landlocked, energy-balanced |

## Why it matters for markets

The "six countries" framing has three investment implications:

1. **Price transmission is regional, not national.** East and West Coast consumers face global commodity pricing regardless of Permian or Gulf Coast output. A [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure raises prices in Boston and Los Angeles as if they were net importing countries — because they are.

2. **Export restrictions are politically tempting but economically destructive.** [[Ed Morse]] warned during the panel that emergency-driven export controls (crude export bans, LNG restrictions) would be "gumming up the works" if implemented hastily. The structural mismatch — Gulf Coast has surplus, coasts have deficit — means restrictions don't solve the problem and create new distortions.

3. **The Jones Act is a binding constraint.** Even if lifted, Morse argued, the economics of shipping crude from the Gulf Coast to the East Coast by sea don't work. The coasts will remain tied to global seaborne markets. This makes the US permanently more exposed to maritime chokepoint risk than the aggregate production numbers suggest.

## Context: the 2026 Hormuz test

The March 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure was the first real stress test of US "energy independence" under conditions of a genuine global supply disruption (~10M bbl/day lost). The result: WTI crude surged from ~$65 to $98+ in two weeks, US gas prices jumped $0.20/gallon in days, and jet fuel/diesel spreads blew out — despite the US being the world's largest producer and a net exporter.

[[Megan O'Sullivan]] ([[Belfer Center]], [[Harvard]]) added a complementary point on the same panel: energy independence never meant energy [[autarky]]. It meant the US was a net exporter in aggregate — but because oil is priced on the global market, Americans still experience every global supply shock. The Trump administration's decision to strike [[Iran]] may have been partially predicated on the assumption that US energy dominance provided insulation. It didn't.
