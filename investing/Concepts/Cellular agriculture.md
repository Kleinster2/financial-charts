#concept #foodtech #biotech #agriculture

Cellular agriculture — production of animal or plant food products from cell cultures rather than from whole organisms. Two main technical routes: cell culture (grow cells from a starter sample in bioreactors, typical for cultivated meat, cacao, coffee) and precision fermentation (engineer microbes to secrete a target molecule such as a protein or fat). A third, adjacent category — ingredient substitution — doesn't use cells at all, but competes for the same end-product market by reformulating from unrelated biomass.

Collectively the category is below 1% of its target end-markets in April 2026, with most funding concentrated in cultivated meat, cacao, coffee, and dairy proteins.

---

## Why it matters now

The combination of the 2024-25 [[Cocoa]] price spike (bean prices to $12,000/tonne; cocoa butter over €40,000/tonne), pressure on coffee supply, and the 2022 global fertilizer shock has shifted corporate interest from plant-based fake meat (a consumer-acceptance problem) toward ingredient-level substitution of commodity inputs that are supply-constrained (a procurement problem). The buyer for cultivated cacao butter is not the consumer — it's the [[Mondelez]] or [[Lindt Spruengli]] procurement team deciding whether to diversify West [[Africa]] exposure.

This changes the economics. Consumers will not pay a premium for lab chocolate; manufacturers may pay a premium for supply security.

## Technology stack

| Layer | Description | Actors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Starter biology | Extract cells from source organism; immortalize or select high-producing lines | Varies by target |
| Bioreactor / scale-up | Feed cells sugars, nutrients, vitamins; maintain sterility at commercial volumes | [[Pluri]] (3D placental platform), specialist CDMOs |
| Downstream processing | Separate target molecule (fat, protein, pigment) from culture media | Shared with [[Biopharma]] techniques |
| Regulatory | FDA (US), Health Ministry (Israel), EFSA (EU), FSA (UK) | Timelines differ sharply — US/Israel fastest, EU slowest |

Cultivated cacao is technically easier than cultivated meat — plant cells don't require growth factors derived from animal serum, don't need vascular structure, and the target product is a fat/compound mixture not a tissue.

## The "moonshot" critique

Raphaël Felenbok (independent adviser to [[Win-Win]]; FT, April 2026) argues cellular agriculture is less scalable than ingredient substitution because of:

- Energy intensity of sterile bioreactor operation
- Capital cost of build-out at ~50,000 tonne annual scale
- Regulatory hurdles requiring safety dossiers in each jurisdiction
- Cost base that requires a commodity supply crisis to be price-competitive

The counter-view from [[Celleste Bio]] CEO [[Michal Beressi Golomb]]: cell-cultured cocoa butter is a genuine drop-in replacement — chocolate manufacturers don't need to reformulate recipes or change processing equipment. The target customer is procurement, not the consumer, so parity with the 2024 cocoa butter price (€5,000-€40,000+ range) matters more than parity with a hypothetical post-climate-shock floor.

## Strategic-backer pattern

The April 2026 map shows three confectionery majors each backing a cell-based cocoa startup as a supply-diversification hedge:

| Backer | Partner | Angle |
|--------|---------|-------|
| [[Mondelez]] | [[Celleste Bio]] | Equity; Cadbury Bournville factory as production site |
| [[Cargill]] | [[Kokomodo]] + [[Voyage Foods]] | Development + distribution across two technology paths |
| [[Lindt Spruengli]] | [[Food Brewer]] | Domestic Swiss partner |

No single backer has bet on only one technology. [[Cargill]] in particular sits across both cell culture (Kokomodo) and ingredient substitution (Voyage Foods), a hedge against uncertain which route scales first.

## Open questions

- Scale-up cost curves — do bioreactor economics bend at 50,000 tonnes/year the way [[Pluri]]/Kokomodo project, or do they look more like the cultivated-meat cohort (stubbornly high)?
- Regulatory geography — if US/Israel approve in 2027 but EU lags past 2030, do chocolate manufacturers build dual supply chains or wait?
- Labeling — can a bar containing cell-cultured cocoa butter be marketed as "chocolate"? Parallel question to the "plant-based milk" labeling disputes of the 2010s.
- Energy source — sustainability claims depend on renewable electricity and water recycling; sceptics argue current pilots run on grid power with unimpressive LCA numbers.
