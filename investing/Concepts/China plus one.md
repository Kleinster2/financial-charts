**China plus one** is a supply chain diversification strategy in which multinational companies maintain their [[China]] manufacturing base but add at least one alternative production location to reduce concentration risk. The strategy gained momentum after the US-China trade war (2018-present), COVID-19 supply chain disruptions (2020-2022), and escalating geopolitical tension around [[Taiwan]].

The logic is pragmatic rather than ideological: China's manufacturing ecosystem (supplier density, infrastructure, skilled labor, scale) remains unmatched for most product categories, so full decoupling is prohibitively expensive and slow. Instead, companies hedge by building parallel capacity in lower-cost or geopolitically safer locations. The primary beneficiaries are [[Vietnam]] (electronics, textiles, footwear), [[India]] (electronics, pharmaceuticals, chemicals), [[Mexico]] (automotive, nearshoring for US market), and [[Indonesia]] (metals processing, textiles). [[JPMorgan Chase|JPMorgan]]'s Asia equity team has identified India as the largest structural beneficiary.

The strategy is easier to articulate than execute. Vietnam's total manufacturing workforce (~18M) is a fraction of China's (~200M+). India's infrastructure gaps (ports, power, roads) remain real bottlenecks. Mexico faces its own political risk under AMLO/Sheinbaum and water scarcity in northern industrial zones. The result is that China plus one has been more incremental diversification than wholesale shift — new capacity gets built outside China, but existing Chinese production rarely gets moved.

For investors, the key sectors to watch are electronics assembly ([[Apple]] supply chain shifting to India/Vietnam), semiconductors (back-end packaging moving to Malaysia/Vietnam), automotive (Mexico nearshoring), and textiles/footwear (already well advanced to Vietnam/Bangladesh).
