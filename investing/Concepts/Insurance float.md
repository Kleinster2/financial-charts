Insurance float is the pool of premiums collected by an insurer before claims are paid out, creating a window during which that capital can be invested. The concept is mechanically simple — policyholders pay upfront, claims arrive later — but the strategic implications are profound. When underwriting is disciplined enough to break even or profit, the float becomes free leverage: investable capital at zero or negative cost.

---

## Synopsis

The economics of float depend on two variables: size and cost. Size is a function of premium volume and claim duration (long-tail lines like reinsurance and workers' comp generate float that persists for years). Cost is determined by underwriting quality — if combined ratios stay below 100%, the insurer is effectively being paid to hold other people's money. [[Berkshire Hathaway]] has turned this into the most durable structural advantage in American finance: $171B in float as of Q4 2024, grown from $39M in 1970, with an average cost below zero over the full period. [[Warren Buffett]] has described float as the "engine" behind Berkshire's compounding — it functions as permanent, non-callable leverage that doesn't dilute shareholders.

What makes Berkshire's float exceptional isn't the amount (large reinsurers like [[Munich Re]] and [[Swiss Re]] also carry massive float pools) but the consistency of its sub-100 combined ratio across decades. [[GEICO]], [[General Re]], and the Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance Group each contribute distinct float profiles: GEICO's is high-turnover auto (short duration, high volume), General Re's is long-tail commercial and specialty, and BHRG's is catastrophe-exposed with extreme duration. The blended portfolio means Berkshire rarely faces a liquidity crunch from synchronized claims. For investors, float is the key to understanding why Berkshire's returns exceed what Buffett's stock-picking alone would produce — the [[Buffett's Alpha]] paper estimated his effective leverage at ~1.6x, almost entirely sourced from insurance float rather than debt.

## How float creates leverage

A simplified model: if an insurer collects $100B in premiums and pays $95B in claims and expenses (combined ratio 95%), it has $100B to invest and $5B in underwriting profit. The investment income on that $100B is pure upside — the capital costs negative 5%. Compare this to a fund manager who borrows $100B at 5% — the insurer's cost advantage is 10 percentage points annually. Over decades, this compounds into an insurmountable gap.

The catch: float is only an advantage if underwriting discipline holds. Insurers that chase premium growth by underpricing risk (as [[AIG]] did with credit default swaps pre-2008) transform float from an asset into a liability. The float still exists, but it costs more than investment returns can cover. This is why Buffett has repeatedly emphasized that Berkshire will "never sacrifice underwriting discipline for premium volume" — the quality of float matters more than the quantity.
