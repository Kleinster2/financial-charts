# Foreign direct investment

Foreign direct investment is cross-border investment establishing a lasting interest in (and meaningful control over) a foreign enterprise — greenfield plants, acquisitions, equity stakes above the portfolio threshold — as distinct from portfolio flows. It is the capital-flow channel most sensitive to [[Geoeconomic fragmentation|geopolitical alignment]]: [[IMF]] work (Aiyar, Malacrino, Presbitero) finds that greater geopolitical distance, or membership in an opposing bloc, sharply reduces bilateral FDI, and that FDI in strategic sectors is increasingly routed to aligned partners — the investable face of "friend-shoring."

This is the mechanism behind the [[Nearshoring thesis]]: as efficiency-maximizing FDI gives way to security-maximizing FDI, the beneficiaries are the geopolitically aligned recipients ([[Mexico]], [[India]], [[ASEAN]]) rather than the lowest-cost ones.
