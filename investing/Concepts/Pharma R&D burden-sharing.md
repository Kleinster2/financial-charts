# Pharma R&D burden-sharing

**Pharma R&D burden-sharing** — the structural problem that the [[United States|US]] finances a disproportionate share of global pharmaceutical research and development because other wealthy countries use price controls and reference-pricing regimes to pay less for the same innovative drugs, and the US policy toolkit ([[Most Favored Nation drug pricing|MFN pricing]], [[Section 301]], [[Section 232 semiconductor tariff|Section 232]] pharma tariffs, bilateral pricing deals) used to force trading partners to absorb a larger share of R&D costs. The recurring framing — US officials call it "ending global freeloading"; health economists call it the "free-rider" problem; the affected governments call reimbursement policy a matter of national jurisdiction.

## Synthesis

A patented drug's price has to recover a fixed global R&D cost (most of which is the cost of the failed molecules, not the one that works) plus a marginal manufacturing cost close to zero. When one large market negotiates prices down toward marginal cost through health-technology assessment and reference pricing, the manufacturer recovers the fixed cost from whichever market is inelastic enough to pay list. That market is the [[United States|US]], which sets few manufacturer price controls and where payers rebates are negotiated but list prices stay high. The result is a persistent, large gap between US prices and prices in every other wealthy market — and an aggrieved US policy posture that the gap is a hidden foreign tax on American patients.

The [[Trump II]] administration made closing that gap a central trade-policy objective, moving drug pricing out of health policy and into trade enforcement. The instruments are the same ones used for steel, semiconductors, and autos: [[Section 301]] investigations, [[Section 232 semiconductor tariff|Section 232]] national-security tariffs, and bilateral deals that exchange tariff exemptions for higher foreign prices. The June 2026 [[US Section 301 investigation of Germany drug pricing (2026)|Section 301 investigation of Germany]] is the clearest expression of the doctrine — using trade law to challenge an ally's domestic health-reimbursement policy.

The structural tension is durable regardless of administration. Pharmaceutical R&D is a global public good with local payment; the burden-sharing fight is the unavoidable consequence of pricing a global fixed cost into nationally administered reimbursement systems.

## The price disparity

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| US vs Germany branded-drug price ratio | ~3.9× | USTR Federal Register notice, Jun 2026 |
| US share of global pharma R&D financing | majority and rising | USTR / industry submissions |
| Mechanism of disparity | reference pricing + mandatory rebates + confidentiality discounts | USTR, AMNOG framework |

The 3.9× figure is the load-bearing number in the [[US Section 301 investigation of Germany drug pricing (2026)|Germany investigation]]: USTR frames it as evidence that "higher US prices support and fund global R&D costs... and thereby unfairly shift Germany's fair share of costs for pharmaceutical innovation onto US patients." The disparity is not unique to Germany — NICE (UK), PBAC (Australia), PMPRB (Canada) and similar health-technology-assessment bodies produce comparable gaps — but Germany is the test case for the trade-law route.

| Country / system | Body | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| [[Germany]] | [[AMNOG]] (2011), G-BA / GKV-Spitzenverband | Added-benefit assessment; reference-priced to comparator if no added benefit; negotiated rebate if added benefit |
| [[United Kingdom]] | NICE | Cost-effectiveness (QALY) threshold; NHS negotiated price |

| [[Canada]] | PMPRB | Price ceiling vs international comparator basket |
| Cross-cutting | Confidentiality rebates | Manufacturer pays supplemental discount in exchange for negotiated-price confidentiality |

USTR's Jun 2026 notice specifically names two instruments as the focus of the Germany probe: supplemental discounts in exchange for confidentiality of negotiated prices, and mandatory variable-rate rebates.

## The economics

The free-rider framing is contested.

- US / industry view: Reference-pricing systems free-ride on US-paid R&D. If every country priced toward marginal cost, the fixed-cost recovery collapses and the pipeline of innovative drugs shrinks. Forcing burden-sharing preserves innovation incentives.
- Health-economics / foreign-government view: Price differences partly reflect differences in per-capita income and willingness-to-pay (standard international pricing), not pure free-riding. Reference pricing is a legitimate monopsony-tool for cost-containing national health systems. US list prices are inflated by the rebate-and-PBM structure unique to the US system, not by foreign underpayment alone.
- Innovation-tradeoff: [[Robert F. Kennedy Jr.|HHS Secretary Kennedy]]'s framing ("fair reimbursement strengthens medical innovation") is the industry-aligned read. Critics (and several pharma CEOs) counter that tying US prices to foreign reference prices risks reducing the return that funds the pipeline.

The empirical literature (Danzon and successors) finds elements of both: some differential is Ramsey-style income-based pricing, some is genuine under-payment by high-income countries that could afford more. The policy argument is over the mix.

## US policy toolkit

| Instrument | Authority | How it works on drug pricing | Status (mid-2026) |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Most Favored Nation drug pricing\|MFN pricing]] | Executive / CMS | Ties US prices to lower foreign reference prices | In effect; 17 largest pharma cos signed voluntary MFN price cuts for tariff exemptions |
| [[Section 301]] | Trade Act 1974 | Investigate foreign "underpayment" as unreasonable/discriminatory → tariffs | [[US Section 301 investigation of Germany drug pricing (2026)\|Germany probe]] opened Jun 18 2026 |
| [[Section 232 semiconductor tariff\|Section 232]] | Trade Expansion Act 1962 | National-security tariffs on imported patented drugs/ingredients | 100% tariff announced Apr 2026; effective Jul 31 (17 majors) / Sep 29 (others) |
| Bilateral pricing deals | Executive | Tariff exemption in exchange for higher foreign NHS-equivalent prices | [[US-UK pharma pricing deal (2026)\|US-UK deal]] (Apr 2 2026) — UK pays more via NHS, no pharma tariffs Jan 1 2026 – Jan 19 2029 |
| IRA Medicare negotiation | Domestic statute | US-side cost containment (complements, doesn't replace, the trade route) | Top-drug Medicare negotiation from 2026 |

The doctrine: combine the domestic lever (IRA Medicare negotiation, MFN reference pricing) with the trade lever (Section 301/232 tariffs + bilateral deals) so that neither side of the disparity is left to the other.

## Why Germany first

Germany is the test case for three reasons: it is a large, wealthy economy that clearly can afford more (not a low-income Ramsey case); its [[AMNOG]] system is the most-studied reference-pricing regime; and the Merz government's 2026 cost-containment bill (additional mandatory rebates on patented drugs) gave USTR a discrete legislative trigger. The [[US-UK pharma pricing deal (2026)|UK precedent]] — bilateral negotiation, no tariffs, higher NHS prices — is the template the US is offering Germany. The German position (Chancellor [[Friedrich Merz|Merz]]): reimbursement is a national jurisdiction matter, and the US should operate under the existing EU trade agreement.
