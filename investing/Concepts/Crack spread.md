# Crack spread

The margin between crude oil input cost and refined product output prices - the refinery's gross profit per barrel. "Crack" refers to the catalytic cracking process that breaks long hydrocarbon chains into lighter products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, heating oil).

---

## How it works

The standard measure is the 3-2-1 crack spread: the cost of two barrels of gasoline plus one barrel of diesel minus three barrels of crude oil input, divided by three. It approximates a typical US refinery's product slate (~2:1 gasoline-to-distillate ratio).

Refineries don't consume crude oil the way end-users consume gasoline. They are processing intermediaries. This means crude oil price moves and refined product price moves can diverge - sometimes sharply - depending on refinery utilization, crude grade availability, and downstream demand patterns.

---

## 2026 Iran conflict - refinery margin explosion

The [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis|Strait of Hormuz closure]] produced a textbook crack spread blowout. [[Spencer Dale]] (visiting professor, [[LSE]], formerly [[BP]] chief economist), speaking at a [[NIESR]] webinar (early April 2026), noted that normal people do not consume crude oil - only refineries buy it. The crude price therefore understates the consumer-facing shock.

![[refined-fuel-price-changes-iran-war-2026.png]]
*Refined fuel prices have risen faster than crude oil or food. Price change since Iran war (%). Crack spread +~150%, EU diesel and jet fuel +100%+, crude oil +~60%. Source: FactSet, FT calculations. Price change between February 17 and April 2, 2026.*

| Metric | Pre-war | April 2026 | Change |
|--------|---------|-----------|--------|
| 3-2-1 crack spread (gasoline+diesel vs crude) | 23% premium | 56% premium | +33pp |
| EU wholesale diesel | - | ~$150/bbl equivalent | - |
| Spot crude oil | - | +~60% | - |
| European natural gas | - | +~60% | - |
| US natural gas | - | Minimal change | Structurally disconnected |

Three drivers of the margin blowout:

1. Physical shortage concerns: diesel and jet fuel inventories drew down faster than crude, reflecting the downstream concentration of [[Strait of Hormuz]] flows (not just crude but refined products transited the strait - [[Kuwait]] became a major European refined product supplier post-2022)
2. Refinery efficiency loss: facilities configured for specific Gulf sour crude grades cannot easily switch to non-Middle East alternatives, reducing throughput per barrel of input. This crude-grade mismatch was flagged earlier by Reynolds ([[IEEFA]]) and [[Amrita Sen]] ([[Energy Aspects]]) - Asian refineries face the worst mismatch, with Middle East benchmarks at $150-160/bbl vs Brent's headline ~$110
3. Jet fuel premium: the [[Iran conflict airline disruption|airline disruption]] combined with Asian jet fuel +80% to create acute aviation fuel shortages

The implication: if the oil supply shock is roughly half of [[Covid-19]] in quantity terms (per Chris Giles, FT), the spot crude price does not yet reflect the severity - but the refined product cost is getting there.

## Apr 17 reopening signal - crude reset faster than products

The Apr 17 [[Strait of Hormuz]] reopening headline compressed paper oil and refiner equities immediately, but it did not clear the refined-product bottleneck at the same speed. That distinction matters because crack spreads are driven by product availability, not just crude direction.

Reuters' April 17 reporting showed Gulf jet-fuel exports from [[Saudi Arabia]], [[Qatar]], the [[United Arab Emirates]], [[Kuwait]], [[Bahrain]], and [[Oman]] had collapsed from **19.6M barrels in February** to just 4.1M barrels across March and April-to-date combined. In other words, the middle-distillate system still looked broken even as [[Brent crude|Brent]] dropped back below $90.

That creates the classic post-shock setup: headline crude relief can compress the panic premium, but crack spreads stay elevated versus normal as long as damaged refineries, crude-grade mismatch, and missing jet/diesel exports keep product balances tight. The market can be right on oil direction and still too early on margin normalization.

See [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] for the full Apr 17 transmission-channel readthrough and [[Jet fuel]] for the aviation-fuel bottleneck that kept the distillate complex tight.

## Historical context

| Period | Crack spread behavior | Driver |
|--------|----------------------|--------|
| Normal | ~$15-25/bbl (3-2-1) | Competitive refining, adequate crude supply |
| Hurricane season | Temporary spike | Gulf Coast refinery shutdowns |
| 2022 Russia-Ukraine | $50+/bbl (record at the time) | Russian diesel/product sanctions, European shortage |
| 2026 Iran/Hormuz | 56% premium (vs 23% pre-war) | Physical shortage + crude grade mismatch + jet fuel crisis |
