#concept #demographics #geography

**Combined Statistical Area** — an [[OMB]]-defined grouping of adjacent [[Metropolitan Statistical Area|MSAs]] (and sometimes micropolitan areas) that share significant commuting ties with each other. CSAs capture the broader economic mega-region that a cluster of metros functions as in practice — labor sheds, housing markets, and supply chains that cross MSA boundaries.

---

## Synthesis

CSAs are the right unit of analysis for any US housing, labor, or consumer thesis. Most federal data reports at the MSA level, but the real economic geography — where people commute, where housing supply competes, where labor pools overlap — operates at CSA scale. The top 10 CSAs account for ~35% of GDP, and post-COVID migration patterns only make sense at this level (people "leaving" San Francisco often just moved to a cheaper MSA within the same Bay Area CSA). For real estate positioning, the MSA is the reporting unit but the CSA is the competitive unit.

---

## Mechanics

The [[Office of Management and Budget]] defines CSAs using a 15% employment interchange threshold: if 15%+ of workers in one CBSA commute to an adjacent CBSA, they get combined into a single CSA. This is looser than the 25% threshold used to define individual MSAs around a core city.

There are 181 CSAs in the US (vs ~384 MSAs) as of 2024. Not every MSA belongs to a CSA — isolated metros like Honolulu stand alone.

## MSA vs CSA

| | [[Metropolitan Statistical Area|MSA]] | CSA |
|---|---|---|
| Unit | Single metro area around one core | Cluster of linked MSAs |
| Commuting threshold | 25% to core county | 15% between adjacent CBSAs |
| Count (US) | ~384 | 181 |
| Primary use | Federal economic reporting (BLS, Census) | Regional planning, housing market analysis |
| Population range | 50K to 20M+ | Up to 23M+ (NY-Newark CSA) |

## Why it matters for investing

Housing and real estate — home prices, rents, and construction activity are better analyzed at the CSA level because commuters arbitrage housing costs across MSA lines. The DC-Baltimore CSA functions as one housing market even though the MSAs have different employers and demographics. [[REITs]] and homebuilders report by MSA, but the competitive dynamics play out at CSA scale.

Labor markets — [[BLS]] reports employment at the MSA level, but tight labor markets spill across MSA boundaries. The San Jose-San Francisco CSA is one tech labor pool — a hiring freeze in SF affects Sunnyvale job seekers immediately. Wage data at the MSA level can miss this.

Economic concentration — the top 10 CSAs contain ~35% of US GDP. For any thesis on US consumer spending, housing, or commercial real estate, these mega-regions are the unit of analysis that matters.

Demographic shifts — post-COVID migration reshuffled population across MSAs, but CSA-level data shows whether people actually left a mega-region or just moved to a cheaper component MSA (e.g., SF proper to Sacramento, still within the Bay Area economic orbit).
