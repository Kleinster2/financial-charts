**Minsky cycle** — Hyman Minsky's framework: stability itself is destabilizing. Prolonged calm breeds confidence, confidence breeds leverage, leverage breeds fragility, and a small shock triggers collapse. Applied to regulation: distance from the last crisis erodes the political will to maintain safety margins.

---

## Synthesis

The Basel III Endgame (Mar 2026) is a textbook regulatory Minsky moment. Eighteen years after the [[GFC]], [[Michelle Bowman]] announced net *decreases* in bank capital requirements — explicitly arguing that post-2008 rules had "unintended consequences" and were "pushing activity into the less-regulated nonbank sector." The same rules that Bowman acknowledged had "substantially increased bank capital and strengthened financial system resilience" are now being rolled back because the resilience they created made the costs feel unnecessary.

This is the Minsky mechanism applied to regulation itself: the success of post-crisis rules (no major bank failure since 2008, except [[Silicon Valley Bank]] in 2023 which proved the point) becomes the argument for dismantling them. The system looks safe *because* the buffers exist — then the buffers get cut *because* the system looks safe.

The investing vault is tracking several threads that fit the Minsky frame simultaneously: the [[Private credit]] retail liquidity crisis (stability of private credit returns attracted retail money into structures with fundamental liquidity mismatch), the [[Competitive deregulation]] spiral (Basel III loosening triggering European pressure to match), and the broader Trump II deregulatory agenda across AI, energy, crypto, and antitrust.

## The financial cycle

Minsky identified three progressively fragile borrowing types:

| Type | Description | Can service... |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| Hedge finance | Cash flows cover principal + interest | Everything |
| Speculative finance | Cash flows cover interest only; must refinance principal | Interest only |
| Ponzi finance | Cash flows cover neither; depends on asset appreciation to refinance | Nothing — needs rising prices |

During stable periods, the mix shifts from hedge → speculative → Ponzi as lenders and borrowers gain confidence. The trigger doesn't matter — what matters is that the system has become fragile enough that *any* shock is sufficient.

## The regulatory cycle

The financial Minsky cycle has a regulatory parallel:

| Phase | Financial | Regulatory |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| Crisis | Leverage collapses, losses revealed | Public demands accountability |
| Re-regulation | Deleveraging, fire sales | Strict new rules (Glass-Steagall, Dodd-Frank, Basel III) |
| Stability | Buffers absorb shocks, system looks safe | Rules "work" — no crises |
| Complacency | Memory fades, new generation of managers | Lobbying intensifies, "excessive burden" framing |
| Erosion | Risk-taking resumes, new instruments | Rules relaxed, exemptions granted |
| Fragility | Leverage rebuilt in new forms | Safety margins thinned |
| Crisis | Repeat | Repeat |

The history vault documents this pattern across 300 years: South Sea Bubble (1720) → regulation → erosion → railway mania (1840s) → regulation → erosion → 1907 panic → Federal Reserve created (1913) → stability → erosion → 1929 crash → Glass-Steagall (1933) → 50 years of stability → repeal (1999) → 2008 crisis → Dodd-Frank/Basel III → erosion (2025-2026).

The cycle length varies but the pattern holds: regulatory memory has a half-life of roughly one generation (~25-30 years). By the time the people who lived through the crisis have retired, the rules they built feel like unnecessary friction to their successors.

## Current application (2026)

| Thread | Minsky phase | Detail |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| [[Basel III]] Endgame | Erosion | Net decrease in bank capital requirements (Mar 2026). 18 years post-GFC |
| [[Private credit]] retail | Ponzi-adjacent | Illiquid loans sold as "quarterly liquid" — depends on no large redemptions. First gate (Feb 2026) |
| [[Competitive deregulation]] | Complacency → erosion | US loosening forces [[Bank of England]]/[[EU]] to follow |
| Bank re-entry to lending | Speculative | Banks freed from capital constraints re-enter markets they exited for safety reasons |
| [[AI infrastructure financing]] | Speculative → Ponzi | Data center borrowers seeking loans exceeding construction costs, assuming future rents |

The inflation/growth adjustment to the [[G-SIB]] surcharge buffer is particularly telling in Minsky terms: it means the buffer automatically shrinks in real terms as banks grow, which is exactly backwards from the macro-prudential logic that created it. Bigger banks = more systemic risk = should need *more* buffer, not less.
