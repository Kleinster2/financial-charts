# Pharma Consumerization

The strategic shift in which prescription drug manufacturers treat their products like consumer goods — relying on advertising, social-media-driven demand, cash-pay channels, subscription models, and direct-to-consumer (DTC) distribution that bypasses pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) and insurance — rather than the traditional sales-rep-and-formulary model. The pattern is most visible in obesity drugs but is spreading across categories under pressure from US pricing reform, IRA Medicare negotiation, and the success of [[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1]] DTC channels.

> Patients increasingly expect a "consumer-like" healthcare experience and are willing to pay cash for medicines they want. DTC may evolve from a niche experiment into a core commercial strategy, particularly for drugs facing price negotiation, loss of exclusivity, or high rebates ([IQVIA, Aug 2025](https://www.iqvia.com/locations/united-states/blogs/2025/08/why-pharma-is-going-direct-to-consumers)).

---

## What changed

The traditional pharma commercial model: detailing reps call on physicians, PBMs adjudicate formulary tier, insurance covers most of list price minus rebate, patient pays a copay. Demand is *prescriber-pulled*. Manufacturers compete on clinical evidence and rebate negotiation.

The consumerized model: manufacturers advertise directly to patients (broadcast, social, influencer), patients self-identify and request the drug, telehealth platforms or in-house ecommerce fulfill the prescription, and a meaningful fraction of patients pay cash through subscription tiers rather than going through insurance at all. Demand is *patient-pulled*. Manufacturers compete on brand, channel relationships, and cash price.

DTC pharma is only fully legal in the US and New Zealand. The US channel has scaled because three structural conditions converged in 2021–2025:

| Condition | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Social media demand creation | Patients learn about drugs from peers, not doctors. Wegovy went from FDA approval (June 2021) to viral cultural phenomenon within 12 months |
| Telehealth platforms with vertically integrated pharmacy | [[Hims & Hers]], [[Ro]], LifeMD provide async intake + pharmacy fulfillment — a complete consumer-style stack |
| Trump MFN executive order + IRA Medicare negotiation | Compressed the price umbrella that supported the rebate model; manufacturers respond by building their own DTC channels at controlled cash prices |

## The strategic playbook

Companies pursuing the consumerized model are restructuring around four moves:

### 1. In-house ecommerce platforms

| Manufacturer | Platform | Launch |
|-------------|----------|--------|
| [[Novo Nordisk]] | NovoCare | 2024 (refresh under [[Mike Doustdar]] 2025–) |
| [[Eli Lilly]] | LillyDirect | 2024 |
| [[Pfizer]] | PfizerForAll | 2024 |

These platforms aggregate prescribing (via affiliated telehealth), fulfillment, and price transparency. The strategic point is not the revenue they generate today (small) but the patient relationship and pricing flexibility they create.

### 2. Telehealth distribution partnerships

[[Novo Nordisk]] (under Doustdar) signed branded distribution deals with [[Hims & Hers]] (after dropping a copyright suit), [[Ro]], Weight Watchers, and LifeMD. The arrangement: telehealth platforms prescribe and dispense branded Wegovy/Ozempic at Novo's self-pay cash price, in exchange for stopping promotion of compounded alternatives.

[[Eli Lilly]] partners with Ro, LifeMD, and several others on the Zepbound/Foundayo side.

### 3. Cash-pay subscription tiers

Both manufacturers now offer monthly subscription pricing through telehealth partners. Novo announced (April 2026) rolling monthly subscriptions with deeper discounts for longer commitments — a textbook FMCG retention mechanic applied to a Schedule IV-adjacent drug.

### 4. Board-level FMCG hires

[[Novo Nordisk]] in 2025–2026 added:

| Hire | Background | Role |
|------|------------|------|
| Poul Weihrauch | CEO of [[Mars]] | Board observer (March 2026); expected non-exec director 2027 |
| Helena Saxon | Executive at H&M | Non-exec director |
| Jamey Millar | [[Procter & Gamble]] / [[Optum]] (UnitedHealth) | Head of US operations |

The hires are deliberate signals: Novo is recruiting consumer-goods, retail, and pharmacy-benefit-management expertise rather than pharma-industry insiders. The brief is to teach Novo how to compete in a category that now behaves like consumer goods.

## The Viagra precedent

The consumerization of weight-loss drugs is not unprecedented. [[Pfizer]]'s Viagra (1998) was the first prescription drug to be marketed at consumer-product scale — Bob Dole TV ads, NASCAR sponsorship, and a category that quickly developed a cash-pay shadow market. The differences from GLP-1: Viagra was a single-dose, episodic drug for a narrowly defined patient population; GLP-1s are chronic, with broader patient populations and far higher annual revenue per patient.

The structural analogy holds: a drug whose social meaning extends well beyond its medical indication, with a cash-pay channel that the manufacturer must learn to control rather than fight.

---

## Why the GLP-1 case is particularly instructive

Three reasons GLP-1 is the canonical case study:

1. **Demand exceeded forecasting models by an order of magnitude.** [[Novo Nordisk]] modeled Wegovy on its older Saxenda franchise; five weeks of post-approval prescriptions exceeded five years of Saxenda. The forecasting failure created the supply gap that compounders filled, which created the gray market that consumer-style platforms scaled into
2. **The cosmetic-medical boundary blurred.** Doustdar himself (FT, April 2026) acknowledged some uses of GLP-1s are "borderline cosmetic." Cosmetic drugs operate on consumer-product economics; medical drugs operate on insurance economics. GLP-1 sits on the boundary, with the consumer-side economics pulling
3. **The rivalry is duopoly, not monopoly.** [[Novo Nordisk]] and [[Eli Lilly]] compete on brand and channel in addition to molecule. In monopoly drug categories, manufacturers can stay in the prescriber-pulled model; in duopoly, the channel war forces consumerization

## Implications

| Stakeholder | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| Manufacturers | Brand and channel become competitive moats alongside molecule. R&D-only differentiation insufficient |
| PBMs | Disintermediation risk. Direct-to-employer deals (Novo and Lilly via Waltz Health) bypass the rebate stack |
| Telehealth platforms | Become structural distribution layer; pharma partnerships replace compounded gray-market revenue (margin compression but durable volume) |
| Insurance carriers | Lose share to cash-pay channel; obesity coverage decisions become public-political rather than purely actuarial |
| Patients | More transparent pricing; less protection from inappropriate prescribing; greater self-direction |
| Regulators | DTC advertising rules (FDA) become more consequential; state pharmacy boards more relevant; FTC active on PBM/orange-book issues |

---

## Resistance

The consumerization thesis has critics. Dr Marie Spreckley (University of Cambridge weight management researcher, FT April 2026) has argued the pattern obscures the fact that GLP-1s remain medical interventions with side-effect profiles requiring oversight, and that cash-pay access maps inversely with deprivation — wealthier patients get easier access despite obesity prevalence skewing toward lower-income populations. Without professional oversight, patients risk bone density and muscle loss complications that prescriber-led care would catch earlier.
