# Weaponized interdependence

"Weaponized interdependence" is the [[Henry Farrell]] / [[Abraham Newman]] framework for how modern globalization's network architecture — payments, information, supply chains, regulatory authority — creates coercive power for whoever controls the central nodes. Introduced in a 2019 *International Security* paper and developed into their 2023 book *Underground Empire*, it has become the dominant alternative framing to the [[Second China shock|goods-trade]] account of how economic power is exercised in the 2020s.

The core insight: globalization doesn't distribute power evenly. Networks have hubs, and whoever controls a hub can surveil, deny access, or coerce. That is a different axis of economic power than either comparative advantage (Ricardian trade) or aggregate trade-flow shares (the [[Global imbalances|imbalances]] account).

---

## The framework

Farrell and Newman identify two coercive mechanisms that states with hub access can deploy:

### 1. Panopticon effect

Control of network nodes provides surveillance capacity — seeing what flows through the network and who is connected to whom. US access to [[SWIFT]] transaction data under the Terrorist Finance Tracking Program is the canonical example; the PATRIOT Act Section 311 authority is the enabling tool. China's [[Great Firewall]] is a domestic-facing parallel.

### 2. Chokepoint effect

Control of a hub allows the controller to deny access to specific actors. SWIFT disconnection of Iranian and Russian banks (2012 and 2022) is the paradigmatic case; so are the Commerce Department's [[Entity List]] restrictions on Chinese access to US and allied semiconductor tooling via [[ASML]], [[Applied Materials]], [[Lam Research]], and [[KLA]].

---

## Why it's a competing frame to the Second China shock

Where the [[Second China shock]] framing argues that aggregate Chinese manufacturing capacity is what hits Western labor markets and industrial policy, weaponized interdependence argues that aggregate flows are a distraction — what matters is who controls the critical nodes.

| Dimension | Second China shock | Weaponized interdependence |
|---|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Sectoral export volumes and prices | Network topology, specific chokepoints |
| Source of coercive power | Scale, subsidized capital, overcapacity | Hub control, regulatory authority |
| Target of analysis | Labor markets, industrial policy, current accounts | Sanctions, export controls, critical mineral refining |
| Policy prescription | Tariffs, state aid, domestic industrial policy | Export controls, friend-shoring, secondary sanctions |
| Risk to US | Gradual industrial erosion via Europe pathway | Chinese counter-hub construction (CIPS, Huawei, rare earth refining) |

Both frames can be true simultaneously. Most serious analysts use both — Farrell and Newman explicitly acknowledge the aggregate-flow story, and [[Michael Pettis]] acknowledges the chokepoint story. But the policy prescriptions differ sharply, and the political coalitions supporting each are different.

## Key empirical cases

| Case | Mechanism | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| SWIFT disconnection of Iran (2012) | Chokepoint | Forced Iran to the negotiating table on JCPOA |
| SWIFT disconnection of major Russian banks (Feb 2022) | Chokepoint | Accelerated Russian financial isolation; spawned CIPS counter-build |
| Huawei Entity List (May 2019) | Chokepoint + panopticon | Disrupted Huawei smartphone business, spawned HiSilicon / SMIC vertical build |
| [[US-China chip export controls|Semiconductor export controls]] (Oct 2022, Oct 2023, Dec 2024) | Chokepoint via ASML/TSMC/Applied/Lam | Slowed Chinese advanced-node progress; spawned [[SMIC]]/Huawei domestic push |
| [[TikTok]] divestment threat (2023-2024) | Chokepoint (data governance) | Unresolved; US leverage real but politically costly |
| [[Nord Stream 2]] sanctions (2021-2022) | Chokepoint | Shifted European energy dependence trajectory |

## Chinese counter-moves

Beijing reads the framework accurately and has spent 2020-2026 building counter-hubs and chokepoint alternatives:

- **CIPS** (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System) as SWIFT alternative
- **Yuan-denominated commodity pricing** (Russia crude, Saudi/Iran oil deals)
- **Rare earth refining dominance** as a counter-chokepoint the US and Europe need to work around
- **BRICS+ expansion** as a non-Western-hub coalition
- **Digital yuan (e-CNY)** as dollar-alternative retail infrastructure

Whether these counter-builds achieve chokepoint-displacement remains contested. Farrell/Newman's view is that network effects take decades to shift, and that US chokepoints (dollar clearing, internet root DNS, key submarine cables, leading-node semiconductors) are durable through 2030-plus even with active Chinese counter-effort.

## Limits of the framework

- **Does not explain aggregate flows** — the framework is silent on why [[China]] runs a trade surplus or why [[Germany]] runs one. For that, you need the [[Second China shock]] or [[Global imbalances]] frames.
- **Overestimates US durability** — some critics ([[Paul Krugman]] among them) argue the framework takes US hub position as static when it is in fact being actively eroded.
- **Underweights fragmentation risk** — Farrell and Newman treat US economic-statecraft power as a single toolkit, but US allies ([[Germany]], [[France]], [[South Korea]]) often resist US chokepoint tactics that hurt their firms.
- **Blurs analysis and advocacy** — *Underground Empire* has an argumentative stance about using this power responsibly; some critics argue this undermines its analytical neutrality.
