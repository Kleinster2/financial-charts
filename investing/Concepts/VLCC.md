# VLCC

A VLCC (Very Large Crude Carrier) is a tanker class of 200,000-320,000 deadweight tonnes carrying approximately 2 million barrels of crude oil per voyage. VLCCs move ~85% of Middle East crude exports and are the backbone of long-haul seaborne oil trade. The global fleet numbers approximately 860 vessels.

---

## Synthesis

VLCCs are the physical layer beneath every oil benchmark. When a refiner in [[China]] or [[Japan]] buys a cargo of [[Saudi Arabia|Saudi]] Arab Light, it arrives on a VLCC. When the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closes, VLCCs are the assets that get stranded — and their rate spikes become the clearest real-time signal of physical market tightness. The March 2026 Hormuz crisis demonstrated this: TD3C (the benchmark Middle East-to-China VLCC route) hit $423,736/day on March 3, an all-time high. At that rate, a modern VLCC would pay for its ~$120M newbuild cost in roughly 10 months. [[Rystad Energy]] estimated ~10% of the global mainline VLCC fleet was locked inside the Persian Gulf in the first week of the closure.

The fleet is aging — 35% is 15+ years old, 16% (170+ vessels) is 20+ years old — while ordering has slowed due to $115-128M newbuild prices, 3+ year delivery slots, and fuel-technology uncertainty (LNG, ammonia, methanol dual-fuel). The replacement need is ~38 VLCCs per year over the next two decades. The orderbook sits at ~18.8% of fleet, with 32-40 deliveries scheduled for 2026 (mostly H2).

## Rate system — Worldscale

VLCC rates are quoted in Worldscale (WS) points. WS100 ("flat rate") is a published $/tonne rate for each route pair, recalculated annually, based on a hypothetical 75,000 DWT vessel at 14.5 knots consuming 55 mt/day. Actual fixtures are quoted as a percentage of flat (e.g., WS60 = 60% of flat rate). The industry converts WS points to time-charter equivalent (TCE) in $/day for comparability.

The benchmark route is TD3C: [[Ras Tanura]] (Persian Gulf) to Ningbo ([[China]]), ~6,500 nautical miles, ~20 days laden. TD3C is published by the [[Baltic Exchange]] and is the most-watched VLCC rate index.

## Voyage economics

| Route | Distance (nm) | Days laden | Notes |
|-------|---------------|-----------|-------|
| Ras Tanura → Ningbo (TD3C) | ~6,500 | ~20 | Dominant crude flow |
| Ras Tanura → Rotterdam (via Suez) | ~6,436 | ~18 | Suez transit required |
| Ras Tanura → Rotterdam (via Cape) | ~11,169 | ~31 | Hormuz/Suez bypass |
| USGC → NE Asia | ~12,000-16,000 | ~21-28 | Via Panama or Cape |
| West Africa → Ningbo | ~10,000 | ~28-35 | Via Cape of Good Hope |
| North Sea → Rotterdam | Short | ~2-3 | Aframax more common |

Cape of Good Hope diversion (avoiding Hormuz/Suez) adds 10-15 days and $1-2M per voyage, or ~$0.50-1.00/bbl. The ton-mile increase from [[Atlantic basin]] sourcing by Asian refiners structurally supports freight rates even if total seaborne volumes dip.

## 2026 Hormuz crisis impact

The [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis|Hormuz closure]] (Feb 28, 2026) was the defining VLCC event of the decade:

- ~10% of mainline VLCCs locked inside the Persian Gulf in the first week ([[Rystad Energy]], Apr 8)
- TD3C hit $423,736/day ATH on Mar 3 — prior record was ~$350,000 during 2008 financial crisis
- Bunker fuel spiked to $1,053/mt
- War-risk insurance added ~$1.2-1.5M per transit (~$250K pre-crisis) — see [[Iran conflict insurance impact]]
- [[Nadia Martin Wiggen]] ([[Stellen Capital]], Mar 23): rates initially surged, then fell when there was no product to fill ships. Once floating storage was exhausted and production was shut in, vessels sat idle. The rate drop then enabled Asian refiners to book cheaper ships for longer [[Atlantic basin]] voyages
- Insurer/vessel caution persists: commercial shipping won't transit until confident in ceasefire and mine deactivation

See [[Physical Oil Trading]] for detailed loading operations, cargo sizes, and VLCC economics.

## Retail exposure — BWET ETF

The [[BWET]] ETF (Breakwave Tanker Shipping ETF, sub-advised by [[Breakwave Advisors]]) is the only US-listed retail instrument offering direct VLCC freight rate exposure, holding ~90% TD3C and ~10% TD20 FFAs. During the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]], BWET rose 836% YTD through April 9 ($18.73 → $175.29) as TD3C hit $423,736/day, becoming the best-performing US ETF of 2026. The fund's mechanics — and the Baltic Exchange's continued publication of TD3C assessments during the Hormuz closure — raised the question of whether the benchmark was tracking physical reality. See [[Imaginary freight rate]].
