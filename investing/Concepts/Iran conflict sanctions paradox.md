# Iran conflict sanctions paradox

The unprecedented situation of the [[United States]] discussing lifting sanctions on both [[Russia]]n AND [[Iran]]ian oil while actively at war with Iran. Part of [[Iran conflict economic disruption]].

---

## The paradox — Mulroy framing (March 20, 2026)

[[Mick Mulroy]] (fmr Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Middle East, ex-CIA officer, ex-Marine, [[Middle East Institute|MEI]] Distinguished Military Fellow) and the panel moderator Ken (ex-CIA) agreed: neither can think of another war in history where a country lifted sanctions on the enemy during combat.

100-140 million barrels of Iranian oil sit in floating storage — waivers would apply to these barrels, effectively allowing Iran to monetize stockpiled crude while fighting the US. This emboldens the hardliners inside [[Iran]]'s remaining regime who believe they are winning the war — if the enemy is lifting sanctions to manage the economic pain of the conflict, the asymmetric pressure strategy is working.

---

## Russia as unintended winner

[[Russia]] benefits enormously from the sanctions paradox:

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Sanctions removed | US lifted Russian oil sanctions to backfill lost Gulf supply |
| Oil prices soaring | Russian crude (Urals, ESPO) now trading at premiums to Brent — the sanctions discount regime is breaking down |
| Attention diverted | [[Ukraine]] is making military gains while world attention is diverted to Iran |
| Strategic leverage | Russia's break-even is $30-40/bbl vs [[Saudi Arabia]]'s ~$90 — windfall at any price above $60 |

Mulroy: "We should be going the other direction with sanctioning Russia." The paradox compounds: the US is simultaneously fighting a war that benefits Russia and lifting sanctions that further benefit Russia, while [[Ukraine]] — a US priority — capitalizes on the distraction.

---

## Strategic implications

The sanctions paradox reveals the fundamental contradiction of the war's economic management:

1. The US cannot sustain a war that disrupts 20% of global oil supply without either opening alternative supply (which means lifting sanctions) or accepting domestic economic pain (gasoline prices, inflation, midterm consequences)

2. Lifting sanctions on Iran during the war signals to hardliners that the strategy of asymmetric economic pain is working — Iran doesn't need to surrender to get sanctions relief, it just needs to make the war expensive enough

3. Lifting sanctions on Russia during the war means the primary geopolitical adversary benefits from a conflict it played no part in starting — and the leverage that sanctions provided over Russia's [[Ukraine]] operations evaporates

4. The $200B Pentagon supplemental request, combined with sanctions lifting, means the US is simultaneously increasing war spending and reducing economic pressure on both adversaries — the opposite of strategic coherence

---

## Historical context

The moderator (Ken) and Mulroy explicitly stated they "cannot think of another war in history where you lifted sanctions on your foe in the middle of a war." The closest parallels — UK trading with continental Europe during Napoleonic wars, US oil-for-food programs — involved economic arrangements with neutrals, not active sanctions relief for the enemy.

The comparison to [[Iraq]] war spending is instructive: the Iraq war cost $800B over 8 years. The current Pentagon request is $200B for a war not even a month old — roughly 1/4 of the entire Iraq war cost at accelerated pace.
