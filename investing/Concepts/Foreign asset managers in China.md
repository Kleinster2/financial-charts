#concept #china #financials

**Foreign asset managers in China** — the largely disappointing record of global asset managers trying to build mainland [[China]] mutual-fund businesses after Beijing relaxed ownership rules in 2020. Five years on, the wholly-owned entrants have captured just 0.1% of the market, and the structure that worked was buying out an existing joint-venture partner, not building greenfield.

---

## The 0.1% problem (2020-2026)

China let foreign firms fully own their mainland mutual-fund businesses in 2020, shortly after a US-China trade deal late in [[Trump II|Trump]]'s first term. Six global managers — [[BlackRock]], Fidelity International, [[Neuberger Berman]], [[Allianz]], AllianceBernstein and [[Schroders]] — set up wholly-owned units and invested about $800mn between them. By the end of Q1 2026 they had attracted only about $5bn (Rmb34bn) of the market's Rmb36.5tn in assets — roughly 0.1%.

The break-even line matters: most of these units are sub-scale and burning capital. As Peter Alexander of consultancy Z-Ben Advisors put it, "essentially all of these companies have been throwing more money at the businesses."

---

## Greenfield failed; JV-buyout worked

The cleaner structural lesson is the contrast between two routes into China:

- Greenfield wholly-owned (the six above): ~0.1% of the market after five years.
- Buying out a long-standing JV partner ([[JPMorgan Chase|JPMorgan]], Manulife, [[Morgan Stanley]]): together ~Rmb373bn ($55bn), about 1% of the market — roughly 10x the greenfield cohort.

The JV-buyout firms inherited distribution, brand familiarity, and local relationships that the greenfield entrants had to build from scratch. [[BlackRock]]'s wealth-management JV with China Construction Bank alone holds ~Rmb42bn ($6bn) — more than its wholly-owned fund unit.

[[Schroders]] is the clearest retreat: it is exiting its mainland mutual-fund business just three years after launch (it ran only Rmb1.7bn of its ~$1tn global AUM there) and has regulatory approval to transfer three funds and an investment team to [[Neuberger Berman]], while keeping a JV presence and saying it "remains fully committed to China."

---

## Why it has been so hard

- Distribution and brand recognition: domestic banks and platforms dominate fund distribution; foreign brands carry little weight with mainland retail.
- Intense domestic competition from entrenched Chinese fund houses.
- Timing: several got their licences just as the property-market downturn hit, which Neuberger COO Andrew Komaroff said "led to a market that was less favourable to scaling new equity strategies."
- Worsening US-China geopolitics over the same window (see [[US-China tech race]], [[EU-China trade]] for the broader decoupling backdrop).
- Slower Chinese economic momentum and weak equity sentiment for much of the period (Komaroff noted "a meaningful improvement in sentiment over the past year").

Peter Alexander (Z-Ben): "Foreign firms have proven unfortunately incapable of adapting to the local rules of engaging and effectively competing locally," though he singled out Neuberger as having done "relatively well" because headquarters granted it autonomy.

---

## Why it matters

This is a concrete case study in the gap between de jure market access and de facto market penetration in [[China]] — the same theme that recurs across foreign firms' China experience. The 2020 opening was a headline trade-deal concession; the 0.1% outcome shows that ownership rights without distribution, brand, and local autonomy do not convert into market share. For the global managers, China remains a call option that has cost ~$800mn in premium for almost no payoff so far — relevant to how [[BlackRock]], [[Schroders]] ([[Nuveen]] deal) and peers frame their growth narratives.
