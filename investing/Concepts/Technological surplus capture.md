# Technological surplus capture

When an economy gets better at making something — chips, models, batteries, software — a surplus is created. Technological surplus capture is the question of *where that surplus lands*: with the producers who make the thing (as higher wages, profits, and national output) or with the users who buy it (as falling prices and rising real purchasing power). The whole question reduces to one variable — appropriability — how much of the value it creates the producer (or a nation) can retain rather than let leak to users and imitators.

This is a hub concept. It names a distributional question that silently organises several existing vault threads — the [[US-Europe productivity paradox]], semiconductor [[Export controls]], the battery-tech case in [[China retaliatory toolkit]], [[Inference economics]], and the production-vs-profit debate inside [[Industrial policy]] — none of which owns the general principle. Specific applications stay in their own notes; this note carries the framework.

---

## One spectrum, three names

The frame looks like it has several moving parts. It has one: a single spectrum running from low to high appropriability. The same line can be read three ways, and conflating the readings is what makes it look multi-dimensional.

| The one spectrum | Low-appropriability end | High-appropriability end |
|---|---|---|
| As a property (how replicable) | Easy to copy; leaks fast | Hard to replicate; holds |
| As a mechanism (what acts) | Market diffusion | Producer / state fencing |
| As an outcome (who gains) | User surplus (user wage) | Producer surplus (product wage) |

These are not three axes; they are one line described by its property, its mechanism, and its result. High appropriability *is* fencing winning *is* the producer capturing the surplus; low appropriability *is* diffusion winning *is* the user capturing it. The 2x2 of outcome-against-mechanism only populates the diagonal — the proof that they are the same axis, not two.

Two things are *not* axes here, and keeping them straight is the whole discipline:

- A technology is a *point*, located on the spectrum by how replicable its capability is. EUV lithography sits high; 1790s spinning sat low; AI inference sits low. Enumerating technologies is listing dots, not adding a dimension.
- A policy is a *force*. Fencing ([[Export controls]], outbound licensing) shoves a technology's point toward the high-appropriability end; openness and easy imitation let it slide toward the low end.

## What sets appropriability

Appropriability is the sum of two components — what the technology is naturally, plus what a state does to it.

### Natural replication difficulty

How hard the capability is to reconstitute, absent any policy. Four largely independent sources:

| Source | Low (slides to users) | High (sticks with producers) |
|---|---|---|
| Embodiment | Skill in one person's head | Ecosystem of thousands of specialised suppliers |
| Cumulative experience | The blueprint is the whole thing | Yield-learning a blueprint cannot shortcut |
| Capital scale | A workshop | A $20-40bn fab |
| Frontier pace | Stationary target — a leak transfers it all | Moving target — a leak transfers a depreciating asset |

For the purpose of the outcome these compress into a single scalar: natural appropriability.

### Engineered appropriability (fencing)

State policy is appropriability added by statute rather than by nature: [[Export controls]], outbound technology licensing, patents, [[Industrial policy]] guardrails. Policy is an attempt to *engineer* appropriability where the market would not supply it. Fencing is not a separate axis; it is the same scalar, moved by hand.

Natural difficulty plus engineered fencing equals where the technology sits — and therefore who gets the surplus. This is why the same producer-capture outcome can arise two ways: naturally (EUV is captured because it is genuinely hard to replicate) or by statute (1790s spinning, which a state tried to fence and failed). Natural-vs-engineered is the one real sub-distinction in the frame, and it exists only on the producer side; user-capture is simply the absence of appropriability.

## The welfare reading: product wage vs user wage

Why the distribution matters for living standards, not just accounting, comes from [[Brad DeLong]]'s May 30 2026 contribution to the [[US-Europe productivity paradox]] debate. Separate the product wage (what producers earn making a good) from the user wage (what consumers gain when it gets cheaper). At the low-appropriability end, gains flow to users through collapsing prices, so a producer-side productivity gap need not be a user-side living-standards gap. DeLong argues the [[Philippe Aghion]]/[[Antonin Bergeaud]]/[[Luis Garicano]] critique commits a neo-mercantilist error — assuming gains accrue to producers — when information-technology gains diffuse to users.

The historical base case is the Lewis-Prebisch-Singer fact: since roughly 1870, productivity gains in primary products flowed to consumers via falling relative prices (low appropriability), while differentiated manufactures held their gains for producers (higher appropriability). DeLong's "bio-info-tech-attention economy" framing is the bet that information goods sit at the low-appropriability, diffuse-to-users end.

## The full range: does all technology leak?

No. Leakage is what low appropriability looks like over time, not a law — and real cases span the entire spectrum:

- *Leaks on first defection (low).* 1790s British textile machinery was a memorisable craft. Britain banned machinery exports and artisan emigration through the 1770s-80s — pure engineered appropriability — but [[Samuel Slater]] memorised Arkwright's water frame, posed as a farm labourer to evade the ban, and rebuilt it from memory at Pawtucket, Rhode Island (1790-93). The capability walked out in one head; Britain called him "Slater the Traitor."
- *Leaks over centuries (medium).* Chinese silk held as a monopoly roughly 2,000 years before Byzantine monks smuggled silkworm eggs out (~552 AD); porcelain held about 1,000 years before Meissen cracked it (1709).
- *Holds for decades (high).* EUV lithography: China has spent a decade and tens of billions and still cannot replicate the [[ASML]]/Zeiss/Cymer supplier ecosystem (see [[Export controls]]). Leading-edge fabs and jet-engine turbine metallurgy have stayed fenced for decades — the barrier is ecosystem, capital scale, and cumulative yield-learning, none of it carriable in a defector's memory.
- *Never leaks — lost instead (the ceiling).* Greek fire and wootz (Damascus) steel were held so tightly they died with their makers rather than diffusing.

The live 2026 read: China's battery-tech licensing on [[BYD]]/[[CATL]] (see [[China retaliatory toolkit]]) is Britain's strategy two centuries on, but the technology sits far higher on the spectrum than 1790s spinning did. Cell-chemistry and gigafactory know-how is embodied in codified process recipes, capital-scale plant, and ecosystem yield-learning — so identical fencing should hold far longer than Britain's did. The master question for any technology-lead thesis is therefore not "will it leak?" but "where on the appropriability spectrum does it sit, and which way is policy pushing it?" (inference).

## Differentiation from neighbours

This note is easy to confuse with adjacent frameworks. The boundary:

- [[Weaponized interdependence]] is the coercive *mechanism* of fencing (control a network hub, deny or surveil). This note is the *distributional outcome* — where surplus lands — which fencing is one way to bend. Weaponized interdependence is a tool; surplus capture is the stakes.
- [[Inference economics]] is the AI-specific *instance* of the low-appropriability end. This note is the general spectrum of which it is one point.
- [[Jevons Paradox]] is a *demand-expansion* dynamic (cheaper → more total use). Adjacent because cheapening is the diffusion channel, but Jevons is about quantity demanded, not who captures the surplus.
- [[Productivity J-curve]] is about *when* gains appear in the data; this note is about *to whom* they accrue once they do.

## Investment read-through

(Inference where unmarked.)

- The single most useful move on any technology-lead thesis: locate the technology on the appropriability spectrum, then read which way policy is pushing it. High and rising (EUV, leading-edge fabs, fenced batteries) means durable producer rents; low or falling (model inference, commoditised hardware) means the surplus is leaking to users and the producer's pricing power is on a clock. Frontier-AI bull cases implicitly assume high appropriability; [[Inference economics]] is the evidence it is low at the model layer.
- Where states fence successfully ([[Export controls]], battery licensing, [[Critical minerals]] processing), the fenced producers hold the rent — the investable edge sits with the chokepoint owner, not the user.
- Where appropriability is low, the durable value migrates downstream to whoever assembles cheap inputs into a user-facing product (the applications layer over the model layer; see [[Jensen Five Layer Cake]]).
- Cross-jurisdiction comparisons that ignore the spectrum mislead: a country can "lose" on producer-side productivity yet not lose on living standards if the technology is low-appropriability and its users capture the diffused surplus (the [[US-Europe productivity paradox]] correction).
