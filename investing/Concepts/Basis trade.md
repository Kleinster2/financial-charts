**Basis trade** — Hedge fund strategy exploiting price gaps between Treasury cash bonds and futures. Now ~$1.5T, up 75% from 2019. Systemic risk if unwinding forces dealer capacity limits.

---

## The trade

**Mechanics:**
- Buy Treasury cash bonds
- Sell Treasury futures (short)
- Profit from small price gap (basis)
- Highly leveraged (via repo financing)

**Leverage:** Up to **$50 borrowed for every $1 of capital** (50:1)

**Why basis exists:** Pension funds, insurers prefer futures (less cash upfront) → futures trade above cash bonds → hedge funds arbitrage the gap.

**Risk:** If cash bonds underperform futures (reverse of expected), positions blow up. Unwinding overwhelms dealer capacity.

## Size growth

| Period | Size | Change |
|--------|------|--------|
| 2019 peak | ~$860B | — |
| March 2020 | ~$500B | Blowup |
| Early 2023 | — | [[Trade]] "roared back" |
| Jan 2026 | **~$1.5T** | **+75% from 2019** |
| Late 2025 (OFR) | **$2.4T long / $1.6T short** | ~3x the level three years prior |

**Morgan Stanley (Jan 2026):** Notional size has outpaced Treasury bond issuance growth.

**Office for Financial Reporting (late 2025):** Hedge funds held $2.4T in long Treasury positions and $1.6T short — nearly triple the level three years earlier. The [[Bank for International Settlements|BIS]] separately flagged an explosion in hedge fund "swap" trades (exchanging Treasuries for other securities), which sits alongside the basis trade as a second vector of leveraged Treasury exposure.

**Why it returned (2023+):**
- Fed rate hikes pushed 10-year to ~5% (highest since 2007)
- Institutional investors piled into Treasury futures
- Treasury ramped up issuance for deficits → cash prices down
- [[Gap]] between futures and cash widened → more arbitrage opportunity

## The Cayman Islands revelation (NY Fed research, 2026)

[[Federal Reserve|NY Fed]] economists discovered that official [[Treasury International Capital|TIC]] data undercounts cross-border hedge fund Treasury flows by ~$1.4T. The reason: most leveraged funds are domiciled in the [[Cayman Islands]] for tax and regulatory purposes, but TIC reporting fails to capture the full scale of their positions.

Correcting for this, the Cayman Islands is the largest foreign holder of US Treasuries — surpassing [[China]], [[Japan]], and the [[United Kingdom]]. Between 2022 and 2024, hedge funds absorbed 37% of net issuance of notes and bonds, nearly the same amount as all other foreign investors combined.

This reframes the foreign demand picture. Official data shows foreign ownership of Treasuries at <30% (down from 46% in 2008), suggesting a secular retreat. The NY Fed work reveals that much of the decline reflects a *compositional shift* — from central banks and sovereign wealth funds to leveraged hedge funds chasing basis and swap spreads. The demand hasn't disappeared; it has moved from price-insensitive holders (governments) to price-sensitive ones (hedge funds).

The distinction matters for stability. Central banks hold Treasuries for reserve management and rarely sell into volatility. Hedge funds hold them for arbitrage spreads and will exit the moment the trade economics change. [[Gillian Tett]] (FT, Apr 3 2026): what matters is not *whether* foreigners buy Treasuries, but *which* foreigners — and whether they'll stay when conditions deteriorate.

*Source: NY Fed research, OFR data, FT (Gillian Tett, Apr 3 2026)*

## 2020 blowup

**What happened:**
- COVID market stress
- Cash bonds underperformed futures (reverse of basis trade expectation)
- Hedge funds forced to unwind + margin calls on futures
- Foreign central banks dumping [[Treasuries]] to prop up currencies
- Dealers couldn't handle volume
- Fed had to intervene

**Fed response:**
- Bought trillions in bonds
- Emergency repo funding
- Kept markets functioning

**Scale then:** ~$500B — **one-third of current size**

**Trump tariffs triggered Treasury selloff:**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|

| 10-year weekly move | **Biggest surge since 2001** |

**Other factors cited:**
- Treasury-swap spread trade unwinding

Market stabilized by April 11, but yields remained elevated.

## Iran war stress test (Feb-Apr 2026)

The [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] provided the third stress episode in six years (after March 2020 and April 2025). 10Y yields rose ~48bp from trough (3.96% on Feb 27) to peak (4.44% on Mar 27) before partially reversing. Some crowded hedge fund trades were "flushed out" at the war's start, but the unwinding did not accelerate into a systemic event.

[[Scott Bessent|Bessent]] used "numerous tricks" to maintain market calm (Tett's characterization). Long-term asset owners — insurance companies, pension funds — showed no desire to exit, providing a stability anchor that the leveraged sector alone cannot.

The context that makes this episode significant: Bessent faces a 33% rollover of US debt next year, equivalent to ~$10T in Treasury sales. If hedge funds remain the marginal buyer at the scale the NY Fed research suggests (37% of net issuance), any deterioration in basis-trade economics — from higher rates, wider repo spreads, or geopolitical shocks — could create a buyer gap precisely when issuance pressure is highest.

*Source: FT (Gillian Tett, Apr 3 2026)*

- Hedge funds face less scrutiny than banks
- Opaque leverage levels

## Investment implications

**Tail risk indicator:**
- Not a direct trade
- Signals leverage in Treasury market
- 2020 showed spillover to all assets
- Watch repo rates, dealer balance sheets

**If basis trade unwinds:**
- Treasury volatility spike
- Risk-off across assets
- Fed likely to intervene (as in 2020)
