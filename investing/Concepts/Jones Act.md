# Jones Act

The **Jones Act** (Merchant Marine Act of 1920) requires that goods shipped between US ports be transported on ships that are US-built, US-owned, US-crewed, and US-flagged. It is the most consequential maritime regulation affecting US energy markets — and one of the least understood constraints on domestic oil and gas distribution.

## Synopsis

The Jones Act creates an artificial wall between America's energy-surplus regions (Gulf Coast, Alaska) and its energy-deficit regions (East Coast, West Coast). Even though the US produces more oil than it consumes in aggregate, it is often cheaper for East Coast refineries to import crude from West Africa, the North Sea, or the Middle East by foreign-flagged tanker than to ship it from Houston on a Jones Act-compliant vessel. The reason is simple economics: Jones Act-eligible ships cost 4-5x more to build and operate than their foreign-flagged equivalents. There are fewer than 100 Jones Act-eligible tankers in the US fleet vs. thousands of foreign-flagged vessels globally. This is the binding constraint that makes [[Ed Morse]]'s [[US Energy Independence Six Countries|"six countries" framework]] structurally true — not just a metaphor.

## Why it matters for energy markets

| Dimension | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Crude oil | East/West Coast refineries import seaborne crude from abroad rather than from Gulf Coast — Jones Act shipping too expensive |
| LNG | Impossible to ship US LNG between US ports economically on Jones Act vessels; none exist with LNG capability |
| Products | Gasoline, diesel, jet fuel face same constraint — product tankers under Jones Act cost multiples of foreign equivalents |
| Crisis response | Every historical attempt to temporarily waive the Jones Act during energy emergencies has failed politically — except tiny, limited waivers |
| Net effect | US coasts behave like net importing countries even when aggregate US production exceeds consumption |

## The waiver problem

[[Ed Morse]] noted during the March 17, 2026 [[Council on Foreign Relations|CFR]] panel on the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war's geoeconomic effects]] that "the Jones Act lifting has failed every time the government has done it, except for a small emergency." The political economy: the maritime lobby (shipbuilders, unions, military readiness advocates) blocks waivers. Even Hurricane Maria (2017) only got a 10-day waiver for Puerto Rico after intense political pressure.

During the 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure, the Jones Act was not waived despite East Coast product tightness — reinforcing the structural vulnerability.

## Economics

| Metric | Jones Act vessel | Foreign-flagged vessel |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Newbuild cost (product tanker) | ~$200-300M | ~$40-60M |
| Daily operating cost | ~$20,000-30,000 | ~$6,000-10,000 |
| US-flagged tanker fleet | <100 | N/A |
| Global tanker fleet | N/A | ~13,000+ |

The cost differential makes it uneconomic to move crude or products coastwise in almost all market conditions. The only exception is when global prices spike high enough that the Jones Act premium becomes a rounding error — which rarely happens.

## Investment implications

1. **East/West Coast refiners** ([[PBF Energy|PBF]], [[Phillips 66|PSX]] East Coast operations, [[Valero|VLO]] East operations) are permanently exposed to seaborne import pricing — they cannot source domestic crude cheaply even when it's abundant
2. **Jones Act tanker operators** ([[International Seaways|INSW]] has some, [[Overseas Shipholding Group]]) benefit from the regulatory moat but the fleet is tiny
3. **Any energy crisis** that disrupts global seaborne trade (Hormuz, Suez, piracy) disproportionately hits US coasts — the Jones Act prevents domestic supply from filling the gap
4. **Jones Act reform** is a perennial policy idea that never passes — not investable as a catalyst
