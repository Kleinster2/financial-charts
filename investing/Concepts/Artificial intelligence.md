Artificial intelligence encompasses machine learning, deep learning, large language models, and autonomous systems. It is the central organizing theme of the current technology investment cycle, driving capital expenditure across infrastructure (chips, power, cooling), cloud platforms, model development, and application layers. The 2023-2026 cycle has been defined by the scaling of transformer-based LLMs and the emergence of agentic AI as the next frontier.

The AI investment stack spans multiple layers: semiconductor design ([[NVIDIA]], [[AMD]], [[Broadcom]]), foundry manufacturing ([[TSMC]]), cloud infrastructure ([[Amazon]], [[Microsoft]], [[Google]]), model development ([[OpenAI]], [[Anthropic]], [[Google DeepMind]]), and enterprise applications ([[Claude Cowork]], [[Microsoft Copilot]]). Hyperscaler capex on AI infrastructure exceeded $200B in 2024 and is projected to grow through 2026. The [[Jensen Five Layer Cake]] framework describes the AI infrastructure stack as: chips, systems, foundries/packaging, data center infra, and power. Investment implications ripple from hardware scarcity (GPU supply constraints) through software disruption ([[Claude Cowork disruption February 2026|SaaSpocalypse]]) to labor market restructuring.
