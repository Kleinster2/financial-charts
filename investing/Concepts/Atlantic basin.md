# Atlantic basin

The Atlantic basin in oil trading refers to the crude-producing and -consuming regions bordering the Atlantic Ocean: the North Sea ([[United Kingdom|UK]], [[Norway]]), West Africa ([[Nigeria]], [[Angola]], Equatorial Guinea, Congo), the US Gulf Coast and onshore [[United States|US]], [[Brazil]]'s pre-salt offshore, [[Canada]]'s oil sands and Atlantic coast, [[Guyana]]/Suriname, and the Mediterranean ([[Libya]], [[Algeria]]). Combined production is approximately 30M bbl/day — roughly 30% of global supply. The region is contrasted with the "Middle East/Asian basin" (or "east of Suez"), centered on Persian Gulf producers pricing off [[Dubai crude]]/[[Murban]] benchmarks.

---

## Synthesis

The Atlantic basin is where oil pricing originates — [[Brent crude|Brent]] and [[WTI]], the two benchmarks that price the majority of the world's internationally traded crude, are both Atlantic basin grades. In normal conditions, Atlantic basin barrels serve European and US refineries through relatively short-haul routes (North Sea to Rotterdam in 2-3 days, US Gulf to Europe in 10-14 days). Only a fraction — historically ~1-2M bbl/day — flows eastward to Asia, with West African crudes swinging between European and Asian buyers based on the [[Brent-WTI spread|Brent-Dubai spread]] ("the East-West arb").

The [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis|2026 Hormuz crisis]] inverted this flow pattern. With 17-20M bbl/day of Gulf supply stranded, Asian refiners bid aggressively for Atlantic basin barrels: [[WTI]] Midland premiums for North Asia delivery surged to $30-40/bbl above [[ICE]] Brent (from ~$20 pre-crisis). Johan Sverdrup ([[Norway]]) premiums hit an all-time high of $11.15/bbl above Dated Brent. [[Rystad Energy]] (Apr 8) described [[Forties]] crude trading $34-35 above Brent futures as "fairly unnatural." The Atlantic basin effectively became the world's marginal oil supplier — a role it is physically capable of filling in volume but not in transit time, since voyages from the US Gulf or West Africa to Asia run 21-45 days vs 15-20 from the Persian Gulf. This ton-mile expansion is structural support for [[VLCC]] freight rates even when total seaborne volumes decline.

## Normal trade flow patterns

Atlantic basin crudes primarily serve:

- European refineries (North Sea, West African, and US exports via Rotterdam hub)
- US Gulf Coast refineries (domestic production, Canadian heavy, some West African)
- US East Coast refineries (imports from West Africa, North Sea)
- Latin American refineries (US product exports dominate; crude from regional producers)

Eastbound flows to Asia (~1-2M bbl/day pre-crisis) have been growing as US exports surged after the 2015 export ban was lifted and Brazil's pre-salt production expanded. West African barrels are the primary "swing" supply between the two basins — they flow east when [[Dubai crude|Dubai]]-Brent narrows (making Asian delivery competitive) and west when the spread widens.

## 2026 Hormuz disruption — flow inversion

The [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis|Hormuz closure]] (Feb 28, 2026) forced a structural rerouting of global crude flows:

- Asian refiners, cut off from 17-20M bbl/day of Gulf supply, bid aggressively for Atlantic basin barrels
- WTI Midland premiums for North Asia delivery: $30-40/bbl above ICE Brent (from ~$20 pre-crisis)
- Johan Sverdrup FOB premium: hit all-time high $11.15/bbl above Dated Brent (Mar 2026)
- [[Forties]] physical cargoes: $34-35 above Brent futures (Apr 7) — [[Rystad Energy]]: "fairly unnatural"
- Atlantic basin inventories drawing down as barrels that normally served European/US refiners headed east

The rerouting doubles the ton-miles per barrel delivered to Asia (US Gulf to China: 35-45 days vs Persian Gulf to China: 15-20 days), structurally supporting [[VLCC]] freight rates and tightening vessel availability.

See [[Iran conflict supply chain cascade]] for Asian refiner Atlantic basin sourcing details.

## Freight dynamics

| Route | Distance (nm) | Days laden | vs Persian Gulf |
|-------|---------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Persian Gulf → China | ~6,500 | 15-20 | Baseline |
| US Gulf → NE Asia (via Cape) | ~12,000-16,000 | 35-45 | 2-2.5x distance |
| West Africa → China (via Cape) | ~10,000 | 28-35 | ~1.5-2x distance |
| Brazil → China (via Cape) | ~11,000 | 30-40 | ~2x distance |
| North Sea → NE Asia (via Suez) | ~10,000 | 25-30 | ~1.5x distance |

When Asian refiners source from the Atlantic basin instead of the Gulf, the global tanker fleet absorbs significantly more ton-miles per barrel. This can raise freight rates even when total oil volumes decline — the "ton-mile effect." During the 2026 crisis, [[Nadia Martin Wiggen]] ([[Stellen Capital]]) noted the rate drop enabled Asian refiners to book cheaper ships for the longer Atlantic basin voyages after initial floating storage was exhausted.
