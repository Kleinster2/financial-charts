# Iran conflict war duration

How long the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|2026 Iran conflict]] lasts is the single most important variable for markets. Duration determines whether the energy shock is a one-quarter earnings hit or a structural repricing of global supply chains. This note tracks the most credentialed assessments and market-implied probabilities.

---

## Gass assessment (March 12)

Sir [[Simon Gass]] (former UK ambassador to Iran, former JIC chair, [[JCPOA]] lead negotiator) — the most credentialed Western analyst on Iranian decision-making — assessed the conflict's trajectory on FT's *Rachman Review* (March 12, 2026):

Duration: Doesn't expect a long war. Military leaders will tell Trump: "We've attacked pretty much all of our primary targets... Boss, if you want us to keep going, we'll keep going. But we are not honestly sure that it's gonna have a great deal of additional effect." War "not popular with the MAGA base"; midterm elections in November. Oil price is "an important determinant in the question of how long this conflict goes on for."

Iran's endgame: Regime aims for narrative victory ("small nation took on the combined might of the United States") + demonstrated [[Strait of Hormuz]] capability as permanent future deterrent. After a US ceasefire, Iran may fire drones "another 24, 36 hours... to show they are ending the war on their terms."

### Market implications

- Supports the "short war" thesis that equity markets priced on Days 3-5 — but with an important caveat: even a short war leaves a [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium|permanent Hormuz risk premium]]. Iran proved it can close the strait; future crises will price this differently.
- [[Gideon Rachman]] (FT, March 16): "Iran has discovered a powerful future deterrent that is quite independent of nuclear weapons. If the regime survives this war, it may yet emerge in a stronger position internationally."
- War aims have collapsed to a single objective: reopen Hormuz. Rachman: "A conflict with Iran that started with vague war aims now has one clear and overriding objective: reopen the Strait of Hormuz." The war ends when the strait reopens, not when military targets are exhausted.
- Escalation signals: [[Kharg Island]] occupation and ground troops near the strait are being mooted. Either would escalate market risk significantly.
- Gulf states "extraordinarily angry" at Iran, Israel, *and* the US — questioning "what is the value of a US security blanket." Some in [[UAE]] and [[Bahrain]] questioning whether Abraham Accords were the right bet. Bearish for Gulf FDI confidence and long-term Gulf equity risk premiums.
- Gass's "existential trigger" thesis: Trump's Day 1 "free the Iranian people" rhetoric (not the strikes alone) is what activated the Hormuz closure. Implies that a ceasefire + face-saving off-ramp that avoids regime-change language could de-escalate faster than the military situation alone suggests.
- Nuclear fatwa lapsed: [[Ali Khamenei]]'s anti-nuclear weapons fatwa died with him. Successor [[Mojtaba Khamenei]] has not reissued it. Creates a longer-term nuclear risk premium for the region — even if the current war ends soon.

*Source: FT, The Rachman Review (March 12, 2026)*

## Pentagon budget as duration signal (March 20)

The Pentagon's $200B supplemental request — roughly 1/4 of current defense budget, roughly 1/4 of entire [[Iraq]] war cost ($800B over 8 years) — signals the Pentagon's own plan is NOT for 2-3 weeks. [[Mick Mulroy]] ([[Middle East Institute|MEI]]): bluster won't work against [[Iran]], which endured maximum pressure for 6-7 years and knows the US faces midterm elections. ## Polymarket prediction markets (March 23)

### Ceasefire market

![[polymarket-iran-ceasefire-mar23.png]]
*Polymarket "US x Iran ceasefire by...?" — $38M volume, March 23, 2026.*

| Contract | Probability | Implied read |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| April 30 | 46% | Coin flip on near-term resolution |
| May 31 | 56% | Slight majority expects Q2 deal |
| June 30 | 63% | Two-thirds chance by mid-year |
| December 31 | 74% | Market prices conflict ending in 2026 |

The spread tells the story: 28 points between April (46%) and December (74%) means the market sees resolution as likely but not imminent. Consistent with Gass's assessment that the war won't be long, but Trump's "unconditional surrender" posture and the MAGA base's discomfort need time to produce an off-ramp.

All four curves have trended down since ~March 15 — April dropped from ~55% to 46%, December from ~80% to 74%. The market grew more skeptical about near-term resolution over the past week, coinciding with the [[Kuwait]] force majeure, continued [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure, and the Pentagon's $200B supplemental request signaling longer-than-expected duration.

Even the April 30 contract at 46% may be optimistic for market-relevant resolution. [[Energy Aspects]] pushed their Hormuz reopening estimate from end of March to end of April and flagged that commercial shipping won't resume on a ceasefire announcement alone — "Every company CEO I'm speaking with is saying that I don't want to be the first one to send my ship through." The [[Iran conflict insurance impact|insurance lag]] (30-60 day incident-free window before standard policies reinstate) means Hormuz shipping recovery trails any diplomatic resolution by months.

### Ground entry market

![[polymarket-iran-ground-entry-mar23.png]]
*Polymarket "US forces enter Iran by..?" — $26M volume, March 23, 2026.*

| Contract | Probability |
|----------|-------------|
| March 31 | 21% |
| April 30 | 47% |
| December 31 | 62% |

Read together, the two markets frame the range of outcomes. The ceasefire market prices April 30 at 46%; the ground entry market prices the same date at 47%. Near-parity on a coin flip — the market sees roughly equal odds of diplomatic resolution and ground escalation by end of April. The contracts aren't mutually exclusive (forces could enter then a ceasefire follows), but the symmetry reveals deep uncertainty about which direction this breaks.

The March 31 contract crashed from ~40% to 21% over the past two weeks — the market initially priced a fast ground entry, then pushed the timeline out as the air campaign continued without a ground phase. The April 30 contract spiked from ~40% to 47% in the same period, absorbing that probability. December 31 at 62% implies a meaningful (~38%) chance of no ground entry at all in 2026.

The Pentagon's $200B supplemental request cuts both ways: it signals duration commitment (supporting both ground entry probability and ceasefire skepticism), but the scale also suggests the administration wants Congressional buy-in before escalating — which takes time and creates a political veto point. [[Mick Mulroy]]'s assessment that the Pentagon's budget signals are "NOT for 2-3 weeks" aligns with the April-not-March shift in the ground entry curves.
