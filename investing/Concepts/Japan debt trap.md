# Japan debt trap

[[Japan]] debt trap is the feedback loop in which high public debt, [[BOJ]] balance-sheet support, rising [[JGB]] yields, and [[Japanese yen|yen]] depreciation stop being separate macro variables and start reinforcing each other. The live 2026 version is not simply "the country has too much debt." It is that the BOJ is trying to restore price discovery in the JGB market slowly enough to avoid a fiscal accident, while the currency market prices the risk that yields are still being held below the level needed to compensate investors for its debt stock.

[[Robin J. Brooks]] framed the loop in May 2026 as a residual yield-suppression problem. If long-end JGB yields are kept below equilibrium by ongoing BOJ purchases, risk premia do not fully appear in bond yields. They leak into the exchange rate instead, pushing the real yen lower and forcing the authorities into periodic FX intervention. The intervention then becomes another denial mechanism: it supports the currency without letting the domestic bond price fully clear.

## Synthesis

The central signal is the sequencing conflict between currency stabilization and bond-market clearing. [[Japan]] can push the adjustment into the yen, into the long end of the JGB curve, or into the public balance sheet through asset sales, but it cannot avoid the adjustment entirely. Brooks' asset-sale route matters because it offers a way to reduce gross debt without relying on BOJ absorption, but the IMF's institutional caveat turns the idea from a clean market transaction into a governance problem.

## Mechanism

1. Japan carries one of the world's highest public-debt burdens.
2. The BOJ remains a large JGB buyer even after ending formal [[Yield curve control|yield curve control]].
3. Ongoing purchases dampen long-end yields and slow the return of private price discovery.
4. Investors express the missing risk premium through yen weakness.
5. Yen weakness raises imported-inflation and political pressure.
6. FX intervention can slow depreciation, but it does not solve the suppressed-yield problem.
7. Higher JGB yields would improve price discovery but worsen debt-service optics and expose fiscal choices.

The trap is the policy sequencing problem: faster normalization stabilizes the currency but risks a bond-market accident; slower normalization cushions the bond market but leaves the yen as the adjustment valve.

## Asset-sale escape route

Brooks' proposed escape is straightforward: sell enough government financial assets to pay down debt, reducing the pressure on JGB yields and the yen. The logic is market-facing. Even a credible first step could tell investors that Japan is using its balance sheet to reduce gross debt rather than relying on BOJ absorption and FX intervention.

The IMF caveat is the implementation constraint. Its 2026 Article IV notes that many general-government financial assets are encumbered, managed by agencies with operational independence, or held by social security funds for future benefits. It estimates that social security funds held nearly half of general-government financial assets excluding domestic equities at end-FY2024. That does not make Brooks' proposal wrong, but it means "sell the assets" is not a clean treasury operation. It is a political and institutional restructuring problem.

## Market implications

The clean market signal is the joint behavior of the yen and the long end of the JGB curve. If the yen keeps weakening while 30-year yields rise, markets are saying that the adjustment is not complete. If yields rise enough and the yen stabilizes, the debt-trap narrative weakens. If FX intervention supports the yen while long-end JGBs keep selling off, the pressure has merely moved from one market to another.

For global markets, the second-order risk is repatriation. Higher domestic yields give Japanese insurers, pensions, households, and asset managers more reason to buy JGBs rather than foreign bonds. That can put pressure on [[Treasuries]] and other developed-market duration even if the initial problem is domestic to Japan.
