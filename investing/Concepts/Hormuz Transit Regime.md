# Hormuz Transit Regime

[[Iran]] has converted the [[Strait of Hormuz]] blockade from a blunt denial-of-service weapon into a gatekeeping operation — charging transit fees, requiring bilateral diplomatic approval, and floating a [[yuan]] settlement condition for oil cargoes. The result is a de facto toll system on the world's most critical energy chokepoint, one that simultaneously generates revenue for a wartime regime, splits the coalition of affected countries into individual supplicants, and probes the structural foundations of the [[Petrodollar|petrodollar]] order.

## The story

The blockade was always going to evolve. When [[Iran]] closed the [[Strait of Hormuz]] on February 28 in response to US-Israeli strikes, the initial posture was binary: closed to everyone except Iranian-flagged vessels bound for [[China]]. But a binary blockade is strategically wasteful — it punishes potential allies alongside enemies and foregoes the leverage that selective access provides. By the second week of March, Iran shifted to a permission-based regime that gives it far more granular control.

The first visible change was bilateral access negotiations. [[China]], [[India]], [[Pakistan]], [[Iraq]], and [[Malaysia]] all began direct talks with Tehran to secure passage for their vessels (Lloyd's List, Foreign Policy, Mar 23). The [[FDD]] confirmed the same list on March 24. By March 20, a trickle of non-Iranian ships had transited — roughly 90 total since the war began, mostly Iranian shadow tankers, but a handful of Indian LPG carriers (including the Shivalik, which reached Mundra Port on March 16), at least one Pakistani oil tanker, and a few others operating with Iranian clearance. [[Japan]] received a public offer of access on March 21 (Al Jazeera). The pattern was consistent: countries negotiate bilaterally with Tehran, obtain case-by-case approval, and their vessels hug the Iranian coast within the 12-24 nautical mile EEZ for protection.

This is already a profound shift from pre-war international maritime law. The [[Strait of Hormuz]] falls under Part III of [[UNCLOS]] (straits used for international navigation), where Article 38 establishes transit passage rights that "shall not be impeded" and cannot be suspended. But all three principal belligerents — the US, [[Israel]], and Iran — are non-signatories to UNCLOS, making enforcement theoretical. Iran has never ratified the convention and has historically claimed sovereign rights over the strait. The war has created the conditions for Iran to operationalize that claim. As [[Lawfare]] noted (March 22), the limits of maritime law are exposed when the state controlling the chokepoint has both the military means and the wartime justification to enforce its own rules.

Then came the money. On March 23, Iranian lawmaker [[Alaeddin Boroujerdi]], a member of Parliament's National Security and Foreign Policy Committee, stated on Iranian television that Iran had established a "new concept of sovereignty" over the strait after 47 years and was now charging certain vessels $2 million for passage. "Collecting $2 million as transit fees from some vessels crossing the strait reflects Iran's strength," he said (Anadolu Agency). Iran's Foreign Ministry initially distanced itself from the statement, calling it personal remarks. But [[Bloomberg]] confirmed on March 24, citing people familiar with the matter, that Iran had indeed begun charging some commercial vessels up to $2 million per trip — on an irregular, case-by-case basis. The payment mechanisms and currencies remain unclear. Bloomberg also reported that while Iran currently demands fees ad hoc, it has floated the idea of legalizing these charges as part of a broader post-war settlement — one unnamed source said parliament is moving forward with a proposal to codify the right to charge transit fees.

[[India]] pushed back immediately, stating that international law guarantees freedom of navigation and that no party has the right to impose fees for using the passage. Arab Gulf producers — whose own crude must transit the strait — view any fees as unacceptable, citing sovereignty concerns and the first-of-its-kind precedent. But their objections carry little weight when their production is shut in and their tankers are idle. The asymmetry Boroujerdi described is real: Iran controls the geography, and geography is charging rent.

The yuan dimension adds a second layer. CNN reported in mid-March, citing a senior Iranian official, that Tehran was considering allowing a limited number of oil tankers through the strait on the condition that their cargo be traded in [[yuan|Chinese yuan]] rather than US dollars. The [[South China Morning Post]] (SCMP) confirmed the report and noted that Chinese analysts reacted cautiously, citing "operational feasibility limits and security risks" and the potential to strain [[China]]-US ties. The proposal has three strategic incentives for Iran, as economist Kashif Hasan Khan (Paragon International University) argued in an Asia Times op-ed: sanctions evasion (moving transactions outside dollar-clearing channels reduces Washington's coercive reach), strategic reciprocity with [[China]] (rewarding Beijing for remaining Iran's oil lifeline), and a symbolic challenge to the [[Petrodollar|petrodollar]] order.

The petrodollar challenge is real in direction but limited in scale. The yuan accounts for roughly 2% of global foreign exchange reserves versus 57% for the dollar (CNN, Feb 2026). China still maintains capital controls, the [[PBOC]] lacks true independence, and the financial system underpinning the yuan is not yet trusted at scale. Even PBOC Deputy Governor Zhu Hexin's claim that the yuan now settles 30% of China's $6.2 trillion goods trade (53% including all cross-border payments) reflects bilateral flows, not global reserve displacement. As Kashif Khan wrote: the yuan-for-Hormuz proposal "should be read less as a technical payment proposal than as a geopolitical signal — a deliberate attempt to fuse military geography with monetary strategy."

That said, the signaling matters precisely because the petrodollar system has always rested on *presumption* as much as infrastructure. The 1970s US-Saudi deal (Saudi prices oil in USD, buys US weapons and Treasuries) worked because no one had the leverage or incentive to challenge it at a critical chokepoint. [[Iran]] now has both. Even if the yuan condition proves impractical or temporary, the precedent — that a state controlling a physical bottleneck can dictate the settlement currency of the cargo transiting it — is a new vector of de-dollarization pressure that didn't exist before February 28, 2026. Antonio Bhardwaj (Foreign Affairs Forum) called it "the most operationally significant manifestation of a structural realignment" toward a [[petroyuan]] framework. That overstates the near-term reality but captures the directional risk.

The [[Trump II|Trump]] administration's own actions are accelerating the conditions for this challenge. [[William Pesek]] (Asia Times, March 24) argued that Trump is "inadvertently making the idea of a petro-yuan great again" — waging simultaneous trade wars (tariffs) and military wars (Iran) while US debt approaches $40 trillion, providing [[China]] with both the motivation and the framing to position the yuan as an alternative. At the March 2026 China Development Forum, Premier Li Qiang pitched China as a "cornerstone of certainty" to 70+ CEOs including Apple's [[Tim Cook]], directly contrasting Beijing's stability with Washington's chaos.

The investment implications cascade across three timeframes. In the immediate term (weeks), the $2 million toll is a rounding error on a VLCC carrying $200 million of crude — it adds ~1% to cargo costs, well below the war-risk insurance premium already being charged. The real cost is the *process*: bilateral negotiation, Iranian approval, route constraints within the EEZ. This adds days of delay and uncertainty to every transit, which compounds into logistics costs and inventory management headaches for refiners. In the medium term (months), if Iran codifies transit fees in legislation and the post-war settlement includes any recognition of Iranian "sovereignty" over strait passage, it creates a permanent extraction mechanism — a chokepoint tax that gets embedded into the cost structure of Gulf crude. In the long term (years), the yuan settlement condition, even if it remains aspirational, adds another data point to the [[De-dollarization|de-dollarization]] trend. The question isn't whether Iran can force yuan settlement on all Hormuz traffic (it can't), but whether this precedent encourages other chokepoint-adjacent states — [[China]] at the [[Taiwan Strait]], future actors at the [[Strait of Malacca]] — to think in similar terms about fusing geography with monetary power.

### IRGC clearance procedure (Lloyd's List, Mar 25)

Ships must contact IRGC-connected intermediaries and submit: vessel identification number, full ownership chain, cargo manifest, destination, and complete crew list. Documentation is forwarded to IRGC Navy's Hormozgan Provincial Command for three checks:

1. Sanctions screening
2. Cargo alignment (oil currently prioritized over all other commodities)
3. "Geopolitical vetting"

If cleared, the IRGC issues a clearance code and route instructions. A pilot boat escorts the vessel through Iranian territorial waters around Larak Island. Since March 15, no vessel has used the normal (non-IRGC) route — all transits are IRGC-controlled.

The legal exposure for compliant shippers is severe. The IRGC is classified as a Foreign Terrorist Organisation under US law. Claire McCleskey (former OFAC compliance official): any payments, even indirect, "risk criminal prosecution" under material support statutes. Manny Levitt (Holland & Knight): even if toll payments were covered under the recent US oil waiver for pre-March 20 loads, they "would not be protected under UK or EU sanctions" and do not shield companies from anti-terrorism liability. Despite the risks, Lloyd's List reports security firms have been "inundated" with requests from ship owners seeking legal clarity.
