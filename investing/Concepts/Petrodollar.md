# Petrodollar

System where oil is priced and traded in USD. Underpins dollar's reserve currency status. Under pressure from [[BRICS]] de-dollarization efforts.

---

## Synthesis

The petrodollar is two coexisting systems with different time horizons. The structural layer — [[US dollar|USD]] pricing for global oil, dollar recycling into [[Treasuries]], reserve-currency demand — is eroding slowly: USD share of allocated FX reserves fell from 65% in 2014 to 57% in Q4 2025 ([[IMF]] COFER), a -8pp decade trend driven by voluntary diversification rather than collapse. The cyclical layer — discrete shocks that compress the timeline — has activated twice since 2022.

The 2022 weaponisation of Russian central-bank reserves was the first shock: it told every central bank that dollar holdings were politically conditional. The response was diversification into [[Gold]] and bilateral local-currency settlement, not abandonment. The 2026 [[Hormuz Transit Regime]] crisis is the second and more consequential — a chokepoint operator attempted to *mandate* non-dollar settlement as a transit condition, shifting the threat from voluntary diversification by reserve holders to coercive imposition by geography-controlling states.

[[Saudi Arabia]] is the system's load-bearing column — largest [[OPEC]] exporter, original 1973 partner, and the only producer whose move to yuan invoicing would be unambiguous rather than symbolic. Saudi has flirted with non-USD pricing repeatedly without committing; the signal to watch is an official pricing change, not the continued speculation.

The investment implication is asymmetric: gradual reserve-share decline does not break the system, but each 1pp compounds — fewer automatic [[Treasuries]] bidders, one fewer support beam. The terminal risk is not sudden collapse but a *rerating* — dollar assets pricing in their political conditionality, with [[Gold]] and non-US energy claims the natural reweighting destinations.

## How petrodollar works

### The cycle
1. [[Oil]] globally priced in USD
2. [[Oil]] importers must hold dollars
3. Exporters receive dollars, recycle into US assets
4. Supports US Treasury demand and dollar value

### Origins (1970s)
- US-Saudi deal after 1973 embargo
- Saudi prices oil in USD, buys US weapons/[[Treasuries]]
- Other OPEC members followed
- Became global standard

## 1970s/80s vs today — what's different

The headline difference: the 1970s/80s petrodollar was a *constructed* system; today's is *residual*. Everything else follows from that.

In 1974, Nixon-Kissinger negotiated an explicit bilateral compact with [[Saudi Arabia]] — price oil in USD, recycle surpluses into [[Treasuries]], in exchange for US military protection. The system worked because (a) the US had abandoned [[Bretton Woods]] in 1971 and needed a new reason for foreign dollar demand, (b) the 1973 embargo had quadrupled Saudi surplus dollars overnight, (c) [[Deutsche Mark]] / sterling / yen were all secondary with no real alternative, and (d) Saudi needed security guarantees post-1973. The compact had named architects, named documents, named beneficiaries. For the full origin mechanics — circular flow, Eurodollar channel, the recycling-into-Third-World-debt-crisis story — see the history vault's [Petrodollar System](obsidian://open?vault=history&file=Themes%2FPetrodollar%20System) note.

Today's system persists by inertia and network effects, not by any explicit arrangement. The original compact has frayed across almost every dimension:

| Dimension | 1970s/80s | Today |
|---|---|---|
| Origin | Explicit Nixon-Kissinger / Faisal-Fahd compact | Inertia + network effects |
| US oil position | Net importer, declining domestic production | World's largest producer (>13 mb/d via [[US Shale|shale revolution]]) |
| Producer structure | [[OPEC]] dominant (~50% global output), Saudi swing producer | OPEC+ ~40%; tripolar with US + [[Russia]] |
| Saudi alignment | Aligned with US, exclusive USD pricing | Multi-vector — [[BRICS]] member, some yuan invoicing, still hosts US bases |
| Recycling channel | Saudi surplus → US Treasuries directly | GCC SWFs ([[Saudi PIF|PIF]], [[ADIA]], [[Kuwait Investment Authority|KIA]], [[Qatar Investment Authority|QIA]], ~$3–4T) diversified across equities, PE, infrastructure |
| USD reserve share | 70–80% (peaked ~1985) | 56.77% ([[IMF]] COFER Q4 2025), declining |
| Alternatives | None real | EUR (~20%), [[Chinese yuan\|CNY]] (~2%, rising), gold (record CB buying), BRICS settlement experiments |
| Pricing vs settlement | Inseparable — both USD | Pricing in USD persists ([[Brent crude|Brent]]/[[WTI]] benchmarks); settlement bifurcates (Russia-China yuan, [[Iran]] sanctions-evasion) |
| Sanctions use | Sparingly ([[Iranian Revolution\|Iran 1979]] was the exception) | Routinely — SWIFT cutoffs, secondary sanctions, 2022 Russian reserve freeze |
| Threat axis | Theoretical de-dollarization | Operational coercion ([[Hormuz Transit Regime]] 2026) |
| US fiscal backdrop | Debt ~30% of GDP | Debt 120%+ of GDP, persistent ~$2T/yr deficits |

### Three structural shifts dominate

The recycling channel has loosened. In the 1980s, Saudi Treasury holdings were a load-bearing pillar — petrodollar surplus flowed almost directly into Treasury auctions, cheapening US borrowing. Today, GCC sovereign wealth funds run ~$3–4T diversified globally, with Treasuries one allocation among many. The mechanical link between oil revenue and US debt demand is weaker.

The pricing/settlement bifurcation. Oil is still *priced* on [[Brent crude|Brent]]/[[WTI]] USD benchmarks — that has enormous inertia (every long-term contract, every pipeline tariff, every hedge references them). But *settlement* is splintering: Russia-China trade in yuan/ruble, some Saudi-China yuan invoicing, Iran sanctions-evasion in yuan. The petrodollar's reserve-currency support runs through settlement volume, not pricing benchmark, so this matters more than it looks.

The threat is now coercion, not voluntary diversification. In the 1980s, the only conceivable challenge was a producer voluntarily switching invoicing. Today's threats are qualitatively different: voluntary diversification by reserve holders (already manifested in the -8pp [[IMF]] COFER trend since 2014), *and* coercive imposition by chokepoint operators (Iran's 2026 yuan-for-Hormuz demand via the [[Hormuz Transit Regime]]). Neither existed as operational threats in the 1980s.

### Bottom line

In the 1970s, the petrodollar *strengthened* dollar dominance against a weak fiscal backdrop ([[Bretton Woods]] had just collapsed; US debt was low; dollar was in crisis but the system gave it a new reason for being). Today, it's one of several support beams that's gradually *weakening* against a much weaker fiscal backdrop (debt at 120% of GDP, persistent deficits, sanctions-driven counterparty incentive to diversify). The system that was a deliberate construction in 1974 is now a structural inheritance in 2026 — durable but eroding, and facing threat vectors that didn't exist when it was built.

## Is "petrodollar" still the right name?

The honest answer: the word still works for the *mechanism* but is misleading about the *system*. Two senses worth distinguishing — and they've come apart.

### Sense 1 — pricing/settlement convention
Oil priced in USD on [[Brent crude|Brent]] / [[WTI]] / Dubai-Oman benchmarks, ~85–90% of physical trade still settled in dollars, ~$2–3T/yr in oil flows still generating structural USD demand. This persists. Mechanically intact.

### Sense 2 — strategic compact
The 1974 Nixon-Kissinger-Faisal arrangement: Saudi USD pricing + direct Treasury recycling, in exchange for US security guarantee. This is dead. The recycling channel has loosened (GCC SWFs run ~$3–4T diversified globally, no longer concentrated in Treasuries). [[Saudi Arabia]] is multi-vector ([[BRICS]] member, partial yuan invoicing, still hosts US bases). No surviving documents, named beneficiaries, or operational rules. The compact dissolved gradually through the 2010s and is gone today.

Using "petrodollar" without qualification today implicitly invokes sense 2 when only sense 1 is operative. That matters analytically:

- Predicting Saudi behaviour through the "compact" lens gets the wrong answer (Saudi acts on economic interest + MbS geopolitical positioning + reserve diversification, not 1974 commitments)
- Treating Treasury demand resilience as a function of oil revenues misses the SWF diversification shift since 2010
- Underestimating settlement bifurcation speed because "the compact will hold" — it already doesn't

### Three serious alternatives in circulation

- [[Bretton Woods III]] ([[Zoltan Pozsar|Pozsar]], March 2022) — the strongest version of the "no, it's not petrodollar anymore" claim. Argues the post-2022 sanctions era is a wholesale system replacement: commodity-and-geopolitics-backed monetary order substituting "outside money" (gold, commodities, real assets) for "inside money" (Treasuries). Track record so far is directionally validated but slower than the most aggressive 2022 framing implied.
- "Dollar system" / "dollar hegemony" ([[Barry Eichengreen|Eichengreen]], [[Adam Tooze|Tooze]]) — broader than oil; emphasises the ecosystem (Treasury depth, SWIFT, sanctions enforcement, network effects) rather than the oil-specific mechanic. Avoids the compact-vs-convention confusion entirely.
- "Post-compact petrodollar" / "residual petrodollar" — preserves continuity with the term while flagging the rupture. Less elegant but most honest if you want to keep the word.

### Working resolution

Use "petrodollar" only for the pricing-and-settlement convention (sense 1), and add a qualifier when the audience might still associate the term with the 1974 arrangement.

For investment work — where the operative question is "how much structural USD demand does oil still generate?" — the convention sense is what matters and "petrodollar" is fine. For geopolitical work — where the operative question is "will Saudi switch invoicing?" — the compact sense is what matters and "petrodollar" without qualification is misleading. The [[Bretton Woods III]] framing is the cleanest alternative when the analysis turns on sanctions-conditionality of dollar reserves rather than oil-specific mechanics.

## [[Hormuz Transit Regime]] — 2026 operational test

The 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] crisis introduced the first operational attempt to fuse physical chokepoint control with currency settlement demands. A senior Iranian official told [[CNN]] (mid-March) that Tehran was considering allowing limited tanker passage only if cargo is traded in [[Chinese yuan|yuan]]. This goes beyond prior de-dollarization efforts (which were voluntary bilateral agreements between willing buyers and sellers) — it attempts to *mandate* non-dollar settlement as a condition of physical transit through the world's most critical energy bottleneck. Even if impractical at scale, the precedent matters: a state controlling geography can attempt to dictate the currency of commerce passing through it. See [[Hormuz Transit Regime]] for full analysis.
