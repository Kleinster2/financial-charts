#concept #macro #fed

**Greenspan put** — Market expectation that the [[Federal Reserve]] will cut rates or provide liquidity to rescue falling asset prices. Named for [[Alan Greenspan]]'s response to 1987 crash.

[[Alan Greenspan]] — Fed chair 1987-2006, "the Maestro" — died June 22, 2026, aged 100. The concept bearing his name has outlived both him and his tenure: the "Fed put" remains the central lens on the central bank's asymmetric reaction function, live right now in the [[Kevin Warsh|Warsh]]-era "turbocharged Greenspan put" tension (below). His death is primarily a macro-history event — the fuller biography and the legacy debates (1990s deregulation, the "irrational exuberance" warning, the 2008 GFC culpability question) belong to the history vault; what persists in markets is the eponymous reaction-function expectation.

---

## Mechanism

| Phase | Fed action | Market effect |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| Market stress | Cut rates, inject liquidity | Limits downside |
| Recovery | Slow to tighten | Encourages risk-taking |
| Next cycle | Repeat | Asymmetric payoff profile |

Moral hazard: investors take more risk knowing the Fed will cushion falls.

---

## Historical examples

| Event | Fed Chair | Response |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 1987 crash | Greenspan | Immediate liquidity |
| 1998 LTCM | Greenspan | Rate cuts |
| 2008 GFC | Bernanke | QE, ZIRP |
| 2020 COVID | Powell | Unlimited QE |

## Warsh tension (Jan 2026)

[[Martin Sandbu]] argues the real challenge for [[Kevin Warsh]] isn't monetary policy but navigating the tension between:
- His "tough love" instinct (let markets correct)
- Trump's expectation of a "turbocharged Greenspan put" at first sign of trouble

> "That tension, rather than monetary policy, may be the real challenge Warsh will sooner or later have to face."
