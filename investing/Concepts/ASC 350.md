#concept #accounting

**ASC 350** — FASB standard governing goodwill and other intangible assets. The standard that determines when companies must write down acquisitions that didn't work out.

---

## Goodwill impairment mechanics

Goodwill arises when a company pays more than the fair value of net assets in an acquisition. Under ASC 350, goodwill is not amortized — it sits on the balance sheet until impaired.

### Testing framework

| Step | Test | Outcome |
|------|------|---------|
| Qualitative screen (optional) | "More likely than not" that fair value < carrying value? | If no → skip quantitative test |
| Quantitative test | Compare reporting unit fair value to carrying value | If fair value < carrying value → impairment = the difference |

Testing is required annually (same date each year) plus whenever a "triggering event" occurs (stock price decline, loss of major customer, adverse regulation, etc.).

### Reporting units and the [[CODM]] link

Goodwill is tested at the reporting unit level — defined as an operating segment or one level below. The [[CODM]]'s segment structure under [[ASC 280]] directly determines where goodwill gets allocated and tested.

This creates a strategic interaction: if a company restructures its segments (changes how the CODM reviews the business), goodwill must be reallocated across the new reporting units. A restructuring that concentrates goodwill into a healthy reporting unit can shield it from impairment. Conversely, splitting a segment can expose previously hidden impairment.

## Why it matters for investors

### The impairment signal

Goodwill impairment charges are almost always confirmation of what the market already knows — the acquisition overpaid. But they matter because:

- They reduce book value and can trigger debt covenant issues
- They signal management acknowledging failure (or being forced to by auditors)
- The timing reveals management incentives — new CEOs often "kitchen sink" impairments in their first year

### IFRS vs US GAAP

| Feature | ASC 350 (US GAAP) | IAS 36 (IFRS) |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Amortization | Not permitted (public cos) | Not permitted |
| Impairment reversal | Never allowed | Never allowed (goodwill only) |
| Testing level | Reporting unit | Cash-generating unit (CGU) — typically smaller |
| Private company option | Can amortize over ≤10 years | No equivalent |

The CGU vs reporting unit difference matters: IFRS tests goodwill at a more granular level, making impairment more likely to be triggered earlier.
