#concept #lithium #geography #latam #mining

**Lithium Triangle** — The geographic-political frame naming the lithium-brine resource basin shared by [[Chile]], [[Argentina]], and [[Bolivia]] around the Puna Plateau of the central Andes. Together the three countries hold roughly 56% of known global lithium reserves; they produce a far smaller share because of political fragmentation — each country has chosen a different development model, and those divergent choices define the investability of their respective producers.

---

## The geography

The Puna Plateau is a high-altitude desert basin (~3,700m) spanning the Andean borderlands. Solar evaporation is viable (low rainfall, intense sunlight), and the brine lithium concentrations are among the richest in the world. The three salares that anchor the triangle:

| Salar | Country | Key producer |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Salar de Atacama | [[Chile]] | [[SQM]], [[Albemarle]] |
| Salar del Hombre Muerto | [[Argentina]] | Arcadium / Rio Tinto, Livent-legacy |
| Salar de Olaroz / Cauchari | [[Argentina]] | Allkem-legacy, Lithium Americas |
| Salar de Uyuni | [[Bolivia]] | YLB (Yacimientos de Litio Bolivianos) |

## Reserves vs production — the gap

| Country | M&I resources (Mt) | 2024 production (t) | Share of known reserves |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------------------|
| [[Bolivia]] | 23 | Few hundred | **~21%** (largest globally) |
| [[Argentina]] | 28 | 18,000 | Substantial |
| [[Chile]] | 13 | **~49,000** | Second-largest |

*Source: USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries 2026, Harvard International Review, regional agencies.*

The inversion is the story: **Bolivia has the most lithium but produces the least.** Chile has less than half Bolivia's reserves but produces more than 100x the output. The difference is political institutional capacity, not geology.

## Three different development models

### Chile — partial state control

Chile runs the most investable model of the three, which is still in flux. In April 2023 the Boric government announced the **National Lithium Strategy** — reclaim global leadership by routing all new lithium concessions through state-anchored public-private partnerships.

The flagship deliverable is the **[[SQM]]-[[Codelco]] partnership**, formalized as **Nova Andino Litio SpA** in December 2025:

- [[SQM]] manages the joint venture 2025-2030.
- [[Codelco]] takes over management 2031-2060.
- The Chilean state receives **~70% of operating margin 2025-2030** and **85% from 2031**.
- Incremental production of **~300,000 tonnes LCE** expected 2025-2030.
- Atacama operation secured through 2060.

**Political transition risk:** Boric handed power to [[José Antonio Kast]] in March 2026 after the Chilean right won the November 2025 presidential election. Kast has pledged to make lithium "concessionable" — removing its classification as a nuclear material and streamlining private access. That would shift Chile's model back toward market-led. The Codelco-SQM partnership has contractual protection through 2060, but new concessions would be governed by Kast's framework.

### Argentina — market-open

Argentina runs the most open investment climate under the Milei administration (2023-). No state-ownership mandates, no royalty hikes, provincial-level negotiations.

Argentina's **installed LCE production capacity has grown from 35,500 tpa to 186,000 tpa** in the 2023-2026 window. The Arcadium/Rio Tinto-backed Rincon DLE expansion ($2.5B) and Livent-legacy Hombre Muerto expansion are the marquee scale-ups. Argentina released a new 10-year lithium plan in early 2026 targeting >250,000 tpa LCE by 2035.

The Argentine model is attracting the fastest capex response: low tax burden, dollar-convertible capital regime (post-2024 reforms), and integration with [[Chile]]'s processing infrastructure via Pacific ports.

### Bolivia — state-led, fragile, in transition

Bolivia's Salar de Uyuni holds ~21% of global lithium reserves, but the country produces almost nothing. The state-owned **[[YLB]]** (Yacimientos de Litio Bolivianos) runs operations, but technical execution has been limited and political instability has undermined partnership deals.

**Key developments:**

- A **$1B deal with Chinese investors** (CBC consortium, includes [[CATL]]) to build two lithium plants targeting 35,000 tpa.
- A Russian (Rosatom-related, Uranium One) $1B deal running parallel.
- **July 2025**: Bolivian Congress descended into water-throwing, shouting matches, and protests over ratifying the China and Russia agreements. Opposition framing: foreign dependency is being swapped for Chinese/Russian dependency without adequate technology transfer. Ratification was blocked.
- **November 8, 2025 — political transition**: [[Rodrigo Paz]] succeeded Luis Arce, the first non-MAS president in two decades. Paz's stated framework is "modernization without privatization" — reviewing the CBC and Rosatom contracts for transparency and local-community terms, with commitment not to "sell out" Uyuni but signaling openness to renegotiated foreign partnerships along Chilean Nova Andino Litio lines.

Bolivia's resource endowment is the largest in the world on paper, but the absence of institutional and technical depth means reserves stay in the ground. The Paz transition is the first major political inflection point for Bolivia's lithium thesis in a decade — whether it produces actual Uyuni output or becomes another stalled cycle remains the binary question. See [[Bolivia]] for full political context.

## Why coordination fails

The triangle does not function as an OPEC-style bloc, for structural reasons:

| Barrier | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Divergent models** | State-led ([[Bolivia]], [[Chile]]) vs market-open ([[Argentina]]) — hard to align price/volume policy |
| **Infrastructure gaps** | No integrated cross-border pipelines or processing; most output moves through [[Chile]]'s Pacific ports or trucked through [[Argentina]] to Brazilian processing |
| **Political cycles** | Chile and Argentina swung right (Kast, Milei) while [[Bolivia]] remains leftist — ideology shifts the model faster than any cartel agreement could stabilize |
| **Asymmetric producer scale** | Chile produces 100x [[Bolivia]]'s output; a three-way agreement would not bind enough volume to move prices |
| **Processing dominance elsewhere** | [[China]] controls ~60% of lithium refining — the triangle lacks midstream leverage even where it has upstream volume |

The absence of coordination is itself useful information. Unlike oil, lithium supply from the triangle is shaped by institutional choices at the country level, not by an organized producer cartel. Each country's political regime change produces a different supply response.

## Strategic importance

The triangle matters for three threads the vault tracks:

1. **Supply-side wildcard to the Apr 16 rally.** The [[Lithium]] Apr 16 sigma-dispersion event was driven by supply discipline in [[China]] and marginal producers (Zimbabwe export suspension). But the triangle represents the *next layer of potential supply* — if [[Argentina]] continues the 186 ktpa → 250 ktpa ramp, if [[Bolivia]] unlocks any meaningful production, or if Kast's concessionable framework pulls new Chilean capacity online, the Apr 16 rally could be short-lived.
2. **Test of resource nationalism as policy tool.** Chile's Codelco-SQM 70%-of-margin framework is the most aggressive state-revenue-capture structure in modern commodity history. Whether it produces durable investment or capital flight (see [[SQM]]'s future concession negotiations) is the real-time experiment.
3. **China's alternative supply source.** Chinese battery and auto groups are investing in all three countries — CATL in Bolivia JV, BYD and Chinese state players in Argentine brine projects, and Chinese off-take from [[SQM]]. The triangle is not an alternative to China; it is a *supply source* that China has partially secured.
