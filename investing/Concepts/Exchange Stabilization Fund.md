# Exchange Stabilization Fund

US Treasury vehicle created in 1934 that can provide foreign-exchange and emergency financing support outside the [[Federal Reserve]]'s standard [[Currency swap lines|swap-line]] architecture. In the 2026 [[Iran]] and Hormuz shock it mattered because the WSJ reported Treasury had recently approved a $20 billion swap arrangement for [[Argentina]] through the ESF and Gulf officials were exploring whether Treasury-backed liquidity could substitute for a formal Fed swap line.

## Why it matters

The ESF is one of the few tools Washington can use when a foreign official-sector borrower wants dollars but the [[Federal Reserve]] is unwilling to extend a standing or temporary swap line. That makes it a bridge between diplomacy and monetary plumbing. The [[UAE]] wartime backstop discussion highlighted exactly that distinction: the Fed was seen as unlikely to open a line, but Treasury had recently used the ESF for [[Argentina]], so Gulf officials had reason to test whether political allies could get Treasury-mediated dollar relief without entering the Fed's inner swap network.

In other words, the ESF is not just a historical stabilization fund. It is the part of the dollar system where geopolitics can still override the Fed's more rules-based crisis architecture.
