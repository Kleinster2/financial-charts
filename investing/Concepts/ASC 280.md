#concept #accounting

**ASC 280** — FASB standard governing how public companies report operating segments. The core principle: segments are defined by what the [[CODM]] actually reviews, not by org charts, legal entities, or what management finds convenient.

---

## Mechanics

A reportable segment must meet any one threshold:

| Test | Threshold |
|------|-----------|
| Revenue | ≥10% of combined revenue (internal + external) |
| Profit or loss | ≥10% of the greater of combined profit or combined loss |
| Assets | ≥10% of combined assets |

All reported segments must collectively account for ≥75% of external revenue. If not, additional segments are added until the threshold is met.

## ASU 2023-07 (Nov 2023 update)

The most significant tightening of segment disclosure in decades, effective FY2024 (calendar-year filers). Driven by investor complaints that segment reporting had become less useful as companies aggregated aggressively.

New requirements:
- Disclose significant segment expenses regularly provided to the [[CODM]]
- Disclose the title and position of the CODM (previously optional)
- Provide a measure of segment profit/loss that the CODM uses for resource allocation
- Interim periods get the same enhanced disclosures (previously annual only)

What it doesn't do: it doesn't force companies to create new segments. If [[Google|Alphabet]] bundles [[YouTube]] inside Google Services and [[Sundar Pichai]]'s formal CODM review is at the Google Services level, ASU 2023-07 won't split it out. But it will force Alphabet to disclose more about the costs within Google Services — which could indirectly reveal YouTube economics.

## Why it matters for investors

Segment reporting is the primary tool investors have for understanding conglomerate economics. When companies game it, valuation suffers:

| Company | Segment structure | What's hidden |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| [[Google\|Alphabet]] | Google Services, Cloud, Other Bets | [[YouTube]] P&L (~$54B revenue, margins unknown) |
| [[Amazon]] | North America, International, AWS | Advertising (~$56B) buried in segments |
| [[Meta]] | Family of Apps, Reality Labs | Reality Labs losses visible (~$16B/yr) — an example of disclosure working |
| Pre-2018 [[Amazon]] | Single segment | AWS profitability was invisible until 2015 breakout |

Amazon's 2015 AWS breakout is the canonical example: once investors saw AWS margins (~30% operating), they revalued the stock from ~$300 to $2,000+ over the next five years. The data was always there — the disclosure unlocked the repricing.

## The aggregation loophole

ASC 280 allows companies to aggregate operating segments if they have "similar economic characteristics" — similar margins, growth, customers, processes. This is subjective and heavily litigated:

- Companies argue segments are "similar" to reduce disclosure
- The [[SEC]] pushes back when aggregation obscures material differences
- The bar for "similar" is qualitative, not quantitative — no bright-line test

This is how companies with clearly distinct businesses (search vs video vs cloud) can argue they're "similar" enough to bundle.
