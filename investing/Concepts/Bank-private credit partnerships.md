# Bank-private credit partnerships

Structural pattern in which a major global bank and a major private-credit firm form a multi-year program to finance sub-investment-grade corporate borrowers, splitting the value chain so the bank originates deals through its client relationships while the private credit firm provides the capital. The pattern emerged in 2024 with the [[Citigroup|Citi]]-[[Apollo]] $25B North American program and replicated in EMEA in May 2026 with the [[Citigroup|Citi]]-[[HPS Investment Partners|HPS]] / [[BlackRock]] €15B program. It represents a transition away from the traditional bank-syndicates-loan model toward an explicit origination/capital separation that is becoming the dominant format for sub-IG corporate financing.

---

## The structure

In every observed instance the value chain splits along the same line:

| Role | Counterparty | What it brings | What it earns |
|---|---|---|---|
| Origination | Major global bank | Client relationships, deal flow, structuring expertise, geographic coverage | Origination fee (no balance-sheet exposure) |
| Capital | Major private credit firm | Scale capital pool, hold-to-maturity capacity, structuring depth on private debt | Interest income + private-credit fee economics |
| (Optional) Sub-capital | Sovereign wealth or insurance affiliate | Additional capital, longer-duration money | Yield on participation |

The economic distinction from a traditional syndicate is that the private credit firm *holds the paper to maturity* with the option to intervene early on covenant breaches, while the bank captures the *fee* without taking the credit risk onto its balance sheet. This is structurally different from the loan-syndication model in which the bank temporarily warehouses then distributes.

Other observed adjacent structures (less canonical, not yet meeting the full pattern):

- [[JPMorgan Chase]] direct-lending arrangements with multiple private credit firms — less formal, no single anchor partner
- [[Wells Fargo]] / [[Centerbridge]] joint venture (2023) — narrower scope
- [[Goldman Sachs]] / [[Mubadala]] private credit partnership — sovereign-anchored, different shape

## Why this pattern emerged

The structural conditions that made this format inevitable:

| Condition | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Post-GFC capital requirements | Banks under [[Basel III]] cannot economically hold middle-market and sub-IG loans on balance sheet at scale |
| Private credit accumulation | Major private credit firms accumulated $1.8T+ in AUM by 2024, looking for deal flow that matched their underwriting capabilities |
| Bank client retention pressure | Banks need to keep serving corporate clients who want larger / faster / more flexible financing than syndicated markets provide |
| Origination-fee economics | Banks earn high-margin fees on origination without consuming the regulatory capital that holding the loan would require |
| Private credit fundraising pressure | $234B raised in 2025 (PitchBook) needed to be deployed; capacity is now hunting for high-quality flow |

The result is a Pareto-improvement structure: banks earn fees without balance-sheet cost; private credit gets first-look access to bank-originated deals; corporate borrowers get faster certainty than syndicated markets can provide.

## Structural consequences

### For [[Banks|major banks]]

The format converts the most valuable bank asset — long-standing global corporate relationships — into a recurring fee stream. The implication is that the *return-on-tangible-equity* of a global bank can improve materially without growing the balance sheet, which is exactly the kind of structural earnings re-rating that turnaround banks like [[Citigroup]] need to close the valuation gap with [[JPMorgan Chase]] and [[Bank of America]]. The earlier expectation that private credit was a *threat* to bank lending is partially inverted by these programs: it becomes a *fee-generating distribution channel* for the bank instead.

### For [[Private Credit|private credit firms]]

The format gives the largest private credit firms structural distribution that no organic platform can replicate. This is a moat-deepener for the top names ([[Apollo]], [[HPS Investment Partners|HPS]] / [[BlackRock]], [[Ares Management|Ares]], [[Blackstone]], [[KKR]], [[Blue Owl]]) and a barrier-to-entry against smaller and emerging private credit firms. The capital concentration trend the [[Private Credit|sector hub]] has tracked (85.1% of 2025 private debt capital went to funds >$1B) is reinforced by this structure — only firms with scale can credibly anchor a bank's regional program.

### For the corporate and sponsor borrower

The format compresses execution timelines (typical private credit close: weeks vs months for syndicated). Pricing certainty is higher. Flexibility on terms (PIK, covenants, structure) is greater. The trade-off is that covenant breach triggers earlier intervention from a single concentrated holder rather than a dispersed syndicate — which can be a feature or a bug depending on the borrower's situation.

### For credit cycle dynamics

A market where sub-IG corporate debt is concentrated in private credit holders behaves differently in a downturn than one where the same debt is held in widely-syndicated loans. Recovery rates may be higher (private credit firms have institutional capacity to restructure rather than force fire-sales), but liquidity in the secondary loan market is thinner, and mark-to-market valuations are less transparent. The [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]] showed one side of this — when retail capital flows reverse, gated redemptions emerge. The [[JPMorgan private credit collateral markdown]] incident showed another — bank exposures to private credit fund structures carry their own marking and risk-transmission characteristics.

### For [[AI infrastructure financing risk|circular financing]] concerns

When the same bank is originating loans, the same private credit firm is funding them, and the same sovereign or insurance affiliate is providing sub-capital, the structural distance from a vertically-integrated capital pool is small. The [[AI infrastructure financing risk]] concerns about reflexive financing have a private-credit-side parallel: bank-private credit partnerships concentrate origination, capital, and (sometimes) sub-capital into closely-related counterparties, which compresses the system's natural diversity of capital sources.

## What to watch

| Signal | What it would indicate |
|---|---|
| Third regional Citi partnership (Asia/Pacific) | Citi has built a globally-distributed origination utility |
| JPM, BofA, GS, or MS launching equivalent multi-year programs | The format becomes the new standard for sub-IG corporate financing |
| Bank-private credit programs expanding to investment-grade | Pattern extends beyond its current sub-IG core |
| Disclosure of fee splits or economics | Currently confidential — disclosure would change competitive dynamics |
| Credit-cycle stress on a bank-private credit program portfolio | First real test of the structure's loss-recognition mechanics |
| Regulatory attention from US/EU bank supervisors | Bank capital treatment of these arrangements is currently favourable; rule changes could shift incentives |

The format is on a trajectory to be the dominant structure for sub-IG corporate financing across major economies by 2027-2028. The variables that determine whether it stays favourable include regulatory treatment, credit cycle behaviour, and whether the implicit assumption that private credit can hold-to-maturity through a real downturn proves out.
