#concept #accounting

**CODM** (Chief Operating Decision Maker) — the [[ASC 280]] term for the person or group that allocates resources and evaluates performance of a company's operating segments. Segment reporting obligations are defined by what the CODM actually reviews, not by org charts or legal structure.

---

## Why it matters for investors

ASC 280 doesn't let companies choose which segments to disclose based on what's flattering — it's based on how the top decision-maker actually consumes financial data. If the CODM reviews a standalone P&L for a business unit, that unit is likely a reportable segment, even if the company would prefer to keep it bundled.

ASU 2023-07 (effective FY2024) tightened the standard further: companies must now disclose "significant segment expenses regularly provided to the CODM." This closes the loophole where companies reported segment revenue but hid segment costs.

## Who is the CODM?

Usually the CEO, but not always:

| Company | CODM | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| [[Google\|Alphabet]] | [[Sundar Pichai]] (CEO) | Reviews Google Services, Cloud, Other Bets. Does he see a YouTube P&L internally? Probably — but YouTube isn't a separate segment |
| [[Amazon]] | Andy Jassy (CEO) | Split into North America, International, AWS |
| [[Meta]] | [[Mark Zuckerberg]] (CEO) | Family of Apps vs Reality Labs — the CODM split is why we see Reality Labs losses |

The CODM designation matters most when companies resist breaking out profitable or unprofitable units. [[Meta]]'s Reality Labs losses (~$16B/year) are visible precisely because Zuckerberg reviews them as a separate segment. [[Google\|Alphabet]] keeps [[YouTube]] bundled inside Google Services — if the [[SEC]] determined Pichai reviews YouTube as a standalone segment, it could force separate disclosure.

## Where CODM appears

The concept is defined in ASC 280 but referenced across other standards:

| Standard | Domain | How CODM is used |
|----------|--------|------------------|
| [[ASC 280]] | Segment reporting | Defines CODM; segments based on what the CODM reviews |
| ASC 350 | Goodwill impairment | CODM's reporting unit structure determines where goodwill is tested |
| ASC 360 | Long-lived assets | Asset grouping can follow CODM review level |
| IFRS 8 | Operating segments (international) | Same CODM concept as ASC 280, different standard-setter (IASB vs FASB) |

ASC 280 is where CODM is defined and where it matters most for disclosure. The other references are downstream — they piggyback on whatever segment structure the CODM review creates.

## The disclosure game

Companies have incentives to structure CODM reviews to avoid granular disclosure:
- Bundling winners with losers — hides underperforming units (YouTube's costs inside Google Services)
- Aggregating similar segments — ASC 280 allows combining segments with similar economics, reducing transparency
- Keeping the CODM review high-level — if the CEO only reviews consolidated numbers, fewer segments are reportable

The [[SEC]] has pushed back on aggressive aggregation. The 2023-07 update was a direct response to investor complaints that segment disclosures had become less useful over time.
