USD/INR hit 91.98 (record low, Jan 31, 2026). Worst monthly move since Sep 2022. Then broke further to ~93.2 record low on Mar 19 as Iran conflict compounded energy import costs and triggered capital flight (~$8B equity exits in March alone). RBI responding with massive dollar sales (>$20B since Feb 28), record bond purchases, and rate holds.

PE return impact: 57 Stars and Aksia flagged at IVCA conclave (Mar 2026) that rupee depreciation + 10-15% capital gains tax at exit are capping dollar-denominated PE returns. Combined with leverage constraints (RBI restricts bank financing for share acquisitions), India PE must rely on operational value creation — no LBO playbook available.

## Structural dynamics

### Why INR trends weaker
1. Twin deficits — current account + fiscal deficit
2. Oil import bill — ~$150B annually
3. Inflation differential — India runs hotter than US
4. Dollar strength cycles — EM currencies suffer

### Why INR doesn't collapse
1. RBI reserves — ~$563B (Mar 2026, down from ~$600B+; gold holdings push total near $730B)
2. Remittances — world's largest ($100B+/year)
3. IT services exports — $200B+ annual inflows
4. FDI — steady manufacturing/tech investment

## Iran war FX defense (March 2026)

The [[Reserve Bank of India]] is fighting the most intense FX defense since at least the 2013 taper tantrum. Since the [[Operation Epic Fury|Iran conflict]] began on Feb 28, the rupee has fallen 2.3% vs the dollar — hitting another record low on Mar 19 (~93.2/USD). The central bank has burned through >$20B in foreign exchange reserves in under three weeks (Mumbai bankers to FT), with ~$10B spent in the first week alone, reducing reserves to $563B (official data). The RBI's net-short book — its forward selling of greenbacks — has grown to >$100B (Mumbai bankers).

Simultaneously, the RBI has been buying government bonds at record pace: ₹1T ($10.7B) in March alone, aimed at increasing bank liquidity and supporting the fixed-income market. Despite this, the 10Y yield has climbed ~0.2pp YTD to ~6.7%.

### Why the rupee is exposed

| Vulnerability | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| Crude imports | ~90% imported; soared since Feb 28 |
| Natural gas | ~50% imported |
| Gulf energy sourcing | ~half of energy imports from Gulf states |
| Remittances | >$50B/year from millions of Indian workers in Gulf — crucial buffer for current account |
| Gulf trade | Largest trading partner |
| FPI outflows | ~$10B equity exits YTD; ~$8B in March alone (post-war) |

### Analyst views

[[Goldman Sachs]]: expect INR to weaken from ~93.2 to 95/USD over 12 months. Ranks [[India]] alongside [[Thailand]] and [[Philippines]] as most vulnerable Asian economies/currencies to war fallout.

[[Emkay Global Financial Services|Emkay Global]] (Mumbai-based): extended war "likely to trigger stress across all financial markets in India." RBI "cannot hold out if this situation persists." Scenario: INR to 95, 10Y yield to 7%, corporate bond spreads surge.

[[Standard Chartered]] Anubhuti Sahay (head of India econ research, Mar 19): rate hike "less compelling unless the external sector comes under immense pressure — we are not there yet." Expects rates on hold with focus on rupee liquidity via bond purchases. Current account deficit could widen to 2.5% of GDP (up from previous top-end ~1%). Import cover at 10 months; 9 months is the threshold where RBI appetite to defend wanes. *"India runs the risk of a third year of a balance of payments deficit. The rupee has to be the shock absorber."*

[[Citigroup|Citi]]: $25B (0.6% GDP) widening of current account deficit if oil/commodity prices persist at current levels for 3 months and Gulf remittances fall.

[[Moody's]] Christian de Guzman (senior VP, Mar 19): "room to manoeuvre" — inflation still below 4-6% target band, robust growth, reserves including gold still near all-time high of ~$730B (end-Feb).

Priyanka Kishore ([[Asia Decoded]], Mar 19): *"[Rupee] is among the more exposed EM currencies to the Iran war, not least because about half of India's energy imports come from the Gulf states. Also at risk is the sizeable flow of remittances from the Middle East."*

Tanay Dalal ([[Axis Bank]], Mar 19): depreciation "to remain gradual and protracted, given continued RBI intervention."

### Monetary policy backdrop

RBI governor [[Sanjay Malhotra]] told the FT (Dec 2025) that favourable conditions — strong growth, low inflation — would allow rates to remain low for a "long period" following 1.25pp of cuts. The US-India trade deal (Feb 2026, tariffs cut from 50% to 18%) briefly buoyed the rupee. The Iran conflict has now reversed both tailwinds: the energy import bill is surging and the rate-cut cycle may stall.

## April 2026 crackdown — "toughest measures in a decade"

The rupee hit a string of new record lows through late March, touching **95.21** on April 1 — the Goldman/Emkay war target reached in barely two weeks. March was the worst month in six years: **-4.24%** vs USD. The RBI responded with a two-step crackdown that Bloomberg called the most aggressive FX intervention since the 2013 taper tantrum.

### Step 1: Net open position cap (March 27)

On Friday evening March 27, the RBI directed banks to cap their **net open rupee positions at $100 million** by end of each business day, with compliance by April 10. This replaced the previous board-driven limits of up to **25% of total capital** — a massive reduction. The aim was to kill proprietary directional bets on the currency.

**Backfire:** On Monday March 30, the rupee briefly rallied past 93 but ended flat at ~95/USD. Banks were able to transfer or sell their positions to corporates, hedge funds, and other clients, who then exploited the **onshore-NDF arbitrage** spread. The market had an estimated **$40B open position** in dollar-rupee. RBI had inadvertently created the arbitrage opportunity by tightening one side but leaving the NDF channel open.

### Step 2: NDF ban + rebooking prohibition (April 1)

Late Wednesday April 1, the RBI closed every escape route:

| Measure | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| **NDF ban** | Banks barred from offering non-deliverable rupee derivatives to residents or non-residents |
| **Rebooking ban** | Cancelled forward contracts cannot be rebooked — clients must carry open positions |
| **Related-party ban** | Banks cannot make rupee derivative transactions with related entities (e.g., foreign trading desks) |
| **Hedging proof** | Deliverable contracts only with documented hedging need |

The NDF ban disrupted what [[Jefferies]] estimated was a **$149 billion-a-day market**. By severing onshore-offshore linkage, the RBI effectively said: speculation against the rupee offshore will no longer translate into onshore dollar demand or deplete FX reserves.

### Market impact

On April 2, the rupee surged to **92.8350** intraday (peak) and closed at **93.10** — up **1.8%**, the best single day since **September 2013**. The weekly gain was ~2%, best since November 2022.

The onshore-NDF spread widened sharply as banks squared positions by selling dollars domestically and buying abroad. [[Clearing Corporation of India]] imposed additional margin requirements on dollar-rupee forwards.

### Banking sector losses

[[Jefferies]] estimates:

| Metric | Estimate |
|--------|----------|
| Total sector losses | **₹4,000-5,000 crore** ($470-590M) |
| vs. sector PBT (FY25) | ₹5.5 lakh crore — losses ~0.1% |
| Foreign banks share | ~45% |
| Private banks share | ~40% |
| State-owned banks | ~15% |

Losses manageable but may dampen medium-term INR trading activity and market depth.

### Sustainability question

The crackdown buys time but faces structural headwinds:

| Tailwind | Headwind |
|----------|----------|
| Short squeeze forced $40B position unwind | Oil imports ~90%, Brent 80% above early-2026 |
| NDF ban severs offshore contagion channel | $12B+ equity outflows in March alone |
| RBI reserves still ~$563B + $170B gold | Current account deficit widening to 2.5%+ GDP |
| Positive carry (~4% vs USD) | Iran war ongoing — Gulf remittances at risk |

**Andy Mukherjee (Bloomberg Opinion, Apr 6):** Administrative measures delay the slide but may not stop 100/USD. The core dilemma — defend the exchange rate OR control domestic interest rates — remains unresolved. Governor [[Sanjay Malhotra]] let the rupee weaken to buffer tariff impact, but the oil shock made that untenable. Raising rates to save the currency would stifle growth; letting the rupee go means imported inflation hits the middle class.

**Jefferies:** "Sudden and retrospective changes to rules, while appropriate in the current extreme situation, may affect the depth of INR's forex trading over the medium to long term."

**MUFG (Michael Wan):** "RBI seems quite serious to follow through... further policy changes by RBI and the India government to manage INR weakness could be likely."

**[[Axis Bank]] (Neeraj Gambhir):** "RBI has effectively broken the direct link between the onshore market and the offshore market."

### RBI MPC meeting (April 6-8)

Markets expect rates **on hold** despite the currency crisis. SBI Research: first policy since the Iran war, cautious communication likely. The tension between growth support (rate cuts) and currency defense (rate hikes or at least holds) is the central question for Malhotra's RBI.
