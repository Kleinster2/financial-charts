# Indian Depositary Receipts

An Indian Depositary Receipt (IDR) is a rupee-denominated [[Depositary receipts|depositary receipt]] issued by a domestic Indian depository against the shares of a foreign company — the mechanism by which a non-Indian company could raise capital and list on Indian exchanges. SEBI created the framework in 2009.

## Status — defunct

The IDR is effectively a dead instrument. [[Standard Chartered]] PLC was the first and only company to issue IDRs: a 240-million-IDR offering that opened on 25 May 2010 on the BSE and NSE. SEBI restrictions on redemption (the limited two-way fungibility) plus thin liquidity left the program troubled, and Standard Chartered announced on 9 March 2020 that it would terminate the programme; the IDRs were delisted from the BSE and NSE on 22 July 2020. No other company ever issued IDRs, so with Standard Chartered's exit the format went dormant.

## Synthesis

The IDR is the depositary-receipt template under Indian rules and the family's clearest failure case: one issuer, one decade, then delisting. The redemption and fungibility restrictions meant to protect the domestic market instead made the receipt unattractive, and India never developed as a capital-raising venue for foreign issuers. It survives as a regulatory framework with no live program — the negative space that makes the [[American Depositary Receipts|ADR]]'s success legible.
