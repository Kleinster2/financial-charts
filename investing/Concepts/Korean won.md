#concept #fx #currency #asia

**Korean won** — the currency of [[South Korea]]. A high-beta, semiconductor-cycle-sensitive Asian currency that, in 2026, sits at financial-crisis lows against the US dollar even as the country runs a record chip-export surplus — the "DRam dollars" puzzle.

---

## Why the won matters

The won is one of Asia's most liquid currencies and trades as a proxy for the global semiconductor cycle and risk appetite toward [[South Korea]]'s memory-chip complex ([[Samsung]], [[SK Hynix]]). Because the chip cycle now runs through AI hardware demand, the won has become a currency expression of the AI-memory boom — which makes its 2026 weakness, against that boom, the puzzle.

---

## 2026 weakness — the "DRam dollars" puzzle

The won fell about 4% against the US dollar in 2026, among Asia's worst performers — ahead of only the Philippine peso, Indian rupee and Indonesian rupiah, all energy importers exposed to the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war energy shock]]. Unlike those, South Korea has a record trade surplus: a Q1 2026 current-account surplus of $73.8bn on memory-chip demand from US AI companies, with net profit of $28.3bn at [[Samsung]] and $22.4bn at [[SK Hynix]]. Those earnings lifted Samsung shares more than 400% and SK Hynix more than 970% over the prior year, taking both to a $1tn market capitalisation.

Yet the won trades around ₩1,500 to the dollar, its worst level since the 2008-09 financial crisis. [[Brad Setser]] ([[Council on Foreign Relations]]) called it a fundamental puzzle: one of the economies that most visibly profited from surging semiconductor demand still has a currency at crisis-typical levels. Setser labelled the phenomenon "DRam dollars" — likening South Korea's manufacturing surplus recycled into US assets to the petrodollar pattern of oil exporters reinvesting dollar earnings.

## Why: two flow mechanisms, not weak fundamentals

The [[Bank of Korea]] this month said the won looked "excessively weak relative to economic fundamentals" and warned authorities would take "decisive action" if necessary. Analysts point to two flow explanations rather than a fundamentals story:

1. Equity-rally-driven selling. The [[Kospi]] roughly doubled from October 2025 to May 2026 (Abbas Keshvani, RBC Capital Markets). [[MSCI]] raised its South Korea index weights but could not keep pace with the rally, so global managers had to sell Korean stock to avoid being overweight the country or specific names. Foreign investors were net sellers of a record $79bn of Korean equities in 2026 (Korea Financial Supervisory Service). The dollars from those sales leave the won.
2. Non-repatriation of export earnings. Currencies usually strengthen when exporters convert foreign earnings home. Instead, large exporters such as [[Samsung]] and [[SK Hynix]] are holding a significant share of revenue in foreign currency to fund increasingly global operations (Patrick Han, SK Securities), and are reluctant to convert dollars into won while they expect further weakness.

Secondary drag: Mitul Kotecha ([[Barclays]]) cited higher Middle East energy prices and a weak [[Japanese yen]] (highly correlated with the won) as additional pressure. This is the channel that links the won to the [[Iran conflict FX dynamics|Iran-war FX overlay]] — but it is a contributor, not the core driver.

---

## The repatriation catalyst (asymmetric upside)

The same mechanics that suppress the won set up a sharp potential reversal. Keshvani (RBC) forecasts a rush of repatriation should the won begin to strengthen, which could cause a sharp upward move — exporters sitting on large unconverted dollar balances would convert at once. Sam Konrad ([[Jupiter Asset Management]]) expects both the won and the [[Taiwan dollar]] — another chip-exporter currency with the same surplus-but-weak-currency profile — to appreciate, though he could not name the catalyst or timing.

The Bank of Korea's own read reinforces the fundamentals case: governor Shin Hyun-song said the gain from semiconductor exports would exceed the economic hit from the Iran war, projecting 2026 GDP growth 0.7 percentage points higher thanks to booming chip sales.

---

## Synthesis

The won is the cleanest 2026 case of a currency disconnected from its own trade fundamentals. Textbook logic says a record current-account surplus from the AI-memory boom should pull the won higher; instead it sits at crisis-era lows. The reconciliation is that the surplus is being recycled into dollars rather than converted home — through index-driven foreign selling of a doubled [[Kospi]] and through exporters parking earnings offshore. That makes the weakness a flow phenomenon layered on strong fundamentals, which is why the [[Bank of Korea]] frames it as "excessive" and why the setup is asymmetric: the same unconverted dollar pile that suppresses the won today is the fuel for a sharp repatriation-driven snapback if sentiment turns. The Iran-war energy shock and a weak [[Japanese yen]] add drag at the margin but are not the engine. The [[Taiwan dollar]] shares the profile, making this a chip-exporter-currency pattern rather than a Korea-only quirk.
