#concept #finance #venture #debt

**Venture debt** — non-dilutive loan financing designed for VC-backed, high-growth companies. Borrowers get capital without giving up equity (beyond small warrant packages). Lenders get higher interest rates (8-15%+) and warrant coverage as compensation for lending to companies that often have negative cash flow. The US venture debt market is ~$28B (2025, Statista), growing as late-stage companies delay IPOs and seek to minimize dilution.

---

## How it works

Venture debt is typically a 3-4 year term loan, secured by the borrower's assets (IP, receivables, sometimes all assets). Unlike traditional bank loans, underwriting is based on the company's venture backing, growth trajectory, and ability to raise future equity — not profitability or hard asset collateral.

### Typical terms

| Component | Range |
|-----------|-------|
| Interest rate | 8-15% (SOFR + 4-10% spread) |
| Term | 3-4 years |
| Amortization | Often interest-only for 12-18 months, then amortizing |
| Warrant coverage | 0.1-0.5% of company equity |
| Loan-to-last-round-raised | 25-50% of last equity round |
| Covenants | Revenue minimums, cash runway, reporting |

Warrants give the lender upside if the company succeeds — typically 0.1-0.5% dilution vs 10-20% dilution from an equivalent equity round. This is the core value proposition: capital at a fraction of the dilution cost.

---

## When companies use it

- **Bridge to next equity round** — extend runway 6-12 months to hit milestones that command a higher valuation
- **Bridge to profitability** — avoid raising a dilutive round when cash flow breakeven is near
- **Growth acceleration** — fund hiring, R&D, or expansion without diluting existing shareholders
- **Pre-IPO** — build war chest before going public, preserving equity economics for insiders

The [[Intercom]] $250M venture debt deal from [[Hercules Capital]] (Mar 2026) is a textbook example: the company is cash-flow positive with $129M cash and Fin AI growing at 393% annualized, but the last equity valuation ($1.3B from 2018) is stale. Taking debt instead of equity avoids repricing at an unfavorable moment and preserves the cap table for a future IPO or fundraise at a much higher valuation.

## Risks

For borrowers:
- Debt service obligation regardless of business performance — unlike equity, it must be repaid
- Covenants can trigger default if growth slows or cash drops below minimums
- Warrants still create some dilution
- Can accelerate cash burn if growth investments don't pay off

For lenders:
- Lending to unprofitable companies — default risk is real
- Recovery in default is difficult (tech company assets are often intangible)
- Warrants partially compensate but don't eliminate loss risk
