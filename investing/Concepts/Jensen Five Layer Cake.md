AI infrastructure as a five-layer stack where each layer taxes the ones above it. Framework developed by [[NVIDIA]] CEO Jensen Huang, notably presented at Davos (January 22, 2026) in conversation with [[BlackRock]] CEO Larry Fink. [[NVIDIA]] published a detailed blog post about the framework on March 13, 2026.

## Synopsis

The Five Layer Cake represents AI as essential infrastructure—"like electricity and the internet"—rather than just clever apps or models. Each layer creates its own ecosystem with distinct winners, and companies controlling lower layers effectively tax all layers above them. The framework explains why we're in the "largest infrastructure buildout in human history" with trillions still to be invested, and maps the economic dynamics across the entire AI value chain from energy constraints to application-layer value creation.

## The Five Layers

### 1. Energy
The foundational constraint. Energy is the "first principle of AI infrastructure"—without sufficient, reliable power, nothing above functions. This layer includes:
- Power generation capacity
- Grid infrastructure
- Data center power delivery
- Energy efficiency optimization

Investment thesis: Energy constraints will gate AI scaling. Companies solving power delivery, grid stability, and energy density capture fundamental value.

### 2. Chips  
Transform energy into computation at massive scale. This layer includes:
- AI training chips (H100, TPUs)
- Inference accelerators
- Memory systems (HBM, GDDR)
- Networking silicon (InfiniBand, Ethernet)

Investment thesis: Chip companies tax every application. The layer with highest barriers to entry and strongest competitive moats.

### 3. Infrastructure
"AI factories" designed not to store information but to manufacture intelligence in real time. This layer includes:
- Hyperscaler data centers
- AI training clusters  
- Edge inference infrastructure
- Networking and interconnects

Investment thesis: Infrastructure providers capture recurring revenue from AI workloads while owning the physical assets that scale intelligence.

### 4. Models
AI systems that understand multiple domains: language, biology, chemistry, physics, finance, medicine, the physical world. This layer includes:
- Foundation models (GPT, Claude, Gemini)
- Specialized domain models
- Model training and fine-tuning platforms
- AI research labs

Investment thesis: Model layer creates the intelligence that applications consume, but requires massive capital investment in the layers below.

### 5. Applications
Where economic value is ultimately created and captured. This layer includes:
- Drug discovery platforms
- Autonomous vehicles
- Humanoid robotics
- AI-powered software applications

Investment thesis: Applications capture end-user value but depend entirely on the infrastructure stack below.

## Key Insights

Breaking the prerecorded software model: Traditional software executes pre-written code. AI generates intelligence in real time, requiring fundamentally different infrastructure that's always-on and computationally intensive.

Lower layers tax upper layers: Each layer captures value from all layers above it. Energy providers tax chip makers, chip makers tax infrastructure providers, infrastructure providers tax model developers, and so on.

Infrastructure buildout scale: "We are a few hundred billion dollars into it. Trillions of dollars of infrastructure still need to be built." This represents the largest infrastructure investment cycle in human history.

AI threshold crossed: In the past year, AI models became good enough to be useful at scale—reasoning improved significantly and hallucinations dropped to acceptable levels for real applications.

## Investment Framework

Layer positioning determines margins: Lower in the stack = higher margins and better positioning. Energy and chips have structural advantages over applications.

Ecosystem plays within layers: Each layer contains multiple winners, not winner-take-all dynamics. Different use cases and geographies create room for multiple players.

Cross-layer integration: Companies that can integrate across multiple layers (like [[NVIDIA]] combining chips and software) create additional competitive advantages.

## Reinforcement: Dwarkesh interview (Apr 15, 2026)

Jensen reused the five-layer framework three months after Davos as the scaffold for his [[Export controls]] rebuttal on the Dwarkesh Patel podcast. The framework now serves as his default analytical lens for multiple debates:

**On China / export controls.** Only a full-stack player captures every layer. Single-layer denial (chips only) while ceding models, applications, and infrastructure is strategically incoherent — the five-layer logic argues for ecosystem diffusion, not containment. See [[Export controls]] and [[Jensen Huang]].

**On ASICs.** [[Broadcom]]-class custom silicon operates at the chips layer only. NVIDIA operates at all five (GPUs + infrastructure via neoclouds + models via [[NVIDIA AI Foundations]] + apps via Omniverse). The 5-point gross-margin gap misses the cross-layer value capture.

**On hyperscaler capex.** The right unit of analysis is layer-by-layer value capture, not aggregate spend. Chips layer (NVIDIA) captures more per dollar than infrastructure layer (neoclouds), which captures more than applications layer (most startups). Understanding where capex lands in the stack is the key portfolio question.

**On inference economics.** Adding [[Premium inference tokens]] as a sub-framework: the models/applications layers are disaggregating into latency tiers, with each tier served by purpose-built silicon underneath.

The framework is no longer a single-speech device — it's Jensen's durable analytical lens, and it now carries the reasoning behind his major strategic claims.
