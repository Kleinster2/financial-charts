#concept #privateequity #secondaries #liquidity

**Continuation fund** — A vehicle where a PE sponsor transfers assets from an expiring fund into a new fund it also manages, giving existing LPs the choice to cash out or roll over. The fastest-growing segment of the secondaries market, now ~$100B/yr. Inherently conflicted: the GP sits on both sides of the transaction.

---

## Synthesis

The continuation fund market has gone from niche workaround to dominant PE exit channel in five years. With $3T+ in unrealized buyout value and LP distributions running at 17% of NAV vs. 26% historical average, GPs need liquidity mechanisms that don't depend on frozen IPO/M&A markets. CVs now represent 14% of all PE exits (up from 5% in 2020-21) and 89% of GP-led secondary volume. The structural appeal is obvious: GPs keep their best assets, reset carry economics, and maintain management fees. The structural conflict is equally obvious — and the ADIC vs. EMG lawsuit (Dec 2025) is the first time this conflict has been litigated publicly. Whether Mubadala wins or loses, the case is reshaping how every GP thinks about LPAC process, fairness opinions, and information symmetry. ILPA guidance and SEC scrutiny (stalled by the Fifth Circuit's 2024 vacatur of private fund adviser rules) remain the two regulatory vectors to watch.

---

## Mechanics

A continuation fund transaction works as follows:

1. GP identifies asset(s) in a maturing fund (typically years 8-12) that it wants to keep managing
2. GP creates new vehicle (the continuation fund) and raises capital from secondary buyers, other LPs, or existing investors
3. Existing LPs choose: cash out at the stated valuation, or roll their interest into the new vehicle
4. GP rolls over — best practice is 100% of crystallized carried interest plus new capital commitment
5. New economics apply — fresh management fees, reset carry hurdle, new fund term (typically 3-5 years)

| Structure | Description | Share of market |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Single-asset CV (SACV) | One "trophy" asset transferred | ~50% of GP-led volume |
| Multi-asset CV (MACV) | Multiple remaining portfolio companies | ~50% |

Key fact: 80-90% of existing LPs typically cash out rather than roll forward.

## Economics and conflicts

The core conflict: the GP is effectively both buyer and seller.

GP incentives to undervalue:
- Lower valuation → cheaper buyout of cashing-out LPs
- Carry reset on transferred assets → new waterfall, new hurdle
- Extended management fee stream (typically 1.5-2% on new vehicle)
- If the asset wouldn't generate carry in the old fund (below hurdle), transferring resets the clock

LP protections (in theory):
- LPAC approval required for conflicted transactions
- Independent fairness opinion or valuation opinion
- 30+ calendar day review period for LPs to decide (ILPA standard)
- GP rolls 100% of crystallized carry (ILPA best practice)

Return targets:
| Structure | Target MOIC | Target IRR |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| Single-asset CV | 2.0x | 20% |
| Multi-asset CV | 1.8-2.0x | 17.5-20% |

GP commitment: 5-25% ownership stakes in CVs (vs. 2-5% in traditional funds). Tiered carry structures common — 10-15% at lower returns, scaling to 15-20% above IRR/MOIC thresholds.

Performance: [[Morgan Stanley]] analysis (2018-2023 vintages) found CVs outperformed buyout fund MOICs across all quartiles with lower loss ratios. LPs who systematically avoid rolling incur opportunity costs of ~8% per vintage year (Chronograph).

## ADIC vs. EMG — the landmark case

The first public litigation over continuation fund conflicts. Case No. 2025-1389-NAC, Delaware Court of Chancery.

Parties:
- Plaintiff: [[ADIC|Abu Dhabi Investment Council]] (unit of [[Mubadala]], ~$300B AUM)
- Defendant: [[Energy Minerals Group]] (EMG), Houston-based PE firm
- Asset: 30% stake in [[Ascent Resources]] — largest private natural gas company in the US
- CV size: ~$800M initially marketed; EMG ultimately targeted $1.1B
- Implied valuation: $5.5B ($23.87/share)

ADIC's allegations:
- EMG told existing LPs that Ascent's valuation was "depressed compared with peers" with "no prospect of an exit through an IPO or M&A"
- Simultaneously told prospective CV investors the opposite — value was "competitive with peers" and "an IPO could be expected"
- Fairness opinion based on "inaccurate assumptions" leading to undervaluation
- Inadequate LPAC process: insufficient notice, refused in-camera sessions without EMG present, information asymmetry
- No exploration of alternative exits (IPO, M&A, third-party sale)

Third-party intervention:
- [[Mason Capital]] (Ken Garschina, ~$1.5B AUM) — held Ascent through 2018 bankruptcy restructuring, challenged the transaction and [[Kirkland & Ellis]]'s dual representation of Ascent's board and EMG
- [[Kimmeridge Energy Management|Kimmeridge]] submitted a competing bid at $6.0B (~10% premium over EMG's $5.5B) in Dec 2025

Timeline:
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Nov 2025 | EMG proposes CV transaction at $5.5B valuation |
| Dec 3, 2025 | ADIC files suit in Delaware Chancery |
| Dec 4, 2025 | Court approves stipulation halting transaction |
| Dec 12, 2025 | Mason Capital publicly discloses competing bid above $5.5B |
| Dec 16, 2025 | Kimmeridge bids $6.0B |
| Feb 27, 2026 | Arbitrator deadline for ruling |
| Mar 2026 | Bloomberg reports Mubadala "lost its fight" — ruling allows EMG to proceed |

Implications: The ruling — allowing the CV to proceed despite a sovereign wealth fund's objections and the existence of higher competing bids — sets a precedent for GP-led transactions. It validates the LPAC approval process as sufficient even when challenged, and may embolden GPs to push forward on conflicted deals. Conversely, it increases pressure on LPs to negotiate stronger governance protections ex ante (in the LPA), since post-hoc litigation proved insufficient.

## Regulatory landscape

[[SEC]] Private Fund Adviser Rules (vacated):
- Aug 2023: SEC adopted rules requiring fairness opinions for adviser-led secondaries, disclosure of material business relationships with opinion providers
- Jun 2024: Fifth Circuit vacated the rules in their entirety (*National Association of Private Fund Managers v. SEC*)
- Rules would have required registered advisers to obtain independent fairness/valuation opinions and distribute to investors
- Status as of Mar 2026: no federal regulatory framework specifically governing CV conflicts

ILPA Guidance (May 2023):
- GP should roll 100% of crystallized carry
- LPAC consulted early on rationale and adviser selection
- Minimum 30 calendar days / 20 business days for LP election period
- Fairness opinions already market practice (ILPA codified existing norms)
- Information provided to existing LPs should match what CV investors receive

Practical reality: With SEC rules vacated, governance depends entirely on what's in the LPA and how the LPAC functions. The EMG case exposed how easily these protections can be circumvented.
