# TIPS

Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) are US Treasury bonds whose principal adjusts with CPI, so they pay a fixed real coupon on an inflation-indexed face value. They are the cleanest direct [[Inflation|inflation]] hedge in the [[Treasuries|US government bond]] complex and a core sleeve in inflation-aware allocation — the asset built to work in the inflationary quadrants of the [[Economic Environment Framework]] where nominal bonds fail.

## Mechanics

The principal is indexed to headline CPI; the coupon rate is fixed but applies to the adjusted principal, so the dollar coupon rises with inflation. At maturity the holder receives the greater of adjusted or original principal (a deflation floor). The traded yield is a real yield, and the nominal Treasury yield minus the TIPS yield of the same maturity is the breakeven inflation rate — the market's priced inflation expectation.

## Why they matter for allocation

- Real return in fixed-income form: TIPS deliver a contractual real return, protecting purchasing power when inflation surprises higher — the [[Economic Environment Framework]] Q2 (reflation) and Q4 (stagflation) quadrants, where nominal [[Treasuries]] lose.
- A diversifier with a regime catch: TIPS hedge inflation but still carry duration, so in a sharp real-rate shock (2022) they fell alongside nominal bonds — inflation protection did not mean price protection.
- Breakevens as a signal: the TIPS-vs-nominal breakeven is a widely watched market gauge of inflation expectations, distinct from realized CPI.

## In risk parity

TIPS are a standard sleeve in [[Risk Parity]] and All Weather portfolios precisely because they cover an environment the others do not: equities and nominal bonds both struggle when inflation rises, leaving TIPS plus commodities as the inflation-quadrant hedge. The risk-parity critique of relying only on nominal bonds is, in part, the case for holding TIPS.
