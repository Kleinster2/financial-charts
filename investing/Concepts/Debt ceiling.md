# Debt ceiling

Statutory cap on total federal debt that the [[US Treasury]] can issue. When outstanding debt approaches the limit, Treasury relies on "extraordinary measures" (accounting maneuvers that free up headroom) to keep paying obligations without new net issuance. Once those are exhausted, Treasury would default on obligations unless Congress raises or suspends the ceiling. Flashpoints force Treasury to draw down the [[Treasury General Account]] and suspend bill issuance, creating distinctive market structure effects.

## Mechanics during a standoff

When Treasury cannot issue net new debt, it runs down the [[Treasury General Account]] and pauses bill auctions. Paradoxically, this is *liquidity-supportive* — TGA drawdown moves cash from Treasury into private accounts, raising bank reserves. The post-deal rebuild then drains reserves rapidly as Treasury issues heavily to replenish cash, often coinciding with [[Fed balance sheet QT|QT]] drawdown — the combination that contributed to the June 2024 QT taper.
