# Diamond market collapse

Natural diamond prices have fallen ~40%+ from 2022 peaks to multi-decade lows, driven by lab-grown disruption, China demand collapse, and the unraveling of the supply management regime that sustained artificial scarcity for nearly a century.

---

## Synthesis

The diamond industry is past the point of cyclical recovery. Lab-grown diamonds used in ~50% of US engagement rings (BriteCo, Jan-Aug 2025) means the substitution isn't marginal — it's structural. The [[Surat Diamond Bourse]] sitting 95% empty two years after opening is the physical manifestation of a market that won't come back: $350M of concrete built for a trade that's moved to laboratories.

[[De Beers]]' response tells the story of a monopolist losing control. The Sightholders system — the mechanism that enforced artificial scarcity for a century — is now running on side deals and opacity, with up to half of 2025 sales at undisclosed discounts. De Beers' book value cratered from $9.1B to $2.3B in three writedowns. [[Anglo American]] is trying to offload the business entirely. The Feb 2026 price increase for >5ct stones offers a glimmer — the very top of the market (large, rare stones) may retain scarcity value even as the middle collapses. But the structural floor keeps dropping: [[Angola]] ramped production 70% in 2025 selling at market prices, actively undercutting De Beers' supply management.

The losers are already visible: [[Botswana]] in its second year of economic contraction with mineral revenue halved, [[India]]'s diamond exports at a two-decade low with ~1M jobs at risk, [[Antwerp]] trade halved from peak. The geopolitical overlay (Iran conflict, Russia sanctions, gold competing for jewelry spend) is accelerating a decline that was already structural. This is the template for what happens when a commodity's value rests entirely on manufactured scarcity and a perfect substitute arrives at 90% lower cost.

## Three forces

### 1. Lab-grown diamonds
The existential threat. Lab-grown diamonds are chemically, optically, and physically identical to mined diamonds — even gemologists can't tell the difference without specialized equipment. Prices have cratered as production scaled (mainly India and China), with retail prices falling throughout 2024. Lab-grown went from a novelty to ~20%+ of engagement ring sales.

[[De Beers]] tried to maintain the "natural is special" narrative but simultaneously launched its own lab-grown brand (Lightbox) at $800/carat in 2018 — inadvertently validating the product it was trying to suppress.

### 2. China demand collapse
The 2011 diamond spike to ~8,500 was largely [[China luxury boom and bust|China's luxury boom]] — a newly wealthy middle class discovering Western luxury brands all at once. [[De Beers]] deliberately imported the engagement ring tradition with targeted Chinese campaigns. That bid is now in full reverse: China's mainland luxury market fell 18-20% in 2024 (Bain), Chinese retailers are offloading $2B+ in polished diamond inventory (Fortune, Feb 2025), and younger Chinese consumers have embraced lab-grown diamonds faster than Western markets — less cultural attachment to the "natural is special" narrative.

### 3. De Beers / Anglo American breakup
[[Anglo American]] announced it will spin off or sell [[De Beers]] (May 2024), ending an almost century-long relationship. De Beers essentially invented artificial diamond scarcity — controlling supply through its Central Selling Organisation, stockpiling rough diamonds, and spending billions on "A Diamond is Forever" marketing. With Anglo cutting it loose and writing down $5.2B+ in value, the entity that managed the diamond cartel is losing its grip.

## Historical context

The diamond market was never a free market. [[De Beers]] controlled 80%+ of global rough diamond supply through the mid-20th century, declining to ~30% today. The entire value proposition — "diamonds are rare and eternal" — was manufactured marketing. Diamonds are among the most common gemstones geologically. The 2011 peak (~8,500) and 2022 peak (~6,500) were demand spikes (China luxury boom and post-Covid revenge spending), not structural. Both faded.

## Evolution

- **1888**: [[Cecil Rhodes]] founds De Beers Consolidated Mines, begins monopolizing supply
- **1938**: De Beers hires N.W. Ayer → "A Diamond is Forever" campaign (1947). Invented the engagement ring norm
- **1930s-2000s**: Central Selling Organisation controls 80%+ of rough diamond distribution
- **2000s**: Cartel weakens — Russia, Australia, Canada mine independently. De Beers share falls to ~35%
- **2011**: Price peak (~8,500) on China luxury demand
- **2015-2018**: Lab-grown diamonds achieve gem quality at commercial scale
- **2018**: De Beers launches Lightbox (lab-grown brand) — unintentionally legitimizes competitors
- **2022**: Post-Covid revenge spending spike (~6,500), then collapse begins
- **2024 May**: [[Anglo American]] announces De Beers spinoff/sale
- **2024-2025**: Rough prices -40%+. Anglo writes down De Beers by $5.2B cumulative
- **2025**: Debswana cuts output 40%. Diamond price index hits 20-year lows (~3,400)
- **2025**: De Beers begins offering discreet side deals (up to 20% off) — by late 2025, half of sales
- **2025**: [[Angola]] diamond sales +70%, overtakes [[Botswana]] as Africa's largest producer
- **2025**: Lab-grown used in ~50% of US engagement rings (BriteCo). Factories in Surat adding lab-grown production lines
- **2026 Jan**: De Beers cuts prices for stones >3/4 carat, pulls smaller stones from sale
- **2026 Feb**: De Beers raises prices >5% for stones above 5 carats — first sign of stabilization

### Surat Diamond Bourse — monument to a dying trade

The [[Surat Diamond Bourse]], a $350M complex with nine towers and more floor space than the Pentagon, opened in 2023 as a showcase for [[India]]'s dominance (9 out of 10 diamonds globally are cut and polished in Surat). As of March 2026, only ~250 of 4,700 offices are operational. The central corridor stands silent, cafés are unfinished concrete shells, and some merchants who purchased offices are already trying to sell. Authorities brought in a priest to bless the complex.

### De Beers Sightholders system under strain

[[De Beers]]' sales model — 10 choreographed "Sights" per year where selected buyers purchase pre-allocated stones at company-set prices with no negotiation — began cracking by early 2025. Official prices stood well above secondary market levels. Rather than cut benchmark prices, De Beers began offering discreet side deals — up to 20% discounts to favored customers. By late 2025, side deals accounted for up to half of sales. The opacity angered clients who paid full price while others received undisclosed discounts.

De Beers cut prices in January 2026 for stones over three-quarters of a carat and pulled smaller stones from sale. At its February sale, prices rose >5% for diamonds above five carats — interpreted as early confidence that the glut is easing.

### Angola undercuts supply management

[[Angola]]'s diamond sales jumped ~70% in 2025, overtaking [[Botswana]] as Africa's largest producer. Selling at prevailing market prices — well below De Beers' official rates — Angola further undermined attempts to revive prices. Combined with [[Alrosa]]'s sanctioned Russian supply (~25% of global output) requiring costly workarounds, the supply management regime that sustained the industry for a century has effectively collapsed.

### India export collapse

US tariffs (at one point 50% on exports) proved the final blow for [[India]]'s diamond trade. Export demand fell to a two-decade low. In Surat's Mahidharpura bazaar, activity has slowed sharply. Many factories are adding lab-grown production lines — labs from Surat to Shenzhen can now produce a gem-quality stone in ~6 weeks, upending a supply chain employing ~1M people in India.

[[Botswana]] posted two consecutive years of economic contraction. Mineral revenue projected to fall to 10.3B pula ($733M) this fiscal year — less than half the historical average. In [[Antwerp]], trade dropped to ~$19B from a 2022 peak of $41B.

### Geopolitical overlay

US-Israel strikes on [[Iran]] are disrupting trade flows and stoking inflation risks, further curbing discretionary spending on luxury goods. Record-high gold prices are discouraging jewelry purchases as buyers favor bars and coins. Chinese luxury demand collapse sent hundreds of millions of dollars worth of unsold diamonds flowing back from [[China]] to India.

## Investment implications

**This is a commodity losing its moat.** Unlike gold (which has industrial uses, central bank demand, and millennia of monetary history), diamonds' value was always narrative-driven. Lab-grown diamonds broke that narrative by proving the stones aren't rare — they're manufacturable.

**Winners:**
- Lab-grown producers (though margins are compressing as production scales)
- Consumers — engagement rings getting much cheaper
- Other luxury goods (watches, handbags) that retain scarcity

**Losers:**
- [[De Beers]] / [[Anglo American]] — stranded assets, massive writedowns
- Mining-dependent nations: [[Botswana]] (diamonds = 30% GDP), Namibia, South Africa
- Retailers holding natural diamond inventory

**Template for disruption:** The diamond collapse is a case study in what happens when a commodity's value is based on artificial scarcity and a competitor can produce an identical substitute at 90% lower cost. Potential parallels in other "prestige" commodities.
