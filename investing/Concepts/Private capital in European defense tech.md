Private capital in European defense tech — the regional slice of [[Private capital in defense tech]]. The story is that Europe's most ambitious defense-tech companies are largely backed by US venture capital, with thin domestic institutional participation. The structural consequence: the capital that builds European sovereign defense capability is itself cross-border, creating sovereignty tensions, valuation gaps versus US peers, and political frictions when US investors take stakes in European defense names.

See [[European defense spending]] for the macro demand driver and [[Private capital in defense tech]] for the global capital picture.

## The sovereignty tension

The political problem: [[Germany]] is rebuilding its defense-industrial base under [[Schuldenbremse reform]]'s "unlimited" spending exemption, framed as a German sovereignty project. But the private companies being awarded contracts — [[Stark]] (Thiel + Sequoia), [[Helsing]] (General Catalyst + Prima Materia) — have significant US capital on their cap tables.

The [[Peter Thiel]] — [[Stark]] case:

- February 2026 [[Bundeswehr]] awards [[Stark]] €269M upfront (alongside [[Helsing]])
- Green-party defense budget specialist Sebastian Schafer publicly demands transparency about Thiel's ownership and suggests replacing him with another investor
- [[Stark]] responds that Thiel has "no information rights relating to our products or business operations" and that any technical transfer would require German export-control approval
- Contract proceeds; controversy persists into April 2026

The tension is structural: a German government pushing sovereignty narratives is using taxpayer money to fund companies whose ultimate economic upside flows to US venture LPs. The [[Daniel Ek]] / [[Prima Materia]] / [[Saab|SAAB]] participation in [[Helsing]] partially softens this critique for Helsing but not for Stark.

See [[European sovereignty]] for the broader frame.

## Scale gap versus US

The European defense-tech capital base is structurally smaller:

| Metric | Europe | US |
|--------|--------|----|
| Largest company valuation | [[Helsing]] €12B | [[Anduril]] ~$60B (~5×) |
| Number of unicorns | ~4 | ~10+ |
| Thesis-driven dedicated funds | Few; [[NATO Innovation Fund]] is sovereign | Several ([[American Dynamism]] at [[Andreessen Horowitz]], [[Founders Fund]]) |
| LP pool willing to invest in defense | Thin | Deep |
| Single-contract ceilings | €1B per supplier ([[Bundeswehr]] three-supplier model) | Up to €20B over 10 years ([[Anduril]] US Army) |

**Why the gap:**

- European pension and endowment LPs applied tighter defense restrictions than US equivalents; some maintained them even post-2022
- European SWFs (Norwegian GPFG, Netherlands ABP) have weapons-exclusion rules
- No European equivalent of [[a16z]]'s [[American Dynamism]] thesis gained traction
- Domestic procurement ceilings (see [[Bundeswehr three-supplier drone model]] within [[European defense spending]]) cap the addressable home-market revenue
- Single-market fragmentation in [[EU]] procurement means scaling across borders is slower than US federal contracting

The result: European defense-tech can build credible products ([[Helsing]]'s autonomy, [[Auterion]]'s OS, [[Stark]]'s loitering munitions) but cannot raise at US-comparable valuations without US capital leading.

## Liquidity and exit status

Realized liquidity events in European defense-tech are minimal. Secondary marketplaces list [[Helsing]] lines with limited depth; [[Stark]], [[Auterion]], [[Quantum Systems]] do not have active secondary quotes as of April 2026.

Positions relative to IPO capacity:

- [[Helsing]] at €12B is approaching typical European defense-prime listing scale ([[Saab|SAAB]] ~€15B, [[Leonardo]] ~€25B)
- [[Auterion]] is profitable on ~€100M 2025 revenue — an unusual base for a defense-tech startup, though revenue scale remains below typical IPO thresholds
- [[Stark]] at $500M and ~$100M raised is early-stage relative to peers

## Active structural dynamics

- [[Schuldenbremse reform]] per-supplier cap structure — whether the €1B ceiling stays, lifts, or gets replicated across other European states
- [[NATO Innovation Fund]] scale — €1B fund; successor or expansion vehicles under discussion
- [[Helsing]] capital trajectory — round cadence and any pre-IPO signaling
- [[Rheinmetall]] / [[Auterion]] stake — whether the significant minority becomes a controlling position
- German coalition dynamics — Green-party and coalition-partner posture toward US-investor participation in domestic defense contracts
- [[EU]] procurement harmonization — [[EDF]] scale and cross-border defense-industrial consolidation
