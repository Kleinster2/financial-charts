# Hukou

China's household registration system — a Mao-era administrative tool, formalised in 1958, that ties each citizen to a specific locality (typically rural-vs-urban + a specific city/county) and gates access to social services on the basis of that registration. The system is the structural source of much of the urban-rural inequality that [[Common Prosperity]] policy targets: rural-hukou residents working in Chinese cities are effectively second-class for purposes of public schooling, healthcare access, social housing, and pension benefits. [[Li Shi]]'s May 2026 lecture (translated by [[Pekingnology]]) identified hukou reform as one of the four central policy mechanisms for the 2026 "invest in people" version of Common Prosperity (per Li Shi). The May 22 2026 State Council implementation opinion turned part of that academic-policy agenda into an operational public-services mandate: basic services should be provided based on where people actually live, not only where their hukou is registered.

Hukou reform is one of the most politically difficult structural reforms in Chinese policy — the rents to urban-hukou holders (in housing, schooling, healthcare) make full liberalisation electorally and administratively contentious, while the productivity and growth costs of restricting labour mobility have been visible since the 2010s.

---

## How the system works

Each Chinese citizen has a hukou registration with two attributes:

| Attribute | Categories | Implication |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Type | Agricultural (rural) vs non-agricultural (urban) | Determines social-services entitlements |
| Location | Specific province / city / county | Determines where services can be accessed |

A worker who moves from rural Hubei to Shenzhen for work retains rural-Hubei hukou unless they go through a hukou transfer — which is restricted, especially for tier-1 cities. The result: ~290M migrant workers in 2023 living and working in Chinese cities with rural hukou, and therefore degraded access to urban public services.

---

## Why hukou reform matters

Three structural effects (per Li Shi, Pekingnology May 15, 2026, plus general economic literature):

1. Labour market inefficiency — workers can't fully follow productivity gradients across cities; firms can't fully reach the deepest available labour pool. The aggregate productivity drag is estimated at 1-2 pp of annual GDP growth in various academic studies (rough range; figures vary)
2. Education access gap — children of migrant workers often cannot attend public schools in the city where their parents work, forcing many families to either leave children behind in rural areas (the "left-behind children" cohort, estimated at ~70M in the 2010s) or pay for private schooling
3. Healthcare and pension portability — fragmented systems across localities create high transaction costs for migrants who try to use services outside their hukou jurisdiction

The cumulative effect is to suppress consumption growth (migrants save heavily because they can't access urban safety nets), constrain labour productivity, and entrench rural-urban inequality.

## Reform attempts

| Period | Reform | Outcome |
|--------|--------|---------|
| 2003-2014 | Gradual loosening for small cities | Modest impact; large cities largely exempted |
| 2014 | National plan to grant urban hukou to 100M migrants by 2020 | Partially achieved; large-city restrictions intensified |
| 2019 | Cities under 3M pop instructed to remove hukou restrictions entirely | Implemented inconsistently |
| 2022-2024 | "Common prosperity" framing emphasised hukou reform | Mostly rhetorical; tier-1 cities still heavily restricted |
| 2026 ([[Li Shi]] proposal + State Council opinion) | Equal access to public services for migrants; employee social insurance at place of employment; improved education, rental housing, medical coverage, employment services, and safety-net access | Partly operationalized through May 22 State Council guidelines; gradual implementation and local-fiscal capacity remain the binding constraints |

The pattern: small-city hukou is now largely liberalised; tier-1 and tier-2 cities still enforce significant restrictions through points-based systems that favour high-skill workers, property owners, and long-tenure residents.

---

## May 2026 public-services implementation opinion

The State Council's May 22 2026 guidelines are best read as service portability and migrant integration, not abolition of hukou. Official Xinhua/State Council language says China will provide basic public services based on actual residence and aims to let residents without local hukou access services on the same basis as local-hukou residents. The listed measures map closely to the social-mobility bottlenecks in the Li Shi frame: education for migrant children, broader public rental housing coverage for stably employed migrants, full removal of household-registration restrictions on employee social-insurance participation, better basic medical coverage and employment services, and gradual extension of child welfare, elder care, social assistance, and disability support.

The constraint is implementation. [[Sinocism]]'s public Sharp China preview (May 28 2026) frames the reform as meaningful but not revolutionary: Beijing and Shanghai are still likely to protect top-tier entry barriers, while local governments are already fiscally stressed and may need central-transfer support to fund services for the resident population rather than the registered population. The market read should therefore be slow household-demand plumbing, not a sudden consumption stimulus.

## Investment-thread implications

Three structural reads (inference):

1. Consumption-sensitive sectors gain from hukou liberalisation. Migrants entering full urban services consume more (safety net, less precautionary saving). Retail, services, and consumer-durables firms benefit, but the May 2026 opinion should be modeled as gradual disposable-income support rather than a one-quarter demand shock
2. Tier-1 city property prices have a hukou-driven scarcity premium. Liberalisation would compress this premium; conversely, the persistence of hukou restrictions is part of why tier-1 property has held up better than the broader [[Three Red Lines]] property correction
3. Education-services firms (post-tutoring-ban era) sit downstream of hukou reform — equal-access education proposals would expand the addressable market for any compliant education provider, especially in second-tier cities receiving large migrant populations
4. Local-government finance is the gating item. If fiscal transfers are not reweighted toward where migrants actually live, the reform becomes an unfunded mandate and the consumption multiplier stays muted
