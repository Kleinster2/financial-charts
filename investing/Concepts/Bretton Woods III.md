# Bretton Woods III

Framework coined by [[Zoltan Pozsar]] in March 2022, immediately after the [[Russia]]n central-bank reserve freeze, describing the post-2022 monetary order as a structural successor to the post-1971 dollar-Treasury system. Core claim: the weaponisation of dollar reserves accelerates a shift from "inside money" (USD-denominated claims, [[Treasuries]]) to "outside money" (gold, commodities, real assets) for sovereigns wary of US sanctions reach. The framing is influential enough that "BWIII" is now load-bearing shorthand in policy and sovereign-wealth circles regardless of whether one fully accepts the thesis.

---

## Synopsis

Three monetary eras in Pozsar's framing:

| Era | Period | Anchor | Architecture |
|-----|--------|--------|--------------|
| Bretton Woods I | 1944–1971 | Gold ($35/oz convertibility) | Fixed exchange rates, IMF-managed |
| Bretton Woods II | 1971–2022 | US Treasuries as global reserve asset | Floating rates, dollar-denominated reserves, [[Petrodollar]] recycling |
| Bretton Woods III | 2022–present | Commodities + geopolitics ("outside money") | Bifurcated settlement, commodity-collateralised credit, sanction-resistant assets |

The inflection: February 2022, when the US and G7 allies froze ~$300B of Russian central bank reserves. Pozsar's claim is that this transforms reserve-currency calculus for every non-aligned sovereign from "is the dollar liquid and stable?" to "will my dollars still be mine when I need them?"

---

## Five core claims

1. Commodity-anchored reserves displace Treasury-anchored reserves — [[Gold]] central-bank buying surged to record 1,000+ tonnes/year 2022–2024, led by [[China]]'s [[PBOC]], Poland, Turkey, [[India]]. Capital controls limit the [[Chinese yuan]] as a direct substitute, so gold absorbs the diversification.

2. Bilateral local-currency settlement gradually displaces SWIFT-dollar settlement — Russia-China yuan/ruble, India-Russia rupee/ruble, partial Saudi-China yuan invoicing, [[BRICS]] Cross-Border Payments Initiative, [[CIPS]] expansion to 1,573 indirect / 193 direct participants by 2025.

3. The [[Petrodollar]] convention persists but its strategic role erodes — oil still priced in USD on [[Brent crude|Brent]] / [[WTI]] / [[Dubai]]-Oman benchmarks (~85–90% of physical trade), but settlement increasingly bifurcates, and the 1974 Saudi-US compact is no longer operative.

4. Western inflation is structurally higher because commodity exporters demand real assets (gold, real estate, equity stakes, infrastructure) rather than recycling into US Treasuries at suppressed real rates.

5. The dollar isn't replaced by any single alternative — it's diluted by many. No competitor needs to challenge primacy; the cumulative effect of bilateral arrangements, gold accumulation, and sanctions-resistant settlement does the work.

## Track record so far (Mar 2022 – May 2026)

| Claim | Status |
|-------|--------|
| Gold central-bank buying surges | Confirmed — 2022–2024 broke records (1,136t / 1,037t / 1,000t+) |
| USD reserve share declines | Confirmed but slower than implied — [[IMF]] COFER 58.4% Q4 2022 → 56.77% Q4 2025 (-1.6pp over the period) |
| Commodity-collateralised credit emerges | Partial — Russia-China oil-for-yuan flows operational, but no major sovereign reserve shift to commodity-backed instruments |
| Western inflation structurally higher | Partial — 2022–2024 inflation spike clearly commodity-supply-shock-driven; persistence post-2024 contested |
| Dollar terminal decline | Not yet — DXY held near 100–105 range 2024–2026; USD still dominates FX transactions (~88%) and trade invoicing (~40%) |

The thesis is directionally validated but the magnitudes have been slower than Pozsar's most aggressive 2022 framing implied. Critics characterise the current state as "BWII wounded, not replaced."

## Why it matters for investment work

The framing changes which questions are load-bearing.

Under BWII assumptions, the dominant questions are about the Fed reaction function — rates, real yields, dollar cyclicality, term structure.

Under BWIII assumptions, an additional question becomes load-bearing: is the asset I'm holding politically conditional? That adds a sanctions-risk premium to USD-denominated reserve assets held by sovereigns wary of US foreign policy.

Practical applications:

- [[Gold]] structural bid from central-bank diversification (the BWIII portfolio default)
- US Treasury foreign demand composition shift — central banks declining as marginal buyer, hedge funds and private investors filling in (see [[Dollar reserve status erosion]] for the [[Cayman Islands]] / hedge-fund Treasury holdings finding)
- Commodity producers (energy, mining, agriculture) carry an embedded "real-asset reserve" premium
- Bilateral payment infrastructure (CIPS, mBridge, BRICS Cross-Border Payments Initiative) deserves tracking as the operational substrate of the new system
- USD-denominated EM debt revaluation as bilateral local-currency credit lines expand

## Caveats and criticisms

The thesis is structurally a forecast, not a description. Pozsar called the inflection in March 2022; the actual transition (if it is one) plays out over decades. Mid-period the dollar can remain dominant on most measures while the BWIII framing is still correct about the direction.

Network effects are sticky. Treasury market depth (~$28T outstanding), SWIFT dollar-clearing share (~88% of FX), and the dollar invoicing standard for oil benchmarks all benefit from network effects that don't unwind on the same timescale as central-bank reserve composition.

No coordinating alternative exists. Unlike Bretton Woods I (designed at the 1944 conference) or Bretton Woods II (de facto post-Nixon shock), there is no operational replacement architecture for BWIII — only fragmented bilateral arrangements. The system is *post-something* rather than *pre-something*.

The "outside money" claim is partial. Gold's role has grown but is structurally limited (no yield, costly storage, finite supply). Commodities are not fungible reserve assets the way currencies are.

Sanctions weaponisation isn't new. The US has used dollar-clearing as a weapon since the 1990s ([[Cuba]], [[Iran]], North Korea). The 2022 Russian reserve freeze was unprecedented in scale but not in kind — the "BWIII inflection" framing arguably overweights one episode.
