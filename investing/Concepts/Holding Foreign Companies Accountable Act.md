#concept #us-china #capital-markets #regulation #accounting

**Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act** (HFCAA) — the December 2020 US law that threatens to delist foreign issuers from US exchanges if their auditor cannot be inspected by the [[PCAOB]] for three consecutive years (shortened to two by the 2021 Accelerating HFCAA). Aimed squarely at US-listed Chinese companies, whose mainland/Hong Kong auditors Beijing had long shielded from PCAOB inspection on state-secrets grounds. It is the audit-transparency track of US-China capital-markets decoupling — distinct from, and complementary to, the ownership-based [[Executive Order 13959]].

---

## Synthesis

HFCAA reframed US listing as a privilege conditioned on audit transparency, and for two years (2020–2022) it was the single largest delisting risk hanging over the ~$2tn of US-listed Chinese ADRs. The crisis was defused, not resolved: an August 2022 audit-access protocol and the PCAOB's December 2022 confirmation of complete inspection access reset the clock and lifted the immediate threat for roughly 200 issuers ([[Alibaba]], [[Baidu]], [[JD.com]], PDD, and the rest). But the access must be re-confirmed annually, and the statute remains on the books — a standing lever that can be re-armed if China re-blocks inspections or if Washington chooses escalation. The threat re-emerged as a live policy topic in 2025 amid renewed US-China tension, and the broader repatriation it helped drive is visible in the numbers: US-listed Chinese market cap fell from ~$2.1tn to ~$1.09tn by March 2025, with at least 13 companies voluntarily delisting and most large names securing Hong Kong dual-primary listings as insurance.

---

## How it works

- HFCAA (signed December 18, 2020): if the PCAOB is unable to inspect an issuer's audit firm for three consecutive years, the SEC must prohibit trading in that issuer's securities — i.e., delisting. The act also requires issuers to disclose government ownership.
- Accelerating HFCAA (2021, via the 2023 appropriations): shortened the window from three years to two, pulling the potential delisting date forward to 2024.
- The premise, in the [[SEC]]/PCAOB framing: access to US capital markets is "a privilege, not a right," and that privilege is conditioned on the same audit oversight every domestic issuer faces.

The bind for Chinese issuers: PRC law (state-secrets and data-security rules) had prohibited their auditors from handing inspection materials to a foreign regulator. HFCAA forced a choice between US-market access and that prohibition.

---

## The 2022 breakthrough

- August 26, 2022 — the PCAOB signed a Statement of Protocol with the CSRC and PRC Ministry of Finance, creating a pathway to inspect mainland China and Hong Kong audit firms.
- December 2022 — the PCAOB announced it had secured complete access to inspect and investigate those firms for the first time in history, vacating its prior negative determination and resetting the HFCAA clock.

This removed the near-term delisting risk for the bulk of US-listed Chinese issuers. It did not repeal HFCAA; it satisfied it for the moment.

## Distinct from EO 13959

HFCAA is frequently conflated with the [[Executive Order 13959]] delistings, but they are different vectors of the same decoupling:

| | HFCAA | [[Executive Order 13959]] |
|---|---|---|
| Basis | audit-transparency (PCAOB access) | ownership / military-link (CCMC / NS-CMIC list) |
| Trigger | 2–3 years of no inspection access | designation by DoD/Treasury |
| Scope | all China-based issuers using non-inspectable auditors | specific designated firms |
| Status | defused 2022, standing lever | active list, expanded under Biden |
| Marquee names | [[Alibaba]], [[Baidu]], [[JD.com]], PDD | the three telecoms ([[China Mobile]] etc.) |

Together they form a two-track capital-markets pressure system: one keyed to disclosure, one to security ties. The telecoms hit by EO 13959 were delisted for *who they are*; the broader ADR complex was threatened by HFCAA for *how they were audited*.

## Market impact

- US-listed Chinese market cap: ~$2.1tn → ~$1.09tn (March 2025).
- At least 13 voluntary delistings since 2020; most large ADRs ([[Alibaba]], [[JD.com]], [[Baidu]]) added Hong Kong dual-primary or primary listings as delisting insurance — the listing-repatriation wave that also pulled the [[China Mobile|telecoms]] home to Shanghai.
- The standing risk keeps a structural discount on US-listed China ADRs versus their Hong Kong lines and contributes to the home-listing migration.

---

## Standing risk

The 2022 deal is conditional and annual: the PCAOB re-confirms access each year, and any re-blocking by Beijing — or a US decision to tighten the standard — re-arms the statute. The 2025 re-emergence of delisting as a Washington policy topic shows the lever has not gone away; it is dormant, not removed.
