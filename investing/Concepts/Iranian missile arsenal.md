**Iranian missile arsenal** — [[Iran]]'s domestically developed ballistic missile, cruise missile, and space launch vehicle program, managed primarily by the [[IRGC]] Aerospace Force. The largest and most diverse missile inventory in the [[Middle East]], with over 3,000 missiles across 16+ types, ranging from short-range battlefield systems to intermediate-range ballistic missiles. The program's strategic ceiling is defined by the [[range-payload tradeoff]]: [[Iran]] can reach approximately 2,000 km with a militarily useful warhead, or attempt 3,500-4,000 km with a payload too light for a first-generation nuclear device — a constraint the March 2026 [[Diego Garcia attack 2026|Diego Garcia attack]] tested and failed to overcome.

---

## The story

Iran's missile program was born from the trauma of the [[Iran-Iraq War]] (1980-1988), when [[Saddam Hussein]]'s [[Iraq]] launched hundreds of Scud missiles at Iranian cities during the "War of the Cities" (1985-1988). Iran had no indigenous response — it had to import Scud-Bs from [[Libya]] and [[Syria]], later from [[North Korea]] and [[China]]. The lesson was existential: a country without its own missile capability is defenseless against a neighbor with one. The [[IRGC]], not the regular military, was given the mandate to ensure Iran would never be caught without retaliatory reach again.

The technology pathway traces directly through [[North Korea]]. In the 1990s, Pyongyang transferred Scud-B and Scud-C technology, which Iran reverse-engineered and extended into the Shahab series. The Shahab-3 (first tested 1998) was a direct derivative of North Korea's Nodong-1 — liquid-fueled, ~1,300 km range, capable of hitting [[Israel]]. This was the program's first strategic milestone. The Ghadr-110 and Emad variants stretched the Shahab-3 airframe to ~1,800-2,000 km with improved guidance, but remained liquid-fueled — meaning hours of fueling time, vulnerability to preemptive strikes, and the logistical burden of toxic propellant.

The decisive pivot came with solid-fuel technology. Liquid rockets are strategic liabilities: they take 30-60 minutes to fuel, must launch from fixed pads, and the propellant itself is hazardous. Solid-fuel missiles launch in minutes from mobile transporter-erector-launchers, can be hidden in tunnels, and require no field fueling. Iran's [[Sejjil]] (first tested 2008, two-stage solid-fuel, 2,000 km range) represented a generational leap — the first Iranian missile that could be launched on short notice against [[Israel]]. The Kheibar Shekan (2022) refined this further: single-stage solid-fuel, 1,450 km range, maneuverable warhead, launchable from a commercial vehicle chassis. The [[Fattah]]-1 (2023, claimed Mach 13-15 terminal speed) and [[Fattah]]-2 (hypersonic glide vehicle variant, first operational use March 2026) represent Iran's attempt at defeating missile defense systems through speed and maneuverability rather than range.

The longest-range confirmed ballistic missile is the [[Khorramshahr]], derived from North Korea's BM-25/Musudan (itself derived from the Soviet R-27 submarine-launched missile). At full 1,800 kg payload, range is approximately 2,000 km. In 2019, Iran displayed a variant with a smaller reentry vehicle that the [[United Kingdom|UK]]/[[France]]/[[Germany]] assessed could reach 3,000 km — but this was never flight-tested at that distance. The [[Khorramshahr]] is liquid-fueled, which limits its tactical utility, but its payload capacity makes it the only confirmed Iranian missile theoretically capable of carrying a first-generation nuclear device.

The program's strategic ceiling is the [[range-payload tradeoff]]. The math is unforgiving: the [[Khorramshahr]] at 1,800 kg payload reaches 2,000 km. Drop to 750 kg and it reaches ~3,000 km. Drop to 300-500 kg and it might reach 3,500-4,000 km — but a crude first-generation nuclear device weighs an estimated 750-1,000 kg. At that weight, the [[Khorramshahr]] tops out around 2,000-2,500 km. This means Iran can threaten [[Israel]] (~1,200 km from western Iran) with a nuclear-capable missile, but cannot threaten [[Diego Garcia]] (~3,700 km) or targets in [[Europe]] without either radical warhead miniaturization or a fundamentally more capable delivery system.

The space launch vehicle pathway offers a theoretical breakout. Iran has launched satellites using the Simorgh (liquid-fuel, two-stage) and [[Qaem-100]] (solid-fuel, three-stage, IRGC-developed). The [[Qaem-100]] is the more militarily relevant: it placed a 60 kg satellite into 550 km orbit in 2024, and if reconfigured as a ballistic missile, analysts estimate it could deliver ~1,000 kg to 3,000-4,000 km. The [[DIA]] assessed that Iran's SLV development "would shorten the timeline" for ICBM development. The [[Diego Garcia attack 2026|Diego Garcia attack]] on March 21, 2026 — where Iran fired two intermediate-range missiles at the US/UK base ~3,700 km away — may have been a test of this [[Qaem-100]]-derived weapon. Israeli analysts identified the missiles as "two-stage ballistic missiles with a range of 4,000 km." One failed mid-flight. The other was intercepted by a US Navy [[SM-3]] interceptor. Neither reached the target.

[[Israel]]i strikes in October 2024 reportedly destroyed 12-20 planetary mixers used to produce solid rocket fuel — the machines that blend ammonium perchlorate oxidizer with HTPB binder into propellant grains. This was a precision hit on the bottleneck of Iran's solid-fuel program, severely degrading production capacity for the [[Sejjil]], Kheibar Shekan, [[Fattah]] series, and [[Qaem-100]]. A ship carrying 1,000 tons of sodium perchlorate (a precursor) arrived at Bandar Abbas in late 2025 — evidence of attempted reconstitution. The June 2025 Iran-Israel war (Operation True Promise III) expended hundreds of missiles from the arsenal, and Israeli estimates put the remaining inventory at ~1,500 missiles and 200 launchers by war's end.

The investment read is layered. Iran's missile program is the primary driver of missile defense spending across the [[Middle East]] and [[Europe]] — every range extension announcement triggers procurement cycles for [[Patriot]], [[THAAD]], [[Arrow]], and naval [[SM-3]]/SM-6 systems. The October 2024 planetary mixer strikes demonstrated that the supply chain of proliferant missile programs is itself a targetable vulnerability, with implications for how markets should price missile defense versus strike capability. The Diego Garcia attack's failure — both technologically (mid-flight breakup) and operationally (SM-3 intercept) — suggests Iran's extended-range capability is aspirational, not reliable. But the attempt itself represents a doctrinal shift: Iran is reaching beyond the regional envelope, and the next generation of the program will be designed to close the gap.
