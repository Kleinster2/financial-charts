# Three Red Lines

August 2020 [[China]] policy that imposed leverage caps on property developers, segmented by debt-to-asset, net-gearing, and short-term-debt-to-cash ratios. The policy was the proximate catalyst for the [[Evergrande]] crisis (default 2021), the broader Chinese property correction (2021-2026 ongoing), and the asset-side stress that became the most consequential domestic-policy outcome of [[Xi Jinping]]'s third term. The Three Red Lines is the canonical example of CCP regulatory tightening producing intended financial discipline plus a much larger, unintended macroeconomic contraction.

The structural significance for the 2026 vault thread: the property correction the Three Red Lines triggered is still working its way through Chinese household balance sheets, local-government finance ([[LGFV]]), and consumption dynamics — and is the fiscal constraint that limits how much [[Common Prosperity]] reform can be funded at the local-government level.

---

## The three lines

Each developer was scored against three balance-sheet metrics; the number of lines crossed determined the allowed growth in interest-bearing liabilities (per PBOC + MOHURD August 2020 framework):

| Line | Threshold | Tested |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Liability-to-asset ratio (excl. customer deposits) | < 70% | Developer's overall leverage |
| Net gearing ratio | < 100% | Net debt relative to equity |
| Cash-to-short-term-debt ratio | > 1.0 | Liquidity buffer |

| Lines crossed | Allowed annual growth in interest-bearing liabilities |
|---------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| 0 | 15% |
| 1 | 10% |
| 2 | 5% |
| 3 | 0% — no new debt |

The harshest tier (all three crossed) was effectively a forced deleveraging — developers couldn't roll over debt without shrinking. Several large developers, including [[Evergrande]], were in this tier at the time of policy launch.

---

## What it did

The policy worked as intended on the leverage side — developers shrank their balance sheets and corporate leverage ratios came down. But it also collapsed the property sector's growth model, which depended on continuous land acquisition financed by short-term debt and presales (per general consensus in 2022-2024 China-macro analysis):

| Channel | Mechanism | Outcome |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Developer financing | New debt blocked for the most leveraged firms | [[Evergrande]] default Sep 2021; [[Country Garden]] / [[Sunac]] / [[Kaisa]] / [[Shimao]] etc. followed |
| Presale deposits | Presale revenue constrained; deposits restricted from operational use | Stalled projects; "unfinished homes" protest movement 2022 |
| Local-government revenue | Land sales to developers (>30% of municipal revenue in many cities) collapsed | [[LGFV]] balance-sheet stress; local-government fiscal capacity impaired |
| Household balance sheets | Property = ~60% of urban household wealth; price declines hit balance sheets directly | Consumption suppression, precautionary savings rise |
| Construction-linked sectors | Cement, steel, copper, appliances, white goods | Sustained demand depression 2022-2026 |

The combination produced the longest sustained property contraction in modern Chinese history. By 2024-2026 the policy had been partially relaxed (the strictest tier requirements selectively waived for systemic developers), but the structural correction was still unfolding.

---

## Why it was launched

Two stated motivations (per PBOC public communications + analyst readings):

1. Reduce systemic financial-stability risk from over-leveraged property sector — the "grey rhino" risk that PBOC had been signalling since 2017
2. Redirect capital flows from property speculation toward "real economy" investment — consistent with broader [[Common Prosperity]] framing

The unstated political read (inference): the policy was part of the Xi-era consolidation of state power over the private sector. The most-leveraged developers — Evergrande, Sunac, Kaisa — had founder-CEOs with political-network exposure and were natural targets for a "discipline the high-flying private founders" enforcement wave (this read is contested; the financial-stability rationale alone is sufficient to explain the policy).

---

## The 2024-2026 partial unwind

By 2024 the macro damage from the Three Red Lines was severe enough that policy began selective relaxation:

- "Three Arrows" support package (Nov 2022) — bank lending + bond issuance + equity financing for surviving developers
- "Whitelist" mechanism (2023) — designated systemic developers eligible for continued financing despite line-crossing
- Reduced minimum down payments + relaxed mortgage rates (multiple rounds 2023-2024)
- Local-government home-purchase support programmes
- 2024-2025: PBOC + Politburo signals of "stabilisation" rather than further correction

By 2026 the policy framework is effectively dormant rather than formally repealed — developers no longer face hard line-crossing penalties, but they also can't access the pre-2020 financing patterns. The sector operates in a hybrid regime where leverage caps are advisory and the property growth model is permanently changed (inference).

---

## Investment-thread implications

Three structural reads (inference):

1. Chinese property is in a multi-year balance-sheet adjustment that will not return to pre-2020 growth patterns. Developers that survive are leaner, smaller-scale, and dependent on central-government policy support; cyclical recovery doesn't restore the pre-2020 model
2. Construction-linked commodity demand (cement, steel, copper) is permanently lower than pre-2020 trend. China's share of global construction-materials demand declines from peak levels
3. Local-government finance is the persistent overhang. Until LGFV balance sheets clear and a new municipal revenue model emerges, fiscal capacity for Common Prosperity reform (including [[Hukou]] reform, public childcare, healthcare expansion) is constrained
