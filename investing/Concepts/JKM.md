# JKM

Japan-Korea Marker — the benchmark for spot LNG pricing in Asia, published by Platts (S&P Global). The reference price for the world's largest LNG-importing region. JKM and [[TTF]] together define the global LNG pricing landscape — TTF for Europe, JKM for Asia.

## Why JKM matters

Japan and South Korea are the world's largest LNG importers per capita — neither has domestic gas production at scale. Their electricity grids, heating systems, and industrial sectors depend on imported LNG, much of it from [[Qatar]] and [[Australia]] under long-term contracts indexed to oil prices. JKM captures the spot premium above those contracts — when supply tightens, JKM spikes and importers face either paying up or curtailing industrial production.

Oil indexation is a secondary amplifier: long-term LNG contracts tied to crude prices see indexed gas prices rise as oil rises — a compounding effect on top of direct supply loss. [[Colby Connelly]] (MEI panel, March 20, 2026) flagged this mechanism as a major driver of Asian energy costs beyond the headline LNG spot price.

## March 2026 — Iran conflict

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Move since start of war | +90% |
| Move since [[Ras Laffan]] attack | +25% |
| Context | ~20% of global LNG supply halted |

The +90% surge reflects Asia's acute vulnerability: [[Qatar]] (world's second-largest LNG exporter after the US, ~20% of global shipments per [[Kpler]]) declared force majeure, and [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure trapped cargoes. [[Japan]], [[South Korea]], and [[Taiwan]] — all dependent on Gulf LNG — are competing with European buyers for the remaining supply, bidding JKM higher.

Asian governments responded with emergency measures: [[South Korea]], [[Taiwan]], and [[Japan]] banned refined fuel exports and hoarded domestic supply. [[India]] suspended LPG shipments to commercial operators to prioritize households.
