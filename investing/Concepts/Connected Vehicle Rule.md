#concept #policy #automotive #china #usa

Connected Vehicle Rule is the U.S. Commerce / [[BIS]] rule restricting connected vehicles that use covered vehicle-connectivity or automated-driving hardware and software tied to [[China]] or [[Russia]].

---

## Synthesis

The Connected Vehicle Rule turns car software into an import-control surface. It is not only an EV-tariff rule: it reaches the vehicle-connectivity system, automated-driving software, and ultimately the hardware stack that lets a car communicate outward, update remotely, or sense and navigate its environment (Federal Register final rule). That makes the car part of the same decoupling perimeter as chips, cloud, telecom equipment, and AI models.

The [[Polestar US connected-vehicle ban 2026]] is the first useful market test because it separates brand domicile from control. [[Volvo Cars]] and [[Polestar]] both sit in the [[Geely]] ecosystem, but Volvo received U.S. authorization while Polestar did not, forcing Polestar to stop future U.S. model-year sales while keeping existing inventory and service support alive (The Verge; MarketWatch). The read-through is that Washington is screening operational control, software stack, and China-linked ownership at the vehicle level, not just asking whether the grille badge looks European (inference).

## Rule mechanics

The rule restricts importing or selling connected vehicles in the U.S. when the covered software or hardware has a prohibited nexus to [[China]] or [[Russia]]. The software restriction arrives first because code, data flows, remote updates, and automated-driving features create the near-term security surface; hardware follows on a longer replacement cycle because redesigning vehicle electronics and supplier chains takes longer.

The policy object is broader than infotainment. Vehicle-connectivity systems include the components that let the car communicate with networks, external devices, and cloud services. Automated-driving systems include the sensor-and-software stack that can execute driving functions. That puts the rule directly next to [[Automotive AI]], [[Autonomous vehicles]], and wireless-module security rather than in a narrow tariff bucket.

## First enforcement signal: Polestar

In June 2026, reporting said U.S. authorities had not granted [[Polestar]] authorization under the rule, barring future U.S. sales of model-year 2027 vehicles. [[Volvo Cars]] received authorization, creating the clean contrast inside the [[Geely]] ecosystem. Polestar said it would keep selling existing U.S. inventory and continue service, while shifting strategic focus toward [[Europe]].

That distinction matters for the [[Chinese EVs enter US]] thesis. The earlier workaround map treated Swedish/British/European badges, U.S. manufacturing, and Mexico production as possible paths around tariffs. The Connected Vehicle Rule adds a different gate: even if assembly geography works, the software/control stack can still be disqualified.
