# Iran conflict insurance impact

Insurance and reinsurance market reaction to [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]]. The defining story is not premium increases — it is how insurance withdrawal alone can shut a waterway without a single mine or blockade ship. Part of [[2026 Iran conflict market impact]].

---

## Synthesis

The [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure proves that insurance is the actual enforcement mechanism of maritime security — not navies, not mines, not blockade ships. The four-link chain (insurance withdrawal → charter party frustration → bank financing collapse → ships halt) achieved de facto closure within days, with no physical obstruction of the waterway. This is the central insight: a 300-year-old London market committee ([[Lloyd's of London|Lloyd's]] JWC) has more operational control over global shipping than any single navy.

The [[US Development Finance Corporation|DFC]]-backed program represents an unprecedented attempt to nationalize this function. If DFC pricing (~1% vs Lloyd's 3-7%) holds, and if US military intelligence becomes the primary risk-assessment input, the center of marine war risk migrates from London to Washington — permanently. The Ukrainian grain corridor precedent shows government backstops work, but the 2026 version is adversarial: Washington competing against [[Lloyd's of London|Lloyd's]] rather than working through it.

The post-ceasefire lag is the most underpriced dimension. Markets will treat a ceasefire as resolution. But the 30-60 day incident-free window before standard policies reinstate, combined with the opportunity cost of Gulf deployment (why pay $5M war risk when Atlantic cargoes cost $250K?), means Hormuz shipping recovery will lag any diplomatic resolution by months. The [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]] is structural.

## How marine insurance works — the layered architecture

Marine insurance is not one product. It is four distinct layers, each independently underwritten, each separately cancellable:

| Layer | What it covers | Who provides it | Normal cost |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Hull & Machinery (H&M) | Physical damage to the vessel | Commercial insurers, [[Lloyd's]] syndicates | Annual premium based on vessel value |
| Protection & Indemnity (P&I) | Liability — crew injury, environmental damage, third-party claims | Mutual clubs ([[Gard]], [[Skuld]], [[NorthStandard]], etc.) | Member assessments (mutual, not commercial) |
| Cargo | Damage to or loss of cargo | Commercial insurers, often arranged by cargo owner | Varies by commodity and route |
| War risk | Losses from military action, terrorism, piracy | Specialty syndicates at [[Lloyd's of London]], some P&I clubs | Add-on premium, % of hull value per transit |

War risk is the critical layer. It sits on top of the other three as an optional add-on. Pre-2026, a routine Gulf transit cost ~0.05-0.25% of hull value in war risk premium — for a $100M tanker, roughly $50K-$250K per crossing. Affordable, invisible, and almost never invoked.

The four layers are independent. A shipowner can have full H&M coverage, full P&I membership, full cargo insurance — and still be uninsurable for war risk. That single missing layer breaks the entire chain.

## The LMA Joint War Committee — the gating mechanism

The [[Lloyd's of London|Lloyd's]] Market Association's Joint War Committee (JWC) is the operational switch that turns insurance withdrawal into shipping closure. It maintains a list of "Listed Areas" — zones where standard marine policies automatically exclude war-related losses.

How it works:

1. Conflict breaks out or escalates
2. JWC convenes (extraordinary sessions possible within hours — they held one March 2, 2026)
3. JWC designates the area as "Listed"
4. Standard form policies contain automatic exclusion clauses that activate when an area is listed — insurers don't renegotiate, coverage simply ceases
5. War risk must then be purchased separately at repriced rates — if available at all

The JWC has centuries of institutional memory. It defines the boundaries of insurable risk in global shipping. When the JWC lists an area, it is not a recommendation — it is a contractual trigger embedded in virtually every marine policy written through [[Lloyd's of London|Lloyd's]], which underwrites ~40% of global marine cargo.

P&I clubs operate on a parallel track. They can cancel war risk cover with short notice — the five major clubs (Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, London P&I Club, American Club) cancelled Gulf war risk effective March 5, 2026. These clubs collectively cover ~80% of global shipping tonnage. When they cancel, they are not repricing — they are exiting.

## Why insurance withdrawal closes a waterway

The mechanical chain from insurance cancellation to shipping halt has four links. None require a physical blockade.

```
Insurance unavailable or prohibitively priced
    → Charter party impossible (insurance clause unsatisfiable)
        → Bank financing evaporates (letter of credit won't call without proof of insurance)
            → Ships don't sail
```

Link 1 — Charter party. A standard charter party (contract to hire a ship) includes an insurance availability clause. The charterer must demonstrate that the vessel carries adequate insurance for the route. When war risk is cancelled or priced at 3-7% of hull value ($2.4M-$5.6M per transit on an $80M tanker), the charter party becomes impossible to perform — "charter frustration." [[Maersk]], [[MSC]], [[CMA CGM]], and [[Hapag-Lloyd]] all blocked Gulf bookings by March 5, citing insurance unavailability.

Link 2 — Bank financing. A typical oil or LNG cargo moves on a letter of credit. The issuing bank requires proof of insurance before releasing payment. Without war risk cover, the LC is not called. The charterer's financing dries up — they cannot pay the shipowner for fuel, crew, or port fees. Banks will not finance a Gulf transit without war insurance; it is a regulatory and counterparty credit violation.

Link 3 — Crew and operations. Even if a shipowner were willing to transit uninsured (gambling on no attack), the crew dimension binds. Seafarer contracts and maritime labor conventions require adequate insurance. P&I cover includes crew liability — without it, crews won't board. The International Transport Workers' Federation can designate a zone as unsafe, creating legal grounds for crew refusal.

Link 4 — Physical halt. Ships don't sail. Not because they physically can't, but because every commercial and legal mechanism that enables a voyage has broken.

[[Kpler]] (Mar 1, 2026): *"Insurance withdrawal is doing the work that physical blockade has not — the outcome for cargo flow is largely the same."*

This is why [[Helima Croft]] ([[RBC Capital]], Mar 2) framed the Hormuz closure as "an insurance event, not a military one" — Iran's drone strikes near the Strait caused insurers to refuse transit, achieving de facto closure without a physical blockade.

## Premium mechanics — how war risk is priced

War risk premiums are quoted as a percentage of hull value, per transit. The premium reflects the underwriter's estimated probability of total loss.

| Date | Premium (% of hull value) | Implied loss probability | Cost on $100M tanker |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Pre-conflict | ~0.05-0.25% | Routine risk | $50K-$250K |
| March 1 (early adjustment) | ~0.2-0.4% | Elevated | $200K-$400K |
| March 2-5 (initial crisis) | ~1-1.5% | ~1-in-100 to 1-in-67 | $1M-$1.5M |
| March 5 (US/UK/Israel-linked vessels) | ~3-4.5% | ~1-in-33 to 1-in-22 | $3M-$4.5M |
| March 19 (latest) | 3-7%+ | ~1-in-33 to 1-in-14 | $3M-$7M |
| March 20 (bespoke, Tooze) | ~5% | ~1-in-20 | $5M |

Repricing is nearly instantaneous. The JWC can change area designations overnight. Syndicates reprice within hours depending on Gulf news. One London broker (Mar 19): *"When you have your vessel pointed [at the strait] with the engines on, call us and we'll find cover."*

The economics: a VLCC voyage from the Gulf to China earns roughly $12M-$20M in freight revenue. At 5% war risk premium on a $100M hull, the insurance cost alone is $5M — consuming 25-40% of gross revenue before crew, fuel, port fees, and cargo insurance. For most operators, this is uneconomic. The premium doesn't need to reach 100% to stop shipping; it just needs to exceed the margin.

War risk is a tiny premium pool globally — less than $3B/year across marine and aviation, with little standalone disclosure. This means even modest aggregate losses can spike premiums dramatically, because the pool has no depth to absorb them.

### Tiered pricing — flag and ownership matter

[[Marsh McLennan|Marsh]] broker Dylan Mortimer confirmed tiered pricing to the FT (Mar 5): US/UK/Israel-linked vessels quoted at 2-3x the base rate. Iranian targeting has been flag-agnostic (17+ vessels attacked through Mar 7, flags of convenience from Palau to Malta), but insurers price perceived targeting risk by ownership and flag state.

## The Hormuz crisis — what happened

### Week 1: coverage withdrawal (Feb 28 – Mar 5)

P&I clubs announced cancellation of war risk cover for the Persian Gulf effective March 5. [[Japan]]'s MS&AD Insurance Group suspended war risk underwriting for waters around Iran, Israel, and neighboring countries. The LMA Joint War Committee held an extraordinary meeting March 2.

This was not a hard market repricing — it was underwriters choosing to forgo premium income rather than hold the risk. By March 5, premiums had soared 12-fold: from ~0.25% to as high as 3% of hull value (FT, Lee Harris). At least seven tankers had been struck in the Strait and surrounding waters. Ships reported radio messages, apparently from the IRGC, telling them to stay out.

Some insurers cancelled policies specifically to reinstate at higher prices reflecting current risk; others exited the market entirely and refuse to provide Strait transit cover at any price.

*Source: FT, "Gulf insurance costs soar 12-fold despite Trump guarantee," Lee Harris, March 5, 2026*

### DFC government backstop (March 3-11)

Trump ordered the [[US Development Finance Corporation|DFC]] to provide "political risk insurance and guarantees for the financial security of ALL maritime trade, especially energy" through the Gulf. The announcement was a Truth Social post, not a signed executive order. The DFC is a development finance institution — it has never underwritten marine war risk insurance.

March 6: [[Ben Black]] (DFC CEO) and [[Scott Bessent]] (Treasury Secretary) announced a $20B maritime reinsurance facility. Legal basis: DFC's political risk insurance authority, stretched to cover kinetic military risk. DFC authorized to use up to ~$150B of its $205B statutory cap through 2031.

March 11: [[Chubb]] named lead underwriter. [[Evan Greenberg]] (Chubb chairman/CEO): *"Chubb is proud to lead and manage this program in partnership with the United States Government."* [[AIG]], [[Liberty Mutual]], and [[Lloyd's]] acknowledged active participation in negotiations.

Program structure: DFC provides the sovereign backstop (first-loss or extreme risk), Chubb leads front-end underwriting, reinsurance partners provide capacity behind Chubb.

Scale problem: [[JPMorgan]] analysts estimate 329 vessels operating in the Gulf requiring ~$352B total coverage — far exceeding DFC's $205B statutory cap. Covering beyond the cap would require Congressional action.

Industry reaction was skeptical. David Smith ([[McGill and Partners|McGill]], Mar 5): *"We've heard absolutely nothing more, other than that Truth Social statement."* Ed Finley-Richardson (Contango Research): *"It might have been a way to take the edge off the oil price, but on the face of it I don't see how it changes anything."*

### Lloyd's pushback — "not an insurance issue" (March 11)

[[Patrick Davison]] (Lloyd's head of underwriting, Mar 11): *"All the insurers at Lloyd's are still quoting business, and will still provide cover to basically anyone who asks."* The traffic slowdown is *"not an insurance issue — it's a question of vessel and crew safety."*

This is technically true and practically irrelevant. Cover is available at 3-7% of hull value. At those rates it is prohibitively expensive — but from Lloyd's perspective, the market is functioning. The distinction matters: Lloyd's is defending its market's reputation, not denying that shipping has stopped. [[Sir Charles Roxburgh]] (Lloyd's chair) met UK chancellor [[Rachel Reeves]] March 11; Reeves agreed there is "not really a problem with insurance."

Senior London figures framed the conflict as a growth opportunity: political violence insurance demand surging from Gulf data centers and infrastructure.

### DFC as competitive weapon — Lloyd's vs Washington (March 19)

*Source: FT, John Gapper, "Trump sails into battle with Lloyd's of London," March 19, 2026*

The DFC-backed program shifted from a supplement to direct competition with [[Lloyd's of London]]:

| Dimension | Lloyd's market | DFC/Chubb program |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Pricing | 3-7% of hull value | ~1% expected (Navy-escorted) |
| Backing | Private syndicates | US government reinsurance |
| Intelligence | Private networks | US military intelligence |
| Escort requirement | None | Navy-escorted ships may be required to take DFC-backed insurance |

The intelligence asymmetry is the structural threat. London's war insurers rely on private intelligence networks — if US military intelligence about shipping movements and dangers is filtered only to DFC-backed providers, Lloyd's loses its information edge. This would be "formidable" for London to compete against (Gapper).

Ukrainian precedent: government-backed insurance through [[Lloyd's of London|Lloyd's]] underwriters reduced vessel policy prices heavily when launched in 2023 to guarantee grain supplies during the Russia-Ukraine war. Trump is now using the same model — government backstop to suppress premiums — but against Lloyd's rather than through it.

If DFC-backed insurance becomes permanent, the center of marine war risk could shift from London to Washington. Lloyd's JWC has centuries of institutional memory, but government-subsidized pricing plus exclusive military intelligence is hard to compete with.

## Post-ceasefire lag — why recovery is slow

Even if shooting stopped tomorrow, normal war insurance won't reinstate immediately. The recovery has its own mechanics:

| Phase | Duration | What happens |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Active conflict | Ongoing | No standard war risk available; bespoke only at 3-7%+ |
| Ceasefire declared | Day 0 | Nothing changes for insurance — clock hasn't started |
| Incident-free period begins | Day 1-30 | Underwriters monitoring; no standard policies issued |
| Grade 1 reinstatement | Day 30+ | Some standard war risk policies resume at elevated premiums |
| Grade 2 reinstatement | Day 60+ | Broader coverage resumes; premiums begin normalizing |
| Full normalization | Months-years | Premiums return to pre-conflict levels only if risk is demonstrably lower |

[[Adam Tooze]] (Columbia, investor conference in Hong Kong, Mar 20): bespoke war insurance for Hormuz transit running ~5% of ship value — implying a 1-in-20 total loss probability. For a $100M tanker, that is $5M per trip. Given shipping margins, this renders Gulf transit uneconomic for most operators even before crew safety.

The lag mechanism: a week-long ceasefire produces zero insurance relief. A month-long ceasefire *begins* the clock. Full normalization requires months of sustained peace. Each new incident — a mine discovered, a drone spotted, a vessel fired upon — resets the 30-60 day window.

And there is always a choice of where to deploy assets. A shipowner with a tanker available asks: why route through Hormuz and pay $5M in war risk when I can trade Atlantic basin cargoes at $250K in standard coverage? The opportunity cost of Gulf deployment persists long after the shooting stops.

This asymmetry — instant closure, slow recovery — is why the [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]] is structural, not cyclical.

*Source: Ones and Tooze Ep. 234, Foreign Policy, March 20, 2026*

## Historical precedents

### Operation Earnest Will (1987-88)

US Navy escort operation during the Iran-Iraq Tanker War. 259 Kuwaiti tanker transits escorted, ~30 US warships deployed. Tankers re-flagged to US registry to justify military protection. The Strait remained open, but at enormous operational cost.

Why the 2026 situation is harder: modern asymmetric warfare (drones at $10-50K each vs $100M tankers) makes escort far more difficult. The 1987-88 threat was mine warfare and anti-ship missiles; modern drones are cheaper, smaller, and harder to defend against. US Navy decommissioned its last active minesweeper wing in January 2026 — weeks before the war. Navy officials privately told tanker executives there was "presently no availability for an escort mission" ([[Lloyd's List]]).

### Ukraine grain corridor (2023)

Government-backed insurance through [[Lloyd's of London|Lloyd's]] underwriters reduced vessel policy prices to enable Black Sea grain shipments. The model: sovereign backstop → lower premiums → shipping resumes. Trump's DFC program uses the same architecture, but directed against Lloyd's rather than through it.

### Red Sea / Houthi disruption (2024-25)

[[Houthi]] attacks on Red Sea shipping forced rerouting around the [[Cape of Good Hope]], adding 10-14 days per voyage. War risk premiums for Red Sea transit spiked to ~0.5-1% of hull value. The key lesson: even low-probability attacks (Houthis hit far fewer ships than Iran has in 2026) were sufficient to redirect global shipping routes. Insurance markets amplify physical risk into commercial impossibility.

## The convoy insurance problem

Insuring a single vessel transiting under Navy escort is one thing. Insuring a convoy of 20 oil tankers is structurally different — a single successful attack on one vessel could trigger claims across all policies simultaneously. This is the aggregation risk that makes reinsurers nervous.

London market figures acknowledged (Mar 11) that convoy insurance is *"where the DFC could potentially help"* — the government backstop absorbs the correlated tail risk that private syndicates cannot price. But the JPMorgan estimate ($352B total coverage needed for 329 Gulf vessels) dwarfs DFC's $205B statutory cap. The gap between what the market needs and what DFC can legally provide means partial confidence restoration, not full replacement of the private war-risk market.
