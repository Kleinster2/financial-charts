#concept #energy #oil #usa

**US Shale** — Hydraulic fracturing revolution that made US world's largest oil producer. Broke [[OPEC]]'s pricing power. Now mature, consolidating.

---

## The shale revolution

Before shale (pre-2010):
- US oil production declining for decades
- Dependent on imports
- OPEC controlled global prices
- Peak oil fears

After shale (2010s):
- US production: 5M → 13M bpd
- Net exporter by 2019
- OPEC lost pricing power
- Abundant supply crushed prices (2014-2016, 2020)

## Why shale disrupted [[OPEC]]

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Short-cycle | Wells drilled in weeks, not years |
| Scalable | Respond quickly to prices |
| Private land | No government approval needed |
| Technology gains | Costs fell 50%+ |
| Decline rates | But wells deplete in 2-3 years |

[[OPEC]]'s dilemma: Cut production → shale fills gap. Flood market → lose revenue.

## Supply discipline (post-2020)

The defining structural feature of US shale in 2026 is no longer growth — it is the explicit refusal to grow. Public-company capital discipline, breakeven inflation, and Tier-1 acreage exhaustion combine to break the pre-2020 reaction function in which crude above $80 brought a wall of barrels within months.

The [[Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas|Dallas Fed]] Q1 2026 Energy Survey is the cleanest empirical read: 73% of producers expect <=+0.25 mb/d of US production growth in 2026 despite WTI near $100, Brent above $110, and explicit White House pressure ([[Chris Wright]] + [[Doug Burgum]] producer call, April 2026). 50% expect the same in 2027. By comparison, 2017-2019 averaged ~+1.0 to +1.5 mb/d annually.

The late-May rig-count response shows the discipline regime is not absolute inertia. [[Baker Hughes]] reported oil rigs up 10 to 425 in the week ended May 22, 2026, the biggest weekly gain since April 2022. The [[Financial Times]] then supplied the company-level color: [[Diamondback Energy]] added rigs, [[Continental Resources]] raised 2026 capex by about $300M to $2.8B, and [[Enverus]] saw public-producer 2026 capex guidance up $490M versus guidance issued three months earlier. That is a lagged response to a sustained [[Strait of Hormuz]] physical shortage, not a full return to the 2010s supply wall: [[EIA]] and [[IEA]] still describe a 10+ mb/d [[Persian Gulf]] disruption and record inventory draws, while the US rig response is measured in dozens of rigs and a few hundred million dollars of incremental guidance.

Permian new-well breakevens have risen from ~$45-50/bbl (2020) to ~$63-70 (2026); Eagle Ford ~$63. The forward strip — the prices producers actually plan against — did not initially move enough to unlock activity, but the FT's May 6 [[Brent crude]] futures curve shows the Iran-war premium migrating into 2027-2028. That explains selective rig additions without invalidating the core discipline thesis.

For full mechanism, empirical anchors, and OPEC implications, see [[Shale supply discipline]].
