**Hong Kong dollar** — The currency of [[Hong Kong]], pegged to the US dollar since 1983 under a linked exchange rate system maintained by the Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA) within a convertibility band of 7.75-7.85 HKD per USD.

---

## Synopsis

The HKD peg is one of the most consequential currency arrangements in global finance. By linking the Hong Kong dollar to the USD, the HKMA effectively imports US monetary policy: when the [[Federal Reserve]] raises rates, Hong Kong rates must follow to maintain the peg, regardless of local economic conditions. This creates periodic mismatches — during 2022-2024, Hong Kong's economy was weakening (post-COVID, China slowdown, emigration) while US rates were at multi-decade highs, forcing the HKMA to tighten into a downturn. The result was a property crash (Hong Kong residential prices down ~25% from peak) and equity market weakness.

The peg has survived every test since 1983 — the 1997 Asian financial crisis, the 2008 GFC, the 2019 protests, and the 2020 National Security Law — because the HKMA holds over $400B in foreign reserves, making it one of the most heavily backed currency boards in the world. Abandoning the peg would require a political decision that would undermine [[Hong Kong]]'s role as an international financial center, which is why periodic speculation about de-pegging (most recently in 2022-2023) has never materialized. Three commercial banks are authorized to issue HKD banknotes: [[HSBC]], [[Standard Chartered]], and [[BOC Hong Kong]]. For investors holding Hong Kong-listed equities ([[EWH]], [[Sun Hung Kai Properties]], [[Techtronic Industries]]), the peg means their returns are USD-denominated in practice — the currency risk is to the peg itself breaking, not to gradual depreciation.
