**Wealth Enhancement** — the umbrella term wealth management firms use for tax-efficient strategies that preserve and grow after-tax wealth, particularly in retirement. The core idea: the tax code is not symmetric. Different account types, income sources, and timing of realizations face different rates, and the gap between the worst and best outcomes on the same portfolio can exceed 1-2% annually over a multi-decade retirement. Most strategies reduce to three levers — deferral (delay taxable events), conversion (shift income between tax regimes), and elimination (exploit step-up, charitable vehicles, or 0% brackets to permanently avoid tax).

---

## The story

### Tax-advantaged retirement accounts

The US retirement system sorts savings into three tax buckets:

1. Pre-tax (Traditional [[IRA]], [[401(k)]]): contributions deducted from income, grow tax-deferred, withdrawals taxed as ordinary income. The government's implicit deal: skip the tax now, pay it later.
2. After-tax/Roth ([[Roth IRA]], Roth 401(k)): contributions from after-tax income, grow tax-free, withdrawals tax-free. The deal: pay tax now, never again.
3. Taxable (brokerage accounts): no deduction, no deferral, but capital gains rates (0/15/20%) instead of income rates (up to 37%), and the step-up in basis at death eliminates unrealized gains for heirs.

Each bucket has different rules for contributions, withdrawals, and estate transfer — and the interplay between them is where the wealth enhancement industry lives.

### Required Minimum Distributions

[[IRS]] rules force owners of pre-tax retirement accounts to begin withdrawals at age 73 (raised from 72 by the [[SECURE Act]] 2.0, effective 2023). The annual Required Minimum Distribution is calculated by dividing the account balance as of December 31 of the prior year by a life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table. The percentage grows with age because the divisor shrinks:

| Age | Approx. RMD % |
|-----|---------------|
| 73 | 3.8% |
| 75 | 4.1% |
| 78 | 4.5% |
| 80 | 5.0% |
| 85 | 6.3% |
| 90 | 8.2% |
| 95 | 11.2% |
| 100 | 15.6% |

The table extends to age 120. RMDs are taxed as ordinary income regardless of what generated them inside the account. The penalty for missing an RMD is 25% of the amount not withdrawn (reduced from 50% by SECURE 2.0, and further reduced to 10% if corrected within two years).

RMDs are a percentage of the remaining balance, not a fixed drawdown — the account never reaches zero from RMDs alone. But the rising percentage, combined with account growth, can push retirees into higher brackets over time, creating a tax acceleration problem that worsens with age.

### The Roth conversion window

The most discussed wealth enhancement strategy is converting Traditional IRA assets to [[Roth IRA]] — paying income tax on the converted amount now in exchange for tax-free growth and withdrawals forever. The conversion is a bet that today's tax rate is lower than the rate the money would face later (either through RMDs or inheritance).

The optimal conversion window opens between retirement (income drops because salary ends) and age 73 (RMDs begin forcing income). During these "gap years," taxable income may be near zero, allowing conversions at the 10% or 12% bracket — money that would otherwise be forced out at 22-32% during RMD years. The math depends on current bracket, expected future bracket, investment horizon, and state taxes.

The strategy has limits. The converted amount is taxable income in the year of conversion. Large conversions can trigger [[IRMAA]] surcharges on [[Medicare]] premiums (based on income two years prior), push [[Social Security]] benefits into higher taxation bands, or disqualify the taxpayer from [[ACA]] premium subsidies. Modeling the interaction effects is where advisors earn their fees — or claim to.

After RMDs begin at 73, Roth conversions remain possible but the RMD itself must be taken first (the RMD amount cannot be converted). Additional amounts above the RMD can still be converted to shrink the account's future RMD base.

### Qualified Charitable Distributions

For retirees aged 70.5+ with pre-tax IRAs, [[Qualified Charitable Distributions]] allow direct transfers from the IRA to a qualified charity, up to $105,000/year (2024 limit, indexed for inflation). The QCD counts toward the RMD but is excluded from taxable income — the money goes straight from custodian to charity without appearing on the tax return. For retirees who are donating to charity anyway (synagogue, university, nonprofit), QCDs are strictly superior to taking the RMD, paying tax, and then donating from after-tax funds.

### Capital gains harvesting

The inverse of [[Tax-loss harvesting]]. In years when taxable income is low — sabbatical, gap year between retirement and Social Security, early retirement — the 0% long-term capital gains rate applies to taxable income up to ~$47,025 (single) or ~$94,050 (married filing jointly, 2024). An investor can deliberately sell appreciated positions, pay zero federal capital gains tax, and immediately repurchase the same securities (no wash sale rule on gains). The cost basis resets to the current price, permanently eliminating the embedded gain.

Combined with the standard deduction (~$15,000 single, ~$30,000 married), a married couple with no other income can realize roughly $124,000 in long-term gains tax-free in a single year. Repeating this across multiple low-income years systematically ratchets up the portfolio's cost basis.

Cautions: [[Social Security]] income counts toward the threshold. [[IRMAA]] surcharges trigger on a two-year lag — a gains harvest in 2026 affects Medicare premiums in 2028. [[ACA]] subsidies depend on modified AGI, creating a cliff where too much realized income destroys the subsidy.

### Step-up in basis at death

Under current law (IRC §1014), the cost basis of appreciated assets resets to fair market value at the owner's death. A stock bought at $10 and worth $100 at death passes to heirs with a $100 basis — the $90 gain is never taxed. This single provision is the foundation of the "never sell, borrow against it" strategy: use [[securities-backed lending]] to access liquidity without triggering capital gains, let the portfolio appreciate, and the step-up eliminates the deferred tax permanently.

The step-up applies to assets in taxable accounts but not to pre-tax retirement accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s), where distributions are always ordinary income regardless of who receives them.

### The 10-year rule for inherited IRAs

The [[SECURE Act]] (2019) eliminated the "stretch IRA" for most non-spouse beneficiaries. Previously, heirs could spread inherited IRA distributions over their own lifetime. Now, most non-spouse beneficiaries must drain the entire inherited IRA within 10 years of the owner's death. This compresses decades of tax-deferred growth into a 10-year forced liquidation, often during the heir's peak earning years at their highest marginal rate.

This rule is the primary driver behind pre-emptive Roth conversions by older investors: convert now at a known (often lower) rate rather than leaving the account for heirs to drain at an unknown (often higher) rate under the 10-year clock.

### The no-heirs scenario

Without heirs, several core strategies lose their rationale:

- Step-up in basis provides no benefit if there is no one to inherit the clean cost basis
- The "never sell, borrow against it" endgame disappears
- [[Tax-loss harvesting]] remains a deferral but never achieves permanent elimination via step-up
- Estate planning trusts ([[GRAT]]s, IDGTs) are pointless — they exist to transfer wealth to the next generation

The optimization shifts entirely to the investor's own lifetime. Key moves: capital gains harvesting in low-income years (ratchet up basis while alive), [[Charitable Remainder Trust]]s (donate appreciated assets, receive lifetime income stream, avoid capital gains, get a deduction), [[Donor Advised Fund]]s (bunch charitable deductions, distribute over time), and annuities (guaranteed lifetime income with no concern about zero terminal balance). Without a will, intestate succession routes assets through state law to distant relatives or ultimately the state — making a will directing assets to chosen beneficiaries (friends, institutions, causes) essential regardless of family structure.

### People without IRAs

Investors without pre-tax retirement accounts — common among the self-employed, those with international careers, finance professionals who preferred taxable accounts, or immigrants who entered the US workforce later — face a simpler but different landscape:

- No RMDs. No forced withdrawals, ever. Assets are sold on the investor's schedule.
- Capital gains rates (0/15/20%) instead of ordinary income rates (up to 37%) on investment gains.
- Step-up in basis at death cleans the entire unrealized gain for heirs.
- No 10-year drain rule for inherited taxable accounts.
- Tax-loss harvesting and [[Direct indexing]] work without cross-account wash sale complications.

Self-employment income (tutoring, consulting, freelancing) can still fund a [[SEP-IRA]] (up to 25% of net self-employment income) or [[Solo 401(k)]] (employee + employer contributions up to $69,000 in 2024), providing immediate deductions. A [[Roth IRA]] via backdoor conversion remains available regardless of income level.

### Withdrawal sequencing

For retirees with multiple account types, the order of withdrawals matters. The conventional wisdom:

1. Taxable accounts first — benefits from capital gains rates and preserves tax-advantaged space for continued growth
2. Tax-deferred (Traditional IRA/401(k)) second — ordinary income rates, but delays as long as possible
3. Roth last — tax-free growth for the longest period

The conventional wisdom is often wrong. Active management of the sequence — pulling from tax-deferred in low-income years, taxable in high-income years, Roth to avoid bracket thresholds or IRMAA cliffs — can outperform the simple ordering by managing the effective tax rate year by year. This is where modeling software and competent advisors add measurable value.

### Social Security optimization

Claiming [[Social Security]] at 62 (earliest), full retirement age (66-67), or 70 (maximum) changes the benefit by ~6-8% per year of delay. The 70-vs-62 gap is roughly 77% more monthly income. The breakeven age for delayed claiming is typically 80-82, making delay a bet on longevity.

Taxation of Social Security adds a layer. Up to 85% of benefits become taxable when combined income exceeds $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (married). "Combined income" includes half of Social Security plus all other income — meaning IRA withdrawals, Roth conversions, and capital gains all interact with Social Security taxation. The effective marginal rate in the phase-in range can exceed 40% as each additional dollar of income makes more Social Security taxable.

## The wealth management business model

Wealth enhancement strategies are the primary client acquisition tool for [[Registered Investment Advisors]] and wealth management firms. The typical pitch: "your tax savings will exceed our fee." The standard fee structure is 0.75-1.0% of assets under management (AUM), meaning a $1M portfolio costs $7,500-$10,000/year in perpetuity. The fee covers investment management, tax optimization, financial planning, and behavioral coaching.

The economics are straightforward: the firm makes more money when the portfolio grows and when the client adds assets. Tax-loss harvesting, Roth conversion modeling, and withdrawal sequencing are genuine value-adds — but they also serve as the hook that brings assets under management. The question for the prospective client is always whether the after-tax alpha exceeds the all-in fee, and whether the strategies require active management or could be implemented with periodic CPA advice and self-directed accounts.

Free dinner presentations are the classic prospecting tool for wealth management firms targeting retirees. The format — educational content followed by a soft-sell consultation offer — is designed to build trust before asking for the portfolio. The economics work because a single new $500K+ client relationship generates $3,750-5,000/year in recurring revenue, covering dozens of free dinners.

IRMAA is based on income from two years prior (2024 premiums use 2022 MAGI). A large Roth conversion or capital gains harvest in year N triggers surcharges in year N+2.
