#concept #commodities #critical-minerals

**Companionality** — the degree to which a metal's supply is structurally tethered to the production economics of a different host metal. 61% of all metals are primarily obtained as byproducts, making their supply unable to respond independently to their own price signals. Companionality is a core element of the [[China retaliatory toolkit]].

---

Most commodities follow a feedback loop: price rises, supply expands, price stabilizes. Companion metals break that loop. A companion metal rides along in another metal's ore — [[Gallium]] in bauxite (aluminum ore), [[Germanium]] in zinc concentrates, [[Cobalt]] in copper and nickel deposits, tellurium and selenium in copper anode slimes, rhenium in molybdenite (itself a byproduct of copper). The mine exists for the host metal. The companion has, in the words of the foundational academic paper, "no effect on the decision about the appropriate profit-maximizing level of production for the mining operation" (Nassar, Graedel & Harper, Yale, 2015). The companion metal is along for the ride.

The scale is staggering. Of 62 metals studied by Nassar et al., 38 — 61% — have more than half their production obtained as a companion. The World Economic Forum estimates that more than 60% of critical minerals whose supply gap is expected to widen are produced as byproducts. These are not obscure elements. [[Cobalt]] powers EV batteries (~99% is a byproduct of copper or nickel mining). [[Gallium]] enables [[5G]], radar, and LED lighting (~98% from aluminum processing). [[Germanium]] is essential for fiber optics and infrared military systems (~100% from zinc refining). [[Silver]] — 70-80% byproduct of copper, lead, zinc, and gold mining. Even helium, which runs MRI machines and cools quantum computers, is 100% recovered from [[LNG|natural gas]] processing. The metals that the energy transition and defense systems depend on are structurally captive to the economics of metals nobody thinks about.

This captivity creates three perverse dynamics. First, supply cannot respond to price — even a 10x spike in the companion metal may not justify expanding host metal production, because the companion revenue is trivial relative to the host. [[Gallium]] prices rose 150% after China's 2023 [[Export controls|export controls]]; global non-Chinese production remains ~15 tonnes/year against demand of ~700 tonnes. Second, supply can decline when the companion metal's price is rising — if the host metal price falls, mines close and companion supply drops regardless of companion demand. Third, supply can increase when the companion metal's price is falling — if host production expands for its own reasons, companion supply floods the market and crashes the price. The result, measured empirically by Renner & Wellmer (2016), is that byproduct metals carry roughly 50% higher annual price volatility than host metals over 50-year periods.

The investment implications compound when you map the host-companion chains. [[Cobalt]] is 55% from copper mines, 29% from nickel mines, and 14% from dedicated operations (2020 data). As [[Indonesia]]'s [[Nickel|nickel laterite]] production expands — driven by nickel economics and Chinese processing investment — cobalt supply from nickel could reach 41% by 2030 (Benchmark Minerals), shifting [[Cobalt]]'s supply dependency from [[DRC]] copper to Indonesian nickel. The supply chain risk doesn't disappear; it migrates. Rhenium is the extreme case: a byproduct of molybdenum, which is itself a byproduct of copper — a byproduct of a byproduct, with less than 12% of ore content actually recovered. Rhenium is irreplaceable in jet engine superalloys, and its entire global production is ~53 tonnes/year.

The [[China retaliatory toolkit]] exploits companionality with a specific playbook. Step one: dominate the host metal's processing chain (the world's largest aluminum producer and a major zinc refiner). Step two: mandate that refineries extract the companion metal — in the late 2000s, Beijing required aluminum smelters to install gallium extraction circuits, converting a globally wasted byproduct into a strategic asset at near-zero marginal cost. Step three: weaponize the resulting monopoly through [[Export controls|export controls]]. [[Gallium]] and [[Germanium]] licensing requirements began July 2023; a full US sales ban followed December 2024. The price impact: [[Germanium]] in March 2026 trades at $1,949/kg inside China versus $5,475/kg at US warehouses — a 181% premium. CSIS estimates a full gallium embargo could cause $8 billion in US GDP losses; 85% of US defense gallium supply chains include at least one Chinese supplier across 11,000+ individual DOD parts.

Diversification is structurally harder than for standalone commodities because you cannot build a gallium plant without building an alumina refinery. Every non-Chinese gallium project announced — Metlen in Greece (50 tonnes/year by 2028, EUR 295M), Alcoa/Sojitz in Australia (100 tonnes/year), ATALCO in the US ($150M Pentagon equity plus $300M private) — is an aluminum-first project with gallium extraction bolted on. Timelines run 3-5 years minimum. The same logic applies across all companion metals: diversifying germanium means building zinc refining capacity; diversifying cobalt means influencing copper and [[Nickel|nickel]] mining decisions in the [[DRC]] and [[Indonesia]].

There is one lever that bypasses the host metal bottleneck: recovering companion metals from existing waste streams. A 2024 study (Greffe et al., Environmental Science & Technology) compiled data from 3,422 deposits across 141 countries and found that gallium, germanium, and indium supply potential is "at least twice current production rates without host demand increase." Tellurium and selenium recovery rates from copper anode slimes are below 5% — two orders of magnitude below theoretical potential. A 2025 Science paper estimated that 90% recovery of existing byproducts from US metal mines could meet nearly all domestic demand for critical minerals. The constraint is not geological scarcity but capital investment in processing steps that host metal economics never justified. China's [[Export controls|export controls]] may be the forcing function that finally makes that investment rational — the same dynamic playing out in [[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction]], where rare earths trapped in fertilizer waste become worth extracting only after geopolitical pricing makes the economics work.

The pattern generalizes beyond metals. The [[Nitrogen Trap]] documents the same structural captivity for [[Elemental Sulfur]] (byproduct of sour gas and crude desulfurization — ~50% of global sulfur trade tied to conflict-exposed countries) and helium (byproduct of [[LNG|natural gas]]). Phosphogypsum rare earths are captive to [[Phosphoric Acid]] production. In each case, the supply of a critical input is hostage to the economics of a commodity the end user has no relationship with. The farmer who needs sulfur-based fertilizer cannot influence the desulfurization decisions of a Gulf oil refiner. The MRI manufacturer who needs helium cannot influence [[QatarEnergy]]'s [[LNG]] production schedule. The defense contractor who needs gallium cannot influence Chinese aluminum smelting policy. Companionality severs the link between demand and supply response — and that severed link is where strategic vulnerability lives.
