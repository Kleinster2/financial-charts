#concept #energy #oil #trade

**Global oil trade flows** — the physical movement of ~100M bbl/day of crude oil and refined products across maritime routes, pipelines, and chokepoints. Understanding who produces, who refines, who consumes, and how the barrels move between them is the foundation for pricing any energy disruption — from the [[Iran conflict economic disruption|2026 Hormuz closure]] to sanctions rerouting to refinery margin shifts. About 60% of globally traded oil moves by sea, making maritime chokepoints and tanker economics central to oil pricing.

---

## The barrel's journey: production → trade → refining → consumption

Global oil demand: ~104M bbl/day (2024). Of this, ~40-45M bbl/day crosses international borders as crude, plus another ~25M bbl/day as refined products. The rest is consumed domestically by producing nations.

## Maritime chokepoints

About 73M bbl/day of oil transits maritime chokepoints. A disruption at any one changes global pricing, routing, and freight costs.

| Chokepoint | Volume (M bbl/d) | Key flows | Bypass options | 2026 status |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| [[Strait of Malacca]] | ~24 | Middle East/Africa → East Asia | None viable at scale | Open |
| [[Strait of Hormuz]] | ~20 | Persian Gulf → Asia, Europe | East-West pipeline ([[Yanbu]], ~3M bpd loading); [[Fujairah]] pipeline (400k bpd, attacked) | Effectively closed since Feb 28 |
| Suez Canal + [[SUMED pipeline]] | ~5 + ~2.5 | Middle East → Europe; also reverse flow | SUMED handles VLCCs that can't transit canal; Cape of Good Hope rerouting adds 10-14 days | Open but Houthi disruption reduced traffic in 2024 |
| [[Bab el-Mandeb]] | ~4.2 | Red Sea → Gulf of Aden (Suez approach) | Cape of Good Hope | Houthi attacks 2024 diverted ~50% of traffic |
| [[Turkish Straits]] (Bosporus) | ~2.5-3.0 | Black Sea crude (Russia, Kazakhstan) → Mediterranean | BTC pipeline (Baku-Tbilisi-Ceyhan, 1M bpd); Burgas-Alexandroupolis (planned) | Open; congestion chronic |
| [[Danish Straits]] | ~3.2 | Russian Baltic exports → Atlantic/global | Druzhba pipeline (limited) | Open; key for Russian crude reaching world markets |
| [[Panama Canal]] | ~0.9 | US Gulf Coast ↔ Asia-Pacific | Cape Horn; cross-isthmus pipeline (limited) | Water level constraints recurring |
| [[Cape of Good Hope]] | Variable | Reroute for Suez/Hormuz disruptions | — | Surge route during 2024 Houthi + 2026 Hormuz crises |

## Refined product trade flows

Refined products (~25M bbl/day traded internationally) follow different routes than crude. Key products: gasoline, diesel/gasoil, jet fuel, naphtha, fuel oil, LPG.

### US — the world's refinery

US exported a record 6.6M bbl/day of petroleum products in 2024:
- Diesel/distillates: 1.3M bpd → Mexico (21%), Europe, Latin America
- Gasoline: 877k bpd → Mexico, Latin America
- Jet fuel: 209k bpd → Europe, Latin America
- US diesel exports to Europe more than doubled Jan 2025→Jan 2026 (167k → 396k bpd) as European refining capacity shrinks

### Key product flows

- Middle East crude → India/Singapore/S. Korea → refined products → Asia-Pacific consumers
- US Gulf Coast crude → US refineries → diesel/gasoline/jet fuel → Mexico, Latin America, Europe
- Russian crude → India/China refineries → products re-exported (sanctions arbitrage)
- Middle East crude → European refineries (shrinking) → European consumption
- West African crude → European/Indian refineries → products to regional markets

## Sanctions and rerouting (2022-present)

Sanctions have reshaped global crude flows more than any event since the 1973 embargo:

- [[Russia]]: EU embargo (Dec 2022 crude, Feb 2023 products) + G7 price cap ($60/bbl). Russia redirected ~3-4M bpd from Europe to China/India via [[shadow fleet]]. Europe replaced with US, Norwegian, Middle Eastern, West African crude
- [[Iran]]: US sanctions limit but don't eliminate exports (~1.5M bpd, mostly to China via ship-to-ship transfers). Hormuz closure in 2026 halts even these flows
- [[Venezuela]]: US licensed [[Chevron]] to resume limited exports (2022-23); post-Maduro capture (Jan 2026) opens path to rebuild. [[Gulf Coast refiners]] positioned for heavy sour
- Net effect: Asia (especially China/India) absorbs discounted sanctioned crude; Europe pays premiums for alternative supply; freight rates elevated as ton-miles increase from longer routes

## Tanker economics

| Class | Capacity (k bbl) | Typical route | Draft limit |
|-------|------------------|---------------|-------------|
| VLCC | ~2,000 | Persian Gulf → Asia; W. Africa → China | Cannot transit Suez loaded; uses SUMED or Cape |
| Suezmax | ~1,000 | W. Africa → Europe; Black Sea → Mediterranean | Max Suez draft |
| Aframax | ~750 | Short-haul; North Sea, Caribbean, Mediterranean | Flexible |
| Panamax | ~500 | Caribbean → US; transits Panama Canal | Panama lock size |
| MR/LR | ~300-600 | Clean products (gasoline, jet fuel, diesel) | Flexible |

85% of Middle East crude exports move on VLCCs. VLCC rates from Persian Gulf to Asia surged 139% YoY in November 2025 before the Hormuz closure.

The [[shadow fleet]] (~3,300 tankers, ~18% of world tonnage) operates outside Western insurance/classification, carrying Russian, Iranian, and Venezuelan crude at discounted rates but elevated environmental/safety risk.

## 2026 Hormuz disruption — flow impact

The Feb 28, 2026 closure of the [[Strait of Hormuz]] is the largest oil flow disruption since the 1990 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait:

- ~20M bbl/day of crude + ~4M bbl/day of refined products/LNG halted
- Bypass capacity: Saudi East-West pipeline to [[Yanbu]] (~3M bpd loading, ~1.9M actual); [[Fujairah]] pipeline (400k bpd, attacked and unusable)
- Net loss: ~16-17M bbl/day with no near-term alternative
- [[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]] max drawdown (4.4M bpd) covers <30% of lost Hormuz volume
- [[US Development Finance Corporation\|DFC]] deployed $20B maritime reinsurance facility to break insurance paralysis
- Rerouting: Saudi oil to [[Yanbu]] → Red Sea (but Bab el-Mandeb also under Houthi threat); Russia/US/Brazil/W. Africa/Norway crude gains share as non-Hormuz supply

See [[Iran conflict economic disruption]] for full crisis analysis.

## Investment implications

The trade flow map determines who wins and who loses from any disruption:

- Hormuz closure: [[Saudi Aramco]] pipeline-constrained; [[Reliance Industries|Indian refiners]] feedstock-starved; [[Marathon Petroleum|US Gulf Coast refiners]] gain as alternative suppliers; [[Cheniere|US LNG exporters]] benefit; non-Hormuz producers ([[Brazil]], [[Norway]], [[Nigeria]], US) gain market share
- Sanctions rerouting: [[India]]/[[China]] refiners arbitrage discounted crude; [[shadow fleet]] operators capture spread; European refiners lose feedstock optionality
- Refinery closures (Europe): US product exports gain structurally; Rotterdam's role shifts from refining to trading
- New capacity (Middle East, Asia): crude-exporting nations move up the value chain; legacy refiners face margin compression
