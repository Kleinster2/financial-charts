# Laffer curve

The **Laffer curve** is an economic theory positing that tax revenue is a non-linear function of tax rates — at 0% and 100% tax rates, revenue is zero, and somewhere between exists an optimal rate that maximizes revenue. Named after economist Arthur Laffer, who reportedly sketched it on a napkin for [[Dick Cheney]] and Donald Rumsfeld in 1974 (though the concept dates to Ibn Khaldun in the 14th century and was formalized by French economist Jules Dupuit in 1844). The curve became the intellectual foundation for supply-side economics and the Reagan tax cuts of 1981.

## Synopsis

The practical debate is always about where you sit on the curve. Proponents argue most developed economies are on the right side (above the revenue-maximizing rate), so cutting rates increases revenue through increased economic activity and reduced avoidance. Critics note that empirical evidence rarely supports this at the aggregate level — the Reagan and Bush tax cuts both increased deficits rather than paying for themselves, and the 2017 [[TCJA]] similarly produced revenue shortfalls relative to CBO baseline. The curve is theoretically unassailable (the endpoints are mathematically true) but practically unfalsifiable at the macro level, because isolating the tax-rate effect from all other economic variables is impossible.

The strongest real-world evidence for Laffer effects comes from [[Capital flight]] scenarios, where mobile capital (wealthy individuals, corporations) relocates to lower-tax jurisdictions. This is the Laffer curve operating not through aggregate supply-side growth but through competition between tax jurisdictions. [[Norway]]'s 2022 [[Wealth tax]] increase triggered the departure of 50+ wealthy residents including [[Kjell Inge Røkke]], with estimated net revenue loss. [[Sweden]] repealed its wealth tax in 2007 after similar capital flight patterns. The insight is that the Laffer curve works most convincingly for the most mobile tax bases (wealth, capital gains, corporate headquarters) and least convincingly for the most immobile ones (labor income, consumption). This distinction matters for policy: wealth taxes and corporate taxes face real Laffer constraints in a world of open capital accounts; income taxes and VAT generally do not.
