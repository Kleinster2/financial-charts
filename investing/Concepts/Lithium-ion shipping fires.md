# Lithium-ion shipping fires

Lithium-ion battery fires on container ships and car carriers are a growing supply chain risk — water only cools the flames instead of extinguishing them, and no chemical, foam, or water solution can fully put out a large-format battery fire. Demand for lithium-ion batteries is projected to more than double by 2030, scaling the risk alongside it.

---

## Incident data

| Year | Container ship fires | Source |
|------|---------------------|--------|
| 2024 | 250 (decade high) | [[Allianz]] |

Many fires result from misreported cargo — hazardous goods not handled correctly because shippers misdeclare contents.

## Why the fires are inextinguishable

Gene Seroka, CEO of [[Port of Los Angeles]] (FT, Mar 15, 2026): *"We don't have a solution [for extinguishing] lithium-ion batteries. We've been shipping batteries that we would put in radios or in household appliances for decades... and we have ways of putting those fires out."*

Seroka: *"Lithium-ion batteries are huge, they're dense. There is no solution, chemical, foam or water that can extinguish a battery fire today of that size."*

Water cools but does not extinguish. The chemistry sustains its own combustion (thermal runaway) regardless of external oxygen supply.

---

## Compounding factors

Geopolitical congestion: The Middle East conflict is forcing more containers to be discharged at smaller ports far from original destinations. Seroka warned that congestion building in these ports "makes firefighting more complex" — denser stacking, less space for emergency response.

Cargo misdeclaration: [[Allianz]] identified misreported cargo as a primary driver of the 250 fires in 2024. Hazardous goods not flagged → not handled with fire precautions.

Demand trajectory: Lithium-ion battery shipments have "vastly increased" with EV sales growth and grid storage demand. Projected to more than double by 2030.

---

## Industry response

[[World Shipping Council]] (Sep 2025): Launched an AI tool to scan bookings in real time and flag risky containers. 75%+ of the industry by tonnage has signed up.

[[World Shipping Council]] CEO Joe Kramek: *"We have seen too many tragic incidents where misdeclared cargo has led to catastrophic fires, including the loss of life."*

[[Wallenius Wilhelmsen]] (world's largest car carrier fleet): CEO Lasse Kristoffersen noted the fire risk is associated with used vehicles, not necessarily EVs. All cars — combustion and electric — have small lithium-ion batteries for radios. Since Wallenius Wilhelmsen began disconnecting the radio battery, "we have had no fire incidents on our vessels."

This is a narrower fix that works for car carriers but not for container ships carrying bulk battery cargo.

## Investment read-through

| Dimension | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|
| Port operators | Shutdown risk — 70% of LA port closed for 2 days in one incident. [[SIPG]], [[DP World]], [[PSA International]] all exposed |
| Marine insurance | [[Allianz]] data shows decade-high fires. P&I clubs likely repricing cargo risk for battery shipments |
| Shipping lines | [[Maersk]], [[MSC]], [[CMA CGM]], [[COSCO]] face vessel damage, route disruption, insurance cost increases |
| Car carriers | [[Wallenius Wilhelmsen]] found a practical fix (battery disconnect); used vehicles are the specific risk vector |
| Battery demand | Not a demand headwind — no one is shipping fewer batteries. But logistics cost/complexity increases as screening tightens |
| Screening tech | [[World Shipping Council]] AI booking scanner — early-stage solution. Opportunity for cargo verification/inspection tech |
