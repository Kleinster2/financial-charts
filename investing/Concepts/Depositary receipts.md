# Depositary receipts

A depositary receipt (DR) is a negotiable certificate issued by a local depositary bank against shares of a foreign company held in custody abroad, letting that company's equity trade on a domestic exchange in local currency without a direct cross-listing. It is the general instrument class behind several national variants — the [[American Depositary Receipts|ADR]] in the US, the GDR across global markets, and the [[Brazilian Depositary Receipts|BDR]] on [[B3]] — all built on one template: a depositary holds the underlying shares, issues receipts against them at a set ratio, and the receipt tracks the underlying price adjusted for FX. The holder gets economic exposure; voting and other rights pass through, or not, depending on the program. The two axes that organize every DR market are the same — whether the issuer sponsors the program, and what listing/disclosure level it accepts — which is why the BDR's níveis and the ADR's levels are the same idea in two jurisdictions.

## What a depositary receipt is

A DR is a receipt, not the share itself. A depositary bank holds the foreign shares with a custodian and issues certificates against them that trade and settle locally. The economics track the underlying one-for-one, adjusted by the paridade/ratio (how many receipts equal one share) and the exchange rate. The receipt is an access wrapper: it lets a domestic investor hold a foreign equity inside their own market, in their own currency, without an offshore account — at the cost of a depositary layer, usually thinner liquidity than the home line, and frequently no vote.

## Sponsored vs unsponsored

The universal first split. A sponsored DR is created with the foreign company's involvement: it appoints a single depositary, signs a deposit agreement, and accepts disclosure obligations. An unsponsored DR is created by a depositary bank on its own initiative, off the company's existing foreign listing, without the company's participation — and multiple depositaries can issue competing unsponsored receipts on the same stock. Unsponsored programs sit at the lowest disclosure tier in every market (ADR Level I / OTC; BDR não patrocinado Nível I). The bulk of the megacap US-stock BDRs and the OTC foreign-stock ADRs are unsponsored.

## The national variants

| Receipt | Market | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| [[American Depositary Receipts]] | US — NYSE / Nasdaq / OTC | Levels I–III + Rule 144A; the largest DR market |
| GDR (Global Depositary Receipt) | Multi-market — London, Luxembourg, often via 144A / Reg S | for issuers tapping several markets at once |
| [[Brazilian Depositary Receipts]] | B3 (Brazil) | Níveis I–III; the 31–35 ticker-suffix convention |
| Others | HDR (Hong Kong), IDR (India), JDR (Japan), EDR | the same template under other regulators |

## Why companies cross-list via DRs

- Reach foreign investors and capital without a full direct listing in that market.
- Add liquidity and a local-currency trading line.
- Qualify for local index inclusion and the passive flows that follow.
- Raise capital — but only the top sponsored levels (ADR Level III, BDR Nível III) permit a primary offering.

## The foreign-private-issuer overlap

On US markets the DR wrapper and [[Foreign private issuer]] status travel together: a non-US company listing via a sponsored ADR is almost always an FPI, filing 20-F/6-K under IFRS rather than 10-K/10-Q. So the data-and-accounting traits documented under FPI — empty vendor statements, IFRS artifacts, no mandated quarterly — apply to most listed ADRs. The DR is the instrument; FPI is the reporting regime that comes with it.

## Synthesis

Depositary receipts are one instrument in national clothes. The mechanism (depositary holds shares, issues receipts at a ratio, receipt tracks underlying × FX) and the two organizing axes (sponsored vs unsponsored, listing level) are identical across markets; what differs is the regulator, the disclosure tiers, and the local index and tax treatment. That is why the vault models them as a hub with market-specific children: [[American Depositary Receipts]] carries the US level structure and the China-ADR delisting cluster; [[Brazilian Depositary Receipts]] carries the níveis, the 34-suffix retail BDRs, and the Brazilian tax treatment; GDR and the other national variants are the same template elsewhere. For a cross-border investor the practical question — "can I hold this foreign stock locally?" — almost always resolves to "is there a DR program, sponsored or not, at what level?", the same question in every market.
