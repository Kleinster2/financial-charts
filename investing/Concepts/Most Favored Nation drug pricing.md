# Most Favored Nation drug pricing

**Most Favored Nation (MFN) drug pricing** — the [[Trump II]] policy of tying US prices for certain patented drugs to the lower prices paid by a basket of foreign reference countries, the domestic-side counterpart to the trade-law route for forcing [[Pharma R&D burden-sharing|global R&D burden-sharing]]. The mechanism imports foreign reference pricing into the US market so that American patients pay no more than the lowest price among the reference basket — the inversion of the historic pattern in which the US was the high-price market that cross-subsidized global R&D.


Chart data not applicable; MFN drug pricing is a policy mechanism, with market reaction captured in [[US Section 301 investigation of Germany drug pricing (2026)]] and company-level pharma notes.

## Synopsis

MFN originated as a Trump 1.0 executive action (Sep 2020) tying Medicare Part B drug prices to an international price index; it was blocked by the courts before taking effect. The [[Trump II]] revival broadened the mechanism and embedded it in a larger stack: voluntary MFN price cuts by the 17 largest pharma companies in exchange for tariff exemptions, paired with the [[Section 301]] / [[Section 232 semiconductor tariff|Section 232]] pharma tariffs and bilateral deals (the [[US-UK pharma pricing deal (2026)|US-UK arrangement]]).

The doctrine combines a domestic lever (MFN reference pricing compressing US list prices) with a trade lever (tariffs + bilateral deals raising foreign prices), so the US-foreign gap closes from both sides. Critics — including several pharma CEOs ([[Pascal Soriot|Soriot]] et al.) — argue MFN stifles innovation by importation of foreign price controls and risks launch delays in reference countries.
