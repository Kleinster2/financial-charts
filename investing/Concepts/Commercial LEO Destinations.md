**Commercial LEO Destinations (CLD)** — [[NASA]] program structuring the post-ISS commercial space-station market. Conceived under the 2018 Trump-era ISS commercialization framework: NASA shifts from owning and operating the [[ISS]] to being a *customer* of one or more commercial providers when ISS retires (~end of 2030). ~$4B in private capital has been deployed across candidates ([[Vast Space]], [[Voyager]] [[Star Lab]], [[Axiom Space]], [[Orbital Reef]]). Phase 2 selection is targeted mid-2026 — and the Phase 2 acquisition strategy has been re-litigated under [[Jared Isaacman]]'s administration with a Q1 2026 alternative proposal that would partially recentralize station design under NASA.

---

## Why CLD matters

The CLD program is the institutional mechanism by which Low Earth Orbit transitions from a government-owned and operated platform (the ISS) to a commercial market with NASA as one of multiple customers. The program is the LEO analog of NASA's Commercial Resupply Services (CRS, 2008) and Commercial Crew (2014) decisions — both of which seeded [[SpaceX]] and produced a long arc of commercial dominance in their respective tiers.

For the space-economy investment thesis, the CLD framework is the structural premise underwriting ~$4B in private capital. If NASA reverses course on commercialization, the deployed capital is at risk and NASA's standing as a reliable anchor customer for future commercial programs (lunar base, Mars infrastructure, in-space manufacturing) is damaged.

## Program structure

| Phase | Period | Mechanism | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | 2021- | Funded Space Act Agreements (SAAs) for design/demonstration | Awarded Dec 2021 |
| Phase 2 (originally planned) | 2026-2030 | Firm fixed-price contracts for certification + services | Deferred Q1 2026 |
| Phase 2 (revised) | 2026-2030 | Multiple funded SAAs (3-year base + optional 5-year milestones) | Procurement strategy under review Q1 2026 |
| Operational | Post-ISS retirement (~2030) | NASA as customer of selected commercial stations | TBD |

Phase 1 awards (Dec 2021):

| Awardee | Phase 1 award | Station |
|---|---|---|
| [[Axiom Space]] | $140M (separate, ISS-attached path) | Initially ISS modules, then free-flying |
| [[Blue Origin]] (Orbital Reef partner) | ~$130M | [[Orbital Reef]] |
| [[Voyager]] (Starlab consortium) | ~$160M | [[Star Lab]] |
| [[Northrop Grumman]] | (Phase 1 award; later withdrew) | — |

[[Vast Space]] received a NASA contract in 2024 for crewed Haven-1 access, joining the candidate field outside the original Phase 1 awards.

## Q1 2026 — Isaacman alternative framework

[[Jared Isaacman]] proposed an alternative CLD path during his early months as NASA administrator. Key elements (per [[Max Haot]] interview, Apr 2026 [[Space Capital]]):

| Original 2018 framework | Isaacman alternative proposal (Q1 2026) |
|---|---|
| NASA as customer of commercial stations | NASA designs and *owns* a "core module" |
| Industry builds + operates stations | Industry builds the core module under contract (similar to SLS) |
| Multiple commercial winners | NASA-owned core module (transition vehicle) |
| Free-flying stations from inception | Core module attaches temporarily to ISS, then detaches |

Isaacman publicly framed this as an idea, not a decision — soliciting industry feedback via RFI process. RFIs were due April 2026; Vast (Haot), Voyager, Axiom, and other candidates filed responses. Industry-side argument (per Haot): the original commercialization framework is what unlocked ~$4B in private capital, and reversing course risks both the deployed capital and NASA's standing as an anchor customer for future commercial space programs. Vast's specific position: the existing CLD budget can support two commercial winners under the original framework, with crew utilization shared across both stations.

### Status as of Apr 2026

RFI responses submitted; NASA decision pending; original Phase 2 SAAs targeted for early 2026 awards. The Phase 2 procurement strategy is genuinely fluid.

The differentiation matters for the Phase 2 selection question. Star Lab's single-Starship-launch deployment is enabled by the $90M / $900/kg pricing disclosed in [[Voyager]]'s 10K (the cleanest publicly available primary source on Starship economics for a real customer contract). Vast's Haven-1 → Haven-2 path takes the demonstrate-then-scale approach. Axiom's ISS-attached precursor strategy reduces technical risk but creates schedule dependency on ISS lifetime. Orbital Reef has the integrated rocket + station + customer cloud play within Blue/Bezos.

## Strategic implications for NASA

### Why the commercialization framework matters

[[Max Haot]] (Apr 2026): Once NASA commits as a customer, "Japan, UAE as well as Saudi Arabia" follow as additional sovereign-agency customers, plus "many additional European countries that were not part of ESA doing it." The growing market of sovereign-agency demand is what makes the commercial business model viable on existing customer revenue alone — without requiring speculative new markets (in-space pharma manufacturing, media, private missions) to clear.

If NASA reverses to a NASA-owned core module model, the supplier ecosystem narrows back to the cost-plus contractor cohort that built ISS, and the ~$4B private capital + foreign-agency follow-through dynamic doesn't materialize.

### Why Isaacman might propose recentralization anyway

The proposal isn't pure conservatism. Possible motivations:
1. ISS deorbit timeline pressure — [[ISS]] leak issues + [[Russia]] partnership constraints suggest the platform may not last to 2030 cleanly. A NASA-owned bridging module preserves continuity if commercial providers slip schedule.
2. Budget friction — Phase 2 SAA funding for multiple winners may exceed political tolerance under the broader space-budget restructuring.
3. Architecture coherence — A NASA-designed core module integrates with [[Artemis]]/lunar architecture more directly than commercial free-flying stations.

These motivations are speculative. The publicly stated framing is that Isaacman wants industry input via the RFI process before deciding.

## Investment lens

The Phase 2 selection (mid-2026) is the single biggest equity catalyst for [[Voyager]] (NYSE: VOYG, where Star Lab is the post-IPO thesis), an IPO catalyst for [[Vast Space]] (private), and a balance-sheet event for [[Blue Origin]] (Orbital Reef cancellation would consolidate Blue around [[Project Sunrise]] and lunar). [[Axiom Space]] is private but has filed S-1 paperwork; CLD selection affects its public-market path.

For [[SpaceX]], CLD outcomes affect Starship launch cadence (Star Lab is a 2029 contracted launch; Haven-2 likely needs Starship at scale; Orbital Reef is Vulcan-based). Starship's commercial-flight inventory becomes denser if CLD goes commercial.
