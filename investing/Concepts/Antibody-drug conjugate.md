#concept #oncology #biopharma

**Antibody-drug conjugate** (ADC) — a targeted-oncology modality that fuses a tumor-homing monoclonal antibody to a potent payload via a chemical linker, delivering the payload selectively to cancer cells while sparing healthy tissue.

---

## Structure

An ADC has three parts: the antibody (tumor-selective targeting), the linker (holds the payload in circulation, releases it inside the target cell), and the payload (the active drug). Linker stability is the central engineering problem — premature release in the bloodstream causes off-target toxicity, the recurring "Achilles heel" of the class.

---

## Variants

- Classic ADCs carry a cytotoxic chemotherapy payload delivered selectively to tumor cells.
- [[Degrader-antibody conjugate|Degrader-antibody conjugates]] (DACs) swap the cytotoxin for a targeted [[Protein degrader|protein degrader]] payload, which catalytically destroys a disease-driving intracellular protein rather than poisoning the cell directly. [[Firefly Bio]] (acquired by [[Johnson & Johnson]], June 2026) is built on this DAC approach with a proprietary stability linker.

---

## Why it matters

ADCs are one of the hottest targeted-oncology modalities, drawing heavy pharma M&A and licensing. Big pharma is paying up for next-generation conjugate platforms (DACs, novel linkers, new payload classes) to rebuild oncology pipelines.
