# Iran conflict airline disruption

Airline and aviation impact of [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]] — airspace closures, flight cancellations, fuel costs, and structural reshuffling of global long-haul routing. Part of [[2026 Iran conflict market impact]].

---

## Flight cancellations

More than 46,000 scheduled flights to and from the Middle East were canceled between February 28 and March 11, according to [[Cirium]] ([[Bloomberg]], Mar 12).

| Carrier group | Status (as of ~Mar 11) |
|--------------|------------------------|
| [[Emirates]] | Reduced schedule, significantly fewer destinations than normal |
| [[Qatar Airways]] | Even smaller number of flights than Emirates |
| [[Etihad Airways]] | Even smaller number of flights than Emirates |
| [[Kuwait]] airspace | Closed |
| [[Bahrain]] airspace | Closed |

## Airspace squeeze

The conflict has funneled global long-haul traffic through an increasingly narrow corridor. Airlines were already avoiding [[Russia]]n airspace since the 2022 Ukraine invasion. The Iran war closed off Iran, Iraq, and large areas of the Gulf — leaving flights between Europe and Asia squeezed through a strip over Georgia and Azerbaijan.

[[Azerbaijan]] briefly shut airspace over parts of the country, leaving only a ~50-mile path for aircraft to pass through. [[Flightradar24]] data confirmed the bottleneck.

### Dubai airport disruption

On at least four occasions since [[Dubai]] resumed air traffic, inbound aircraft were placed in holding patterns as drones hit the airport or missiles were being intercepted overhead (Flightradar24 data). This is not normal operational disruption — it's active combat interfering with civilian aviation.

[[Emirates]] has to add more than one hour on most routes as it avoids the Gulf, Iranian, and Iraqi airspace. Additional flight time means more fuel carried — an expensive burden at current jet fuel prices.

The [[UAE]] and [[Qatar]] said they were able to establish safe air corridors amid ongoing attacks.

## Fuel cost shock

The [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure drove jet fuel prices parabolic. [[Jeff Currie]] ([[Carlyle]]) noted Singapore jet fuel hit $230/bbl by March 11 — see [[Iran conflict energy disruption]] for the full energy picture.

Airlines responded by:
- Hiking fares with fuel surcharges to cover ballooning costs
- Panic-buying oil derivatives to hedge against further price swings

### Updated cost data (Mar 10-13)

Jet fuel prices surged to $150-200/bbl (from $85-90 pre-conflict). US Gulf Coast jet fuel hit $4.12/gal — a four-year high.

- Rerouting cost per flight: up to $60,000 additional on a single long-haul sector
- Flight time added: 90-120 minutes per rerouted long-haul flight, 300-800 nautical miles added
- [[Cathay Pacific]] announced doubling of fuel surcharges on tickets starting Mar 18
- Industry-wide cost estimate: could exceed $1B if conflict extends
- 1M+ passengers stranded; [[Qatar Airways]] used [[Oman|Muscat]] as relief hub, scheduling repatriation flights to LHR, CDG, MAD, FCO, FRA (Mar 7)

*Source: TIME, CNBC, Al Jazeera, The National (Mar 10-13, 2026)*

### Hedging exposure

Hedging practices vary widely across the global airline sector and are limited in Asia ([[Bloomberg Intelligence]]):

| Carrier | Hedging status |
|---------|---------------|
| [[Cathay Pacific]] | ~30% of near-term fuel consumption hedged |
| China's Big Three (Air China, China Eastern, China Southern) | Most exposed to fuel-price swings |
| [[IndiGo]] | Most exposed to fuel-price swings |

China's Big Three and IndiGo are among the carriers with the greatest vulnerability to sustained high fuel prices.

## Winners — route reshuffling

The disruption is giving European and Asian airlines a rare chance to claw back market share from Gulf carriers that have long siphoned off passengers via Dubai and Doha transfer hubs.

| Carrier | Action |
|---------|--------|
| [[British Airways]] | Adding flights to Asia and Africa |
| [[Deutsche Lufthansa]] | Adding flights to Asia and Africa |
| [[Air France-KLM]] | Deployed bigger jets on Tokyo, Shanghai, Mumbai routes |
| [[Air India]] | Mounting extra services (existing ones selling out) |
| [[Cathay Pacific]] | Mounting extra services (existing ones selling out) |
| [[Oman Air]] | Increased European frequencies, renting additional jets |

[[Oman Air]]'s Muscat International Airport — normally a backwater tourism facility — surged with demand from travelers fleeing through Muscat.

The dynamic reveals the structural fragility of the Gulf hub model: decades of positioning Dubai, Doha, and Abu Dhabi as global transfer points created a single point of failure. When the hub is disrupted, the entire web of connecting flights collapses, stranding passengers and planes out of position.

## Demand destruction risk

Beyond immediate operational disruption, sustained high oil prices threaten longer-term demand:
- Higher jet fuel → higher fares → passengers rethink long-haul trips
- Travelers likely to favor cheaper holidays closer to home
- Middle East transit routes — even if restored — carry lasting safety perception damage
- Global inflation from oil shock reduces discretionary travel spending

---

## Stranded travelers

Thousands of passengers were stranded in the early days. Social media filled with accounts of elaborate, costly journeys to reach airports in [[Saudi Arabia]] and [[Oman]] to escape the conflict zone. The Gulf carriers' hub-and-spoke model amplified the stranding effect — when flights canceled, passengers, crews, and aircraft were all out of position with no easy rerouting.

[[Qatar Airways]] and [[Etihad Airways]] aircraft were spotted parked for several days at Barcelona airport, unable to fly back due to the war (photographed Mar 9).
