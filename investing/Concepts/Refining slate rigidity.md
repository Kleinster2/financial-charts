# Refining slate rigidity

The fixed-yield problem in [[Oil|crude]] refining: a refinery cannot meaningfully prioritise one product over another, so demand destruction in any single refined product (jet fuel, diesel, gasoline) does not by itself reduce crude purchases. For [[Oil|crude prices]] to fall, demand must be destroyed across the whole product slate — and the products with the least price-elastic users set the floor.

## Synopsis

When a refiner cracks a barrel of [[Oil|crude]], the output is a barrel's worth of products in fairly fixed proportions: [[LPG|butane/LPG]], [[Gasoline|gasoline]], [[Naphtha|naphtha]], [[Jet fuel|jet/kerosene]], [[Diesel fuel|diesel/gasoil]], [[Fuel oil|fuel oil]], and [[Bitumen|bitumen]]. By choosing crude grade, refinery configuration (cracking, hydrocracking, coking), and cut points, refiners can shift the slate by a smattering of percentage points — typically a 5-10% swing in any single yield. Once the plants are built, the system as a whole is structurally rigid.

The implication is the central counter-intuitive feature of oil supply shocks. If the [[Strait of Hormuz|Strait]] removes 15% of global crude and product supply, demand has to fall about 15% in *each* product for the market to balance. Airlines feel the pain first because [[Jet fuel]] rose fastest and fuel is a large share of their cost, but airlines reducing jet demand only lowers refiner revenue from one cut — the refiner can still profitably bid for $100 crude as long as the *aggregate* slate clears at sufficient prices. Demand destruction in jet rebalances the *jet* market; it does not rebalance crude.

Crude rebalances only when prices for the less price-elastic products — diesel, gasoline, and ultimately petrochemical feedstocks and heating fuels — also reach their own demand-destruction thresholds. Because those users are progressively less able to substitute or postpone consumption (a trucker can't park the truck the way a tourist can postpone a flight), the marginal crude clearing price is set by the slowest-flexing slice of the slate, not the fastest-flexing one.

## The mechanism

| Step | What happens |
|------|--------------|
| Supply shock | A 15% global crude/product loss removes barrels across all yields proportionally |
| Refiner response | Cannot re-engineer plants to shift slate beyond ~5-10%; can choose feedstock at the margin, but aggregate yield is roughly fixed |
| Single-product demand fall | Reduces the price of *that* product but leaves the rest of the slate at the new shock-elevated level |
| Refiner economics | Refiner profitably bids for crude as long as the weighted product slate covers crude + processing cost, even with one weak cut |
| Rebalancing condition | Demand in *every* product falls by ~15% (or near-equivalent across the slate) before crude purchases drop |
| Burden distribution | Falls on the users with the most inelastic demand — those who cannot substitute, postpone, or pass through cost |

## The April 2026 case

Per [[Financial Times|FT]] (Apr 27), [[Deutsche Lufthansa|Lufthansa]], [[Delta Air Lines|Delta]], [[Cathay Pacific]], and [[Qantas]] reacted to the doubling of [[Jet fuel|jet fuel]] prices by cutting flights. Per [[Bernstein]], fuel and related expenses amounted to about 35% of [[Ryanair]]'s revenue in 2025, which is why airlines felt the burn first.

### Why airlines felt it first, not why crude reset

The structural answer is two-step:

1. Jet fuel rose faster than other refined products because Middle Eastern exports were particularly focused on naphtha and jet/kerosene — the loss was concentrated in the jet/distillate cut. See [[Jet fuel#Iran conflict impact (March 2026)]] and [[Iran conflict airline disruption]].
2. Airlines have one of the highest fuel-cost shares in any industry (jet ~25-35% of total operating cost), so a doubling shows up immediately on margin and triggers an early operational response (capacity cuts, surcharges).

Imagine that jet at ~$200/bbl (twice unrefined crude) is enough to lop 15% off global airline jet consumption. That demand cut does nothing to balance crude. Refiners — who can still earn money on $100 crude given the *whole* product slate — keep competing to buy it. The refiner doesn't care which cut clears; only the weighted average.

### The next price-rationing layers

For crude to actually fall, demand must keep destroying through the slate in approximate price-elasticity order:

| Layer | Product | User | Why later than airlines |
|-------|---------|------|--------------------------|
| 1 | [[Jet fuel]] | Airlines | High cost share, postponable demand (tourism, business travel) — feels it first |
| 2 | [[Diesel fuel]] / [[Gasoline]] | Trucking, freight, drivers | Hard to substitute or postpone short-term; pass-through to consumer prices is the lever |
| 3 | [[Naphtha]] / [[LPG]] | Petrochemicals, plastics | Substitution to ethane (where infrastructure exists, e.g. US ethane crackers) is slow capex; otherwise sticky |
| 4 | [[Fuel oil]] | Marine bunker, heating, power generation | Often regulated or contracted; longest substitution timeline |

The [[BASF]] 30% Ludwigshafen price hike (Mar 19) is the layer-3 transmission already documented — naphtha "moonshoting" and chemical plants slowing or closing. See [[Iran conflict European impact]].

This sequencing also explains the [[Crack spread|crack spread]] blow-out: as different products burn through demand at different speeds, their prices fan out from crude. The 3-2-1 crack moved from ~23% premium to ~56% premium during the shock, a textbook joint-product blow-out, not a refiner pricing-power story.

## Why refiners' "flexibility" is overstated in the public discussion

Refinery yield can be tweaked, not redesigned. Three real-world levers:

1. Crude grade — choosing lighter or heavier feedstock biases the slate toward different cuts. But crude grade availability is itself supply-constrained, and complex refineries are configured for specific gravity/sulfur ranges.
2. Cut points — the temperature thresholds at which the distillation tower separates products. Shifting cut points moves a few percent between adjacent products (e.g. a heavy naphtha-jet boundary).
3. Downstream conversion — [[Crack spread|catalytic cracking]], hydrocracking, and coking can convert heavier cuts into lighter products. But conversion units have their own capacity constraints and require capex on multi-year horizons.

In aggregate, the global refining system can shift its slate by a smattering of percentage points — not the 15% required to absorb a Hormuz-scale shock by re-routing yield. This is the structural reason oil shocks transmit through *price* rather than through *re-yielding*.

## Why this matters for the [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]]

The slate-rigidity argument tightens the case for a permanent risk premium. The conventional rebalancing story ("high prices destroy demand, market clears") understates how much demand has to be destroyed and where the burden falls. Cross-product rationing means:

- Headline crude reset (the "best cure for high prices is high prices" reflex) requires demand destruction in multiple products at once, including products whose users cannot easily pull back.
- The first-mover demand response — airline capacity cuts — registers in equity prices and travel data but does not fix the upstream problem. The market can read airline cuts as the *start* of rebalancing when they are actually the *symptom* the system can absorb without rebalancing.
- The structural premium therefore needs to be priced not just on the supply side (chokepoint risk demonstrated) but on the demand side (the rebalancing path is harder than headline economics suggest).

This is the demand-side counterpart to [[Shale supply discipline]] on the supply side: both make the price ceiling harder to reach.

## Historical and adjacent cases

| Period | Slate-rigidity expression | Source |
|--------|---------------------------|--------|
| 1973 | Gasoline rationing in the US even as fuel oil and jet were less constrained | [[OPEC]] embargo episode |
| 2022 [[Russia]]-[[Ukraine]] | Diesel and middle-distillate squeeze, gasoline more contained | [[Crack spread]] note |
| 2026 [[Iran]]/Hormuz | Jet/naphtha exports concentrated in Gulf; jet rose furthest, fastest | [[Jet fuel]] § Iran conflict impact |

The 2026 case is unusual because the loss was concentrated in the lightest end of the slate (Gulf product exports were heavily naphtha and jet). That's why airlines and petrochemicals — both naphtha/jet-exposed — felt the burn earliest, while gasoline stayed comparatively mild.

## Investment implications

| Implication | Read |
|-------------|------|
| Refiner equities | Margin-blowout beneficiaries while the slate rebalances; see [[Valero]], [[Marathon Petroleum]], [[Phillips 66]], [[Gulf Coast refiners]] |
| Airlines | First-mover loss but also first-mover relief; high-hedge carriers ([[Ryanair]], [[EasyJet]] at 70-80%) buffer the spike |
| Petrochemicals | Naphtha-exposed names ([[BASF]] confirmed Mar 19) carry the layer-3 burden; ethane-advantaged US crackers ([[LyondellBasell]], [[Dow]]) gain feedstock advantage but lose the war-premium tailwind on reopening |
| Crude beta | Headline crude moves understate physical product tightness; paper-physical disconnect is structural, not transient |
| Demand-destruction signals | Watch the *slowest-flexing* product (fuel oil, petrochemical naphtha, residential heating) for genuine rebalancing — not the first-mover (jet) |
