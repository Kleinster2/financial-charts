**Physical oil trading** is the market where actual barrels of crude oil change hands — loaded onto tankers, pumped through pipelines, delivered to refineries. It sits beneath the financial futures market that dominates headlines but above the final delivered price that refiners actually pay. The pricing chain cascades from exchange-traded futures ([[ICE]] Brent, [[NYMEX]] WTI) through forward physical markets (Cash BFOE, Dated Brent) to cargo-level differentials to the final CIF/DES delivered price. Each layer adds information about real supply and demand that futures alone cannot capture. In normal markets, these layers track each other closely. In dislocations, they diverge violently: during the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|March 2026 Strait of Hormuz closure]], physical Dubai/Oman crude traded at $150-170/bbl while Brent futures sat near $100, a $50-70 gap that exposed the fundamental difference between paper promises and wet barrels.

## The story

The price of a barrel of oil is not one number. It is a stack of prices, each layer reflecting a different market with different participants, different time horizons, and different information. Understanding how these layers connect — and when they disconnect — is essential to reading oil markets.

At the top of the stack sit the exchange-traded futures: [[ICE]] Brent on the Intercontinental Exchange in London and WTI on [[NYMEX]]/[[CME Group|CME]] in New York. These are standardized contracts — 1,000 barrels each, cash-settled (Brent) or physically deliverable (WTI at Cushing, Oklahoma). They trade electronically, 23 hours a day, accessible to anyone with a brokerage account. Daily trading volume in petroleum futures, options, and OTC derivatives exceeds 5 billion barrels — roughly 50 times global physical production of ~100 million bbl/day. ICE Brent alone averaged 1.5 million contracts/day in 2025, hitting record open interest of 3.2 million contracts (3.2 billion barrels) on December 22, 2025. This is the world of "paper barrels" — financial claims on oil that overwhelmingly never result in physical delivery. Banks, hedge funds, CTAs, pension funds, and algorithmic traders dominate. The futures price is the number on CNBC. It reacts to headlines in milliseconds.

One layer down is the forward physical market. For Brent, this is the "Cash BFOE" market — forward contracts for physical cargoes of crude from the BFOET basket (Brent Ninian Blend, Forties, Oseberg, Ekofisk, Troll, and since June 2023, WTI Midland delivered CIF Rotterdam). These trade as "25-day forward" contracts: a buyer commits to take a cargo loading at least 25 days out, but without a specific loading date yet assigned. When the seller nominates a loading window (a 3-day laycan), the forward contract becomes a "Dated" cargo — it now has a specific date range. This conversion from forward to dated is where the Brent complex bridges paper and physical. The forward physical market is smaller than futures but carries more information about real supply-demand balances because every participant either has barrels to sell or needs barrels to process. Commodity traders ([[Vitol]], [[Trafigura]], [[Gunvor]], [[TotalEnergies]]) and major oil companies ([[Shell]], [[BP]], [[Saudi Aramco]]) dominate. [[Jeff Currie]] at [[Carlyle]] and other physical market practitioners consider forward physical prices the "real" signal — it is harder to manipulate a market where you must eventually load a tanker.

The Dated Brent assessment is where the physical market gets its daily benchmark number. [[S&P Global|Platts]] (S&P Global Commodity Insights) publishes it every business day at 16:30:00 London time, at the close of its "Market on Close" (MOC) assessment window. During this window (roughly 16:00-16:30 London), traders submit bids, offers, and execute trades for physical North Sea cargoes. Platts assesses the price of the most competitive grade in the BFOET basket for cargoes loading 10-25 days forward. The standard cargo size is 700,000 barrels (Aframax-sized), with +/- 5% operational tolerance. For WTI Midland cargoes included since 2023, Platts takes the CIF Rotterdam price and nets it back to an FOB-equivalent using a freight adjustment factor (80% of assessed freight across five North Sea loading ports: Sullom Voe, Hound Point, Sture, Teesside, Mongstad). This methodology means Dated Brent is not a single crude grade — it is the delivered-equivalent price of whichever grade in the basket is cheapest on that day.

The same MOC structure applies in Asia, but on a different clock and with different crudes. Platts assesses Dubai and Oman crude at 16:30:00 Singapore time. The Dubai partials mechanism works through 25,000-barrel clips traded in the Platts window. When a buyer accumulates 20 partials (500,000 barrels) of the same grade from the same seller within a calendar month, the partials converge into a physical full cargo deliverable as Dubai, Oman, Upper Zakum, Al Shaheen, or [[Murban]] crude at the seller's option. Anything less than 20 partials is cash-settled. This mechanism allows price discovery in a market where Dubai crude production itself has declined but where the benchmark still needs to reflect Middle East sour crude values. [[Argus Media]] provides the competing assessment, particularly influential in US markets (Argus Sour Crude Index) and European products.

Below the benchmark sits the world of differentials — the premium or discount a specific cargo trades at relative to the benchmark. A cargo of [[Nigeria]]n Bonny Light might trade at "Dated Brent plus $2.50" — meaning the buyer pays the Platts Dated Brent assessment for the relevant loading dates, plus $2.50/bbl. These differentials reflect grade quality (API gravity, sulfur content), location (freight advantage or disadvantage relative to the benchmark delivery point), and timing (how urgently the seller needs to move barrels or the buyer needs feedstock). In the Middle East, [[Saudi Aramco]]'s Official Selling Prices (OSPs) function as the dominant differential mechanism. Aramco announces OSPs around the 5th of each month for the following month's loading. For Asia-bound crude, the OSP is a differential to the average of Platts Dubai and Oman assessments. For [[Europe]], the differential is to ICE Brent Weighted Average (BWAVE). For the US, it is to the Argus Sour Crude Index (ASCI). [[Kuwait]], [[Iraq]], [[Iran]], and [[UAE]] generally follow Aramco's pricing lead, adjusting their own differentials in response. These monthly OSP decisions collectively set the price for roughly 15-20 million bbl/day of Gulf crude exports.

The final layer is the delivered price — what a refiner actually pays when the cargo discharges at its terminal. This adds freight cost, insurance, port charges, and any demurrage (penalty for exceeding the contractual loading/unloading window). A CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight) price bundles all of this — the seller delivers to the buyer's port. An FOB (Free on Board) price stops at the loading port — the buyer arranges the ship. DES (Delivered Ex-Ship) means the seller retains ownership until discharge. Most Middle Eastern crude sells FOB (the buyer charters the tanker), while Atlantic Basin cargoes increasingly trade CIF. The freight component alone can be $3-8/bbl on the Persian Gulf-to-Asia route, and freight swings dramatically with tanker market conditions: VLCC rates on the benchmark MEG-China (TD3) route hit $125,000/day in late 2025, up from $29,600/day in November 2024.

How does crude actually get bought and sold? The market divides into term contracts and spot. Term contracts are annual or multi-year agreements between a producer and a refiner for a fixed monthly volume — say, 2 million bbl/month of Arab Light from Aramco to a Japanese refiner. The price is not fixed; it floats at the OSP differential. But the volume commitment is firm (subject to allocation cuts — Aramco has trimmed Asian term volumes by 2-10% during tight markets). Term contracts provide security of supply for the refiner and revenue predictability for the producer. Globally, term contracts account for roughly 60% of crude oil sold, with spot making up the remaining ~40%. Asian refiners in [[Japan]], [[South Korea]], and [[Taiwan]] tend to be heavily term-contracted for Gulf crude (their refinery configurations are optimized for specific Middle Eastern grades). [[China]]'s independent "teapot" refineries in Shandong province are more spot-oriented, buying Russian, West African, and Brazilian cargoes on the open market. European refiners have shifted more toward spot since losing Russian Urals supplies post-2022 sanctions.

The timeline from deal to tanker departure follows a structured sequence. For term contracts, the buyer knows by the 5th of the month (when Aramco publishes OSPs) what price they will pay for next month's loading. At least 25-35 days before the loading month begins, the buyer submits a proposed lifting program — how many cargoes, preferred date ranges, vessel nominations. The seller must respond at least 15 days before the loading month. Specific vessel names must be provided at least 7 business days before the laycan (the agreed loading window, typically 3 days). If parties cannot agree on a vessel at least 5 business days before laycan, the seller can cancel that lifting. For spot cargoes, the cycle is compressed. A North Sea cargo trading in the Platts window as "25-day forward" will have its loading date nominated within days. In the Middle East spot market, traders typically work 2-3 months ahead of loading: a deal struck in early March might be for May loading barrels.

When traders say "April loading is gone," they mean all available cargoes loading in April have been committed — term allocations are placed, spot availability has been bought. Buyers needing April barrels now face either finding a reseller at a premium or shifting to May loading. This is why loading month schedules drive physical market tightness more visibly than any futures spread. The transition from one loading month to the next creates predictable trading patterns: early in the cycle (M-2 to M-3), the program is being assembled and there is optionality; late in the cycle, remaining cargoes command premiums if the month is tight, or discounts if demand is soft.

The physical loading of a VLCC is an industrial operation. A modern VLCC (Very Large Crude Carrier, 200,000-320,000 DWT, ~2 million barrel capacity) loads at an offshore buoy or terminal. Three cargo pumps rated at ~5,000 m3/hr each give a theoretical throughput of ~15,000 m3/hr — roughly 94,000 bbl/hr. A full 2-million-barrel loading takes approximately 21-24 hours under ideal conditions, though real-world times range from 24-72 hours depending on terminal infrastructure, crude viscosity, and weather. Discharge takes similar time. A full round-trip voyage — load in the Persian Gulf, sail to [[China]], discharge, ballast return — takes roughly 45-50 days. The voyage leg alone (Ras Tanura to Ningbo) runs about 19-20 days at economic speed (~12-14 knots). To [[Europe]] via Suez, the same origin to Rotterdam takes ~18 days; via the Cape of Good Hope (when Suez/Hormuz is unavailable), ~31 days. US Gulf Coast to Asia runs 21-28 days depending on route. [[West Africa]] (Nigeria/Angola) to China via the Cape takes 28-35 days.

The paper-physical disconnect is not a bug — it is a structural feature of how oil markets are organized. Futures trade the average expected price over a contract month. Physical cargoes trade the specific price for a specific grade at a specific location on a specific date. In stable markets, arbitrage keeps them aligned: if futures drift above physical, traders sell futures and buy cargoes; if below, they do the reverse. The mechanisms that link them — the Exchange of Futures for Physical (EFP), the Brent CFD (Contract for Differences between Dated Brent and the first Cash BFOE month), and the Dated-to-Frontline (DFL) swap — normally keep the basis tight to within $1-2/bbl. But in a supply shock, the physical market reprices instantly while futures, dominated by financial players with no physical exposure, reprice slowly. The managed-money crowd reads the same macro models, the same OPEC reports, the same IEA forecasts. They price the expected average over months. Physical traders price today's cargo for today's refinery that needs to run tomorrow. This is why, during the March 2026 Hormuz crisis, cash Dubai hit $157/bbl while Brent futures traded at $100 — a $57 gap. The EFS (Exchange for Swaps, the Brent-Dubai differential) blew out from $0.69 at the start of 2026 to $10.42. [[Natasha Kaneva]] at [[JPMorgan]] called the apparent stability in Brent/WTI a "mirage." She was describing this exact structural feature: futures can be wrong about reality for weeks because the players setting futures prices are not the same players loading tankers.

Contango and backwardation take on different meaning in the physical market. In financial terms, backwardation (front month above back months) simply reflects market structure. In physical terms, backwardation means storage is being liquidated — it is more profitable to sell now than to store and sell later. Contango means storage pays — the spread between future and spot exceeds the cost of carry (storage + financing + insurance, roughly $0.50-1.00/bbl/month for onshore tanks, more for floating storage on tankers). The April 2020 super-contango, when front-month WTI went to -$37.63, was a physical market phenomenon: Cushing storage was full, and anyone holding a physically deliverable contract had nowhere to put the oil. The March 2026 situation is the mirror image: extreme backwardation (Brent front-month $14.20 premium over next month, a record) reflects acute physical scarcity — refiners will pay any premium for barrels available now.

## Hedge mechanics and supply-shock losses

The canonical commodity trader book is long a physical cargo and short the matching number of futures contracts. In theory, the book has no price risk — whatever the front-month future does, the physical cargo owned offsets it on the opposite ledger. In practice, the two sides of the hedge do not settle on the same schedule, and that mismatch creates the working-capital drain that dominates trading-house P&L during supply shocks.

The futures leg of the hedge is marked to market and margined daily by the exchange. If Brent futures rise $10/bbl overnight and the trader is short, the trader must wire cash equal to $10 × contract size × number of contracts to the exchange the next morning. On a 2M-barrel VLCC cargo, a $20/bbl overnight spike means a $40M variation margin outflow within 24 hours. The physical side of the hedge does not settle until the cargo discharges at the refinery weeks later — so the trader is cash-flow-negative on the hedge long before the physical P&L offset arrives. In a stable market, this working-capital cost is absorbed by the trader's committed revolving credit facilities. In a price shock, it can exhaust those facilities within days.

The second channel of loss — structurally worse than the margin-call squeeze — is the stranded-cargo problem. A trader with a cargo loading in the Gulf in late February 2026 had already been pre-sold to a refiner at April delivery, at a price negotiated weeks earlier. When Iran closed the [[Strait of Hormuz]] on March 2, the cargo was trapped behind the closure; the buyer still expected delivery. To honor the contract, the trader had to buy replacement barrels from outside the Gulf (North Sea, West African, US Gulf Coast), at post-shock prices. The trader is now double-long physical (the trapped cargo, the replacement cargo) and both positions were funded at pre-war prices against short hedges that have moved against them. This is why the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] produced billions in early-week losses at [[Vitol]], [[Trafigura]], and [[Mercuria Energy Group|Mercuria]] even though their hedge books were structurally correct. See [[Commodity trading houses]] for the industry P&L view and the March 2026 credit-facility build-up ($3B each at Vitol and Trafigura, $1.5B at [[Gunvor]]).

The Vitol naphtha loss on March 12, 2026 — two vessels trapped in the Gulf, set on fire by Iranian forces, one crew member killed — is the rare case where the physical hedge fails entirely: the underlying cargo is destroyed, leaving only the short futures and the sale obligation. Marine cargo insurance covers the hull loss but does not make the trader whole on the forward delivery commitment. This is the tail risk physical trading carries that pure financial players do not.
