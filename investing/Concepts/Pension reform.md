# Pension reform

UK pension reform — the package of voluntary and proposed-mandatory measures pushing UK defined-contribution (DC) pension schemes to allocate more to UK equities and private markets. The two named anchors are the [[Mansion House Compact]] (2023, voluntary, signed under [[Jeremy Hunt]]) and the Mansion House Accord (2025, voluntary "beefed-up" version under [[Rachel Reeves]]). A proposed mandation provision in the [[Pension Schemes Bill 2025]] would give the government power to legally require allocation if the voluntary route stalls — currently being debated in the House of Lords on fiduciary-duty grounds.

The structural problem the reforms try to solve: UK DC schemes have ~6–9% UK equity allocation as of 2024, down from ~50% in the early 2000s. The de-allocation runs across two decades and reflects both the [[Defined benefit pension|defined-benefit]] decline / [[DC pension|DC]] rise and DC default funds gradually diversifying overseas. The reform package wants to bend that curve without breaching fiduciary duty — voluntary commitments first, mandation as backstop. It sits alongside parallel [[UK]] capital-markets reforms (listing rules, [[Stocks and shares ISA|ISA]] allowance changes, [[AIM]] review) as the components of a broader push to deepen [[UK]] equity markets and capture more domestic capital for [[LSE]]-listed names.

## Why this matters

| Reason | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Scale of capital | Mansion House Accord targets £50bn+ mobilised over 5 years; £25bn for [[UK]] specifically |
| Domestic listings push | Direct corollary of the [[Revolut]] / [[Monzo]] London-listing courting — what good is listing in London if domestic institutional bid is shallow? |
| Structural reversal | First serious attempt in two decades to reverse UK pension de-allocation from UK equities |
| Mandation precedent | Whether government can legally direct private-sector pension allocation sets a precedent that goes beyond fintech listings |

## Mansion House Compact (Jul 2023)

The original voluntary commitment, signed under Conservative Chancellor [[Jeremy Hunt]] at the Lord Mayor's Mansion House speech. Nine large UK DC pension providers signed an "expression of intent" to allocate at least 5% of default-fund assets to unlisted equities by 2030.

| Signatory | Type |
|-----------|------|
| Aviva | Insurer/pension provider |
| Scottish Widows | Insurer/pension provider |
| L&G | Insurer/pension provider |
| Aegon | Insurer/pension provider |
| Phoenix | Insurer/pension provider |
| NEST | Master trust |
| Smart Pension | Master trust |
| Mercer | Master trust |
| M&G | Asset manager |

The compact was an expression of intent, not a binding commitment. Critics noted that "by 2030" gave seven years of soft-pressure compliance and that 5% of default-fund assets was small relative to the de-allocation it aimed to reverse. Sympathetic readers noted that any allocation target with public signatories was an improvement on the prior status quo of pure fiduciary-duty discretion.

## Mansion House Accord (May 2025)

[[Rachel Reeves]]'s "beefed-up" successor. 17 signatories (vs nine), with a doubled headline allocation target.

| Commitment | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| Total allocation | Minimum 10% to private markets across all main default funds by 2030 |
| UK-specific | At least 5% of total to UK private markets |
| Capital mobilised | £50bn+ over five years per [[HM Treasury]] estimate |
| UK component | £25bn for UK investments |
| Signatories | 17 of the largest UK DC pension schemes |

Voluntary again — but with explicit signal that mandation legislation is in the pipeline as a backstop. The Accord is structured to give signatories cover ("we already signed up voluntarily") while keeping the legislative threat live for non-signatories.

## Mandation (Pension Schemes Bill 2025)

The Pension Schemes Bill 2025 includes a proposed power giving the [[UK]] government legal authority to direct DC pension schemes' default-fund allocation — capped at 10% of assets / 5% UK-specific (i.e., aligned with the Accord's voluntary targets, so the mandation can't go beyond what's already been voluntarily agreed).

The provision has been the focus of intense parliamentary debate:

- House of Lords has voted against the mandation clause multiple times on fiduciary-duty grounds — fund trustees are legally required to act in beneficiaries' best interest, and a state-directed allocation would override that
- [[Rachel Reeves]] and the [[HM Treasury]] argue mandation is a fallback if voluntary commitments stall, not a default mode
- Industry response is mixed: large signatories (already aligned) are relaxed; smaller schemes and active trustees flag the precedent risk
- The mandation cap explicitly matches the Accord targets, narrowing the political flashpoint to whether *any* state direction of private capital is acceptable

## Components of the broader reform package

[[Pension reform]] is one of four parallel UK capital-markets reform threads under [[Rachel Reeves]]:

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Pension reform | This note — DC allocation push via Compact, Accord, mandation |
| [[Listing rule modernisation]] | [[Financial Conduct Authority|FCA]] reforms — dual-class shares permitted, free-float requirements eased, sponsor regime simplified |
| [[Stocks and shares ISA|ISA]] reform | Increased allowance for UK-listed equities; "British ISA" concept (deferred but kept on the policy menu) |
| [[AIM]] review | Junior-market structure reform; tax-relief preservation debates |

The four threads are coordinated to address one structural problem (eroded UK equity market depth) from four angles (supply side / listing rules; demand side / pension capital + retail ISAs; lifecycle / junior market). The pension thread is the largest by capital scale.

## LGPS asset pooling (parallel reform)

The [[Local Government Pension Scheme]] (LGPS) — UK local authority workers' pension fund, ~£400bn AUM — runs a parallel reform track. The government has been pushing LGPS to consolidate from ~86 individual authority schemes into a handful of "asset pools" since 2015. The Reeves government accelerated this in 2024–2025, targeting completion by March 2026 (the LGPS target predates the DC Accord by years but feeds the same broad goal of UK-allocated institutional capital).

LGPS pooling matters because (a) ~£400bn is large in [[UK]] equity-market terms; (b) pooled funds have lower governance overhead and can take bigger allocation positions; (c) the pooling architecture is the model that DC mandation would likely use if enacted.

## Implications for actors

### UK fintech / IPO candidates

[[Revolut]] (leaning [[NYSE]]) and [[Monzo]] ("not in a hurry") are the test cases. The Reeves pitch is: list in London because the reform package will deepen domestic institutional bid by 2030, so the IPO valuation gap vs [[NYSE]] should narrow. The catch: reforms take 5+ years to land in P&L; IPO timing is now.

### Pension providers

Signatories ([[Aviva]], [[L&G]], [[Phoenix Group]], [[M&G]], etc.) gain political cover, lose some allocation discretion, and pick up private-markets sourcing requirements. Larger signatories are best-positioned because they have in-house private-markets capability; smaller schemes may need to outsource via sub-advisors — modest tailwind for [[BlackRock]] / [[Schroders]] / mid-market UK PE.

### UK private-markets

The £25bn UK allocation target is large for the [[UK]] private-markets ecosystem. Beneficiaries likely include [[British Patient Capital]], [[Cambridge Innovation Capital]], [[Pension SuperFund]]-style consolidators, plus growth-stage UK names not yet IPO'd.

### Asset managers

Net winner if reform succeeds — more UK-allocated AUM means more fee income. [[Schroders]], [[M&G]], [[Aberdeen]] are direct beneficiaries.

## Risks / open questions

| Risk | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Fiduciary-duty challenge | Mandation could be legally challenged by trustees citing duty to beneficiaries |
| Allocation quality | Forced UK allocation could chase poor risk-adjusted returns; "buy what you must" rather than "buy what's best" |
| Capacity bottleneck | UK private markets may not absorb £25bn cleanly — risk of crowding effects in mid-market PE / growth equity |
| Timing mismatch | Reform horizon (5+ years) vs near-term decisions ([[Revolut]] / [[Monzo]] IPO venue choice in 2026–2027) |
| Political continuity | Reform requires Labour to remain in government through implementation; Conservative reversal possible if elected pre-2030 |

## Synthesis

The reform package is a structural bet that [[UK]] equity-market depth can be rebuilt by redirecting domestic pension capital. Three distinct mechanisms are working in parallel: voluntary signalling (Compact + Accord), credible mandation threat (Pension Schemes Bill), and parallel reforms removing other obstacles (listing rules, [[Stocks and shares ISA|ISA]], [[AIM]]). The political cost-benefit favours voluntary: if signatories deliver, mandation never activates; if they stall, mandation is the legally-prepared backstop.

The hardest question the reforms don't answer: do UK private markets have £25bn of investable opportunities that meet pension-fund fiduciary thresholds at current valuations? If yes, the reform is a clean win — pension funds get diversification, UK companies get capital. If no, the forced allocation will either chase poor risk-adjusted returns or pool into a handful of mega-deals that don't broaden market depth.

The reform is also a real-time test of whether [[UK]] domestic listings ([[Revolut]], [[Monzo]], future fintechs) can be retained by deepening the domestic bid. The 5+ year reform horizon vs near-term IPO decisions means listing venue choice in 2026–2027 is happening before the reform's effects materialise. If both [[Revolut]] and [[Monzo]] go to [[NYSE]] despite the courting, the reform's IPO-attraction logic is weakened even if other goals (private-markets allocation, retail-savings deepening) succeed.
