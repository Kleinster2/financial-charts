# Common Prosperity

[[Xi Jinping]]'s framing for redistributive economic policy in [[China]], rooted in CCP doctrine on socialism's end-state, escalated into an enforcement campaign in August 2021 that wiped $1.5T+ from tech equity values and destroyed the for-profit education sector, then quietly reframed by 2026 into a mainstream academic agenda emphasising human-capital investment and "invest in people" rather than coercive redistribution. The May 15, 2026 [[Pekingnology]] translation of [[Li Shi]]'s lecture at [[Peking University National School of Development]] is the canonical articulation of the reframed version.

The concept is load-bearing for understanding China's 2020s macro shift away from the asset-accumulation-led growth model: it is the policy lens that justifies the [[China platform labor regulation|gig-worker]] regulation, the [[Three Red Lines|property]] deleveraging, the antitrust action against [[Alibaba]] / [[Ant Group]] / [[Meituan]], the gaming restrictions, the luxury-import headwind affecting [[LVMH]] and [[Kering]], and the broader rebalancing toward consumption + services. It is also the doctrinal anchor for the rehabilitation arc that culminated in the May 2025 [[Private Economy Promotion Law]] and the post-[[DeepSeek]] private-sector rapprochement.

---

## Synopsis

Common Prosperity (共同富裕, *gongtong fuyu*) is the CCP's policy framework for addressing inequality without abandoning the socialist-market-economy hybrid. Its core claim is that allowing inequality to widen indefinitely is incompatible with the CCP's legitimacy and with the "second centenary goal" of building a "great modern socialist country" by 2049. Operationally, it has gone through three distinct phases (see #Doctrinal lineage).

The framework crystallised as a coordinated policy push at Xi's August 17, 2021 [[Central Financial and Economic Affairs Commission]] speech, which named tech platforms, after-school tutoring, real estate, and high-net-worth wealth concentration as targets for "third distribution" — voluntary redistribution by wealthy individuals and corporations beyond taxation. The enforcement phase that followed was the largest sectoral wealth reset of the 2010-2025 era in any major economy (see #The 2021 enforcement phase). By 2024-2026, the framing shifted toward the academic "invest in people" model that [[Li Shi]] articulated in his March 29, 2026 lecture (translated by Pekingnology May 15, 2026; see #The 2026 academic reframing).

Whether the reframing reflects a genuine pivot or a tactical adjustment to a deteriorating macro environment (5% growth, private-fixed-asset investment turning negative -6% by December 2025, ~16% youth unemployment per Li Shi) is the central interpretive question (inference — Li Shi himself does not stake out this comparison, but the trajectory implies it). The vault's working read is that the reframed version is closer to the policy reality being pursued in 2026, while the 2021-2022 enforcement-era version is the version most foreign investors still price into Chinese assets.

## Doctrinal lineage

The concept has been in CCP discourse since Mao's era but its policy meaning has shifted significantly across leadership generations (inference — the lineage compression is mine; Li Shi's piece focuses on the current iteration and references only Marx).

| Phase | Years | Leader | Framing |
|-------|-------|--------|---------|
| Mao-era egalitarianism | 1949-1976 | [[Mao Zedong]] | Levelling through collectivisation; rejected by Deng as the cause of stagnation |
| "Let some get rich first" | 1978-2012 | [[Deng Xiaoping]] → Jiang Zemin → [[Hu Jintao]] | Allow inequality during catchup phase; common prosperity deferred as a goal for the future |
| "Harmonious society" | 2002-2012 | [[Hu Jintao]] + [[Wen Jiabao]] | First serious distributive pivot — rural healthcare, hukou reform attempts, minimum income standards |
| Xi consolidation | 2012-2021 | [[Xi Jinping]] | "Targeted poverty alleviation" (declared complete 2020); preparing the rhetorical ground for active common-prosperity policy |
| Enforcement phase | Aug 2021-2023 | Xi + [[Liu He]] | Third distribution, tech crackdown, edtech ban, gaming licence freeze, "patriotic capital" pressure |
| Quiet recalibration | 2024-2025 | Xi + [[He Lifeng]] | Rhetoric fades from headlines; structural targets continue (hukou reform, healthcare, regional rebalancing) |
| Academic reframing | 2026 | Li Shi / Zhejiang University | "Investing in people," endogenous-growth theory, freedom as productivity input |

The doctrinal anchor Li Shi invokes is Marx's "free and all-round development of all people" as humanity's ultimate goal — positioning common prosperity within socialist-orthodox tradition rather than as a tactical inequality response (per Li Shi, Pekingnology May 15, 2026). This is rhetorically careful: it preserves the doctrinal lineage while reducing the coercive policy implications.

## The 2021 enforcement phase

The August 17, 2021 [[Central Financial and Economic Affairs Commission]] speech operationalised Common Prosperity through a multi-sector enforcement push. The macro context was the post-Covid recovery and the consolidation of Xi's third term, which gave the leadership unusual freedom to absorb wealth-destruction in service of redistributive signalling.

| Target | Mechanism | Asset-class outcome |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Tech platforms ([[Alibaba]], [[Tencent]], [[Meituan]], [[Pinduoduo]], [[JD.com]]) | Antitrust enforcement + "third distribution" donations + algorithm regulation | ~$1.5T+ market-cap destruction 2021-2022; [[Hang Seng Tech Index]] -75% peak-to-trough |
| After-school tutoring ([[New Oriental]], [[TAL Education]], [[Gaotu]]) | July 2021 "Double Reduction" — non-profit conversion required, foreign capital banned | Sector effectively destroyed; $100B+ market cap wiped; tens of thousands of jobs eliminated |
| Online gaming | License freezes 2021-2023 + youth playtime limits + content review | [[Tencent]] gaming revenue stagnated through 2023; sector recovered partially with 2024 license resumption |
| Real estate ([[Evergrande]], [[Country Garden]], [[Sunac]]) | [[Three Red Lines]] deleveraging policy + presale-deposit restrictions | Sustained property crisis 2021-onward; sector still impaired through 2026 |
| Luxury imports ([[LVMH]], [[Kering]], [[Hermès]]) | Anti-corruption + ostentation discouragement + customs enforcement | Sustained China luxury-demand headwind, visible in [[LVMH]] FY2024 / FY2025 sales |
| Private equity / wealth management ([[Hillhouse]] / [[Zhang Lei]]) | "Patriotic capital" framing + IPO timing constraints + foreign-LP scrutiny | Quieter private-side adjustment; major PE funds repositioned toward hard-tech and SOE-aligned themes |
| High-net-worth individuals | Tax enforcement (Fan Bingbing 2018 precedent extended) + "voluntary" donations | Visible donation surge from Tencent (¥100bn pledge Aug 2021), [[Alibaba]] (¥100bn), [[Pinduoduo]] (¥10bn) |
| Celebrity culture | Crackdown on "fan circle" economy, livestreaming tax enforcement (Viya 2021), entertainment-industry pay caps | Sustained suppression of celebrity-economy adjacencies |

The structural read at the time: Xi was demonstrating that the CCP's claim on the legitimacy of the private-sector wealth was contingent on alignment with party objectives, and was willing to absorb large-scale wealth destruction to make that point credibly (inference — this was the consensus read among Western China analysts in 2021-2022; not Li Shi's framing). The enforcement phase peaked in 2022 and faded into the broader 2023-2024 economic-malaise discussion without an official wind-down statement (inference).

## The 2026 academic reframing — Li Shi

[[Li Shi]] is Senior Professor and Dean of the [[Institute for Common Prosperity and Development]] at [[Zhejiang University]], the academic body specifically chartered to develop the framework. His March 29, 2026 lecture at [[Peking University National School of Development]], translated by Pekingnology on May 15, 2026, is the canonical mainstream-academic statement of the reframed version.

The two slogans:

1. "Make the cake bigger" — sustain growth via productivity, not asset accumulation
2. "Divide the cake better" — redistribute via human-capital investment and public goods access rather than coercive wealth transfer

The mechanism Li Shi proposes is "investing in people" — directing public spending toward education, health, and childcare, especially for low-income households, on the theoretical basis of endogenous growth theory. Human capital is positioned as "an endogenous engine of economic growth" rather than as a redistribution category (per Li Shi, Pekingnology May 15, 2026). This is the doctrinally important move: it reframes inequality-targeting as growth policy rather than as fairness policy.

The freedom argument is the most rhetorically striking element: "Human beings have consciousness, ideas, values, and beliefs. They pursue freedom"; "freedom is not only a right ... it is also a source of productivity and creativity"; create "an environment in which people can develop more freely, so that society's creative potential can be more fully released" (per Li Shi, Pekingnology May 15, 2026). For a senior figure at the Institute for Common Prosperity and Development to argue that freedom is a productivity input is significant — it implicitly limits how far coercive redistribution can be pushed without sacrificing the growth function (inference — Li Shi doesn't stake out this limit explicitly).

Specific policy recommendations Li Shi advances:

- Income share: raise household income share in national distribution; increase public spending on livelihoods
- [[Hukou]] reform: remove barriers to population mobility; ensure equal access to public services for migrants
- Education: abolish discriminatory school access; allow college entrance exams outside household registration; redirect resources to disadvantaged children; reform teaching from exam-oriented to "knowing how to create"
- Preschool / childcare: expand ages 0-3 services
- Governance: strengthen teacher roles; deepen university administrative reform

The hukou and education recommendations are the most policy-substantial; they would represent a significant departure from the implicit social contract that has produced the urban-rural wage gap (per Li Shi: urban wage Gini rose from 0.27 in 1988 to >0.4 in 2023).

## What's measured — Li Shi's inequality numbers

The Pekingnology piece is unusual in cataloguing concrete inequality metrics from a mainstream Chinese academic source. Most prior Common Prosperity discourse used the framing without the specific numbers (inference — these numbers have not appeared in vault references to Common Prosperity prior to this ingestion).

| Metric | Trajectory | Source |
|--------|------------|--------|
| National Gini coefficient | ~0.46-0.47 for a decade | Li Shi citing official + academic estimates |
| Urban wage Gini | 0.27 (1988) → >0.4 (2023) | Li Shi |
| Wealth Gini | 0.45 (1995) → >0.7 (2023) | Li Shi |
| Population with monthly income < ¥1,000 | ~300M (2021) | Li Shi (matches [[Li Keqiang]] 2020 famous remark) |
| Population with monthly income < ¥500 | ~98M (2021) | Li Shi |
| Household income growth | 8% (2013-2018) → <5% (2018-2023) | Li Shi |
| Bottom-decile income growth | <2% annual recently | Li Shi |
| Urban wage growth | -3 pp recently | Li Shi |
| Private fixed-asset investment | -6% YoY by December 2025 | Li Shi citing NBS |
| Youth unemployment | ~16% extended period | Li Shi |

Wealth-Gini going from 0.45 to >0.70 in 28 years is the most striking number: China shifted from approximately US 1990s levels to among the highest in the world. The wealth-vs-income Gini gap is the structural concern — most of the inequality is now in asset holdings, particularly housing, which is also the channel through which the [[Three Red Lines]] deleveraging has hit household balance sheets.

## Synthesis

The framework's most useful structural read is the 2021-vs-2026 gap (see #Doctrinal lineage). The 2021 enforcement-era version is what Western investors still price into Chinese assets — coercive redistribution risk, sudden sector-level wealth destruction, "patriotic capital" pressure. The 2026 academic reframing represents a much more conventional human-capital-and-public-goods agenda that would be uncontroversial in most OECD policy discussions (inference — the contrast with Western policy discourse is mine).

The investing implication is asymmetric mispricing. If the 2026 academic version is closer to the policy reality, then Chinese tech, consumer, and education assets are over-discounting Common Prosperity risk — a multi-year mean reversion in their multiples becomes plausible (inference — this hinges on the academic reframing reflecting real policy rather than rhetorical adjustment). If the 2021 enforcement-era version is closer to the underlying policy posture and the reframing is tactical, then current discount levels are about right and any rerating reverses on the next enforcement wave (inference). The [[Private Economy Promotion Law]] (May 2025) and the post-[[DeepSeek]] private-sector rehabilitation arc support the first interpretation; the [[China platform labor regulation]] gig-worker compliance and the still-impaired property sector support a hedged read.

The policy substance Li Shi proposes — hukou reform, equal-access education, public childcare, healthcare expansion — would represent the largest distributive shift in China since the 1990s if implemented at scale (inference). The fiscal capacity to do this is constrained by the property-revenue collapse at the local-government level and the unwound [[LGFV]] debt overhang. Whether the central government substitutes for local fiscal capacity is the constraint that determines whether the framework gets translated into delivery (inference — this fiscal-architecture question is a vault-side analytical extension, not Li Shi's framing).

The freedom-as-productivity argument is the deepest rhetorical move. It positions the framework as compatible with creative-economy growth rather than as a brake on it — the inverse of how the 2021 enforcement-era version was read. Whether the underlying CCP political surface actually permits this is the open question that goes beyond economic policy (inference — Li Shi sidesteps this in the lecture).

## Asset-class implications

The framework's market-relevant outputs cluster by sector (inference — this synthesis pulls Li Shi's diagnosis together with the 2021-era impact data from existing vault references).

| Sector | 2021-2022 enforcement impact | 2024-2026 trajectory | Common Prosperity 2026 read |
|--------|-------------------------------|----------------------|-----------------------------|
| Internet platforms ([[Alibaba]], [[Tencent]], [[Meituan]], [[JD.com]], [[Pinduoduo]]) | $1.5T+ destroyed | Recovery from DeepSeek moment; [[Private Economy Promotion Law]] | Reframing supports rerating; structural multiple compression vs 2020 peak |
| Online education | Sector destroyed | Mostly impaired; [[New Oriental]] pivoted to livestream commerce | Li Shi's education recs reopen part of the demand pool (tutoring quality + access) |
| Gaming ([[Tencent]] / [[NetEase]] / [[miHoYo]]) | License freeze + monetisation caps | Normalising 2024-2026 | Lower regulatory premium on the sector |
| Real estate developers ([[Vanke]], [[Country Garden]], [[Longfor]]) | [[Three Red Lines]] crisis | Ongoing; consolidation; central-government backstop selective | Common Prosperity discourse no longer focused here |
| Property-linked services / appliances | Demand depression | Slow normalisation | Indirect beneficiary of any household-balance-sheet repair |
| Luxury / spirits ([[LVMH]], [[Kering]], [[Hermès]], [[Pernod Ricard]]) | Demand headwind via anti-ostentation | Persistent through 2025-2026 | Reframing does not relieve this; "divide the cake better" does not legitimise luxury growth |
| Private equity ([[Hillhouse]] / [[Zhang Lei]], [[Sequoia China]] / HongShan) | Foreign-LP scrutiny + "patriotic" repositioning | Quieter; hard-tech focus | Reframing modestly supportive; doesn't resolve the structural foreign-capital question |
| Wealth management / private banking | HNW assets re-disclosure pressure | Sustained; offshore growth | The "third distribution" framing has not been revoked but is dormant |
| Healthcare ([[Ping An Healthcare]], [[Alibaba Health]], [[JD Health]]) | Limited 2021 impact | Common Prosperity 2026 directly supportive of public-health investment | Sector beneficiary if delivery materialises |
| Education-services (post-tutoring-ban: livestream commerce, vocational, ed-tech) | Pivot beneficiaries | Mixed | Beneficiary of Li Shi's "equal-access education" line |
| Childcare / preschool operators | Limited 2021 impact | Demographic-policy and Common Prosperity 2026 both supportive | Direct beneficiary cluster |

The two emergent asset-class implications visible only in the 2026 reframing — childcare/preschool and equal-access education — are not yet pricing the framework as a structural tailwind (inference; no major Chinese childcare or education operators have repriced on the Li Shi piece as of May 18, 2026).
