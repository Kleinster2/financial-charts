#concept #privateequity #secondaries #privatemarkets

**Private equity secondaries** — Trading in pre-existing private equity fund stakes, rather than primary commitments to new funds. Includes LP-led sales (limited partners offloading positions in maturing funds) and GP-led transactions ([[Continuation fund|continuation vehicles]] established by sponsors to retain assets past fund life). Global volume reached $240bn in 2025 — a 6x expansion in a decade — and has become the dominant exit channel as IPO and M&A markets remain shallow.

## Synthesis

The secondaries market has gone from niche workaround to mainstream channel for the entire private capital industry in five years. Three forces fused: a record stock of unrealized buyout value (~$3T) trapped by frozen IPO and M&A markets after 2022 rates rose, institutional LPs that need cash distributions to fund new commitments, and a flood of retail money into evergreen vehicles that can deploy capital faster through secondaries than primary funds. The result is a structural shift in PE economics — elite firms that once dismissed secondaries as a low-prestige cleanup business have spent $5bn+ acquiring secondaries specialists in 2026 alone ([[EQT]]–[[Coller Capital]] $3.7bn, [[KKR]]–[[Arctos]] $1.4bn), following earlier consolidation moves by [[Ares Management]], [[Apollo]], [[CVC Capital Partners|CVC]], [[Blackstone]], and [[Carlyle]]. The high paper IRRs that secondaries funds report in their early years — 19% after fees in four years for [[Blackstone]]'s flagship vehicle versus 11% for its buyout fund — partly reflect the mark-up of assets bought at a discount and the use of leverage; the realized returns over a fund's life converge toward more normal 16% targets as discounts dissipate and underlying companies determine outcomes. The risk overhang sits in the retail channel: evergreen vehicles deploying into secondaries advertise the early-vintage paper returns to monthly-subscription wealth investors who may not understand the structural decay — and the proximate cause of the secondaries boom (the lack of exits) is the same condition that would make a future drawdown ugly.

## Two halves of the market

The secondaries market splits cleanly between transactions initiated by selling LPs and transactions structured by the GP managing the underlying funds.

### LP-led secondaries

An institutional investor sells its stake in one or more PE funds to a secondaries buyer. The selling LP exits early; the buyer assumes the position at the agreed (typically discounted) price.

![[ft-secondaries-lp-led-step1.png]]
*LP-led starting point: institutions invest in both buyout funds (typically 10-12 year locked structures) and secondaries funds (shorter-duration vehicles that purchase fund stakes from other institutions). Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

![[ft-secondaries-lp-led-step2.png]]
*LP-led mechanism: when an institutional investor wants liquidity from a buyout fund stake, the secondaries fund buys the position. The institution exits; the secondaries fund takes over the LP interest, including remaining capital calls and distribution rights on the underlying portfolio. Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

LP-led volume: ~$125bn in 2025 (Jefferies estimates), and $400bn cumulative since 2021 — driven by pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, and family offices rebalancing or harvesting liquidity.

### GP-led secondaries

The sponsor of a maturing fund restructures rather than exits — transferring assets into a new vehicle (a [[Continuation fund]]) it also manages. Existing LPs choose: cash out at the stated valuation, or roll forward into the new vehicle.

![[ft-secondaries-gp-led-step1.png]]
*GP-led starting point: a PE firm has portfolio companies in a maturing buyout fund and a secondaries fund standing by as financing partner. Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

![[ft-secondaries-gp-led-step2-continuation.png]]
*GP-led mechanism: the PE firm sets up a continuation vehicle that buys selected portfolio companies from the maturing fund. The secondaries fund provides financing, and the GP retains management. Existing LPs in the original fund can cash out or roll into the new vehicle. Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

GP-led volume: ~$115bn in 2025 (Jefferies), with [[Continuation fund|continuation vehicles]] now ~89% of GP-led activity. Cumulative GP-led ~$362bn since 2021. See [[Continuation fund]] for the full mechanics, the ADIC vs EMG litigation, ILPA guidance, and the vacated SEC fairness-opinion rules.

## Why the market exists

Private equity funds have a fixed life — typically 10 years plus extensions. The first half is invested; the second half is harvested through exits. The model assumes a functioning exit environment.

| Constraint | Implication |
|---|---|
| Buyout fund lock-up | Institutional LPs cannot withdraw before fund termination |
| IPO market shallow since 2022 | GPs cannot exit portfolio companies into public markets at expected valuations |
| M&A volumes compressed by elevated rates | Strategic and financial buyers paying less for assets |
| LP distributions below historical average | Distributions running ~17% of NAV versus 26% historical (Bain Capital data via Continuation fund note) |
| LPs need cash to fund new commitments | Fundraising contracts when distributions slow |
| ~$3T in unrealized buyout value (2025) | Latent supply seeking exits |

LP-led secondaries solve the LP-side liquidity problem; GP-led secondaries solve the GP-side hold-period problem. Secondaries funds intermediate both — buying assets that the natural exit channel cannot absorb at current pricing.

## How returns are juiced

Secondaries vehicles routinely report higher early IRRs than their underlying buyout funds. Three mechanisms produce the lift, all of which are technically leverage or leverage-like.

### 1. External borrowing

The secondaries fund finances purchases with bank debt, putting equity into only a fraction of the deal.

![[ft-secondaries-external-borrowing-step1.png]]
*External borrowing — purchase side: the secondaries fund pays $90mn for a portfolio of fund stakes using $60mn of its own equity plus $30mn debt from an external lender. The fund only needs to deploy 67% equity to control the full position. Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

![[ft-secondaries-external-borrowing-step2.png]]
*External borrowing — exit side: when the underlying companies are realized over time for $130mn, the lender is repaid $35mn (principal plus interest) and the secondaries fund nets $95mn — a 1.6x return on the $60mn equity rather than ~1.4x on the unlevered $90mn purchase price. Same juicing mechanism as a mortgage or LBO. Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

### 2. Overcommitment

The fund commits to deals adding up to more than its committed capital, relying on projected cash flows from earlier deals to fund later commitments.

![[ft-secondaries-overcommitment-step1.png]]
*Overcommitment — first portfolio: the secondaries fund purchases a portfolio of fund stakes giving it exposure to underlying portfolio companies. Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

![[ft-secondaries-overcommitment-step2.png]]
*Overcommitment — second portfolio: rather than waiting for distributions to refill committed capital, the fund commits to buying a second portfolio of fund stakes based on expected (not yet received) cash from the first portfolio's exits. The fund's total commitments exceed its actual equity — a leverage effect without explicit borrowing. Source: FT, Apr 2026.*

### 3. Deferred payment

The fund negotiates with the selling LP to pay the purchase price in instalments rather than upfront. This delays capital calls from the fund's own backers and inflates the IRR (which is calendar-time sensitive).

The selling pension fund may accept a smaller headline discount in exchange for upfront cash plus a deferred tranche, but the time value of money means the deferred component is worth less than its face — a hidden discount from the LP's perspective.

| Technique | Source of return lift | Risk amplifier |
|---|---|---|
| External borrowing | Levered equity returns | Amplifies losses if underlying companies underperform |
| Overcommitment | Effective leverage without debt | Cash-flow risk if exits delay or impair |
| Deferred payment | IRR inflation via timing | Limited — but obscures realized discount to seller |
| Discount to NAV | Buying at 5-25% below par, marking up to par | Wears off as the asset is held |

All four mechanisms shrink the return premium over time. A vehicle reporting 24% early IRR routinely settles to 16% or below as discounts dissipate and underlying company performance dominates.

## The mark-to-NAV problem

A core feature of how secondaries funds report performance interacts badly with retail-investor expectations.

Sample mechanics: a secondaries manager buys a portfolio of fund interests with $100mn underlying NAV at a 20% discount, paying $80mn. Accounting rules instruct the buyer to mark the position to NAV — $100mn — immediately. The fund records a $20mn unrealized gain on day one, producing an enormous reported IRR.

If the underlying buyout funds then exit those companies at $100mn one year later, the secondaries fund realizes its $20mn gain. The realized IRR will be roughly 25% — material but below the day-one unrealized IRR. If exits take three years instead, the realized IRR drops further. If exits come in below NAV, the gain compresses.

[[Blackstone]]'s public secondaries vintages illustrate the pattern:

| Vintage | IRR (after fees) | Reported when |
|---|---|---|
| 2021 secondaries | 24% | 2023 |
| 2021 secondaries | 20% | September 2025 |
| Seven-year vintage | 32% | 2023 |
| Seven-year vintage | 20% | September 2025 |
| Flagship buyout fund (six-year vintage) | 11% | September 2025 |
| Long-term secondaries fund target | ~16% | Stated by management |

The Blackstone numbers say two things at once. First, secondaries vehicles outperform flagship buyouts substantially in early years — 19% versus 11% in the four-year-old comparison the FT highlighted. Second, they decay materially toward fund-stated targets as the discount effect and leverage effect dissipate. The institutional LP understands this trajectory; the retail wealth investor adding to an evergreen vehicle in year three may not.

## Elite-firm consolidation (2024-2026)

Where buyout firms once dismissed secondaries as a low-prestige cleanup business, they have spent the past five years acquiring secondaries specialists. The pattern accelerated through 2025 and into 2026.

| Acquirer | Target | Date | Value | Strategic logic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Carlyle]] | [[AlpInvest]] | 2011 | n/d | First elite acquirer; AlpInvest now ~$85B AUM secondaries+co-invest arm |
| [[Blackstone]] | [[Strategic Partners]] (ex-Credit Suisse) | 2013 | n/d | Created Blackstone's flagship secondaries platform |
| [[Ares Management]] | Landmark Partners | 2021 | $1.08bn | Founded the Ares Secondaries Group (~$38B AUM by 2025) |
| [[CVC Capital Partners\|CVC]] | Glendower Capital | 2022 | n/d | Built secondaries practice into European PE platform |
| [[Apollo]] | (organic + acquihires) | 2023-25 | n/d | Apollo S3 platform; evergreen secondaries vehicle |
| [[EQT]] | [[Coller Capital]] | Apr 2026 | $3.7bn | Most expensive in the sequence; brings $36B+ secondaries AUM |
| [[KKR]] | [[Arctos]] | Feb 2026 | $1.4bn (+ up to $550mn) | Sports + secondaries platform; ~$15B AUM; new "KKR Solutions" vertical |

The pattern: every top-six alternative asset manager has now bought or built a major secondaries platform. The two largest acquisitions both closed in 2026.

Why the urgency: secondaries deals deploy capital faster than primary buyouts, plug naturally into evergreen wealth vehicles, and reduce vintage risk by diversifying across many existing deals. The acquirers are paying premium multiples because their evergreen retail funds need deal flow that locked-up traditional buyouts cannot supply.

## Retail crossover via evergreen vehicles

The fastest-growing distribution channel for secondaries is the [[Evergreen private capital vehicles|evergreen vehicle]]: a perpetual-life fund that accepts monthly subscriptions and redemptions, sold to accredited and high-net-worth individuals rather than the locked-up institutional channel.

Secondaries are operationally ideal for evergreen vehicles because:
- Capital deploys faster (existing portfolios, not new commitments)
- Cash flows back faster (later-stage assets closer to exit)
- Diversification across many companies dilutes single-name risk
- Discount-to-NAV mark-ups produce the high early returns that attract subscriptions

| Evergreen vehicle | Manager | Secondaries allocation |
|---|---|---|
| [[BXPE]] | [[Blackstone]] | ~5% secondaries |
| [[StepStone Group\|STPEX]] | [[StepStone Group\|StepStone]] | High |
| [[Hamilton Lane]] PE evergreen | [[Hamilton Lane]] | ~50% secondaries |
| Apollo Global Wealth | [[Apollo]] | Material; product launched 2024-25 |
| Ares Wealth Solutions (AWMS) | [[Ares Management]] | Material |
| Flagship US fund | [[Partners Group]] | ~17% secondaries |

The structural risk that comes with this distribution shift: retail investors subscribing in year three of an evergreen vehicle inherit the post-mark-up returns, not the pre-mark-up ones. [[Partners Capital]] partner Euan Finlay (Apr 2026 FT): if early returns "had been driven by buying secondaries at a discount and marking them up, you will not benefit from that uplift" as a later investor.

And the secondaries boom's proximate cause is itself the risk: the same lack of IPO and M&A exits that produces supply is the condition that would make a forced unwind during a drawdown ugly. Wealth investors in evergreen vehicles have monthly liquidity rights, but the underlying secondaries assets are not monthly-liquid — a redemption surge could force gating, as has already happened in adjacent retail private credit ([[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]]).
