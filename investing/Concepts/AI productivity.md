**AI productivity** — the macro question of whether AI adoption is lifting measured productivity, and the evidence track for it. Distinct from [[AI labor displacement]] (who loses work) and from the [[US-Europe productivity paradox]] (measurement methodology); this note tracks whether the aggregate take-off is visible in the data.

---

## The 2026 take-off claim

[[Erik Brynjolfsson]] (Stanford Digital Economy Lab director) argued in an FT op-ed ("The AI productivity take-off is finally visible," 2026) that the aggregate signal had finally arrived:

| Metric | Value | Baseline |
|--------|-------|----------|
| US productivity growth, 2025 | ~2.7% | ~1.4% prior-decade annual average |
| US real GDP growth, Q4 2025 | 3.7% | — |

The micro mechanism pairs with the macro print: entry-level hiring cooled in AI-exposed sectors while workers using AI to augment skills saw employment grow — firms delegating codified junior tasks to software ("Canaries in the Coal Mine"; see [[AI labor displacement]] for the study detail).

## Caveats (Brynjolfsson's own)

- Productivity metrics are volatile; more periods of sustained growth are needed to confirm a durable trend.
- Macro headwinds — geopolitical trade wars, fiscal or monetary mismanagement — could swamp the efficiency gains.
- Deployment remains narrow: many businesses still use AI for translation/summarisation rather than workflow automation, so the measured gains may front-run the adoption depth.

## How this connects

- The displacement-side burnout finding (UC Berkeley via Bloomberg, Feb 2026: AI raises output expectations faster than it cuts workload) means the productivity gain is partly pace intensification, not pure efficiency — see [[AI labor displacement]] "The productivity paradox."
- The [[AI capex arms race]] thesis needs exactly this conversion: capex → capability → measured productivity → revenue. The 2.7% print is the first macro datapoint on that bridge.
- Measurement risk: the [[US-Europe productivity paradox]] shows productivity-growth comparisons can flip on methodology choices; single-year US prints inherit the same fragility.
