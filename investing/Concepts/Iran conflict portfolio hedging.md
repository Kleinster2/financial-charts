# Iran conflict portfolio hedging

The traditional bond-equity hedge — the foundation of the 60/40 portfolio — has broken down since [[Operation Epic Fury|the Iran conflict]] began. Government bonds are falling alongside stocks as the [[Strait of Hormuz]] disruption drives oil prices above $90, creating stagflation conditions that make the standard policy response (aggressive rate cuts) unavailable. Fund managers across [[Goldman Sachs]], [[Pictet]], [[Invesco]], [[Aberdeen]], and a half-dozen Asia-based firms are scrambling for alternatives: dollar cash, options overlays, Hormuz-shipped commodities, and selective equity picks in nuclear energy and digital economy names.

---

## Synthesis

The 60/40 model worked for four decades because stocks and bonds were negatively correlated — one went up when the other went down, providing automatic portfolio insurance. That correlation flipped positive during the 2022 Russia-Ukraine energy shock, and it's flipping again now. The mechanism is the same both times: an energy-driven supply shock raises inflation expectations, which pulls bond yields *up* (bond prices down) at the same moment that growth fears pull equities *down*. The Fed can't cut because inflation is rising; can't hold because the economy is weakening. Traditional hedges — Treasuries, inflation-linked bonds, even gold — stop working when the macro regime shifts to stagflation.

What's different from 2022: the dollar positioning. When Russia invaded Ukraine, markets were roughly neutral on the dollar. This time, the market came in short — the deepest net-short dollar positioning since 2020 (see [[Iran conflict FX dynamics]]). So the dollar rally is both a safe-haven bid *and* a massive short squeeze, making it a more attractive tactical hedge than it was in 2022. [[Barclays]] strategist Mitul Kotecha (Mar 12): the pre-war stance was "hedging America" — now "the dollar's back as a safe haven."

The practitioner response clusters into five strategies: (1) non-linear options overlays ([[Goldman Sachs|GSAM]], [[Pictet]]), (2) dollar cash overweight ([[Gama Asset Management]], [[Goldman Sachs]]), (3) Hormuz-bottleneck commodities ([[Invesco]]), (4) selective short vol — selling puts on assets you'd own cheaper ([[SGMC Capital]]), and (5) Asia-focused equity picks leveraging the cheapest hedging costs in a year. The common thread: active, tactical, and idiosyncratic. Nobody's running a textbook anymore.

## The correlation breakdown

Since Feb 27, 2026 (war start), the [[iShares]] Core 60/40 Balanced Allocation ETF has *underperformed* a pure S&P 500 position — the opposite of what a balanced portfolio is supposed to do in a risk-off environment.

![[60-40-vs-spx-iran-war-bloomberg-mar2026.png]]
*60/40 portfolio (black) lagging pure S&P 500 (red) since Iran war began. Normalized as of Feb 27, 2026. Source: Bloomberg (Mar 12, 2026).*

On Thursday Mar 12: 2Y US yields rose ~9bp to their highest since August while the S&P 500 dropped 1.5%. Bonds and stocks sold off together — the classic stagflation signature.

[[Gama Asset Management]] portfolio manager Rajeev de Mello (Mar 13): *"Since correlations have shifted, the obvious rebalancing between equities and bonds, and instruments such as inflation-linked bonds and gold isn't protecting portfolios. The opportunity set for effective risk diversifiers has narrowed materially."*

## Fund manager strategies

### Goldman Sachs — options overlays + cash

[[Goldman Sachs]] Asset Management has reduced portfolio sensitivity with non-linear equity downside protection — options strategies that limit losses during large selloffs — plus credit hedges and increased cash in risk hedging strategies.

Separately, [[Goldman Sachs]] GIR (Christian Mueller-Glissmann, head of asset allocation research, Mar 13): turned tactically neutral on stocks, overweight cash, citing mounting risks the conflict sparks an energy shock comparable to the 1970s.

Specific GS recommendations:
- Selective bearish option spreads
- Calls on the [[Euro Stoxx 50]] Volatility Index
- Puts on European industrials and German equities
- Up-in-quality trades across equity, credit, and currencies
- Allocations to alternatives and dynamic risk allocation

### Invesco — Hormuz commodities

[[Invesco]] recommending buying commodities shipped through the [[Strait of Hormuz]], including aluminum and grains — directly hedging the supply disruption channel rather than betting on the macro outcome.

### Pictet Asset Management

[[Pictet]]'s multi-asset team:
- Cut equities
- Added put options on stocks and corporate bonds
- Increasing dollar exposure

### Gama Asset Management

[[Gama Asset Management]] (Rajeev de Mello, global macro PM):
- Raised dollar cash
- Hedging via equity futures

### SGMC Capital — selling vol

[[SGMC Capital]] (Mohit Mirpuri, partner, Mar 13): *"When volatility spikes sharply, we often look to sell it rather than buy it, for example by selling puts on assets we are comfortable owning at lower levels."* Also maintaining buffers through short-duration high-quality bonds and precious metals (gold and silver).

### Allspring Global Investments

[[Allspring Global Investments]] (Gary Tan, fund manager, Mar 13): *"Traditional hedges aren't attracting the usual safe-haven flows, so we are leaning less on broad cross-asset hedges and more on selective stock picking and targeted equity risk management."* Reduced active risk heading into March by raising cash and rotating into defensives.

### Aberdeen

[[Aberdeen]] (Fesa Wibawa, Singapore-based investment manager, Mar 13): *"It still feels early to reposition aggressively, especially as recent price action has been quite choppy and would have penalized overly decisive changes."* Light adjustments to currency risk using valuation and relative fundamentals as guide, looking through near-term volatility.

### Tokio Marine Asset Management

[[Tokio Marine]] Asset Management (Hironori Akizawa, fund manager): raising cash levels on prolonged Middle East crisis and stagflation probability.

### Areca Capital

[[Areca Capital]] (Danny Wong, CEO): focusing on stocks tied to high dividends and local demand — the classic defensive rotation.

## Dollar as hedge

Unlike 2022, this conflict is catching a market positioned for dollar weakness. The [[Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index]] is near its strongest in two months, and option metrics show traders betting it can rise to its highest since December.

[[Barclays]] strategist Mitul Kotecha (Mar 12, Bloomberg TV): *"When you look at the dollar pre-war, the dominant stance was 'hedging America.' Now, it suddenly looks like the dollar's back as a safe haven."*

Goldman Sachs' Mueller-Glissmann and team: tactically neutral on stocks, overweight cash — citing risk of 1970s-style energy shock.

For dollar-funded investors, Asia hedging costs have fallen to an average of 0.28% for eight major Asian currencies — the lowest in over a year (3-month forward implied yields compiled by Bloomberg).

## 2022 comparison

| Dimension | Russia-Ukraine (Feb 2022) | Iran conflict (Mar 2026) |
|-----------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Oil price impact | Brent +32% first 10 days | Brent +33% first 10 days (similar magnitude) |
| Dollar positioning pre-war | Roughly neutral | Deep net-short (since Dec 2025) |
| Dollar move | Rally on safe-haven bid | Rally = safe-haven + short squeeze |
| Bond-equity correlation | Went positive (correlation breakdown) | Positive again (same pattern) |
| Fed posture | Early tightening cycle (about to hike) | On hold; rate cuts delayed by oil inflation |
| Policy response available | Hikes to fight inflation | Trapped — can't cut (inflation) or hike (weak economy) |
| Hormuz / supply chokepoint | No (Russia pipelines + tankers) | Yes (~20M bbl/day, 20%+ of global supply) |

The Hormuz dimension makes this potentially worse: Russia's oil found alternative buyers; Hormuz closure removes supply entirely.
