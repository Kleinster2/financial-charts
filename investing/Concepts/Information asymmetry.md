**Information asymmetry** — A condition where one party in a transaction possesses more or better information than the counterparty. One of the core market imperfections in financial economics, driving phenomena from adverse selection in insurance to insider trading in equities.

---

## Synopsis

Information asymmetry is the foundational market failure that justifies most securities regulation. When corporate insiders know the true state of a business while public shareholders do not, the insiders can trade on that knowledge at the public's expense. When a used car seller knows the car's defects but the buyer does not, the market collapses toward low-quality goods (Akerlof's "lemons" problem). The entire architecture of financial disclosure — 10-Ks, 10-Qs, earnings calls, material event filings — exists to reduce this gap. [[Regulation Fair Disclosure]] (Reg FD, 2000) was the most direct intervention, prohibiting companies from selectively disclosing material information to analysts or institutional investors before the public.

The concept is gaining fresh relevance in the AI era. Large language models trained on public filings, transcripts, and alternative data are narrowing the information gap between institutional and retail investors — a form of democratized asymmetry reduction. At the same time, firms with proprietary data (satellite imagery, credit card transaction data, web scraping) are creating new asymmetries that did not exist in the traditional disclosure framework. The question for investors is whether AI tools level the playing field or simply shift the asymmetry from who-gets-the-call-first to who-has-the-best-model. Private markets, where disclosure requirements are minimal, represent the purest remaining form of information asymmetry in finance — a structural advantage for insiders that [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026|recent events]] have made painfully visible.
