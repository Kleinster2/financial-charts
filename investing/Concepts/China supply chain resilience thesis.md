# China supply chain resilience thesis

Investment framework articulated by Lisa Hall (Asia equity strategist, [[CITIC CLSA]]) on Bloomberg "The China Show" 4/28/2026: under conditions of supply-side disruption to non-China supply chains (Iran-conflict damage to Gulf refineries, sanctions-driven dislocation, geopolitical fragmentation of strategic resources), [[China]]'s manufacturing complex captures share by virtue of geographic distance from the disruption + production-capacity scale + state-supported R&D continuity. The thesis underpins CLSA's "China Supply Chain Resilience 33-0" thematic portfolio of 30 mainland-listed names targeting the share-capture mechanism.

The empirical anchors at the time of the Apr 28 framing: Q1 2026 China GDP +5% (above consensus); industrial profits Jan-Mar +15%; [[Moody's]] upgraded China's outlook from negative to stable while maintaining the credit rating.

---

## Three subthemes

The thesis decomposes into three subthemes, each with distinct beneficiary structures.

### 1. Clean energy substitution

Other regions' fossil-fuel-dependent capacity may be physically destroyed or politically constrained by ongoing conflict ([[2026 Iran conflict]]); China has spent the past decade building clean-energy generation, transmission, and equipment-manufacturing capacity. The premium per Hall: "Whether we should speed up our clean-energy transition or trying to happen to be the clean-energy power-equipment largest provider in the world" — i.e. China captures both the domestic-substitution benefit (less exposure to Gulf oil shock) and the export-supplier benefit (selling into other countries' accelerated transitions).

Beneficiary structure: [[Solar]] equipment manufacturers, [[Wind]] turbine makers, [[Power grid]] equipment, [[BESS]] (battery energy storage), nuclear-equipment OEMs. Cross-link to [[China solar buildout]].

### 2. Global share realignment (upstream chemicals)

Iran-conflict damage to Gulf-region refineries and petrochemical complexes opens addressable share for Chinese refining and chemicals capacity that physically remained safe. The framing per Hall: "Those refineries were hit in the main in the Gulf regions. But for China, our capacity is still safe. So relatively, I think China for the refineries, they will be able to sell the products to the other markets in Global as well."

Beneficiary structure: Chinese refining names with export licenses, upstream chemicals (ethylene, propylene, naphtha derivatives), specialty chemicals. Cross-link to [[Refining slate rigidity]] for the demand-side mechanism that compounds the share-capture argument.

### 3. Strategic resources (rare earths, fertilizers as bargaining chips)

Existing concept note [[Rare earth leverage]] frames the bargaining-chip dynamic from the export-control side; Hall's portfolio expression is the inverse — long the strategic-resource holders ahead of further export-restriction events. Per Hall: "China used the rare earth as one of the bargaining chip to negotiate with US. Right now for this year, there's because of this war, we see that more and more products were forbidden from export, like for Russia, for China also. So we include some of these strategic metals and fertilizers that China has and how to say a certain global share that China may be able to use this as a bargaining chip."

Beneficiary structure: rare-earth miners and processors, [[Fertilizer]] producers (especially phosphate and potash with Chinese export-license control), [[Tungsten]] / [[Antimony]] / [[Graphite]] producers in China.

## What the thesis does not include

Hall explicitly excluded consumer names from the CLSA portfolio: "We still don't include any consumption names here because we have this concern. I mean, this is also universal for the other markets. If you look at the US stock market, I think people also have some concern about the continuity of the consumption."

The framework concedes the downstream-margin-squeeze risk: "For some downstream manufacturers, I think they might suffer some marginal squeeze at this moment." The portfolio is concentrated upstream where pricing power against rising input costs is structurally easier to maintain.

## Liquidity reading

Hall's read on the China-equity flow environment in the segment included two cross-checks:

- Hedge fund China balance at 48-month high (per CLSA flow data, late April 2026)
- Onshore retail account openings (Shanghai Exchange disclosed) at the second-highest level of the past 12 months in March

The combination — institutional re-rating + retail engagement — is consistent with the broader Asia equity outperformance in April (MSCI EM +14% on the month, North Asia leading) and provides flow tailwinds for the structural thesis even if specific events (Iran de-escalation) reverse the disruption-driven share capture.

## Counter-arguments

- **Anti-globalization reversal exposes export-dependent China.** Hall's response: "Equalization is not that we stop trade with each other. I think it's more like the trading regions become more fractured, like China would seek a very closely trading relationship with Asian countries. So we are seeing more and more our exports are going to Asian rather than directly to the US." The portfolio implicitly bets that intra-Asian trade replaces some lost US-bound flow.
- **Quality concession at Chinese refineries.** Korean naphtha buyers historically preferred Middle East quality grades; the share-capture thesis depends on Chinese refineries hitting the quality-grade specification at competitive cost — which is empirically open through 2026-2027.
- **2-3 quarter CPI pass-through risk.** Hall flagged the pass-through question explicitly: "For the next 2 to 3 quarters, it is very critical for us to look at the CPI. So whether the upstream rising prices able to be passed down to the downstream." The thesis depends on upstream capturing the pricing power; a CPI shock that consumer/downstream channels absorb would compress the upstream-margin advantage.
