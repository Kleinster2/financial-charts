# Iran conflict country responses

National policy responses to the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure and [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]] — emergency energy measures, SPR releases, export bans, sanctions waivers, demand destruction, and diplomatic positioning. The response pattern reveals the architecture of global energy vulnerability: countries with strategic reserves bought time, countries without them improvised, and the poorest countries rationed. Part of [[Iran conflict economic disruption]].

---

## The story

The [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure on February 28, 2026 created a live stress test of every country's energy security infrastructure. Within four weeks, the crisis forced 30+ governments into emergency measures ranging from coordinated multilateral releases to weekly public holidays. The responses clustered into five waves, each more desperate than the last, and the sequencing revealed which parts of the global energy architecture were load-bearing and which were decorative.

The first response came from [[China]], and it was unilateral. On March 5 — one week into the conflict — the [[NDRC]] ordered top refiners to suspend all exports of diesel, gasoline, and kerosene. The ban covered $22B in annual fuel exports, with narrow exemptions for jet fuel on international flights and deliveries to [[Hong Kong]]/[[Macau]]. [[China]] holds the world's spare refining capacity and chose to keep every drop domestic. The decision was rational for Beijing but devastating for its neighbors: [[Australia]], [[Bangladesh]], and the [[Philippines|Philippines]] — all heavily dependent on Chinese refined product imports — saw their supply buffers collapse overnight. [[Nadia Martin Wiggen]] at [[Stellen Capital]] called it "the single most destabilizing action" of the crisis. [[South Korea]] followed within days with a partial diesel export ban, and [[Japan]] canceled planned March exports of gasoline, jet fuel, and diesel to prioritize domestic supply. The beggar-thy-neighbor cascade was instantaneous: each country's self-preservation measure became the next country's supply shock.

The multilateral response came six days after China's export ban. On March 11, the [[IEA]] announced a 400M barrel coordinated release from strategic petroleum reserves ([[Oil|oil]]) — the largest in IEA history, dwarfing the 2022 response to [[Russia]]'s invasion of [[Ukraine]]. 32 member countries participated. The [[United States]] contributed 172M barrels from an SPR already only 58% full (415M of 714M capacity). [[Japan]] independently released 80M barrels on March 16 — the largest Japanese drawdown since the SPR system was established in 1978. Prime Minister [[Sanae Takaichi]] ordered a comprehensive review of the entire oil products supply chain, acknowledging that 95.1% of Japanese crude came from the Middle East, with 73.7% transiting [[Strait of Hormuz|Hormuz]]. [[South Korea]] contributed 22.46M barrels while maintaining 208 days of crude reserves. The [[UK]] released 13.5M barrels, [[Spain]] 11.5M over 90 days. The scale was historic, but the arithmetic was grim: 400M barrels against a ~15M bbl/day supply gap ([[Goldman Sachs]] estimate) buys roughly 27 days — and the US maximum release rate of ~1.5M bbl/day meant even the American contribution would take months to flow.

The third wave was geopolitically the most jarring. By mid-March, with SPR releases proving insufficient and prices still climbing, the [[Trump II|Trump]] administration began issuing sanctions waivers — first for [[Russia]]n crude (allowing [[India]] to purchase pre-March 5 loadings), then on March 20 a 30-day general license permitting purchase of [[Iran]]ian oil already at sea. [[India]] moved fastest: [[Indian Oil]] took ~10M barrels of Russian crude, [[Reliance Industries]] at least as much, and on March 24 Reliance purchased 5M barrels from [[NIOC]] — [[India]]'s first Iranian crude since May 2019. Both Russian and Iranian barrels sold at premiums to [[Brent crude|Brent]], an inversion from their historical discounts. The US subsequently expanded the Russian waiver to [[Japan]] and [[South Korea]]. The spectacle of the [[United States]] lifting sanctions on both the country it was actively bombing and the adversary it had spent years trying to economically strangle — while both sold at above-market prices — is analyzed fully in [[Iran conflict sanctions paradox]].

The fourth wave was demand destruction, and it fell hardest on countries without the fiscal space to subsidize. [[Sri Lanka]] made Wednesday a public holiday every week to reduce fuel consumption. [[Vietnam]] and [[Thailand]] imposed fuel rationing. [[Thailand]] ordered coal plants to full capacity. [[Bangladesh]] boosted coal consumption while multiple fertilizer plants halted. The [[IEA]] itself recommended work-from-home globally on March 20 — an institution designed to coordinate supply responses admitting that demand destruction was now the primary tool. Southeast Asian governments ordered public sector employees to work from home. The parallel to [[Covid-19]] demand destruction was explicit: [[Colby Connelly]] at the [[Middle East Institute|MEI]] noted that downtown businesses and restaurants were taking the same hit as during pandemic-era shutdowns.

[[India]]'s domestic crisis was the most granular case study. The government invoked the [[Essential Commodities Act]], suspended commercial LPG supply entirely, and prioritized household cooking gas. Black market LPG cylinders sold at 2-3x normal price in New Delhi. All 32 ammonia plants were ordered to 70% gas. The [[Reserve Bank of India]] burned through >$20B in FX reserves defending the [[Indian rupee]], which hit a record low of ~93.2/USD. Foreign portfolio investors pulled ~$10B from Indian equities year-to-date, ~$8B in March alone. The LPG shortage cascaded through the restaurant sector (80% of restaurants use LPG), affecting ~1M delivery workers at [[Swiggy]] and [[Zomato]]. See [[India#LPG crisis — consumer impact (Week 3, March 2026)|India LPG crisis]] for the full picture.

[[Australia]] exposed the most acute vulnerability among OECD countries. Fuel cover collapsed from 32 days pre-crisis to ~15 days by week 2 to ~10 days by March 23. Hundreds of petrol stations ran dry. The government released ~20% of strategic reserves (roughly one week's supply) and temporarily lowered diesel standards for six months. Gold mines shut down due to diesel shortages — the mining complex transmitting the energy shock into metals. Australia bought 3 cargoes of gasoline from the [[United States|US]] Gulf Coast, a completely abnormal trade given the 30-day sailing distance, because [[Japan]]'s SPR was releasing crude not products, [[South Korea]] had imposed its export ban, and [[China]] had banned refined product exports entirely. After initially declining to send warships, Australia later joined a 22-nation pledge for eventual shipping corridor operations.

[[South Korea]] activated "Crisis Level 3" protocol — the first fuel price cap in 30 years — and used state funds to subsidize freight costs for tankers taking the longer [[Cape of Good Hope]] route. Korean refiners designated naphtha an "economic security item" given the implications for [[Samsung Electronics|Samsung]] and [[SK Hynix]] semiconductor fabs if LNG-fueled power became unreliable. The country lifted operating caps on coal-fired power plants to compensate for LNG shortages. [[Taiwan]]'s [[Formosa Plastics]] declared force majeure on March 10 as naphtha availability collapsed.

The fifth wave was the emergence of alternative supply architectures. In Africa, [[Kenya]], [[Ghana]], and [[South Africa]] turned to [[Nigeria]]'s [[Dangote Refinery]] — 650,000 bpd capacity — which supplied 456,000 tonnes to 5 African countries. This was significant: Africa contains half of [[OPEC]] members but historically lacked refining capacity, importing ~75% of fuel from the Middle East (East and southern Africa). [[Turkey]] emerged as a strategic energy corridor: the Southern Gas Corridor ([[Azerbaijan]]-Georgia-[[Turkey]]-EU) was the only major overland non-Russian, non-Gulf [[LNG|gas]] route to [[Europe]]. [[Dangote Refinery|Dangote]] became a regional alternative overnight. [[Turkey]] also positioned itself as a diplomatic intermediary — Secretary of State [[Marco Rubio|Rubio]] confirmed intermediary countries were "passing messages." Asian refiners from [[Japan]], [[Thailand]], and [[South Korea]] broke decades-old Gulf term contracts and redirected ships to the US Gulf Coast, West Africa ([[Ghana]]), and the [[North Sea]], with sailing times of 20-30 days versus a few days from the Gulf.

The Gulf states themselves split. [[Saudi Arabia]] was the structural winner: Red Sea coast exports via the [[East-West Pipeline]] and [[Yanbu]] bypass [[Strait of Hormuz|Hormuz]] entirely, and [[Saudi Aramco]] rose 7.6% while [[Dubai Financial Market|Dubai]] fell 17%. [[Robin J. Brooks]] quantified the windfall: Saudi pre-war revenues of ~$150B/yr (7 mb/d at $60 [[Brent crude|Brent]]) versus ~$200B/yr now (~5 mb/d at $110). [[Bahrain]] circulated a UN Security Council draft resolution on behalf of the [[GCC]], with 85+ member states co-sponsoring. [[Oman]] mediated indirect US-[[Iran]] talks, with its Foreign Minister stating peace was "within reach." The [[UAE]] declared willingness on March 18 to join a US-led military effort to secure the Strait — the most forward-leaning Gulf position. But [[Bahrain]]'s Aluminium Bahrain (Alba), the world's largest single-site smelter, announced a phased production shutdown on March 16. The GCC was simultaneously benefiting from higher prices and suffering from the infrastructure damage.

[[Adam Tooze]] (Columbia, [[Foreign Policy]], March 27) added the economic differentiation that the "Gulf states" label obscures. [[Qatar]] is a city-state of 2.5 million inhabitants where 70% of government revenue, 60% of GDP, and 85% of exports come from [[LNG]] — a single-commodity economy. The [[UAE]] is 10 million people, with [[Dubai]] fundamentally a trade, banking, finance, and insurance hub (tourism less than 10% of Dubai's GDP despite European perception) while [[Abu Dhabi]] provides the oil. [[Saudi Arabia]] is 38 million people with a relatively diversified economy — oil and gas represent ~22% of GDP (comparable to [[Texas]] at ~15%) and ~55% of government revenue, qualifying it as a petro-state in the fiscal sense but not the economic-structure sense. [[Oman]] is the most financially fragile — lacking the energy revenues of its neighbors and potentially requiring a bailout.

On [[Dubai]]'s resilience, Tooze pushed back on "built on sand" skepticism: the hub's value is location-based, sitting at a nodal point in global trade where demand for oil and gas is not collapsing anytime soon. "Only in really, really bad scenarios does some form of relatively normalized traffic through the straits not resume at some point." When it does, the hubs come back because the underlying demand is structural. Tooze's greatest concern is for the migrant workers who service these economies — the population most exposed and least protected.

The [[EU]] response was collective and cautious. Energy Commissioner [[Dan Jørgensen]] lowered gas storage targets from 90% to 80% (deadline extended from November 1 to December 1). European leaders refused [[Trump II|Trump]]'s request for naval support in [[Strait of Hormuz|Hormuz]] — Will Kennedy ([[Bloomberg]]): their position was "we did not ask to be in this conflict and the way that you are asking us to be involved is not realistic." Within the EU, the crisis exposed the energy-mix divergence: [[Spain]] (15% gas-set electricity hours, ~€42/MWh) and [[France]] (<15%, ~€30) were insulated by nuclear baseload, while [[Italy]] (89%, ~€125) and [[Germany]] (~40%, ~€90) were hammered. See [[Iran conflict European impact]] for the full analysis.

The pattern across all five waves is consistent: countries with strategic reserves, domestic energy production, or nuclear baseload bought time. Countries without them — [[Pakistan]] (near-100% Qatar LNG dependent), [[Bangladesh]] (~2/3 Qatar LNG), [[Sri Lanka]], [[Australia]] (despite being OECD) — were forced into rationing, emergency diesel standard relaxation, or absurd trade patterns within weeks. The fiscal dimension compounds the vulnerability: [[Indonesia]] spends ~6% of household consumption on oil at $100/bbl versus ~1.5% for the [[United States|US]] and [[UK]]. The countries most exposed are precisely the ones with the thinnest fiscal buffers to subsidize their way through — the poor-country trap that [[Natixis]]' Trinh Nguyen identified.
