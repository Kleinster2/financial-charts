**Metropolitan Statistical Area** is an [[OMB]]-defined geographic unit built around a core urban area of 50,000+ population, plus surrounding counties where 25%+ of workers commute to the core. The MSA is the standard unit for federal economic reporting — [[BLS]] employment data, Census demographics, HUD housing data, and CPI calculations all use MSA boundaries. There are approximately 384 MSAs in the US, and multiple adjacent MSAs with 15%+ commuting interchange form a [[Combined Statistical Area]] (CSA). For investors, MSA-level data is critical for assessing real estate markets, retail density, labor market tightness, and regional economic divergence. The distinction between MSA and CSA matters: the New York-Newark-Jersey City MSA (~20M) is a different unit from the New York-Newark CSA (~23M), and which boundary a data source uses affects comparisons. OMB revises MSA definitions after each decennial Census, with the most recent update in 2023.

## Key characteristics

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Defining body | [[OMB]] (Office of Management and Budget) |
| Core threshold | 50,000+ urban population |
| County inclusion rule | 25%+ commuting to core |
| Count | ~384 MSAs in the US |
| Revision cycle | After each decennial Census |
| Latest revision | 2023 (based on 2020 Census) |
| Smaller counterpart | Micropolitan Statistical Area (10,000-49,999 core) |
