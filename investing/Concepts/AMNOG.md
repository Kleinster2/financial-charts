# AMNOG

**AMNOG** (*Arzneimittelmarktneuordnungsgesetz*, "Medicines Market Reorganization Act," 2011) — [[Germany]]'s statutory framework for pricing patented pharmaceuticals under the GKV (statutory health insurance) system. The centerpiece of Germany's reference-pricing regime and the principal mechanism US trade policy now frames as "persistent underpayment" for innovative drugs (see [[Pharma R&D burden-sharing]]).

## Synopsis

AMNOG requires an early-benefit assessment of every new patented drug entering the German reimbursement market. The G-BA (Federal Joint Committee) commissions an IQWiG dossier review comparing the new drug to the appropriate comparator therapy. If the drug shows no *added benefit*, it is grouped into a reference-price cluster and reimbursed at the cluster benchmark. If it shows *added benefit*, the manufacturer negotiates a reimbursement price with the GKV-Spitzenverband (the statutory insurers' association); if negotiations fail within a set window, a binding arbitration chamber sets the price. A 12-month temporary price holds during the process.

The economic effect is that Germany pays less than list for most patented drugs, either through reference clustering or through negotiated rebates. AMNOG is the most-cited example in the global drug-pricing burden-sharing debate because it is transparent, statutory, and applies to the largest economy in the EU.

## The 2026 reform (trigger for the US Section 301 probe)

The [[Germany|German]] Ministry of Health unveiled draft legislation in April 2026 to overhaul statutory healthcare financing and close a looming ~€20B ($23B) funding gap. The pharma-relevant provisions, per the USTR Federal Register notice opening the [[US Section 301 investigation of Germany drug pricing (2026)|Section 301 investigation]]:

| Provision | Detail |
|---|---|
| Additional mandatory rebate — H1 2027 | 3.5% on patented medicines, on top of existing price cuts |
| Variable ("dynamic") rebate — from H2 2027 | Tied to actual drug spend vs target health-insurance outlays |
| Projected dynamic rebate by 2030 | ~20% (estimate cited in the USTR notice) |
| Original proposal | Variable discounts — withdrawn after pharma-industry opposition, replaced by the rebate structure above |

USTR flagged two specific instruments for investigation: supplemental discounts in exchange for confidentiality of negotiated prices, and mandatory variable-rate rebates.

## Why it matters

AMNOG is the load-bearing precedent for the broader [[Pharma R&D burden-sharing|burden-sharing]] fight. If the US succeeds in using [[Section 301]] trade law to force a rewrite of an ally's domestic reimbursement regime, the same template applies to NICE (UK), PBAC (Australia), PMPRB (Canada), and any other reference-pricing system. The [[US-UK pharma pricing deal (2026)|US-UK bilateral deal]] (Apr 2026) is the negotiated alternative the US is offering Germany: higher foreign prices via the national health service, in exchange for a tariff exemption.
