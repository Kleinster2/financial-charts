Gulf pipeline bypass planning 2026 — [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure pushing Gulf states from hypothetical to active planning for new pipeline routes to bypass [[Iran]]ian chokepoint control. Shift accelerated by the [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]], which stress-tested existing bypass infrastructure ([[East-West Pipeline]], [[Habshan-Fujairah Pipeline]]) and exposed total capacity shortfall.

---

## Synthesis

The 2026 Hormuz closure converted four decades of "what if" planning into engineering conversations. Existing bypass capacity (~4.5-6 mb/d) covers only a quarter of normal strait flows (~20 mb/d). The near-term fix is expanding what exists — the [[East-West Pipeline]] and [[Habshan-Fujairah Pipeline]] — but the structural response being discussed is a multi-corridor network that would reshape Gulf energy geography. The binding constraints are not engineering but political: multi-country pipelines require sovereignty compromises that Gulf states have historically avoided, and every terminus carries its own vulnerability ([[Fujairah]] bombardment, [[Salalah]] drone attacks, Red Sea [[Houthis|Houthi]] threat for [[Yanbu]]). The [[IMEC]] revival and Mediterranean pipeline advocacy from [[Israel]] add a geopolitical layer — these routes would formalize energy alliances that predate the conflict but gain urgency from it. The key question is whether the post-conflict mood sustains long enough to justify $5-20B commitments that take years to build.

## FT report — "shift from hypotheticals into operational reality" (Apr 2, 2026)

![[gulf-pipeline-bypass-routes-ft-apr2.jpg]]
*Proposed and existing pipeline bypass routes. Source: FT (Apr 2, 2026)*

[[Maisoon Kafafy]] (senior adviser, [[Atlantic Council]] Middle East programmes): *"I'm sensing a shift from hypotheticals into operational reality. Everyone is looking at the same map and they are drawing the same conclusions."*

The most resilient option is "not a single alternative pipeline but rather a network, a web of corridors" — but also the hardest to achieve. Kafafy: *"The conversations have moved further along the chain. I do not expect [the status quo] to return to where it was pre-conflict."*

### Existing bypass capacity vs. need

| Route | Capacity | Status (Apr 2026) |
|-------|----------|--------------------|
| [[East-West Pipeline]] (Saudi) | 7 mb/d pipeline; ~3-4.6 mb/d port loading | Operational — "main route" per [[Amin Nasser]] |
| [[Habshan-Fujairah Pipeline]] ([[UAE]]) | 1.5 mb/d | Operational but [[Fujairah]] under bombardment |
| [[IPSA Pipeline]] ([[Iraq]]-Saudi) | 1.65 mb/d | Defunct since 1990, needs rehabilitation |
| Normal Hormuz flow | ~20 mb/d | Blocked since Feb 28 |
| Total bypass (operational) | ~4.5-6 mb/d | ~25-30% of normal Hormuz flow |

### Near-term options (most viable)

1. Expand [[East-West Pipeline]] capacity — [[Saudi Aramco]] examining whether to expand further or build new routes. [[Amin Nasser]] told analysts (late March): the pipeline is the *"main route that we are capitalising on right now."*
2. Expand [[Habshan-Fujairah Pipeline]] — [[Abu Dhabi]] has "always had a plan B for a second pipeline to [[Fujairah]]" (senior energy executive). No decisions until long-term Hormuz status clarifies. [[ADNOC]] declined to comment.
3. New [[Saudi Arabia]] Red Sea terminals — including the deepwater port being built for the [[NEOM]] project. Christopher Bush ([[Cat Group]]): *"I am sure they are looking at it as a possibility."*

### Medium-term options (under discussion)

| Route | Estimated cost | Challenges |
|-------|---------------|------------|
| East-West Pipeline replication | ~$5B (Bush estimate) | Blasting through Hijaz basalt, 1,200 km |
| Iraq through [[Jordan]] to Red Sea | $15-20B | Unexploded bombs, [[ISIS]] presence, political complexity |
| Iraq through [[Syria]] to Mediterranean | $15-20B | Security, governance uncertainty |
| Iraq through [[Turkey]] to Ceyhan | $15-20B | Front-end engineering studies exist; Kurdish security corridor |
| Southern route to [[Oman]] ports | Uncosted | Desert + hard-rock mountains; [[Salalah]] attacked by drones (Apr 2026) |

Christopher Bush ([[Cat Group]] CEO — the private Lebanese company that helped build the original East-West Pipeline in the 1980s): *"We have had enquiries about various different pipelines. I have multiple different presentations on my desk."*

Bush on obstacles: *"It has been looked at. There are even front-end engineering studies for [such routes from] Iraq. There is an opportunity that has been discussed."* Security risks include "a lot" of unexploded bombs in [[Iraq]] and continuing [[ISIS]] or militant presence. Political challenge: who operates the pipeline and controls the flow. A network would require Gulf countries *"to abandon their individualist policies and combine. It was always deemed cheaper and safer to bring a ship, load a ship and sail a ship."*

### IMEC corridor revival

One option is revival of US-led [[IMEC]] (India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor) plans — a Gulf official confirmed this is under discussion. Part of the original project included a politically complex pipeline to the Israeli port of [[Haifa]].

[[Yossi Abu]] ([[NewMed Energy]] CEO): *"People need to control their own destinies, with their friends. You need oil pipelines, railway connectivity, throughout the region, onshore, without giving others bottlenecks to choke us."* Confident that pipelines to the Mediterranean will be built, whether terminating at Israeli or Egyptian ports.

### Longer-term strategic picture

Any new pipelines would form part of broader trade corridors for goods beyond oil and gas. The [[UK]] is leading talks between 35 countries to form a coalition to reopen the strait — but structural bypass planning proceeds regardless.

A senior Gulf energy executive on the East-West Pipeline: *"In hindsight the East-West pipeline looks like a genius masterstroke."*

*Source: FT (Malcolm Moore, Mehul Srivastava, Abigail Hauslohner), April 2, 2026*

## Historical construction context — East-West Petroline (1980s)

The original [[East-West Pipeline]] was built by the [[Cat Group]]/Houston Contracting joint venture under contract with Petroline (a division of [[Saudi Aramco|Petromin]]). CAT Houston built the western section; another contractor built the eastern section. Capital outlay: $35M for equipment and workers' camps. 1,200 workers employed. 48-inch diameter pipe, 24m sections brought from [[Yanbu]]. Construction required blasting through the Hijaz mountains — nicknamed "the gun site." Pipes laid in tension to prevent buckling from 80 degrees Celsius crude. All welds 100% x-rayed. Yanbu tank farm holding capacity: 11M barrels.

The December 1980 Mideast Report heralded the planned pipeline, expected to cost $495M, saying it provided an alternative to the *"strategic yet vulnerable [[Strait of Hormuz]], which could eventually come under Iranian guns."* Forty-five years later, that scenario materialized.

*Source: PeriscopeFilm / YouTube (1980s construction documentary, resurfaced March 16, 2026)*
