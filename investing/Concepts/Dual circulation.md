# Dual circulation

China's economic strategy framework articulated by [[Xi Jinping]] in May 2020 — "双循环" (*shuāng xúnhuán*) — that calls for "domestic circulation" (domestic production-consumption loops) as the primary engine of growth, with "international circulation" (exports + foreign investment) as a complementary outer loop. The framework was the strategic response to three converging pressures: US-China trade war and decoupling (since 2018), the Covid-era export disruption (2020), and the longer-term demographic-and-debt constraints on the export-and-investment-led growth model. Dual circulation is the canonical articulation of "China rebalances toward domestic demand" — a policy goal stated repeatedly since 2008 but operationally elusive.

The structural significance for the 2026 vault thread: dual circulation is the framework lens for understanding why every other major Chinese policy of the 2020s — [[Common Prosperity]], [[Three Red Lines]], [[Made in China 2025]], [[Fortress China]] — is internally consistent. They are all instruments for shifting the growth model toward domestic consumption + indigenous technology + reduced external vulnerability.

---

## The framing

The two circulations operate at different priority levels:

| Circulation | Components | Priority |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Inner (domestic) | Household consumption, domestic services, indigenous innovation, regional rebalancing | Primary engine |
| Outer (international) | Exports, foreign investment, [[Belt and Road]], technology imports | Complementary loop |

The framework's strategic intent (per official commentary 2020-2024):

1. Make domestic demand the primary growth driver — reduce dependence on exports
2. Build self-sufficient supply chains for critical inputs — reduce dependence on imports
3. Concentrate technology development domestically — reduce exposure to Western export controls
4. Maintain external openness where it serves the domestic objectives — selective rather than full integration

The "self-reinforcing" version of dual circulation is the [[Fortress China]] defensive posture; the "outward-facing" version is the [[Belt and Road]] + selective trade agreements (e.g., [[RCEP]], application to join [[CPTPP]]).

## Operationalisation

Dual circulation is more strategic than operational — there's no specific budget line or implementing agency. Instead, it functions as the framing for many other policies (per general policy analysis 2020-2026):

| Other policy | How it serves dual circulation |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| [[Common Prosperity]] | Boosts domestic consumption by raising household income share + reducing inequality |
| [[Three Red Lines]] | Redirects capital from property speculation toward productive investment |
| [[Made in China 2025]] | Builds domestic technology capacity to substitute for Western imports |
| [[Fortress China]] | Insulates from external shocks; preserves domestic supply during stress |
| [[Hukou]] reform (proposed) | Expands urban labour pool + consumption base |
| [[China platform labor regulation]] | Stabilises gig-worker incomes; supports consumption |
| [[China semiconductor strategy]] | Reduces dependence on Western chip imports |
| Private Economy Promotion Law (2025) | Stabilises private-sector investment; supports innovation channel |
| Anti-corruption + financial-sector tightening | Channels savings toward productive uses rather than speculation |

The pattern: every major Chinese economic policy of the 2020s can be read as one piece of the dual-circulation puzzle, with different policies addressing different vulnerabilities in the model.

---

## Why it has been operationally elusive

The "domestic demand as primary engine" goal has been stated by Chinese policymakers since at least 2008, with limited operational success. Three structural reasons (per [[Michael Pettis]] and broader analyst consensus):

1. Income distribution — household income share of GDP is among the lowest in major economies (~43%), constraining consumption. Raising it requires explicit fiscal redistribution that has been politically difficult
2. Property sector role — property was historically the channel through which household savings recycled into investment. The [[Three Red Lines]] correction broke this channel without yet replacing it
3. Social safety net — without expanded healthcare, pension, and unemployment insurance, households save precautionarily rather than consume. Hukou-linked service fragmentation makes this worse

[[Li Shi]]'s May 2026 reframing of [[Common Prosperity]] as "investing in people" via [[Hukou]] reform, public childcare, and education access is in part an attempt to address these constraints. Whether it succeeds determines whether dual circulation's "domestic-engine" goal becomes operational or remains aspirational (inference).

## Investment-thread implications

Three structural reads (inference):

1. Chinese consumption-sensitive sectors have a structural tailwind if dual circulation's domestic-demand goal becomes operational — particularly post-property-era. Watch for income-share shifts in NBS data
2. Indigenous-tech sectors are a direct beneficiary regardless of consumption dynamics — the technology-substitution channel of dual circulation is the more reliably executed pillar (visible in [[China semiconductor strategy]], [[Cambricon]], [[Loongson]], etc.)
3. Western firms with Chinese revenue exposure are bracketed by dual circulation as "international circulation" partners — useful when convenient, dispensable when contested. Treat China revenue as structurally lower-quality than domestic-equivalent revenue
