Cobalt — Critical battery metal and aerospace/defense input. [[DRC]] accounts for >70% of global supply, creating extreme geographic concentration risk. Used in lithium-ion cathodes (NMC chemistry), superalloys for jet engines, and cemented carbides for cutting tools. Benchmark price ~$25/lb as of March 2026, up 160%+ from pre-restriction levels.

---

## Synthesis

The cobalt market has undergone a structural regime change since February 2025. [[DRC]]'s export ban — first a blanket ban, then replaced by strict quotas from October 2025 — pushed the market into an 82,000-tonne deficit in 2025 and [[Darton Commodities]] projects shortages persisting through 2030. This is the most significant supply disruption in cobalt's history, and it reveals a fundamental vulnerability: a single country controlling 70%+ of a critical mineral supply can unilaterally reshape the global market with a policy decision.

The price signal is unambiguous — benchmark cobalt up 160%+, hydroxide up 400%+ — but the second-order effects are more interesting. [[China]]'s cobalt refineries (which process ~80% of the world's cobalt) are running on depleting pre-ban stockpiles, with the first new DRC material expected in China only by May/June 2026. Indonesia's MHP production (a form of nickel that contains cobalt) is growing as an alternative source, but faces sulfur supply, ore availability, and environmental risks. Most critically, the export curbs are accelerating material substitution and product diversification efforts that could structurally reduce cobalt demand in certain end-use markets — the classic resource curse dynamic where supply disruption incentivizes demand destruction.

For the battery supply chain, this validates the shift toward [[LFP batteries]] (cobalt-free) and high-nickel/low-cobalt NMC chemistries. [[CATL]] and [[BYD]]'s dominance in LFP is partly a hedge against exactly this kind of supply disruption. For defense and aerospace (where cobalt in superalloys has no easy substitute), the situation is more acute — [[Project Vault]] and strategic stockpile programs gain urgency.

## DRC export restrictions (2025-2026)

The defining event for cobalt markets in 2025-2026.

| Date | Action |
|------|--------|
| Feb 2025 | [[DRC]] imposes blanket cobalt export ban |
| Oct 2025 | Ban replaced by strict export quotas |
| Late 2025-early 2026 | Quotas in place but exports delayed by procedural implementation |
| Expected May/Jun 2026 | First DRC raw material arrivals in [[China]] |

![[cobalt-price-chart-bloomberg-2025-2026.png]]
*Cobalt benchmark prices ($/lb). Source: Fastmarkets via Bloomberg, March 2026. Three distinct price plateaus visible: the initial ban spike (Feb 2025, ~$8 to ~$15), the quota-regime step-up (Oct 2025, ~$15 to ~$20), and the supply-depletion grind higher (Jan 2026, ~$20 to ~$25).*

[[Darton Commodities]] (Mar 2026 report): "The fallout of DRC's export restrictions have highlighted the vulnerability of the cobalt supply chain, prompting increased investment in product diversification and material substitution. These are developments that can ultimately dampen demand growth in certain end-use markets."

Darton projects deficits persisting every year through 2030. Pre-ban surplus inventories are being "structurally depleted." DRC may ease quotas in 2026 to balance revenue maximization against demand destruction risk.

## Demand structure

| End use | Share | Trend |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Batteries (EV + consumer electronics) | ~70% | Growing but LFP substitution accelerating |
| Superalloys (aerospace, defense) | ~15% | Growing, no substitute |
| Cemented carbides (cutting tools) | ~5% | Stable |
| Catalysts, pigments, other | ~10% | Stable |

The battery segment is the swing factor. [[LFP batteries]] (lithium iron phosphate) use zero cobalt and now dominate Chinese EV production. High-nickel NMC chemistries (NMC 811, NMC 9.5.5) reduce cobalt content per cell. But premium EVs, consumer electronics, and energy storage systems still rely on cobalt-containing cathodes for energy density.

## Substitution dynamics

The DRC export restrictions are accelerating substitution across all end markets:

| Approach | Status | Impact on cobalt demand |
|----------|--------|------------------------|
| [[LFP batteries]] | Dominant in China EVs, growing globally | Structural demand reduction |
| High-nickel NMC (811, 9.5.5) | Deployed in premium EVs | Reduces cobalt per cell by 60-70% vs NMC 111 |
| [[Sodium-ion batteries]] | Early commercialization | Zero cobalt, targeting low-cost EVs and storage |
| Cobalt-free high-nickel cathodes | R&D stage | Would eliminate battery cobalt demand entirely |
| Recycling | Scaling ([[Redwood Materials]], Li-Cycle) | Supplements primary supply |

Darton Commodities flagged that the export curbs are prompting "increased investment in product diversification and material substitution" that "can ultimately dampen demand growth in certain end-use markets." This is the classic resource nationalism trap: restriction boosts short-term price but accelerates long-term demand erosion.
