Insider trading is the illegal practice of trading securities based on material nonpublic information (MNPI), enforced primarily by the [[SEC]] under Rule 10b-5 and carrying both civil and criminal penalties.

## Synopsis

The legal framework is deceptively simple: anyone who trades on material information not available to the public violates securities law. In practice, enforcement is complex because the law has evolved through case law rather than statute — there is no single "insider trading" statute in the Securities Exchange Act. The "classical theory" (Chiarella v. United States, 1980) covers corporate insiders (officers, directors, employees). The "misappropriation theory" (United States v. O'Hagan, 1997) extends liability to outsiders who steal information from a duty-holding source — lawyers, bankers, consultants, even spouses. The Preet Bharara-era crackdown (2009-2015) used wiretaps for the first time in financial fraud cases, securing 85+ convictions including Raj Rajaratnam ([[Galleon Group]], $63.8M in illicit profits, 11-year sentence) and SAC Capital ([[Point72]]'s predecessor, $1.8B in penalties).

Insider trading is distinct from legal insider transactions — corporate officers routinely buy and sell their own company's stock, disclosed via [[SEC]] Form 4 filings within two business days. These legal transactions are tracked as [[insider buying]] signals (a cluster of officer purchases often precedes positive catalysts). The gray area is [[Regulation Fair Disclosure|Reg FD]] (enacted Oct 2000), which closed the selective disclosure loophole: pre-Reg FD, companies could legally share earnings guidance with favored analysts before public release, giving institutional investors a structural information edge. The current frontier of enforcement is "shadow trading" — trading in correlated securities (e.g., buying a competitor's stock after learning of a merger in your own company), which the SEC has begun prosecuting since United States v. Panuwat (2022). For investors, the concept matters both as a risk (compliance programs, information barriers) and a signal (patterns in Form 4 filings, unusual options activity flagged by FINRA).

## Key legal framework

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Primary rule | SEC Rule 10b-5 (Securities Exchange Act) |
| Classical theory | Corporate insiders owe duty to shareholders |
| Misappropriation theory | Outsiders who breach duty to information source |
| Disclosure | Form 4 (legal insider trades, filed within 2 days) |
| Reg FD | Bans selective disclosure (Oct 2000) |
| Criminal penalties | Up to 20 years, $5M individual / $25M entity |
| Civil penalties | Up to 3x profits gained or losses avoided |
