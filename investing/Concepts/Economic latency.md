# Economic latency

A framework for economic security that borrows from nuclear strategy: latent capability is the capacity to surge production of a critical input under coercion or crisis, without paying the full cost of permanent onshoring in peacetime. Proposed in concrete policy form by [[Guy Ward-Jackson]] ([[Tony Blair Institute]]) in a May 15, 2026 [[ChinaTalk]] column as a US "Economic Security Latency Fund" — a $20-30bn programme covering 5-10 bottleneck capabilities through contingent production contracts, modular standby facilities, and surge-ready workforce and stockpile investments.

The framework sits between two failure modes of current US industrial policy: the high-cost permanent reshoring of strategically essential capabilities (the [[CHIPS Act]] model) and the negative-only toolkit of sanctions, tariffs, and [[Export controls]] that imposes pain on others without building the target's own resilience. Latency is the middle path — pre-positioned readiness for inputs that don't justify a fab-scale investment but where a 6-month outage would be coercion-grade damaging.

It is the defensive counterpart to [[Weaponized interdependence]]: same network-topology unit of analysis, opposite policy stance. Where Farrell-Newman explain how a state controlling a hub can coerce others by denying access, economic latency explains how a target state can make itself credibly hard to coerce without going to full autarky. The two frameworks together describe the offense-defense dyad of 2020s economic statecraft.

## The nuclear-latency origin

In nuclear strategy, "latency" means the capacity to build a weapon quickly without actually having one — sufficient enrichment capacity, design knowledge, and delivery vehicles to be weeks or months from deployment if the political decision is made (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15). Japan is the canonical case; Iran has been read through this lens for two decades. The deterrence value comes from the *option* to weaponize, not the weapon itself.

Ward-Jackson translates this into economic terms: "the notion not of having a bomb, but of having the capacity to build it in a coercive or crisis context" (per ChinaTalk May 15). Applied to industrial policy, latency means preserving surge-production capability for critical inputs without bearing the full ongoing cost of domestic production. The strategic logic he proposes: "Economic coercion works when it can impose politically salient pain faster than the target can adapt" — and so the response is to compress the time-to-adapt without paying for permanent capacity.

## Three-tier economic-security hierarchy

The framework organises economic-security policy into three tiers, each with a different appropriate instrument (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15).

| Tier | Definition | Examples | Appropriate instrument |
|------|------------|----------|------------------------|
| Tier 1: Strategic Autonomy | Existential capabilities requiring permanent domestic control | Semiconductor fabrication, defense platforms, nuclear fuel cycle | Large-scale permanent investment ([[CHIPS Act]] model) |
| Tier 2: Latent Capability | Concentrated supply-chain nodes where disruption is highly damaging, substitutes exist in principle but cannot be activated quickly, and full onshoring is economically inefficient | Subsea cable repair, specialty chemicals, certain pharmaceutical APIs, critical-mineral processing for non-strategic uses | Contingent latency contracts, surge capacity |
| Tier 3: Market Resilience | Goods where diversification, substitution, and inventories provide adequate resilience without subsidy | Most consumer goods, commoditised industrial inputs | Market mechanisms; no intervention required |

The middle tier is the one the framework targets. Tier 1 is well-served by existing reshoring policy; Tier 3 doesn't need help. Tier 2 is where current US tools are absent, and where the cost-effectiveness argument for a latency approach is strongest (inference — this is the framework's central claim about its own value-add).

---

## Tier 2 qualification — the three tests

To qualify for Latency Fund support, a capability must pass three tests (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15):

1. Low substitutability — few alternative suppliers, years-long replacement timelines
2. High exposure — US imports far exceed exports; replacement ratio (exports/imports) well below 1.0
3. Strategic value — dual-use inputs or systematically important across sectors

Quantitative thresholds proposed:

- Import concentration from a single source above 70% indicates high vulnerability
- Replacement ratio (exports/imports) below ~0.3 signals near-absent existing latent capacity
- A "coercion-relevant" duration test — capabilities where 6-12 months of denial would cause politically salient pain

These thresholds are screening filters, not hard cutoffs (inference — Ward-Jackson presents them as proposed benchmarks rather than statutory criteria).

## Operating mechanisms — the Latency Fund design

The proposed fund operates through a portfolio of instruments rather than direct production subsidies (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15):

| Mechanism | What it pays for | Activation profile |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|
| Contingent latency contracts | Standby production lines, tooling, workforce capacity, raw-material stockpiles maintained by private firms | Scalable on government request within agreed timelines |
| Modular scalable facilities | Capital investment in factories designed for surge ramp-up rather than steady-state output | Ramp activation when triggers met |
| R&D grants | Uncertain-return industrial process work, particularly for non-existing alternatives | Continuous |
| Talent pipelines | Specialized workforce training that wouldn't survive on commercial demand alone | Continuous |
| Fast-track regulation | Pre-cleared permitting, environmental review, and contracting paths for surge activation | Pre-positioned legal architecture |

The contingent contract is the most novel of these. It pays firms a retainer to maintain capability that would otherwise be uneconomic, in exchange for the government's right to activate that capability within a defined response window. The model is analogous to military reservist contracts — paid for readiness, deployed only on call (inference — the comparison to reservist contracts is mine, not Ward-Jackson's explicit framing, but it's the closest existing parallel).

---

## Activation triggers and thresholds

The framework includes pre-defined activation conditions to make latent capability credible to potential coercers (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15):

- Duration-based: "if access cut for 6+ months, activate surge"
- Concentration-based: "if single-source dependence exceeds 80%, invest to reduce"
- Coercive-act-based: "if subjected to specified coercive acts, ramp up within timeframe X"

The deterrence value depends on these triggers being public and credible. If a potential coercer believes the trigger will be met and the surge will close the vulnerability window, the coercion attempt loses its expected payoff. Latent capability without a public commitment device is just an industrial-policy expenditure with no deterrent function (inference — Ward-Jackson implies but doesn't formalise this credibility constraint).

## How it differs from current US tools

| Current US tool | Mechanism | What it does | What latency adds |
|-----------------|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Sanctions, tariffs | Negative — imposes costs on others | Punish coercion ex post; deter ex ante via threat | Doesn't build the target's own ability to absorb retaliation or substitute denied inputs |
| [[Export controls]] | Negative — denies others access to US-controlled hubs | Slows adversary advancement in specific technologies | Same — doesn't address inbound vulnerabilities |
| [[CHIPS Act]]-style reshoring | Positive — permanent onshoring of strategic capability | Builds Tier 1 domestic capacity | Fiscally and politically unsustainable beyond existential sectors; risks misallocated capital, political capture |
| Blanket industrial policy | Positive — broad sectoral subsidy | Captures political constituency | Diffuse, slow, and difficult to target Tier 2 bottlenecks specifically |
| Economic latency | Pre-positioned readiness | Compresses time-to-substitute under coercion | Calibrated cost — pays for surge option, not permanent capacity |

The framework's policy claim is that current US tools cover Tier 1 (reshoring) and the deterrence side of statecraft (sanctions/controls), but leave Tier 2 entirely unaddressed — and that Tier 2 is where most coercion-relevant vulnerabilities actually sit (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15).

## Illustrative case — submarine cable repair

The framework's worked example is subsea cable repair infrastructure (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15):

- Cables carry "roughly 99% by most estimates" of international data traffic — the dependency is near-total
- The vulnerability isn't cable installation but repair timelines — the global repair fleet is "small and ageing"
- Private operators optimize for commercial uptime and routine maintenance, not for crisis surge after deliberate sabotage
- Extended repair delays in a crisis make cable-cutting an attractive coercive instrument; visible latent repair capacity deters the underlying attack

Proposed latency interventions:

- Co-financing of additional repair vessels held as surge capacity
- Stockpiles of spare cable, landing-station equipment, and replacement components at major US coastal hubs
- Workforce pipelines for specialized splice and survey crews

This addresses a market failure visible in the data: subsea-cable repair is genuinely uneconomic at peacetime scale, but the cost of a multi-month outage in crisis would be coercion-grade. The latency approach pays for the option to repair quickly, rather than for the repair capacity itself (the [[Submarine Cables]] cluster tracks the underlying infrastructure; this concept tracks the policy lens).

The case is doing structural work for the framework: it's the cleanest illustration of why permanent onshoring is wrong for the problem (the US doesn't need to manufacture all cable; it needs to be able to fix it fast) and why pure market mechanisms are wrong (private operators won't carry surge capacity that earns nothing in peacetime).

## China's parallel — 自主可控

China has been executing the latency playbook for nearly a decade through its 自主可控 ("autonomous and controllable") doctrine, per commenter Yuzu Xu in the same ChinaTalk piece. The Chinese version is more aggressive in scope and execution than the proposed US latency fund, and it operates through state-directed channels rather than contingent-contract mechanisms.

| Chinese mechanism | Target | Comment |
|-------------------|--------|---------|
| Domestic CPU alternatives — Loongson, Phytium, [[Huawei]] Kunpeng | Reduce dependency on x86 / ARM Western architectures | Operational since mid-2010s |
| Domestic AI accelerators — [[Huawei]] Ascend, [[Cambricon]], [[Biren Tech]] | Substitute [[NVIDIA]] H100 / Blackwell families | Capacity ramping under [[China semiconductor strategy]] |
| Domestic DRAM — [[CXMT]] | Substitute [[SK Hynix]] / [[Samsung]] / [[Micron]] | Reached competitive specs 2024-2025 |
| April 2026 supply-chain security regulations | Inter-ministerial review of foreign dependencies | Operationalises 自主可控 as enforcement architecture (see [[China retaliatory toolkit]]) |

China distinguishes 补链 ("fill chain gaps") from 强链 ("strengthen the chain"). The first is closer to the latency framework — building domestic alternatives that don't yet exist. The second is closer to permanent industrial policy — making existing domestic capacity globally competitive. The April 2026 regulations operate on both dimensions simultaneously (per Yuzu Xu, ChinaTalk May 15).

The structural comparison: the US framework proposes Tier 2 surge readiness through paid private capacity; China achieves the same outcome through state-directed SOE buildout and regulatory enforcement. Both are responses to the same underlying problem (network-topology coercion under [[Weaponized interdependence]] dynamics), but they differ on the institutional architecture used to deliver readiness (inference — the comparative-statecraft framing is mine, building on the Yuzu Xu commentary).

## Relationship to adjacent frames

The economic-statecraft literature now has a small set of competing or complementary frames. Economic latency slots in as the defensive policy concept that has been missing from the US side:

| Frame | Authors / origin | Stance | Mechanism | Economic latency's relationship |
|-------|------------------|--------|-----------|----------------------------------|
| [[Weaponized interdependence]] | Farrell + Newman (2019, 2023) | Offensive | Hub control denies access to coerce | Latency is the defensive counterpart — credibly hard-to-coerce target |
| [[Fortress China]] | Analyst shorthand → mainstream after [[FT]] April 2026 | Defensive (China) | Strategic reserves + dual sourcing + retaliatory leverage + SOE buffers | Latency is the calibrated, non-authoritarian counterpart for a market-economy state |
| [[Second China shock]] | Autor + Hanson + others | Diagnostic of aggregate trade flows | Sectoral overcapacity hits Western labor markets | Operates on different unit of analysis (aggregate vs. node); the two frames don't conflict |
| [[Export controls]] | US Commerce / Entity List architecture | Offensive (US) | Denial of access to US-controlled technology stack | Latency is the inbound-defense response that current export-control policy doesn't supply |
| [[Rare earth leverage]] | China — operational since 2010 | Offensive (China) | Concentration of refining capacity | Concrete case of Tier 2 vulnerability the US has not addressed via latency mechanisms |

The point of the table is that latency is not a substitute for any of these frames — it's the policy concept that closes a specific gap on the defensive side of the dyad. A US economic-statecraft posture in the late 2020s plausibly needs all of: export controls (offensive), latency fund (defensive Tier 2), and selective reshoring (defensive Tier 1).

## Synthesis

The framework's contribution is to name a category of economic-security spending that the current US toolkit has no instrument for: pre-positioned readiness for inputs that don't justify Tier-1 onshoring but where Tier-3 market resilience is inadequate (see #Three-tier economic-security hierarchy). That gap is the operational claim — and it is testable against any given supply chain, not just rhetorical.

Three structural features make latency analytically distinct from adjacent concepts. First, it is deterrence-by-readiness rather than deterrence-by-denial: the [[Export controls]] and sanctions architecture works by closing access to others; the latency fund works by making the target credibly hard to coerce (see #How it differs from current US tools). Second, it operates on the time dimension rather than the volume dimension: traditional industrial policy asks "how much capacity should the US have" while latency asks "how fast can the US ramp capacity if it loses access" (per Ward-Jackson, ChinaTalk May 15). Third, it is calibrated rather than total: $20-30bn / 5-10 capabilities sized to fill specific Tier 2 gaps rather than the [[CHIPS Act]]'s sectoral-reshoring scale (see #Operating mechanisms — the Latency Fund design).

The load-bearing assumption is credibility of activation. Latent capability is worth the retainer only if potential coercers believe the surge will close the vulnerability window faster than their coercion can extract concessions (inference — Ward-Jackson implies but doesn't formalise this as a credibility constraint). Without a public commitment device — pre-defined triggers, statutory activation language, or precedent of the fund being used — latency degrades into a subsidy without a deterrent function (inference). This is the framework's most exposed point.

The comparative-statecraft synthesis is that China has been executing the latency playbook through 自主可控 for nearly a decade via state-directed channels (per Yuzu Xu, ChinaTalk May 15; see #China's parallel — 自主可控). The US framework's bet is that contingent contracts with private firms can substitute for China's command-economy delivery mechanism. Whether that substitution works without the underlying authoritarian compliance surface is the open empirical question (inference — Ward-Jackson does not explicitly stake out this comparison).

For the investing thread, the framework points to a specific cluster of asset classes that benefit from any policy crystallisation around the latency lens: subsea-cable repair operators, specialty-chemicals firms with surge-relevant capacity, critical-mineral processors in allied jurisdictions, and the contracting infrastructure that would deliver the contingent capacity contracts themselves (see #Why this matters for markets). None of these are tradeable on the framework alone — but they become tradeable on the first concrete legislative or executive proposal that adopts the lens (inference — no current administration commitment exists as of May 18, 2026).

## Limits and open questions

The framework is a proposal, not a tested policy, and several load-bearing assumptions remain open (inference — these are my readings of where the framework is exposed, not Ward-Jackson's own caveats):

- Calibration: $20-30bn / 5-10 capabilities may be too small if Tier 2 vulnerabilities turn out to span 30-50 inputs rather than 10. Either the fund scales up by an order of magnitude or it accepts that some Tier 2 vulnerabilities remain unaddressed.
- Contracting risk: contingent latency contracts depend on private firms maintaining genuine surge capacity rather than booking the retainer and degrading the underlying capability. The reservist-contract analogy assumes firms can be audited; this is unproven for production capability.
- Credibility of triggers: the deterrent function requires public, credible activation thresholds. Politically, no US administration has demonstrated willingness to commit to specified responses in advance — vague "all options on the table" language is the standard.
- Allied coordination: an effective Tier 2 strategy probably needs to be coordinated with G7 allies (especially [[Japan]] and [[South Korea]] given their position in many of the relevant supply chains). The framework is silent on the coalition architecture.
- Capture dynamics: any $20-30bn fund creates a political constituency around it. The framework's design needs to avoid the slow drift toward subsidising politically connected firms regardless of latency-relevant economics.
- Treatment of services: most of the framework's worked examples are physical goods. Tier 2 services — cloud computing, financial-clearing, software-supply-chain inputs — may need a different mechanism entirely.

## Why this matters for markets

The latency concept changes the read on three asset clusters (inference — Ward-Jackson's piece is policy-focused; the market read is my synthesis):

1. Critical-mineral processors and specialty-chemicals firms in the US and allied jurisdictions become structurally more interesting if a latency fund materialises — they're natural beneficiaries of contingent contracts that pay for capacity they would otherwise have to retire
2. Subsea cable repair and installation firms ([[NEC]], [[SubCom]], [[Alcatel Submarine Networks]], [[HMN Tech]], [[NTT]] World Engineering Marine) sit in the worked-example case; latency policy crystallising around the framework lifts the entire repair-services sub-sector — see [[Submarine Cables]] for the underlying infrastructure cluster
3. The US fiscal architecture for industrial policy is already crowded ([[CHIPS Act]], [[Inflation Reduction Act]], DOD industrial-base awards). A latency fund slots in as a new line item that competes with these for both budget and political attention; whether it gets traction depends on whether the framework becomes a Treasury-or-Commerce policy proposal in 2026-2027 (inference — no current administration commitment exists)

These reads are speculative until the framework moves from Substack column to legislative or executive proposal. As of May 18, 2026, it remains an analyst framework, not a policy.
