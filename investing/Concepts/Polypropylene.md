# Polypropylene

Thermoplastic polymer made from [[Propylene|propylene]] monomer. Used in packaging, textiles (nonwovens), auto interiors and bumpers, medical disposables, rigid containers, and battery casings. Second-most-produced plastic globally after [[Polyethylene]].

---

## Synopsis

Polypropylene shares most of polyethylene's value-chain logic but with propylene — not ethylene — as the monomer. Propylene comes from three routes: steam-cracker co-product (naphtha or ethane crackers), FCC off-gas at refineries, and on-purpose routes like propane dehydrogenation (PDH) and [[Methanol]]-to-olefins (MTO). The on-purpose share has grown as PE-focused crackers produced too little propylene to satisfy demand, making PDH and MTO swing suppliers.

Domestic [[China]] prices rose sharply during the 2026 [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] — visible on the [[FT]]'s NBS/Wind chart (April 11) spiking from ~6,500 Rmb/tonne to ~9,000 Rmb/tonne in the war weeks. The mechanism is the same as for polyethylene: Middle East feedstock disrupted, domestic CTO/MTO plants squeezed, import parity prices reset higher.

---

## Feedstock routes

| Route | Propylene source | Geography |
|-------|-----------------|-----------|
| Steam cracker (co-product) | Naphtha or mixed-feed crackers | East Asia, Europe, Middle East |
| FCC off-gas | Refinery fluid catalytic cracker | US, Europe, China |
| PDH (propane dehydrogenation) | Propane (NGL) | US Gulf Coast, China coastal |
| MTO (methanol-to-olefins) | [[Methanol]] | China (coal belt), increasingly Middle East |

MTO plants in [[China]] are structurally long on capacity but run on methanol — so when [[Methanol]] prices surge (as in April 2026), MTO plant margins collapse or plants run curtailed.
