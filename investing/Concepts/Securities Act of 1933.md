#concept #regulation #capital-markets #statute

**Securities Act of 1933** — foundational US federal statute governing the offer and sale of securities, enacted in the wake of the 1929 stock market crash. The act establishes the registration framework under which any offer or sale of securities by an issuer to the public must either be registered with the [[SEC]] or qualify for an exemption (the most common being [[Regulation D]] private placements). The statute also creates Section 11 and Section 12 liability — the principal legal exposures faced by issuers, directors, underwriters, and accountants for material misstatements in registration statements.

## Synthesis

The Securities Act's two structural pillars are mandatory registration and strict liability for misrepresentation. The registration framework requires issuers to file extensive disclosure documents (a Form S-1 for IPOs, Form S-3 for follow-on offerings) covering the business, financial statements, risk factors, and use of proceeds — disclosures the SEC reviews and may comment on before declaring the registration effective. The strict-liability framework under Section 11 imposes joint and several liability on issuers, directors, signing officers, underwriters, and experts (such as auditors) for material misstatements or omissions in registration statements, with no scienter requirement for issuers.

Several provisions have outsized importance for current capital-markets practice:

[[Rule 145|Rule 145a]] (added in 1972, amended 2024) — defines what constitutes a "sale" of securities for purposes of registration and liability. The 2024 [[SEC SPAC final rules January 2024|SEC final rules on SPACs]] used Rule 145a to deem de-SPAC transactions a "sale," importing the full Securities Act registration and liability framework into SPAC mergers.

Section 4(a)(2) and [[Regulation D]] — the most commonly used exemptions from registration. PIPE transactions, venture capital rounds, and most non-public capital raises rely on these exemptions.

Sections 11 and 12 — the private rights of action that fund securities litigation. Section 11 provides essentially strict liability for material misstatements in registration statements; Section 12 covers misstatements in prospectuses and oral communications used in selling. Together they shape the diligence cost of IPOs and follow-on offerings.

Sections 5 and 17 — the registration requirement and antifraud provision respectively. Section 5 is the foundational "no offer or sale without registration or exemption" rule; Section 17 is one of the SEC's principal enforcement tools against issuer fraud.

## Why it matters for the modern market

Most current capital-markets reform happens at the margins of the 1933 Act framework rather than replacing it. The [[INVEST Act]] of 2025 modifies disclosure requirements for emerging-growth-company issuers under existing exemptions; the 2024 SEC SPAC rules apply existing 1933 Act liability standards to new transactions; the JOBS Act of 2012 created scaled-disclosure tracks within the existing registration framework. The 1933 Act is the constitutional layer of US securities regulation — most adjustments are interpretive or procedural rather than statutory.
