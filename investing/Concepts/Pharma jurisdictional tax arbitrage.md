**Pharma jurisdictional tax arbitrage** — the practice of locating intellectual property, manufacturing, or profit centers in low-tax jurisdictions ([[Ireland]], [[Switzerland]], [[Puerto Rico]]) to reduce tax liability in the [[United States]], where the bulk of pharmaceutical revenue is generated. New FASB disclosure rules (effective for fiscal year 2025 annual reports) have for the first time quantified the scale: at least $5bn in federal tax savings across the 10 largest US pharma/biotech companies, out of $11bn+ across 40 major corporations analyzed by the [[Fact Coalition]].

---

## The story

The US is the world's largest pharmaceutical market, but many of the biggest US-listed drugmakers pay more in taxes to overseas governments than to the US Treasury. The mechanism is IP-driven: companies assign patents, manufacturing rights, or profit-booking entities to subsidiaries in jurisdictions with corporate tax rates well below the US 21% rate. [[Ireland]] (12.5% headline, with effective rates often lower via R&D credits), [[Switzerland]] (cantonal rates as low as 11-12%), and [[Puerto Rico]] (a US territory with Act 60 incentives offering 4% corporate tax) are the primary destinations.

This has been known in broad strokes for decades. What changed in March 2026 is that new mandatory accounting rules — FASB ASU 2023-09, effective for annual reports covering fiscal year 2025 onwards — now require public companies to disclose country-by-country tax payments and explain which jurisdictions drove their effective tax rate below the US 21% statutory rate. The [[US Chamber of Commerce]] lobbied the [[SEC]] to scrap the requirement, arguing in a December 2025 letter that the disclosures had been pushed by "activists" who wanted to "shame or otherwise vilify companies."

The data published in February-March 2026 annual reports confirmed what critics suspected. [[Eli Lilly]] paid $6.6bn in income tax to [[Ireland]] in 2025 — double the $3.3bn it paid to the US, despite the US being its largest revenue market. Lilly attributed the disparity to "significantly higher" manufacturing in the EU and said US capital investments would generate higher US payments in future years. [[Merck]] paid $2.1bn to [[Switzerland]] versus $1.6bn to the US — the maker of [[Keytruda]], the world's best-selling drug, routes more tax dollars to a country of 9 million people than to its home market. Merck noted it had "paid additional foreign taxes" partly due to global minimum tax rules (the OECD Pillar Two 15% floor) and pointed to $4.8bn in cumulative US taxes from 2023-2025.

[[Pfizer]] paid $1bn to [[Ireland]] versus $2.7bn to the US in 2025, but the deeper story is in the Senate investigation. A probe by Democratic members of the US Senate Finance Committee, led by Senator Ron Wyden, examined Pfizer's 2019 tax return (which is not public) and concluded that Pfizer had reduced its US taxable profit to zero that year by booking all income offshore. Pfizer disputed the analysis as "not accurate" and pointed to $15.7bn in US income tax paid over the past five years.

[[AbbVie]] cut $1.6bn from its federal tax bill through operations in [[Puerto Rico]], where its manufacturing is concentrated. [[Johnson & Johnson]] shaved $808mn off its US bill — 2 percentage points — through income booked in [[Ireland]] and [[Switzerland]], bringing its effective rate to 17.7%. The remaining five companies in the top-10 cohort — [[Biogen]], [[Bristol-Myers Squibb]], [[Gilead]], [[Regeneron]], and [[Thermo Fisher Scientific]] — were identified by the [[Fact Coalition]] as contributing to the ~$5bn pharma total but specific country breakdowns for each were not disclosed in the FT analysis.

The [[Fact Coalition]], a Washington-based tax transparency group, analyzed disclosures from 40 of the biggest US companies and calculated $11bn+ in collective tax savings from booking profits in lower-tax jurisdictions. Approximately half was attributable to the 10 pharma/biotech companies. The coalition noted that the disclosures exclude jurisdictions below a certain reporting threshold, meaning the true savings are understated.

The political dynamics are live. The disclosures arrive as Congress debates the future of the [[Tax Cuts and Jobs Act]] provisions expiring in 2025-2026. The pharma industry's ability to compress effective tax rates — many below 15%, some far below — while simultaneously lobbying against drug pricing reforms ([[IRA]] Medicare negotiation) creates a concentrated political target. The new transparency makes the tax differential visible to investors, voters, and legislators in a way it was not before.

### Key disclosures

- FASB ASU 2023-09: effective for annual reports covering fiscal years ending December 2025 onwards
- Requires country-by-country tax payment itemization where material
- Companies must explain which jurisdictions drove effective rate below 21% US statutory rate
- [[Fact Coalition]]: 40 companies analyzed, $11bn+ total savings, ~$5.5bn from 10 pharma/biotech
- [[US Chamber of Commerce]]: lobbied SEC to scrap disclosures (December 2025 letter)
- Sen. Ron Wyden (D-OR): Senate Finance Committee investigation of [[Pfizer]] 2019 return — found US taxable profit reduced to zero
