#concept #pricing #economics #market-structure

**Rockets and feathers** — the empirically observed pattern where downstream prices rise rapidly ("like a rocket") when input costs increase, but fall slowly ("like a feather") when input costs decrease. Coined by Robert Bacon in a 1991 study of UK gasoline prices. Peltzman (2000) found the pattern in two-thirds of 242 markets studied — far beyond energy.

## Mechanism

When crude oil prices spike, retail gasoline prices adjust upward within days. When crude falls by the same amount, the retail price decline takes weeks. The asymmetry shows up in both speed (how quickly prices adjust) and magnitude (how completely they pass through).

Three main explanations in the literature:

1. Oligopolistic coordination — concentrated retail markets (few gas stations in an area) let sellers maintain elevated margins longer during cost declines. Stations that are physically close together show less asymmetry than isolated ones, consistent with competitive pressure forcing faster pass-through.

2. Consumer search friction — buyers don't monitor input costs. They notice price increases (because they hurt) but don't actively seek out decreases. [[Matthew Lewis]] (2011) showed that when consumers are slow to process gasoline price information, sellers face less pressure to cut prices quickly.

3. Inventory and adjustment costs — firms with physical inventory bought at the old (higher) cost have an economic rationale to pass through decreases slowly, since they're selling through stock acquired at peak prices.

Peltzman (2000) found no relationship between market concentration and the degree of asymmetry, which suggests tacit collusion alone doesn't explain it — search frictions and information asymmetry play at least an equal role.

## Beyond gasoline

The pattern has been documented across:

- Banking — deposit rates fall like feathers when the [[Federal Reserve]] cuts, but lending rates rocket up when it hikes (Hannan and Berger 1991, Neumark and Sharpe 1992)
- Food — farm-gate price drops pass through slowly to retail grocery (Ward 1982, Goodwin and Holt 1999). Fresh vegetables show asymmetry at retail, wholesale, and shipping-point levels
- Electricity — retail electricity pricing shows rockets-and-feathers in deregulated markets (ZEW 2016)
- Shipping — freight rates adjust asymmetrically to fuel cost changes
- China refined products — [[China]]'s state-influenced pricing mechanism produces pronounced asymmetry between international crude and domestic refined product prices

The breadth matters: this is not a gasoline quirk but a general feature of markets with imperfect competition and search costs.

---

## Investment implications

The asymmetry creates a structural margin tailwind for midstream and downstream businesses during periods of falling input costs. When crude drops, refiners like [[Valero]] and [[Marathon Petroleum]] enjoy temporarily inflated crack spreads because retail prices lag the decline. The reverse is also true — rising input costs compress margins faster than they recover.

For macro analysis, rockets and feathers means CPI components tied to energy and food can be sticky on the way down even when commodity prices have already corrected. This has direct relevance to [[Federal Reserve]] rate-cut timing: headline inflation can look elevated longer than raw commodity charts suggest, because the feather effect delays pass-through to consumer prices.
