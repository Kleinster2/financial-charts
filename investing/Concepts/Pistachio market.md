# Pistachio market

Global pistachio production is dominated by two countries — the [[US]] (~40% of global production) and [[Iran]] (~20% of production, 25–30% of exports in some years per [[USDA]]) — with [[Turkey]] a meaningful third producer. Demand has been pushed structurally higher since 2023 by the "Dubai chocolate" viral product (pistachio cream + shredded pastry bars) and the broader pistachio-flavoured boom in nut butters, ice cream, and confectionery. The market is small relative to mainstream soft commodities but has become a clean read-through on Iran sanctions and Strait of Hormuz logistics because Iranian pistachios cannot be cleanly substituted at the high end (oil content / taste).

## Demand structure

The 2023 viral spread of "Dubai chocolate" — pistachio-cream-and-shredded-pastry bars produced originally by Dubai-based confectioners — pulled pistachio into mainstream confectionery in a way it had not been before. Knock-on demand:

- Pistachio-flavoured ice cream growth in Europe and the US.
- Pistachio nut butters, baklava production (especially [[Turkey]]/[[UK]]/Middle East routes), and pistachio inclusions in chocolate bars.
- Independent of the war, [[Borna Foods]] (London-based wholesaler with ~95% turnover in pistachios) cited summer ice cream season as the next demand pinch point.

The chart below shows the run-up: pistachio prices crashed below $3.50/lb at the 2024 trough and have nearly fully retraced toward the prior peak.

## Supply structure

The 2025 harvest came in below expectations across all three major producers — [[US]], [[Turkey]], [[Iran]] — with Iran's crop hit by drought specifically. That set the supply backdrop tight before the [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]] disrupted Iranian export logistics.

Iran's structural export route is [[Bandar Abbas]] on the [[Strait of Hormuz]]. With Bandar Abbas disrupted, the alternatives:

| Route | Detail | Cost / time impact |
|---|---|---|
| Land via [[Turkey]] → [[Mersin]] port → [[Suez Canal]] (to India) | Active | Significantly more expensive and slower; standalone Bandar Abbas → Mersin sea transit alone is normally ~14–18 days through the Persian Gulf and Suez Canal — the war-driven land-then-Mersin alternative adds further trucking and customs steps before that base transit |
| Rail to [[China]] | Active | Costly and complicated |
| [[Bandar Abbas]] direct (preferred) | Disrupted | n/a |

US pistachio exporters had already sold most of their available supply by April 2026 — even buyers willing to pay up cannot fully substitute. [[Borna Foods]] CEO Behnam Heydaripour: Iranian pistachios contain higher oil content than US or Turkish equivalents, which matters for products like baklava (US substitutes burn dry in the oven) and for high-end confectionery taste.

## Why this concept lives in the vault

Pistachios are a small commodity in dollar terms, but they are useful as a clean transmission channel illustration:

- **Iran sanctions / chokepoint mechanics** — pistachio price action is one of the cleanest non-oil signals of [[Strait of Hormuz]] and [[Bandar Abbas]] disruption, and it is being reported in primary commodity-news outlets.
- **Substitution-failure case study** — most disrupted goods can be sourced from elsewhere with a price premium. Iranian pistachios cannot be cleanly substituted, which makes the price response more durable.
- **Dubai chocolate as a demand-shock case** — a single viral product reshaping a supply chain is an instructive pattern that recurs in soft commodities (matcha, açai, manuka honey).

## Apr 2026 update — FT

[[Expana]] benchmark hit $4.57/lb in March 2026, an 8-year high. [[Iran]]'s exports fell 30% over the prior two months (Iranian customs administration). [[Borna Foods]] UK import prices rose from £16/kg to ~£18.50/kg (~+15.6%). The disruption was already in place from sanctions and domestic unrest — periodic communications shutdowns inside Iran were preventing exporters from coordinating sales with international buyers — and the [[Operation Epic Fury|war]] has compounded existing constraints rather than created new ones.

Voices:
- Behnam Heydaripour ([[Borna Foods]]): "It's like gambling — we don't know at what price to sell."
- Nick Moss ([[Expana]]): "The war has amplified existing constraints rather than creating new ones from scratch." Issues were "stacking on top of each other." If Iranian product remains inaccessible for a prolonged period, "prices could face continued upward pressure."
- Behrooz Agah (Iran Pistachio Association): the [[Bandar Abbas]] route was severely disrupted; alternative routes via [[Mersin]]/Suez and rail to China are more expensive and slower.

The piece reads as the small-commodity tell: the war is now visible in items as quotidian as pistachio cream, not just oil and gas.

*Source: FT, Apr 26 2026.*
