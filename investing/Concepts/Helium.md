# Helium

The only commodity Earth is permanently losing. Produced by radioactive decay of uranium and thorium over billions of years, trapped underground by geological accident, too light for Earth's gravity to retain — once vented, it's gone. Cannot be synthesized economically. No spot market, no futures contract, no price transparency — just opaque bilateral contracts between industrial gas majors and end users.

---

## Physical properties

Helium boils at -269degC — second only to absolute zero among elements. Exceptional thermal conductivity. These properties make it irreplaceable for cryogenic applications and extremely expensive to transport (specialized 11,000-gallon cryogenic containers, ~$1M each).

---

## Industrial uses

| Application | Why helium | Details |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Semiconductor etching | Exceptional thermal conductivity | Controls heat during selective material removal; performed hundreds of times per wafer on advanced AI chips with tens of billions of transistors |
| Dilution refrigerators | Helium-3 and helium-4 isotopes required | Cools superconducting quantum computers to ~10-15 millikelvin; see [[Quantum computing#Dilution refrigerators — the hidden chokepoint|Quantum computing — dilution refrigerators]] |
| MRI scanners | Cryogenic cooling | Cools superconducting magnets; historically the largest consumer |
| Fiber-optic manufacturing | Inert atmosphere | Used in drawing optical fibers |
| Aerospace | Purging agent | Purges rocket fuel tanks ([[NASA]] missions); pressurizes fuel systems |
| Welding | Shielding gas | Inert atmosphere for arc welding |
| Defense | Multiple | Drone components, space systems, surveillance blimps |
| Party balloons | Lighter than air | Lowest-priority use; first cut during shortages |

The semiconductor industry surpassed MRI as helium's largest consumer (confirmed Oct 2025, Gasworld Helium Super Summit — [[TechCET]] CEO Lita Shon-Roy). Helium has no substitute for its two critical semiconductor applications (etching thermal control, dilution refrigerators). Recycling is technically feasible but not widely implemented in chipmaking — historically deemed uneconomical when supply was cheap.

## Production geology

Helium collects in certain natural gas reservoirs as a byproduct of uranium/thorium decay. It is extracted during gas processing — separated from methane, nitrogen, and other gases, then shipped as a supercooled liquid. No gas processing = no helium. Production is physically tethered to natural gas operations.

## US National Helium Reserve — and its liquidation

The Mineral Leasing Act of 1920 reserved all helium on federal lands to the government. The Helium Act of 1925 created the National Helium Reserve at the Cliffside Gas Field (Bush Dome) near Amarillo, Texas — the world's only strategic helium buffer. By 1964, 95% of global recoverable helium was produced within 250 miles of Amarillo. The reserve held over 1 billion cubic meters of crude helium by 1995.

Then Congress sold it off. The Helium Privatization Act of 1996 directed the Department of the Interior to liquidate the stockpile to retire $1.4 billion in accrued program debt. Below-market government helium flooded the market for two decades, destroying incentives for new production investment or recycling technology. The Helium Stewardship Act of 2013 slowed the selloff but still mandated full disposal by 2021. By 2023, the reserve was effectively exhausted — the GSA auctioned the remaining facilities.

The parallel to petroleum is damning: after the 1973 oil embargo, the U.S. created the Strategic Petroleum Reserve and never sold it off. The difference is political visibility — oil shortages produce gas lines and angry voters; helium shortages produce delayed MRI installations and inconvenienced scientists. By the time helium became critical to semiconductor fabrication and the AI race, the buffer was already gone.

## Pricing

No spot market, no futures contract. Bilateral contracts between industrial gas majors and end users. Prices nearly doubled from $7.57/m3 (2020) to a historic high of $14/m3 (2023).

## Shortage history

| Shortage | Cause | Duration | Impact |
|----------|-------|----------|--------|
| 1.0 (2006-07) | BLM reserve maintenance + Algeria plant delays | ~18 months | MRI installations delayed |
| 2.0 (2011-13) | BLM pipeline issues + Qatar plant commissioning delays | ~2 years | NMR labs shut instruments; research disrupted |
| 3.0 (2019-20) | Russia Amur delays + Qatar maintenance + BLM auction changes | ~18 months | Prices doubled; party balloon shortages made headlines |
| 4.0 (2026) | War — Qatar offline + Hormuz closed + Ras Laffan struck | Ongoing | See [[Helium supply crisis 2026]] |

Each shortage triggered calls for recycling, strategic reserves, and supply diversification. None materialized at scale. The 2026 crisis is categorically different: not a maintenance hiccup but wartime destruction of production and shipping infrastructure.
