**Permian Basin** — Largest active oil and gas producing region globally. Located in West Texas and southeastern New Mexico. Responsible for ~6 million bpd of US oil production. Key formations include the Wolfcamp, Bone Spring, and Spraberry. Major operators include [[Exxon]], [[Chevron]], [[ConocoPhillips]], and [[Diamondback Energy]]. The reference unconventional basin against which other global plays like [[Jafurah]] and [[Vaca Muerta]] are benchmarked.

## Waha hub — negative gas prices (March 2026)

The Permian's defining infrastructure bottleneck: prolific oil drilling produces associated natural gas, but pipeline capacity to move that gas to market is insufficient. When pipelines fill, producers must **pay someone to take the gas** — negative prices.

| Date/period | Waha spot price | Context |
|-------------|----------------|---------|
| Week of Mar 17, 2026 | **-$9.75/MMBtu** (intraday low) | Record low weekly average |
| YTD 2026 | Negative more often than positive | Structural, not anomalous |
| Expected maintenance season | Could hit **-$10/MMBtu** | Seasonal pipeline maintenance tightens capacity further |

**Flaring at 5-year highs**: since negative prices mean producers pay to offload gas, excess is burned off ("flared"). Flaring events in early 2026 are the highest in five years.

**Why producers don't cut**: oil at ~$100/bbl (WTI up 47% in three weeks from the Iran conflict) makes drilling highly profitable even with gas losses. The associated gas is a byproduct they can't avoid without shutting oil wells.

### The geographic disconnect

The starkest illustration of the Iran conflict's energy market distortion:

| Market | Price | Direction |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| **Waha (West Texas)** | **-$9.75/MMBtu** | Negative — producers paying to offload |
| **Henry Hub (US)** | ~$3.50/MMBtu | Stable — domestic supply insulated |
| **TTF (Europe)** | **~€70/MWh (~$20+/MMBtu)** | Surging — Hormuz closure, Qatar offline |
| **JKM (Asia)** | **~$26/MMBtu** (spring), $30-40 projected | Surging — rationing, coal switching |

The world's largest gas exporter (US) cannot physically increase deliveries fast enough to help — LNG export terminals are at capacity. The surplus stays trapped in Texas while Asia and Europe scramble.
