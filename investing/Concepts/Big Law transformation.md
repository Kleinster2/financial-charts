# Big Law transformation

Structural shift in elite US corporate law — from lockstep partnerships defined by tenure to investment-bank-style firms defined by individual rainmaker compensation. Triggered by [[Paul, Weiss]]'s 2016 hire of [[Scott Barshay]] from [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]] and accelerated by [[Kirkland & Ellis]]'s scale-and-pay strategy through the late 2010s and 2020s. Within the FT's reporting frame the shift is termed *"Kirklandisation"*, after the firm that pioneered it.

The vault relevance is structural, not deal-specific: this is the supply-side reshaping of the advisory layer that sits on top of every major M&A, restructuring, and PE transaction the vault tracks ([[M&A tracker]]). Firms that absorb the new compensation model can scale; firms that resist face partner defection. The model is now testing the most resistant holdout, [[Wachtell Lipton]].

## Synopsis

Through the 1980s-2000s, elite NY firms ran on three principles: lockstep compensation (pay tied to tenure, not revenue), one-partner-one-vote governance, and partnership-for-life. Career mobility was rare; lateral moves were almost taboo. The model worked because corporate clients did not buy individual lawyers — they bought firm reputations, and the firm captured the fee.

Two forces broke that. The rise of private equity generated a continuous deal flow that rewarded firms willing to staff it at scale, and PE clients valued individual relationships over institutional letterhead. [[Kirkland & Ellis]] leaned into both — building a >4,000-lawyer footprint, paying top originators $30M+ annually, retiring underperformers, and aggressively recruiting laterals. Revenue compounded faster than at lockstep peers; by 2025 Kirkland reached ~$10B in annual revenue, the first US firm to clear that mark.

[[Paul, Weiss]]'s 2016 hire of M&A partner [[Scott Barshay]] from [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]] is the conventional starting point of the transition. Cravath had been the archetype of lockstep; the Barshay departure proved a top originator could be moved with money. In the decade since, [[Latham & Watkins]], [[Skadden]], [[Simpson Thacher]], [[Davis Polk]] and others have selectively adopted the same playbook: discretion at the top of the comp band to retain stars, or to recruit them. Lockstep survives in pockets — [[Wachtell Lipton]], [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]] — but in modified form, with discretionary points or signing-bonus structures bolted on.

The result is a bifurcated industry: scale-plus-pay firms (Kirkland, Latham, Paul Weiss) compounding revenues and headcount, vs. small-elite holdouts (Wachtell, Cravath) whose per-partner profits remain extraordinary but whose junior partners have become acquisition targets.

## The defection mechanic

The economic premise of the transformation: in a lockstep firm, a young high-revenue partner is structurally underpaid relative to peer market rates. Lockstep is a tax on stars to subsidise lifetime tenure for the firm's senior generation and adjacent specialties (litigation, antitrust, tax). A scale-plus-pay rival can offer:

| Lever | Lockstep firm | Scale-plus-pay firm |
|---|---|---|
| First-year partner equity | Set by seniority point system | Negotiable, can match outside offer |
| Star-partner annual comp | Capped near firm-wide median + tenure premium | $30M+ for top originators |
| Signing bonuses | Effectively forbidden by partnership culture | Standard, can be multi-year guaranteed |
| Underperformer retention | For-life | Routine de-equitisation by mid-60s |
| Lateral entry | Rare, culturally suspect | Continuous |

A high-revenue partner who would clear $5-6M lockstep can clear $15-30M+ at a scale-plus-pay rival, often with a guaranteed multi-year package that converts the lockstep underpayment into immediate cash. That arithmetic is the engine of the eight Wachtell departures since 2025 (most recently [[Josh Feltman]] to [[Kirkland & Ellis]] on a reported $80M / 3-year package, and four partners to [[Latham & Watkins]]).

## Why elite holdouts persist

Lockstep survives at [[Wachtell Lipton]] and [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]] because the model produces three goods that scale-plus-pay firms cannot reliably replicate:

1. *Cross-practice integration.* Bet-the-company assignments require deal lawyers, litigators, antitrust, tax, and executive comp working as one team without internal billing fights. Lockstep removes the incentive for the deal lawyer to exclude the litigator from the case fee; it makes generalist partner attention to the client default.
2. *Multi-decade institutional knowledge.* A firm that retires partners by mid-60s loses access to lawyers like [[Marty Lipton]] (94, still in office) or [[Edward Herlihy]] (79, biggest rainmaker), whose decades of CEO-level relationships are themselves the product. Scale firms cannot manufacture that.
3. *Premium per-lawyer economics.* Wachtell's 2025 PEP (~$12M) and revenue per lawyer (~$7M+) is the highest in the world. Smaller partner counts × higher fees per assignment = the economics of a hedge-fund flagship rather than an asset-management platform.

The vulnerability: 2 and 3 require that the next generation of partners stays. The defection wave breaks the multi-decade compounding mechanism that 2 depends on. The 2025-2026 lockstep adjustments at Wachtell (lower first-year equity points, more discretion to reward deal originators, points reallocated from litigators to deal lawyers) are an attempt to defend without abandoning the model.

## Vault implications

Every major M&A transaction in [[M&A tracker]] is advised by a small set of these firms. The firm choice tracks a deal's strategic risk profile: bet-the-company defense → Wachtell or Cravath; PE-backed buyout → Kirkland or Simpson Thacher; cross-border or scaled transactional → Latham or Skadden. As the elite holdouts shrink (in mid-tier partner count) the surface area of "bet-the-company defense" capacity narrows. Net winner: the few partners who stay at the holdouts, and the scale-plus-pay firms that absorb the rest.

## Investable proxies

There are no public listings of US Big Law firms (all are partnerships). The [[Big Four|Big Four accounting firms]] and [[McKinsey]] / [[BCG]] (private) are similarly structured. The investable surface is in three rings around the firms.

| [[CS Disco]] | NYSE: LAW | ~$295mn | Cloud-native AI ediscovery / document review pure-play; micro-cap and structurally vulnerable to Harvey / Legora subsuming the workflow above ediscovery |

The Ring 1 thesis is bifurcated. Legal-data incumbents (RELX, TRI, WKL) face an AI-disruption overhang as Harvey, Legora, and Anthropic-class general-purpose models compress the search-and-retrieval value layer. Workflow-and-relationship platforms (INTA) benefit from Kirklandisation directly — the more law firms run like PE shops, the more they buy DealCloud-style infrastructure. Pure-play ediscovery (LAW / CS Disco) is the most exposed of the three categories, both to Big Law transformation (which compresses junior-associate hours) and to AI-platform competition.

The Ring 3 advisory-boutique thesis: boutiques are *complements* to Big Law transformation, not substitutes. Kirkland-style mega-deal and mega-restructuring activity drives mandates to both sides of the table. All five listed boutiques have outperformed bulge-bracket banks during deal-cycle upswings; HLI in particular is a lever on the same restructuring volume Kirkland's Restructuring group captures. Negative read: if AI compresses Big Law's billable-hour rents, the same compression eventually hits boutique advisory fees on smaller-cap deals.

The cleanest combination trade for the Big Law transformation thesis is therefore long Ring 2 platforms (Harvey, Legora — when public) + long Ring 3 restructuring boutiques (HLI) + short or underweight Ring 1 incumbents most exposed to AI substitution (TRI, WKL after the Morningstar moat downgrade). That structure expresses both halves of the cycle: rising adoption of AI-legal infrastructure and rising deal volume in the practice areas the new firms dominate.
