# Poison pill

Defensive corporate-finance structure invented in 1982 by [[Marty Lipton]] of [[Wachtell Lipton]]. Formally a *shareholder rights plan*: a contractual right issued to every shareholder *except* a hostile acquirer who crosses a triggering ownership threshold (typically 10-20%), which then dilutes the acquirer. Effectively gives the board, not the market, the power to gate hostile bids.

## Mechanics

1. The board adopts a rights plan in advance — no shareholder vote required.
2. The plan dormant unless a triggering event occurs (e.g., any single party acquiring 15% of common shares without board approval).
3. On trigger, every shareholder *except* the acquirer receives the right to purchase additional shares at a steep discount, typically 50% off market.
4. Result: the acquirer's stake is sharply diluted. In practice, no acquirer has ever pushed through a fully triggered pill — the structure makes hostile completion economically irrational.
5. The board can choose to redeem the pill at any time, which becomes the bargaining chip: the acquirer must negotiate with the board to get the pill removed.

## Why it shaped US M&A

Before 1982, hostile tender offers were a relatively unconstrained mechanism — a raider with sufficient capital could acquire a public company over the objection of its board, as the 1980s [[Carl Icahn]] / [[T. Boone Pickens]] era was demonstrating. The poison pill changed the equilibrium: boards became gatekeepers, and the locus of M&A control shifted from public-market mechanics to negotiation between acquirer and target board. [[Wachtell Lipton]] built its M&A defence franchise around this structural shift, and the firm's 35-principles foundational document explicitly cites the pill's invention as the firm's most consequential single innovation.

The structure has since been adopted globally and remains standard practice. Variations: NOL pills (preserving net operating losses against ownership change), short-term "tactical" pills (adopted in response to a specific approach), and traditional rights plans with multi-year terms.

## Vault relevance

Many of the deals tracked in [[M&A tracker]] are negotiated in the shadow of an actual or threatened pill. Recent prominent examples involving Wachtell representation:

- [[Twitter]] adopted a poison pill in April 2022 in response to [[Elon Musk]]'s hostile bid; pill was redeemed when the Musk takeover became board-supported.
- [[Electronic Arts]] in the [[PIF]]-led $48.1bn 2025 take-private — board-supported deal, but the deal architecture is shaped by knowledge that hostile alternatives would have triggered defensive structures Wachtell pioneered.
