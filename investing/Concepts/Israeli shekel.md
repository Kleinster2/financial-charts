The Israeli shekel (ILS) is the currency of [[Israel]], issued by the Bank of Israel, and notable among EM currencies for its tech-sector sensitivity and geopolitical risk premium.

## Synopsis

The Israeli new shekel has been one of the stronger emerging-market currencies over the past decade, appreciating from ~3.9/USD in 2015 to ~3.2/USD by 2022, driven by Israel's tech export boom (software, semiconductors, cybersecurity) and persistent current account surpluses. The Bank of Israel intervened repeatedly during 2020-2022 to slow appreciation, accumulating ~$200B in foreign reserves — enormous relative to Israel's $530B GDP. The tech sector matters disproportionately: Israel's ~$70B in annual tech exports generates USD inflows that structurally support the shekel, making it partly a proxy for global tech demand.

The 2023 judicial overhaul crisis and the subsequent [[Israel]]-Hamas war demonstrated the shekel's geopolitical beta. ILS weakened from 3.5 to 4.1/USD in the weeks after Oct 7, 2023, before the Bank of Israel deployed $8.5B in intervention and $15B in FX swap facilities to stabilize the currency. The war's fiscal cost (~4% of GDP in additional defense spending) and the credit downgrade cycle (Moody's cut Israel to A2 in Feb 2024) kept the shekel under pressure through 2024. For investors, the shekel functions as a geopolitical thermometer: strength signals normalcy, confidence in the tech economy, and capital inflows; weakness signals war risk, fiscal deterioration, or political instability. The ILS/USD rate is also relevant for any position in Israeli equities (TASE-listed) or dual-listed companies where shekel revenues translate to dollar earnings.
