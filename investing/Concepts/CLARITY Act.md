# CLARITY Act

The **Digital Asset Market Clarity Act of 2025** (H.R. 3633) — the first US legislation to draw statutory boundaries between digital commodities ([[CFTC]] jurisdiction) and digital securities ([[SEC]] jurisdiction). If enacted, it would make the [[SEC-CFTC Joint Crypto Taxonomy]] binding law rather than interpretive guidance.

The crypto industry's top legislative priority. Racing against a closing window.

## Key provisions

### Commodity vs. security classification

The core function: determines by statute whether a digital asset is a **digital commodity** (CFTC) or a **digital security** (SEC). This single determination drives:
- Which registration requirements apply
- Which exchanges can list the asset
- Which investor protections attach
- Which enforcement mechanism governs violations

### Exemptions

- Exempts certain digital asset offerings from securities registration
- Provides treatment of non-controlling blockchain developers (safe harbor)
- Application of the Bank Secrecy Act to digital assets

### Stablecoin yield — the Tillis-Alsobrooks compromise

The provision that nearly killed the bill. The fight:

| Side | Position | Concern |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Banks** (ABA, community banks) | Prohibit stablecoin yield entirely | Deposit flight — if stablecoins pay interest, money leaves the banking system |
| **Crypto industry** (Blockchain Association) | Allow yield to compete with bank deposits | Without yield, US stablecoins lose to offshore alternatives; US becomes uncompetitive |

**Compromise (March 20, 2026):**
- **Prohibits passive holding rewards** (no interest on idle stablecoin balances)
- **Allows activity-based rewards** tied to transactions and platform usage
- Backed by the White House
- Negotiated by Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) and Sen. Angela Alsobrooks (D-MD)
- The January Banking Committee markup was cancelled over this exact dispute

Sen. Alsobrooks: the compromise aims to "prevent deposit flight that we do not want to see happen, and to allow the innovation to grow at the same time."

### New complication: community bank riders

Senate Banking Republicans are discussing attaching **community bank deregulatory provisions** to CLARITY in exchange for the House accepting the Senate's housing package — potentially complicating passage (reported, FinTech Weekly, March 2026).

## Why the deadline matters

Sen. Bernie Moreno has stated plainly: if CLARITY doesn't reach the Senate floor by **May 2026**, digital asset legislation may not move again for years. The 2026 midterm election cycle would foreclose the window.

The Atkins SEC's term expires **June 5, 2026**. A future administration could reverse the pro-crypto regulatory posture. The taxonomy is interpretive guidance — only CLARITY makes it permanent.

As of March 25, the bill has **five remaining legislative steps** between Banking Committee markup and presidential signature, against a very narrow Senate calendar window.

## Relationship to SEC-CFTC taxonomy

The [[SEC-CFTC Joint Crypto Taxonomy]] (March 17) is the *blueprint*. CLARITY would be the *building code*.

| | Taxonomy | CLARITY Act |
|---|---|---|
| Legal force | Interpretive — persuasive, not binding on courts | Statutory — binding law |
| Permanence | Can be reversed by future SEC/CFTC leadership | Requires new legislation to change |
| Coverage | Five categories, 16 named commodities | Comprehensive statutory framework |
| Enforcement | Agencies committed to administer consistently | Courts must follow |

Both Atkins (SEC) and Selig (CFTC) have said publicly that only Congress can provide the statutory foundation — the taxonomy is their contribution, but the ball is in Congress's court.
