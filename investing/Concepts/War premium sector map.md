#concept #macro #energy

**War premium sector map** — the pattern of where geopolitical risk premium embeds across the equity market during an oil supply shock, revealed when the shock reverses. The key insight: risk premium doesn't sit only in the obvious sectors (energy, airlines). It distributes across the full economy in a hierarchy that only becomes measurable on the unwind.

---

## Framework

When an oil supply shock hits (blockade, war, embargo), the market prices risk into equities through several transmission channels:

| Channel | Mechanism | Where it shows up |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Direct cost | Fuel, feedstock, shipping rates | Airlines, chemicals, industrials |
| [[Consumer]] squeeze | [[Inflation]] → spending pullback | Discretionary, luxury, housing |
| Recession fear | Growth slowdown probability | [[Banks]], EM, cyclicals |
| Safety rotation | Money moves to predictable revenue | [[SaaS]] premium builds, then reverses |
| [[Insurance]] repricing | Marine/energy underwriting risk | Reinsurers, P&C carriers |
| European exposure | [[EU]]/[[Asia]] import dependence | European banks, industrials |

The premium is invisible while the crisis is active — stocks appear to trade on their own fundamentals. The unwind reveals the embedded premium because it unloads in a single session.

## April 8, 2026: Hormuz ceasefire as natural experiment

The US-[[Iran]] two-week ceasefire (Apr 8) produced a +2.5% [[SPY]] session. Using beta-adjusted excess returns (120-day beta, 60-day idiosyncratic vol) reveals which sectors moved beyond what their market sensitivity predicted.

### Sectors with embedded war premium (outperformed beta)

[[Consumer]] staples (the surprise):
- [[Unilever|UL]] +4.6% (+2.9σ, beta 0.04) — near-zero beta, yet jumped
- [[Procter & Gamble|PG]] +2.5% (+2.0σ, beta -0.04) — negative beta, still rallied
- [[Walmart|WMT]] +3.9% (+2.1σ, beta 0.08)

These names have no direct oil exposure. The premium was consumer spending fear — if oil stays at $115+, discretionary spending contracts, and even staples face margin pressure from transport costs. The ceasefire released that embedded anxiety.

Industrials (oil-as-input):
- [[PPG Industries|PPG]] +8.0% (+2.9σ) — coatings use petrochemical feedstock
- [[Deere|DE]] +5.8% (+1.9σ) — ag equipment, fuel + steel costs
- [[PACCAR|PCAR]] +5.1% (+1.8σ) — truck demand = freight economy health
- [[General Electric|GE]] +6.7% (+1.7σ) — aviation engines, power turbines
- [[Honeywell|HON]] +3.9% (+1.6σ) — process industries, refining tech

European banks:
- [[Lloyds|LYG]] +6.7% (+1.9σ)
- [[Barclays|BCS]] +7.3% (+1.6σ)
- [[Heidelberg Materials|HEI]] +6.5% (+2.0σ)

[[Europe]] was the most exposed region to the Hormuz closure ([[Francisco Blanch]] / [[Bank of America]]). European bank rallies on ceasefire reflect recession probability repricing for the eurozone specifically.

[[Insurance]]/reinsurance:
- [[Travelers|TRV]] +2.2% (+1.7σ, beta 0.09)
- [[Chubb|CB]] +1.6% (+1.5σ, beta -0.11)
- [[Arch Capital Group|ACGL]] +1.7% (+1.6σ, beta -0.16)

Marine and energy underwriting risk collapses when the strait reopens. These are near-zero or negative beta names that rallied — the move is pure war premium release.

[[Housing]]/consumer confidence:
- [[Home Depot|HD]] +5.5% (+2.4σ) — housing activity + consumer confidence
- [[Vornado Realty Trust|VNO]] +4.2% (+1.8σ) — commercial real estate, rates-sensitive
- [[Ralph Lauren|RL]] +7.4% (+2.3σ) — luxury, consumer willingness to spend
- [[Tapestry|TPR]] +7.4% (+1.8σ)

EM financials:
- [[HDFC Bank|HDB]] +6.3% (+2.4σ) — [[India]] imports 85% of its oil; ceasefire reprices the entire economy
- [[Futu Holdings|FUTU]] +11.5% (+3.2σ) — [[China]]/HK risk-on

### Sectors that lost war premium (underperformed beta)

Energy (expected — the premium was theirs to lose):
- [[ConocoPhillips|COP]] -5.0% (-2.9σ, beta -0.15)
- [[Marathon Petroleum|MPC]] -5.5% (-2.8σ, beta 0.10)
- [[Chevron|CVX]] -4.3% (-2.5σ, beta -0.20)
- [[Exxon|XOM]] -4.7% (-2.4σ, beta -0.29)
- Plus [[Diamondback Energy|FANG]], [[Occidental Petroleum|OXY]], [[Valero|VLO]], [[Devon Energy|DVN]], [[Phillips 66|PSX]], [[EOG Resources|EOG]], [[Petrobras|PBR]], [[BP]], [[TotalEnergies|TTE]], [[Shell|SHEL]]

Negative betas confirm these names were moving inverse to [[SPY]] for the duration of the crisis — when [[SPY]] rose on peace hopes, energy fell, and vice versa.

Chemicals (the counterintuitive one):
- [[LyondellBasell|LYB]] -7.5% (-2.5σ, beta -0.21)
- [[Dow Inc.|DOW]] -5.1% (-2.2σ, beta 0.30)

Lower oil should mean lower feedstock costs and higher margins for chemicals. But the market priced these as energy proxies, not energy consumers. The negative beta on LYB confirms the market treated it as an oil play, not a margin beneficiary. This classification error persisted through the crisis.

[[SaaS]]/enterprise software (the big finding):
- [[Palantir|PLTR]] -6.2% (-3.1σ), [[Wix|WIX]] -8.2% (-2.8σ), [[Workday|WDAY]] -6.5% (-2.5σ)
- [[Okta|OKTA]] -4.2% (-2.4σ), [[Paycom|PAYC]] -5.1% (-2.1σ), [[Intuit|INTU]] -5.1% (-2.1σ)
- [[Salesforce|CRM]] -3.6% (-2.0σ), [[UiPath|PATH]] -5.1% (-1.9σ), [[DocuSign|DOCU]] -3.5% (-1.8σ), [[Cloudflare|NET]] -2.3% (-1.7σ)

Ten enterprise [[SaaS]] names declined on a +3.5% [[Nasdaq]] day. Three possible explanations:
1. Safety rotation unwind — during the crisis, money hid in subscription revenue predictability. Ceasefire released it back into cyclicals.
2. AI disruption overhang — the [[Conway]]/[[Anthropic]] platform leak (Apr 7-8) may have reminded the market that [[Claude Cowork]] threatens the full [[SaaS]] stack.
3. Growth-to-value rotation — ceasefire reduces recession probability, which favors cyclicals over growth at current multiples.

These explanations aren't mutually exclusive. The [[SaaS]] cluster declining in unison suggests a common factor — the timing with the Anthropic platform revelation adds a second vector on top of the macro rotation.

High-beta ad-tech/speculative:
- [[AppLovin|APP]] -4.7% (-2.6σ, beta 3.62) — expected +9%, got -4.7%
- [[Snap|SNAP]] -1.5% (-2.6σ, beta 2.82) — expected +7%, barely moved
- [[Coinbase|COIN]] -0.1% (-2.1σ, beta 3.52)

Ultra-high-beta names that didn't participate in the rally. These may have been momentum/speculation vehicles during the crisis that lost their bid when real cyclical opportunity returned.

## Correlation cluster analysis

Individual stock screening flags outliers but misses coordinated group moves where no single name is extreme. Correlation clustering (`cluster_movers.py`) on beta-adjusted excess returns reveals the structure: 480 vault tickers produce 72 distinct correlation clusters at corr>=0.30, of which 11 showed statistically unusual coordinated moves on Apr 8.

### Energy splits into three distinct risk clusters

The market does not treat "energy" as one sector. Correlation on excess returns separates it into three groups with distinct risk profiles:

| Cluster | Members | Group σ | Avg beta | Read |
|---------|---------|---------|----------|------|
| US E&P | [[ConocoPhillips\|COP]], [[Occidental Petroleum\|OXY]], [[Diamondback Energy\|FANG]], [[Devon Energy\|DVN]], [[EOG Resources\|EOG]] | -1.8σ | -0.22 | Upstream production; negative SPY beta — moves inverse to market |
| Refiners | [[Marathon Petroleum\|MPC]], [[Valero\|VLO]], [[Phillips 66\|PSX]] | -2.4σ | -0.03 | [[Crack spread]] exposure; near-zero beta |
| Intl majors | [[Petrobras\|PBR]], [[Shell\|SHEL]], [[BP]], [[TotalEnergies\|TTE]] | -2.2σ | +0.09 | European/EM exposure; near-zero beta but distinct from US E&P |

E&P had the most negative beta (they moved *against* SPY during the crisis), refiners were beta-neutral, and international majors carried slight positive beta. The ceasefire hit all three, but for different reasons: E&P lost supply-side war premium, refiners lost crack spread inflation, international majors lost the European energy security premium.

### SaaS: the hidden safety trade (12 members, -2.0σ)

Correlation clustering confirmed the SaaS phenomenon as a statistically significant coordinated move — not a coincidence of individual stocks:

[[Snap\|SNAP]], [[Braze\|BRZE]], [[HubSpot\|HUBS]], [[DocuSign\|DOCU]], [[Paycom\|PAYC]], [[Salesforce\|CRM]], [[LiveRamp\|RAMP]], [[Instacart\|CART]], [[ServiceNow\|NOW]], [[FactSet\|FDS]], [[Adobe\|ADBE]], [[Trimble\|TRMB]]

Average excess return: -5.4%. Average beta: 1.12. The group *should* have rallied ~2.8% based on beta alone — instead it fell ~2.5% in raw terms. The 8+ percentage point gap between expected and actual is the safety premium unwinding.

The clustering algorithm also separated this group from two adjacent but distinct clusters:

| Cluster | Members | Group σ | Character |
|---------|---------|---------|-----------|
| SaaS/enterprise | 12 names above | -2.0σ | Subscription revenue, enterprise buyers |
| High-beta consumer tech | [[AppLovin\|APP]], [[Roblox\|RBLX]], [[Twilio\|TWLO]], [[Shopify\|SHOP]], [[Roku\|ROKU]], [[Spotify\|SPOT]], [[eBay\|EBAY]], [[DoorDash\|DASH]], [[Take-Two\|TTWO]] | -1.7σ | Platform/consumer, ad-driven or marketplace |
| [[Cybersecurity]] | [[Rubrik\|RBRK]], [[Cloudflare\|NET]], [[Zscaler\|ZS]], [[Fortinet\|FTNT]], [[CrowdStrike\|CRWD]], [[Palo Alto Networks\|PANW]] | -1.6σ | Security-specific risk; partially insulated from rotation |

The market treats these as three separate risk factors, even though a fundamental screener would lump them all as "tech" or "software." The correlation structure reveals that SaaS enterprise names share a common factor (subscription revenue predictability → safety bid) that cybersecurity and consumer tech do not.

### Chemicals cluster confirms misclassification (-2.0σ)

[[LyondellBasell\|LYB]], [[Dow Inc.\|DOW]], [[FMC Corp\|FMC]], [[CF Industries\|CF]]

The cluster includes both petrochemical ([[LyondellBasell\|LYB]], [[Dow Inc.\|DOW]]) and agricultural chemical/fertilizer ([[FMC Corp\|FMC]], [[CF Industries\|CF]]) names. All declined despite lower oil theoretically cutting their input costs. The correlation on excess returns proves the market classifies the entire chemicals complex as "energy proxy" during oil shocks — a classification error that creates mispricing on both legs.

[[CF Industries\|CF]] (beta -1.37) is the extreme case: a fertilizer producer whose costs are dominated by natural gas. Its negative SPY beta means the market treats it as a pure commodity play. On the ceasefire unwind, it fell despite lower feedstock costs.

## Analytical takeaways

1. [[Consumer]] staples and insurance are the hidden war premium carriers. They don't appear in any "[[Iran]] exposure" screen, but they carry the second-order consumer/underwriting fear. Cluster analysis confirms these are coordinated group moves, not individual outliers.

2. European banks are the geographic lever. The magnitude of their excess return on ceasefire reveals how much European recession risk the market was pricing from Hormuz.

3. [[SaaS]]-as-safety is a measurable phenomenon. Twelve enterprise software names formed a -2.0σ cluster on the ceasefire day — the safety premium built during the crisis unwinding in a single session. The algorithm correctly separated this from adjacent cybersecurity and consumer tech clusters.

4. Energy is three risk clusters, not one. E&P, refiners, and international majors carry distinct beta profiles and responded to the ceasefire through different channels. Any analysis that treats "energy" as monolithic misses the dispersion.

5. Chemicals get misclassified. The entire chemicals complex — petrochemical and fertilizer — correlated as an energy proxy during the crisis, ignoring that these are net energy consumers. Mispricing opportunity on both legs.

6. Correlation clustering catches what individual screening misses. Individual beta-adjusted screening flagged 18 names at 2.5σ. Cluster analysis found 11 coordinated group moves involving ~70 names — the group signal is richer than the sum of individual outliers.

## Methodology

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Beta window | 120 trading days | Stable structural estimate |
| Idiosyncratic vol window | 60 trading days | Responsive to recent regime |
| Correlation window | 120 trading days | Matches beta window |
| Correlation threshold | 0.30 | On excess returns (post-beta-removal), 0.30 indicates real co-movement |
| Min cluster size | 3 | Avoids paired coincidences |
| Cluster sigma threshold | 1.5σ | Lower than individual (2.5σ) because group moves are inherently more significant |
| Clustering method | Average-linkage | Prevents chaining; stock must correlate with cluster average, not just one member |
| [[Benchmark]] | SPY | Market factor removed before all analysis |

Scripts: `scripts/quick_movers.py` (individual), `scripts/cluster_movers.py` (clusters)
