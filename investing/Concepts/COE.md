# COE

A Certificado de Operações Estruturadas is the Brazilian structured note — a bank-issued instrument that wraps a bond with an embedded derivative (for example, capped equity-index upside with partial or full capital protection). It is the local cousin of the US autocallable; see [[Structured products]] for the broader market.

Chart data unavailable — this is an instrument category, not a single tracked price series.

## Mechanics

- Two families: capital-protected (principal returned at maturity, upside capped) and at-risk (more upside, principal exposed).
- Sold through banks and brokerages with the payoff dressed up as a defined, "safe" bet on an index or basket.

## Risks / watch-outs

- High embedded fees and frequent underperformance — the distributor's cut (often ~5% upfront) is baked into the structure, so the issuer and seller win before the investor does.
- Illiquidity and complexity: hard to value, hard to exit, easy to misjudge the real expected return.
- The canonical "sold, not bought" retail product — the [[Bruno Corano|Carteira Aberta]] reviews use the COE as the example of a seductive wrapper whose economics favor the distributor. Recent Brazilian-press defaults on COEs underline the at-risk variant's tail.
