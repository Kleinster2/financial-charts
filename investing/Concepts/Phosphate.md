# Phosphate

Phosphorus fertilizer — the "P" in NPK. Phosphate drives photosynthesis, root development, and crop maturation. Production begins with phosphate rock mined from a small number of countries, processed into phosphoric acid using [[Sulfuric Acid]], then finished into DAP (diammonium phosphate), MAP (monoammonium phosphate), or NPK blends. The segment is ~44M tonnes P₂O₅ annual consumption (~24% of global fertilizer nutrient demand), has the tightest supply setup of the three nutrients in 2026, and is becoming increasingly strategic because of its second end-market: [[LFP batteries]] for electric vehicles and grid storage.

---

## Synopsis

Phosphate has the worst supply setup of the three primary nutrients in 2026. Four countries control the DAP export market — [[China]], [[Morocco]], [[Saudi Arabia]], and [[Russia]] — and three of them are constrained. [[China]] keeps pulling back exports to protect domestic supply, most recently halting all fertilizer exports on March 16, 2026 (extending phosphate restrictions already in place through August). [[Saudi Arabia]] shipments are caught in the [[Strait of Hormuz]] disruption. [[Russia]] has been operating under sanctions pressure since 2022. That leaves [[Morocco]] as the only major exporter running freely, and even [[OCP Group]] depends on imported Gulf sulfur and ammonia to keep its plants running. When a market relies on four suppliers and three have structural problems, prices stay elevated even when spot demand cools.

The export data makes the pattern visible. After a decade of gradually rising global DAP trade, volumes fell sharply in 2025. [[China]] is the main story: Chinese DAP exports ran above 5M tonnes in 2021, dropped to nearly zero in 2023 as Beijing restricted outflows to protect domestic food security, recovered partially in 2024-25, and are now at risk again as the sulfur squeeze intensifies. Every time the market expects China to come back at scale, a policy move pulls it away. That pattern — China as unreliable swing supplier — is becoming the defining feature of phosphate.

Prices reflect the structural tightness. DAP spot at $661/t (Mar 2026) is off the recent $800/t peak but well above 2023-24 ranges. The floor keeps moving up: the $400/t floor of 2023 became $550/t in 2024, and now $650/t looks like the new base. [[United States|US]] phosphate imports have declined from above 3M tonnes in 2018 to roughly 2M tonnes in 2025, squeezed by tariffs on Moroccan product, a tough farm economy, and shrinking global availability. In March 2026, 64 US agricultural groups petitioned [[Mosaic]] and [[JR Simplot]] — the two largest US phosphate fertilizer producers — to drop support for anti-dumping duties on Moroccan phosphate imports. That letter tells you where the pressure is being felt: buyers are running out of options and want every trade route open.

Demand is steadily growing; supply is not keeping up. Global phosphate consumption rose from ~62M tonnes in 2010 to an estimated 76-79M tonnes in 2025, tracking the long-term trend. But the gap between where demand is heading and what the supply chain can deliver is widening. Phosphate rock is concentrated in a handful of countries ([[Morocco]] controls ~70% of reserves), new mines take years to permit and build, and the entire processing chain from rock to finished fertilizer requires [[Elemental Sulfur|sulfur]] and [[Ammonia]] at every step. When those inputs are disrupted — as they are in the 2026 [[Nitrogen Trap]] crisis — the whole chain slows. Unlike nitrogen, where at least some new capacity is being built, the phosphate pipeline has been quiet.

The second end-market is the wild card. [[LFP batteries]] now account for >60% of the Chinese battery market and are accelerating globally. LFP cathodes require phosphoric acid (the same intermediate used for DAP/MAP), diverting supply from agriculture. [[China]]'s phosphate export restrictions are partly motivated by this — keeping domestic phosphoric acid available for battery cathodes. Agricultural buyers are now competing with the energy transition for the same feedstock, and the battery side is willing to pay more.

## DAP price history

![[dap-price-history-aurelion.png]]
*DAP spot price, USD/t. Prices have pulled back from the mid-2025 $800+/t peak but the floor keeps rising. $400 (2023) → $550 (2024) → $650 (1Q 2026 base). Source: Bloomberg via Aurelion Research.*

The four-year chart shows a pattern: every spike is followed by a pullback, but each pullback settles higher than the one before. This is a market where tight supply-demand balance is real and gradually repricing. The $400/t of 2023 is not coming back under the current setup — short-run producer economics require $550-600/t to cover rising input costs (sulfur, ammonia, labor, energy).

| Period | DAP spot | Driver |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 2022 peak | ~$970/t | Russia-Ukraine shock |
| 2023 trough | $400/t | Chinese re-entry, energy normalization |
| 2024 base | $550/t | Sulfur costs rising, Chinese restraint |
| Mid-2025 peak | ~$800/t | Chinese export restrictions tighten |
| 1Q 2026 | $661/t | Off peak but above new $650/t floor |

## US phosphate imports — supply pressure in action

![[us-phosphate-imports-decline-aurelion.png]]
*[[United States|US]] imports of DAP (light) + MAP (dark) phosphate fertilizers. Peaked above 3M tonnes in 2018, declined steadily to ~2M tonnes in 2025. Source: Bloomberg, USDA via Aurelion Research.*

US phosphate imports are a stress test for what happens when supply tightens. Three factors compressed US imports over the past seven years: anti-dumping duties on Moroccan product (a [[Mosaic]]/[[JR Simplot]] petition win from 2021), a cycle-low US farm economy, and shrinking global availability.

The fallout is starting to hurt US farmers. In March 2026, 64 US agricultural groups published a joint letter urging [[Mosaic]] and [[JR Simplot]] — the two largest domestic phosphate fertilizer producers — to drop their support for duties on Moroccan imports. That letter tells the story: farm-gate buyers are running out of options and want every available trade route reopened. Whether the domestic producers relent, and whether the Trump administration acts on the petition, will shape 2026 planting economics in the US south and midwest.

## Phosphate and the 2026 Hormuz crisis

Phosphate's exposure to the [[Strait of Hormuz]] runs through two upstream inputs, not direct shipping:

### Sulfur

Phosphate rock cannot be processed into fertilizer without [[Sulfuric Acid]], which requires [[Elemental Sulfur]] as feedstock. About 50% of global sulfur trade is tied to Gulf producers — sulfur is a byproduct of sour crude/gas desulfurization. [[OCP Group]] imports ~3.7M mt of Gulf sulfur annually; [[China]] imports ~4M mt. When Hormuz closes, sulfur becomes scarce, which tightens phosphoric acid, which tightens DAP/MAP. This is why Chinese exports are at renewed risk in 2026 — it's not an export policy this time, it's a feedstock shortage.

### Ammonia

DAP and MAP both contain nitrogen (the "A" in DAP) — they're synthesized by combining phosphoric acid with [[Ammonia]]. Ammonia is primarily Gulf-produced from cheap natural gas. Hormuz disruption compresses ammonia availability, which flows directly into DAP/MAP production costs.

The phosphate market is in the unusual position of being hit indirectly from both sides. [[Saudi Arabia]]'s [[Ma'aden]] is the only major Gulf phosphate exporter directly caught in the Hormuz blockade, but the sulfur and ammonia bottlenecks affect every non-US phosphate producer globally.

## The LFP battery crossover

[[LFP batteries]] (lithium iron phosphate) now account for >60% of [[China]]'s battery market and are scaling globally. The cathode production pathway requires phosphoric acid — the same intermediate used to make DAP/MAP for agriculture. This creates direct substitution between food security and the energy transition for the same feedstock.

[[China]] has strategically positioned itself at the center of both. Chinese phosphate export restrictions are partly motivated by domestic LFP demand — keeping phosphoric acid available for battery cathodes rather than shipping DAP to foreign farmers. As LFP adoption accelerates outside China (US IRA battery subsidies favoring LFP, Ford and Tesla LFP rollouts), global phosphoric acid demand will pull further away from agriculture.

This is a multi-year structural tension that compounds with the near-term Hormuz disruption. Agricultural buyers face supply contraction and a new well-capitalized competitor for their feedstock at the same time.

## Phosphate rock reserves vs. production — the Morocco story

[[Morocco]] controls ~70% of global phosphate reserves but produces only 7% of current output. [[China]] produces 68% of rock but holds only ~5% of reserves. The gap is a long-duration strategic asset for [[OCP Group]] — as Chinese reserves deplete, Morocco's dominance compounds. OCP is investing $14.06B (2025-2027) to add 9M mt of capacity.

| Country | Reserves share | 2024 production (M mt rock) | Production share |
|---------|----------------|-----------------------------|------------------|
| [[Morocco]] | ~70% | 30 | 7% |
| [[China]] | ~5% | 303 | 68% |
| [[Russia]] | — | 14 | 3% |
| [[Saudi Arabia]] | — | 9.5 | 2% |
| [[United States|US]] | — | 9 | 2% |

The [[Western Sahara]] territorial dispute adds political risk: much of OCP's reserves sit in disputed territory. Any resolution (or escalation) could reshape the global reserve map.
