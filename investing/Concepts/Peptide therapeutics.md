# Peptide Therapeutics

Peptides occupy the molecular middle ground between small-molecule drugs (<500 Da) and large biologics (>10,000 Da), typically ranging from 500 to 5,000 Da with 2-50 amino acid residues. The global peptide therapeutics market reached ~$52.6B in 2025 (Precedence Research), with the broader estimates ranging up to $140.9B depending on scope (Grand View Research includes insulin analogs), and is projected to reach $100B+ by 2034 at a 8-11% CAGR. The real investment story is not the drugs themselves but the manufacturing bottleneck behind them: demand driven by [[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1]] blockbusters has overwhelmed global peptide CDMO capacity, triggering a multi-billion-dollar capex supercycle in solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) infrastructure that will reshape the contract manufacturing landscape through 2028.

---

## Synthesis

The peptide supply chain is experiencing a structural demand-supply mismatch that GLP-1 success only made visible. Around 120 peptide drugs are on the market, with 193+ obesity assets alone in development (IQVIA, Oct 2025). The peptide CDMO market grew from ~$3.8B in 2024 to ~$4.3-4.6B in 2025, with projections reaching $16.7B by 2032 at a 20.3% CAGR -- the fastest-growing segment in contract pharma. Capacity utilization at leading CDMOs exceeds 85%. Capital is flowing accordingly: $24.86B in total CDMO investment was disclosed in 2025, with 74% directed to the US in a geographic reshoring wave. [[CordenPharma]] alone committed more than EUR 1B ($1.1B) backed by multi-year customer contracts worth EUR 4B+ ($4.4B). But new SPPS capacity takes 18-24 months to become operational, and the 2025 Wang/2-chlorotrityl resin shortage pushed raw material prices up 40%, squeezing margins even at CDMOs running full utilization. The bottleneck has shifted from drug discovery to manufacturing -- and will persist until the current capex cycle delivers qualified GMP capacity in 2027-2028.

The regulatory environment adds a second dimension. HHS Secretary [[Robert F. Kennedy Jr.]] signaled on Feb 27, 2026 (Joe Rogan podcast) that the FDA would reclassify ~14 peptides from Category 2 (restricted) back to Category 1, potentially reopening the compounding pharmacy channel for non-GLP-1 peptides like BPC-157, Thymosin Alpha-1, and CJC-1295. Simultaneously, the FDA is cracking down on the compounded GLP-1 gray market that emerged during the 2022-2025 semaglutide shortage, with DOJ referrals and 50+ warning letters. The net effect: peptide demand is expanding across both the regulated pharma channel (new indications, next-gen molecules) and the wellness/longevity channel (deregulated compounding peptides), while manufacturing capacity remains constrained.

## The story

Peptides are chains of amino acids -- too large to be synthesized like aspirin, too small to be manufactured like monoclonal antibodies. A typical peptide drug has a molecular weight of 1,000-5,000 Da, placing it in a manufacturing no-man's land that requires specialized chemistry distinct from both small-molecule and biologic production. Small molecules dominate oral delivery (>60% of marketed drugs are pills), but peptides degrade in the stomach. Even the best oral peptide formulations achieve ~1% bioavailability -- [[Novo Nordisk]]'s Rybelsus (oral semaglutide) uses salcaprozate sodium (SNAC) as a permeation enhancer, but patients must take it on an empty stomach with limited water, and most of the dose is destroyed before absorption. This is why the vast majority of peptide drugs are injected: subcutaneous, intramuscular, or intravenous delivery bypasses the gastrointestinal barrier entirely.

The manufacturing process that makes peptide supply so constrained is solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS), developed by Bruce Merrifield in 1963 (Nobel Prize 1984). The peptide is built amino acid by amino acid on a solid resin support, with each coupling step requiring protected amino acids, coupling reagents, and solvent washes. For a 39-amino-acid peptide like semaglutide, that means 39 sequential coupling/deprotection cycles, each consuming expensive reagents and generating solvent waste. The alternative -- liquid-phase peptide synthesis (LPPS) -- avoids the resin but requires purification after each step, making it slower and more labor-intensive for longer chains. In practice, SPPS dominates for peptides above ~5 residues, while LPPS or hybrid approaches can be more economical for very short sequences or very large commercial scale. [[Cambrex]] developed an LPPS technology in 2024 that uses batch reactors and continuous flow for peptides up to 12 residues, with fragment coupling for longer chains -- but this remains niche.

The economics of SPPS are punishing at scale. Reagents and consumables command 47.9% of the peptide synthesis market (2025), reflecting the relentless demand for protected amino acids, resins, and coupling agents. Non-canonical amino acids -- essential for macrocyclic and stapled peptides -- carry premiums of up to 5x standard residues. In 2025, a shortage of Wang and 2-chlorotrityl resins (the solid supports used in SPPS) pushed raw material prices up 40%, compressing CDMO margins even as utilization ran above 85%. The cost structure is inverted compared to small molecules: raw materials and reagents dominate, not labor or equipment depreciation. This means scaling up peptide production requires both more SPPS reactor capacity (measured in liters of reactor volume) and secure supply of specialty chemicals.

The GLP-1 explosion turned this niche manufacturing challenge into an industry crisis. [[Novo Nordisk]] and [[Eli Lilly]] together generated ~$45B in GLP-1 revenue in 2024, rising toward $63-70B in 2025 as tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) grew 100%+ year-over-year. Semaglutide is a 39-residue peptide; tirzepatide is 39 residues with a fatty acid sidechain. Manufacturing these at the scale of tens of millions of patients requires SPPS capacity that simply did not exist three years ago. Both companies responded with vertical integration: [[Novo Nordisk]]'s parent Novo Holdings acquired [[Catalent]] for $16.5B in December 2024, with Novo Nordisk taking three fill-finish sites (Anagni, Bloomington, Brussels) for $11B. [[Eli Lilly]] doubled investment at its Concord, North Carolina facility to $2B and committed $5B for ADC manufacturing. Novo guided to doubling US supply capacity by mid-2026 relative to 2024.

But the pharma giants' in-house buildout only solves their own capacity problem. The broader peptide pipeline -- 193+ obesity assets, plus oncology, antimicrobial, autoimmune, and radiopharmaceutical programs -- depends on CDMOs. The CDMO capex response has been massive. [[CordenPharma]] is investing more than EUR 1B ($1.1B): $500M+ at its Colorado site to add 25,000 L of SPPS capacity (doubling to 42,000 L total by 2028), plus a greenfield facility in Muttenz, Switzerland with 5,000 L+ SPPS capacity. Backed by multi-year customer contracts with a total sales volume exceeding EUR 4B ($4.4B), CordenPharma targets EUR 1B+ in peptide platform revenue by 2028. [[Bachem]] (SIX: BANB) reported CHF 695.1M in 2025 sales (+14.8% in CHF, +19.2% in local currencies), with an EBITDA margin of 30.9%. Its Building K in Bubendorf passed inspection and will ramp commercial production in 2026; additional land acquisitions at Sisslerfeld (155,000 sqm for long-term expansion) and Vista, California expansion are underway. Bachem invested CHF 332.6M in capex in 2025 and plans CHF 400M+ for 2026, targeting CHF 1B+ annual sales and 30%+ EBITDA margin in 2026. [[PolyPeptide Group]] (SIX: PPGN) generated EUR 389M in 2025 revenue (+15.6%), with metabolic therapeutics growing 98.2% to 56% of H1 revenue. PolyPeptide invested EUR 110M in capex and doubled SPPS capacity in Malmo (EUR 100M expansion) and Braine-l'Alleud (new large-scale lines). Management targets doubling 2023 revenue by 2028 with 25% EBITDA margins.

The second tier of CDMOs is expanding aggressively to capture the overflow. [[SK Pharmteco]] committed $260M for a new 135,800 sqft facility in Sejong, South Korea with eight production trains capable of tens of metric tons annually, plus $6.1M for SPPS kilo-scale at Rancho Cordova, California -- both online in 2026. [[Almac]] doubled peptide API capacity with a 28,000 sqft GMP facility in Craigavon, Northern Ireland (2024). [[Axplora]] is investing EUR 50M at Mourenx, France for large-scale HPLC/continuous chromatography purification, targeting first GLP-1 supply in 2026. [[Cambrex]] committed $120M to expand US API and peptide manufacturing. [[Neuland Laboratories]] is investing $30M for a dedicated peptide facility in India with 6.37 KL of DSC automated synthesis capacity, Module I completion targeted H1 2026. [[Granules India]] acquired Swiss CDMO Senn Chemicals in April 2025 to enter the peptide space, with two GLP-1 API projects already in development. [[BioDuro]] opened a kilogram-scale SPPS lab in Shanghai in January 2025. [[Sai Life Sciences]] inaugurated a peptide research center in Hyderabad in 2025.

The geographic dimension matters. [[Switzerland]] alone contributes over 12% of global GMP peptide capacity (led by [[Bachem]] and [[PolyPeptide Group|PolyPeptide]]), while the US contributes 15-17% (led by [[AmbioPharm]], CPC Scientific, and CordenPharma Colorado). But 74% of the $24.86B in CDMO investment disclosed in 2025 flowed to the [[United States]], signaling a decisive reshoring of pharmaceutical manufacturing capacity. This is not purely commercial -- it reflects geopolitical incentives (BIOSECURE Act targeting [[WuXi Biologics]] and other Chinese CDMOs), tariff risk, and the [[Trump II|Trump administration]]'s executive order on US-based pharma manufacturing (May 2025).

Beyond GLP-1s, the peptide therapeutic pipeline spans several high-value areas. Peptide-drug conjugates (PDCs) -- peptides linked to cytotoxic payloads for targeted oncology delivery -- represent a ~29% CAGR market through 2032. Two PDCs are approved: lutetium-177 dotatate (Lutathera, neuroendocrine tumors) and melflufen (hematologic malignancies). BT8009 is in trials for advanced solid cancers. The broader antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) market hit ~$16B in H1 2025 sales; [[Eli Lilly]] committed $5B for ADC manufacturing. Peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT) -- using peptides to deliver radioactive isotopes to tumors -- is a high-value, low-volume niche growing at >15% annually (Pluvicto, [[Novartis]]). Antimicrobial peptides target antibiotic-resistant infections, with infectious disease PDCs projected as the fastest-growing segment at 19% CAGR through 2033.

The generic/biosimilar dynamics for peptides sit in a regulatory middle zone. Small-molecule generics use the straightforward ANDA pathway (demonstrate bioequivalence). Large biologics require the 351(k) biosimilar pathway (analytical similarity + clinical studies). Peptides fall awkwardly in between: those approved as drugs use the 505(b)(2) or ANDA pathways, while those approved as biologics require biosimilar applications. The March 2020 BPCIA transition moved many insulin and peptide products to the biologics pathway, making follow-on versions more expensive to develop. Characterizing a 39-amino-acid peptide is harder than a 20-atom small molecule but easier than a 1,300-amino-acid antibody -- so the regulatory burden is intermediate. In practice, this means peptide generics face higher development costs ($50-100M+ vs $1-5M for simple generics) and longer timelines, creating a wider competitive moat for innovators and more sustained pricing power.

The RFK Jr. peptide deregulation adds a distinct demand channel. On February 27, 2026, HHS Secretary Kennedy stated on Joe Rogan that the FDA would move approximately 14 of the 19 peptides previously placed on the FDA's Category 2 restricted list back to Category 1, restoring access through licensed compounding pharmacies with prescriptions. Category 2 peptides include BPC-157 (tissue repair), Thymosin Alpha-1 (immune modulation), TB-500 (muscle recovery), CJC-1295 and Ipamorelin (growth hormone release), AOD-9604 (fat metabolism), and Selank/Semax (cognitive function). These are not GLP-1 drugs -- they serve the biohacking, longevity, and sports recovery markets. Under 503A of the FDCA, lawful compounding requires pharmaceutical-grade API from FDA-registered manufacturers with valid certificates of analysis. If reclassification proceeds, it creates a new regulated demand channel requiring GMP peptide API supply. [[Peptide Sciences]], one of the largest unregulated online peptide sellers, has already announced voluntary shutdown -- a signal that the gray market may be closing as the regulated channel opens. A related lawsuit (Evexias v. FDA, N.D. Tex.) was administratively closed in August 2025 pending a final rule expected no later than March 14, 2027. No formal FDA rule has been published as of March 2026.

The cost-price disconnect in peptide drugs is staggering. A Yale/King's College London study estimated sustainable cost-based prices for GLP-1 agonists at $0.75-72.49/month, compared to list prices of $935-1,349/month. Manufacturing cost represents under 1% of the retail price for semaglutide. The [[Trump II|Trump administration]]'s pricing agreements (November 2025) brought Medicare/Medicaid prices to $245/month and DTC pricing to ~$350/month, but even these negotiated rates imply gross margins exceeding 90%. Generic semaglutide will enter [[Canada]] in Q3 2026, [[India]] and [[China]] in 2026 (expected at $15-40/month in [[India]]), and the US market around 2032 when core patents expire. The pricing trajectory points toward massive margin compression in the late 2020s-2030s, but the manufacturing capacity to produce generic peptides at scale also requires CDMO investment -- creating a second-order demand driver for the same infrastructure.
