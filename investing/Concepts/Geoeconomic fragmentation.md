# Geoeconomic fragmentation

Geoeconomic fragmentation is the policy-driven reversal of global economic integration along geopolitical lines — the umbrella the [[IMF]] research program uses to tie together what the vault otherwise tracks as separate symptoms: tariffs, export controls, friend-shoring, reserve diversification, and the splintering of payment systems. The term was coined by Aiyar et al. in a 2023 IMF Staff Discussion Note as "a policy-driven reversal of global economic integration, often guided by strategic considerations" — national or economic security, or reduced reliance on rivals — as distinct from the market-driven slowdown in trade growth ("slowbalization") that preceded it.

The distinction matters for markets: slowbalization was firms re-optimizing; fragmentation is governments redrawing the map. The first is cyclical and reversible, the second is structural and self-reinforcing once retaliation begins.

## The channels (IMF framework)

Aiyar et al. identify the channels along which the gains from integration once flowed, and along which the costs of fragmentation now fall. The vault holds a dedicated note for most of them — this concept is the spine that connects them.

| Channel | Mechanism | Vault leg |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Trade | Tariffs and barriers raise cost, lower efficiency, reroute flows | [[Trade]], [[US-China decoupling]], [[Global Value Chains]] |
| Technology diffusion | Export controls slow innovation and productivity catch-up | [[Export controls]], [[Weaponized interdependence]] |
| Capital / FDI | Cross-border investment concentrates within geopolitical blocs ("friend-shoring") | [[Nearshoring thesis]], [[Foreign direct investment]] |
| Finance / payments | Reserve diversification and parallel payment rails fracture the dollar system | [[Dollar reserve status erosion]], [[De-dollarization]] |
| Migration | Barriers to labor flows constrain human capital | — |
| Uncertainty | Policy unpredictability depresses investment directly | [[Trump II]], [[Liberation Day tariffs]] |

Aiyar, Malacrino, and Presbitero (2024) gave the framework its empirical teeth: greater geopolitical distance — or membership in an opposing bloc — measurably reduces bilateral FDI, trade, and cross-border bank claims. FDI in strategic sectors is increasingly routed to geopolitically aligned partners, the investable expression of the thesis.

## The measurement

The cleanest single read is "geopolitical distance of trade" — trade weighted by how far apart partners sit on UN General Assembly voting. The [[McKinsey]] Global Institute series shows it peaked around 2020-22 and has since declined: the world is trading more with the geopolitically near and less with the geopolitically far, even as raw goods-trade volumes keep hitting records. Fragmentation is showing up in the direction of trade before it shows up in the level.

## The self-defeating dynamic

The IMF's case against fragmentation is not only that it lowers global output (Fund estimates range to several percent of world GDP in severe scenarios) but that the individual moves are often self-cancelling. [[Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas]], the outgoing IMF chief economist, put it as a warning in June 2026: exploiting control of a chokepoint — critical minerals, rare earths, the [[Strait of Hormuz]] — works "for a little while," but rivals respond by building around it, so the advantage decays while the fixed cost of the parallel system stays. This is the [[Weaponized interdependence|chokepoint-blowback]] dynamic: every weaponization of a hub incentivizes counter-hub construction (CIPS, rare-earth refining outside China, bypass pipelines).

## The financial leg

Gourinchas's sharper claim is sequencing: "It's hard to imagine that we can be a fragmented world on the trade side without [eventually] being fragmented on the financial side." Trade fragmentation is the visible front; financial fragmentation — reserve diversification away from the dollar, parallel payment rails, local-currency settlement — is the larger and slower rupture it sets up. The trigger is dollar weaponization, which "creates the dynamics to move away from it." The vault tracks this leg in detail under [[Dollar reserve status erosion]] and [[Treasury safe asset erosion]]; the [[2022 Russian central bank reserve freeze]] is the inflection both notes date the acceleration from.

## The counter-current — bloc gravity

Fragmentation does not mean autarky; it means reorganization into blocs. The proliferation of bilateral and pluri-lateral deals — [[EU-Mercosur agreement|EU-Mercosur]], EU-India, the expansion of [[CPTPP]] and [[RCEP]], [[BRICS]] enlargement — is the constructive face of the same process. Gourinchas's metaphor: groups of integrating countries "create their own gravity… like forming a new planet." For investors this is where fragmentation becomes a positioning question rather than only a drag: the winners are inside the forming blocs (friend-shoring beneficiaries — [[India]], [[Mexico]], [[ASEAN]]), the losers are the global optimizers and the small economies caught between gravitational fields. The full winners/losers map lives in [[Trade]].

## Why it matters for markets

- Higher structural inflation and lower trade efficiency — fragmentation raises the cost base economy-wide, a tailwind under [[Inflation expectations]].
- A bid under gold and non-dollar reserve assets as the financial leg advances — see [[Dollar reserve status erosion]].
- A premium on supply-chain redundancy and "in-bloc" production; a discount on single-chokepoint dependence ([[Apple]] suppliers, German autos, pure-efficiency plays).
- Sector beneficiaries: defense, critical minerals, friend-shoring industrial capacity, dual-supply-chain operators. See [[Nearshoring thesis]].

## Synthesis

Geoeconomic fragmentation is the spine that connects threads the vault tracked separately: [[Liberation Day tariffs|tariffs]], [[Export controls|export controls]], [[Nearshoring thesis|friend-shoring]], [[Dollar reserve status erosion|reserve diversification]], and [[De-dollarization|payment-rail splintering]] are not five stories but one — the policy-driven, security-motivated reversal of integration the [[IMF]] named. The causal read-through that matters for positioning is the sequencing [[Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas]] flagged: trade fragmentation is the loud, visible front, but it sets up a slower and larger financial rupture, because a world that no longer trusts cross-border trade eventually stops trusting cross-border money. The self-defeating wrinkle — chokepoint advantages decay as rivals build around them — means the equilibrium is not a clean two-bloc world but a costly, redundant one where everyone pays to duplicate supply chains, payment systems, and strategic stockpiles. That redundancy is the investable core: it is structurally inflationary, it bids up [[Gold|gold]] and non-dollar reserve assets, it rewards in-bloc industrial capacity and dual-sourcing, and it discounts single-chokepoint dependence. The constructive counter-current — blocs that "create their own gravity" — turns the macro theme into a map question: which gravity well (US-aligned, China-aligned, or hedging like [[India]]) each economy and supply chain falls into is now a first-order determinant of where capital, FDI, and trade actually flow.
