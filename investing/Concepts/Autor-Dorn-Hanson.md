# Autor-Dorn-Hanson

"Autor-Dorn-Hanson" is the standard shorthand in economics and trade-policy literature for the 2013 *American Economic Review* paper *"The China Syndrome: Local Labor Market Effects of Import Competition in the United States"* by [[David Autor]], [[David Dorn]], and [[Gordon Hanson]]. The paper established the empirical foundation of the original [[China trade|China shock]] framework — and by extension set up the analytical scaffolding that the [[Second China shock]] debate extends and modifies.

The abbreviation "ADH" is used in follow-up literature as a shorthand for the whole program of research tracking trade's geographically-concentrated displacement effects on labor markets.

---

## What the paper actually did

The methodological innovation was the commuting-zone level exposure measure, identified instrumentally. Rather than studying national aggregates (which hide distribution effects) or industries (which hide worker immobility), ADH assigned each US commuting zone an exposure level based on its pre-existing industry mix interacted with the sectoral composition of Chinese imports. This created cross-sectional variation in shock intensity that they could track over time.

The instrumental-variable design used Chinese export growth to *other high-income countries* (Australia, Denmark, Finland, Germany, Japan, New Zealand, Spain, Switzerland) as the shock source — ensuring that the variation being measured reflected Chinese supply-side capacity growth, not US-specific demand.

---

## What they found

| Effect in exposed commuting zones | Finding |
|---|---|
| Manufacturing employment | Sharp and persistent decline |
| Non-manufacturing employment | Did not absorb displaced workers as theory predicted |
| Wages | Fell across the wage distribution, not just for manufacturing workers |
| Disability insurance | Sharp increase in enrollment |
| Other public transfers (food stamps, EITC) | Increased |
| Net fiscal transfers per capita | Increased substantially |
| Household income | Fell, persistently |
| Marriage rates, fertility | Fell |
| Political polarization (in later papers) | Increased |

The finding that exposed places *did not reallocate* to new sectors within a decade of the shock — the absence of Ricardian adjustment — was the key violation of trade-theory predictions and the empirical foundation for subsequent critiques of free-trade orthodoxy.

## Follow-up work extending the framework

- Autor, Dorn, Hanson, Majlesi (2016, 2020): linked China-shock exposure to political polarization, Tea Party vote share, and ultimately Trump 2016 vote share.
- Acemoglu, Autor, Dorn, Hanson, Price (2016): quantified job losses attributable to Chinese import competition at roughly 2.4 million US jobs between 1999 and 2011.
- Bloom, Draca, Van Reenen (2016): showed European evidence of similar employment effects but different firm-level technology-upgrading response.
- The full ADH research program has ~30 papers by 2026, many co-authored with other researchers, extending to robots, migration, and [[AI]].

---

## Why it matters for the Second China shock

The ADH framework provides three things that the [[Second China shock]] debate inherits:

1. **Methodological template** — commuting-zone or regional exposure measures, instrumented using third-country trade flows, are now standard in trade-labor economics.
2. **Empirical validation** — the finding that shocks are persistent and geographically concentrated justifies treating the 2020s wave as potentially another such shock rather than a transient cyclical phenomenon.
3. **Political-economy ammunition** — the link from China-shock exposure to populist voting created the evidence base for Trump-Biden-Trump 2.0 trade policy legitimacy. Whether the [[Second China shock]] produces similar political effects in Europe is an open empirical question the ADH methodology is being extended to answer.

---

## Critiques

- Daron Acemoglu and Pascual Restrepo have argued the ADH estimates of aggregate job losses are too large once general-equilibrium adjustments are considered, though they accept the commuting-zone level findings.
- Pol Antras and coauthors argue the paper underweights the gains-from-trade side of the ledger (lower consumer prices, variety).
- [[Paul Krugman]] has periodically argued the persistence finding may be specific to the 2000s US labor market and may not generalize to other episodes or countries.

These critiques are part of normal academic debate and do not overturn the core finding that trade shocks displace workers in geographically-concentrated ways that persist.
