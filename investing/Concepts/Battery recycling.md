# Battery Recycling

Recovery of critical minerals from end-of-life batteries and manufacturing scrap. Key to reducing [[China]] dependence and closing the battery supply chain loop.

---

## Synthesis

Battery recycling is the structural supply-side constraint on the lithium-deficit narrative that April 2026's sigma rally ([[Albemarle|ALB]] +16.3%, [[SQM]] +8.8%) is pricing. Fastmarkets' 2026 deficit, Wood Mackenzie's 2028 deficit, and IEA's 2030s deficit forecasts all model recycling as secondary supply that subtracts from primary-demand growth. At ~20% of demand by 2030 — roughly 500,000 tonnes lithium carbonate equivalent annually at that point — recycling effectively delivers one [[Sigma Lithium]] Grota do Cirilo Phase 2 worth of lithium without mining. Tesla reports 92% critical-mineral recovery from 4680 cells; Redwood Materials claims 95% lithium recovery from scrap. Hydrometallurgy processes are already 33-53% cheaper than mining new materials across multiple cathode chemistries.

The constraint is scale-up execution, and the 2023-2024 Western commercial push hit real walls. [[Li-Cycle]] paused construction on its Rochester, New York hydrometallurgical plant in October 2023 — the stock fell 90%+ from 2022 peaks, and the project remains stalled in April 2026. The broader issue is structural: freestanding Western recyclers compete on feedstock sourcing and transportation economics without captive battery manufacturing to lock in offtake. [[Redwood Materials]] (JB Straubel, former [[Tesla]] CTO) has scaled faster than peers because of deep OEM partnerships ([[Ford]], [[Toyota]], [[BMW]], [[Panasonic]]) providing vertical demand, but Redwood remains private and is outpaced in absolute capacity by CATL's Brunp subsidiary at 120,000 tonnes per year in Foshan alone. The Chinese model (recycling inside the battery manufacturing entity) dominates the economics; the Western arms-length model has not worked at scale yet.

The counter-example is the timing mismatch between EV-battery lifecycles and current recycling capacity. Most end-of-life batteries reaching recycling in 2026 are from 2015-2018 EV vintages — a small fraction of global production. The 2018-2023 EV wave (global sales 2M → 14M annually) will not reach end-of-life until 2030-2040. Today's recycling feedstock is 60-70% manufacturing scrap (black mass from cell production errors), not EOL batteries. The "20% of demand by 2030" forecast assumes facilities that do not yet exist can ramp as the EV end-of-life wave arrives — so the near-term binding constraint is building recycling capacity ahead of that feedstock wave, not processing what already exists. US [[IRA]] domestic-content tax credits and the EU Battery Passport regulation (effective February 2027) are the industrial-policy attempts to pre-fund that capacity ahead of Chinese vertical integration capturing the 2030s recycling feedstock the way it captured the 2020s primary-mining feedstock.

One line: recycling is the lithium market's built-in 2030s supply backstop — every tonne recovered is a tonne not mined — and the only question is whether Western recyclers scale ahead of the EOL wave or cede another segment to Chinese vertical integration.

## Recycling process

```
Collection → Shredding → Black mass → Hydromet/Pyromet → Refined materials
                                            ↓
                              Lithium, Cobalt, Nickel, [[Copper]]
                                            ↓
                              Cathode/Anode precursors
```

| Method | Process | Recovery rate | Players |
|--------|---------|---------------|---------|
| **Hydrometallurgical** | Chemical leaching | 95%+ | Redwood, Li-Cycle |
| **Pyrometallurgical** | Smelting | 70-80% | Umicore |
| **Direct recycling** | Preserve cathode structure | Highest | Early stage |
