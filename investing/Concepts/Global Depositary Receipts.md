# Global Depositary Receipts

A Global Depositary Receipt (GDR) is a [[Depositary receipts|depositary receipt]] that lets a company — usually from an emerging market — raise capital and trade outside its home country without a direct local listing, most often on the London Stock Exchange or in Luxembourg. After the [[American Depositary Receipts|ADR]] it is the main international DR, and the standard vehicle through which Indian, Russian, Gulf, and other emerging-market issuers historically tapped non-domestic capital. A depositary bank holds the underlying shares with a home-market custodian and issues GDRs against them at a set ratio; holders can convert the receipts back into the underlying shares.

## The two regulatory structures

A GDR is typically issued in two tranches that segregate the investor base:

- Rule 144A GDR — placed privately to US qualified institutional buyers (QIBs) under SEC Rule 144A, giving access to US institutional capital without a full SEC registration.
- Reg S GDR — sold to non-US investors under Regulation S, trading on London or Luxembourg.

Together they let an issuer reach both US institutions and international investors in a single offering, which is why the GDR became the default cross-border capital-raise instrument for EM companies before more of them listed directly.

## Relation to the ADR

The GDR and the [[American Depositary Receipts|ADR]] are close cousins: both are dollar-denominated depositary receipts on foreign shares, and a Rule 144A GDR is functionally a privately-placed ADR. The distinction is venue and audience — the ADR is built for US public exchanges (NYSE/Nasdaq), the GDR for London/Luxembourg and private US institutional placement. Some issuers run both.

## Synthesis

The GDR is the internationally-portable member of the [[Depositary receipts]] family — the one designed not for a single foreign market but to raise capital across several at once. Its defining feature is the 144A/Reg S split that lets one EM issuer tap US institutions and European investors simultaneously. As more emerging-market companies have listed directly or via full ADRs, the GDR's role has narrowed, but it remains the standard wrapper for a cross-border capital raise where a full US public listing is not wanted.
