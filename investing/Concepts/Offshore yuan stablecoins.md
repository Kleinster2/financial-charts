# Offshore yuan stablecoins

[[Stablecoins]] pegged 1:1 to the offshore yuan ([[Chinese yuan|CNH]]). As of April 2026, no sovereign-approved CNH stablecoin has launched, but a pipeline is forming around [[Hong Kong]]'s Stablecoins Ordinance (effective Aug 1, 2025) and adjacent offshore jurisdictions ([[Kazakhstan]]'s AIFC). Structurally distinct from the [[Digital Yuan]] (e-CNY), which is a state-issued CBDC, not a privately-issued fiat-referenced token.

The strategic question: can [[China|Beijing]] internationalize the yuan without opening the capital account? A permissioned offshore stablecoin is one possible answer — foreign counterparties transact in yuan on-chain without triggering onshore flows. The tension: Beijing has spent years building e-CNY as the sovereign digital option, and eight domestic regulators reaffirmed the mainland ban on unauthorized yuan-backed stablecoins after speculative marketing surged in late 2025.

## Regulatory framework

### Hong Kong Stablecoins Ordinance

Passed May 21, 2025. Gazetted June 6, 2025. Effective August 1, 2025. [[HKMA]] is the issuer licensor. Scope: any fiat-referenced stablecoin issued in Hong Kong, plus any HKD-pegged token issued anywhere. Core requirements:

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Reserve backing | 1:1, high-quality liquid assets, segregated |
| Custody | HKMA-approved custodians |
| Redemption | Par-value redemption within a reasonable window |
| Fit-and-proper | Senior management vetting, operational resilience |
| Application window | Deadline was Sept 30, 2025 |

First license batch expected in early 2026. As of April 2026 no CNH-pegged token has been approved, though several major applicants are in review.

### Kazakhstan AFSA

Kazakhstan's Astana International Financial Centre (AIFC) operates as an offshore common-law financial hub under the Astana Financial Services Authority (AFSA). AFSA granted [[AnchorX]] preliminary approval for AxCNH — the first clearance of a CNH-pegged stablecoin anywhere. The approval targets cross-border settlement for [[Belt and Road]] trade corridors, positioning AIFC as a yuan-settlement gateway for Central Asia.

### Mainland China

Eight state regulators ([[PBOC]], CAC, SASAC, MPS, and others) reaffirmed China's ban on all crypto activity — including unauthorized issuance of yuan-backed stablecoins — in a joint 2025–2026 statement following a wave of speculative promotions on Chinese social media. The mainland ban does not restrict Hong Kong or offshore jurisdictions from operating licensed CNH stablecoins, but it signals Beijing's preference for [[Digital Yuan]] on the domestic rail.

## Why this matters

### Yuan internationalization without capital-account opening

[[Chinese yuan]] internationalization has been constrained by the PBOC's unwillingness to open the capital account. The CNY/CNH split is the workaround — onshore yuan stays controlled, offshore yuan trades semi-freely. A permissioned CNH stablecoin extends that logic onto programmable rails: foreign counterparties hold and transfer yuan via smart contracts without touching onshore banking infrastructure.

### BRI trade settlement

A meaningful share of [[China]]'s trade settles via USD correspondent banking. For smaller commodity and manufactured-goods flows with [[Belt and Road]] partners — particularly in Central Asia, Southeast Asia, and Africa — SWIFT fees and USD FX spreads are a friction. A CNH stablecoin routed through [[Conflux Network]] or similar infrastructure could compete on cost, aligning trade settlement with Beijing's diplomatic economic map.

### Response to USD stablecoin dominance

USD-pegged stablecoins ([[Tether|USDT]] ~$187B, [[Circle|USDC]] ~$45B) dominate the ~$299B global stablecoin market and function as the de facto dollar-access layer in emerging markets. The reserve mechanics — [[Tether]] alone holds $90B+ in US T-bills — create indirect fiscal and monetary extraterritoriality that Beijing has long flagged as a strategic concern. A CNH alternative would not displace USDT/USDC in core dollar-demand markets but could anchor a parallel rail in yuan-denominated trade zones. See [[US-China finance]].

## CNH stablecoin vs [[Digital Yuan]]

Beijing has spent years building e-CNY as a sovereign-issued, two-tier retail CBDC. A private CNH stablecoin is a structural rival, not a complement:

| Feature | e-CNY (CBDC) | CNH stablecoin |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| Issuer | [[PBOC]] | Private, licensed |
| Users | Primarily domestic retail | Primarily offshore institutional |
| Programmability | Limited (PBOC-controlled) | Smart contract native |
| Reserves | Sovereign liability (n/a) | 1:1 fiat / T-bills / deposits |
| Surveillance | Full PBOC visibility | Issuer-level KYC |
| Cross-border | Pilot (mBridge) | Native |

The likely equilibrium: e-CNY as domestic retail rail, CNH stablecoin as offshore institutional rail for BRI corridors under tight [[HKMA]]/AFSA oversight. [[JD.com]] and [[Ant Group]] are positioning as the licensed private issuers.

## Synthesis

The yuan stablecoin pipeline is a proxy for a twenty-year contradiction: [[China]] wants a globally-used currency without ceding PBOC control over domestic capital flows. Every prior workaround — the [[Chinese yuan|CNY]]/CNH split, swap lines with [[BRICS]] partners, the Shanghai Gold Exchange's yuan-denominated bullion contract, mBridge — has hit the same wall. Offshore financial plumbing grows, but meaningful foreign demand never follows, because the exit optionality depends on a capital account the PBOC will not open.

A licensed offshore CNH stablecoin is the next attempt, and its structural logic is cleaner than earlier tries: the token lives entirely offshore, the reserves sit in HKMA-approved custodians, and the rail is programmable. Trade counterparties in [[Belt and Road]] corridors can hold and transfer yuan without ever needing to cross the mainland border. If it works, it routes around the capital-account constraint instead of arguing with it.

The counter-example is AxCNH. [[AnchorX]] has preliminary clearance, Conflux infrastructure, and TokenPocket distribution — but no demonstrated trade volume, no audited reserves, no mainland counterparty confirming they will settle in it. The infrastructure question is solved; the demand question is not. [[JD.com]] and [[Ant Group]] bring distribution scale but cannot move without a PBOC nod that hasn't come.

The binding constraint isn't technology or regulation — it's whether Beijing decides an institutional offshore yuan rail is worth the mainland-leakage risk it creates.

## Risks and open questions

- Reserve transparency — will CNH stablecoin reserves sit with [[HKMA]]-approved custodians (visible) or with mainland banks (opaque)? The answer decides whether institutional counterparties outside China will trust the token.
- Capital-control leakage — an offshore CNH token creates new potential channels for mainland residents routing funds through crypto. Beijing's response to any perceived leakage could tighten licensing on the offshore side.
- Addressable market — [[Chinese yuan|CNH]] daily FX turnover is a small fraction of USD turnover. The CNH stablecoin opportunity is structurally smaller than the USD stablecoin market, even in success cases.
- Beijing policy reversal — if the mainland crypto ban extends pressure to offshore HK activity (as happened in 2021 with crypto exchanges), licensed issuers could be squeezed mid-operation.
- US response — the [[GENIUS Act]] framework gives USD stablecoins a regulatory moat. If CNH stablecoins scale in BRI corridors, watch for US restrictions on bridges between the two ecosystems.
