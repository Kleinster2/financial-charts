**Section 301** — Trade Act of 1974 provision (19 U.S.C. §§2411-2420) authorizing [[USTR]] to investigate and retaliate against foreign trade practices that are unjustifiable, unreasonable, or discriminatory. Now the primary US tariff authority after [[SCOTUS IEEPA ruling|SCOTUS struck down IEEPA tariffs]] (Feb 20, 2026). No rate cap, no duration limit — the most powerful trade remedy tool Congress has delegated to the executive.

---

## Synthesis

Section 301 has gone from a Cold War-era crowbar for prying open Japanese markets to the central pillar of US trade policy. The trajectory: heavy use in the 1980s (130 cases, mostly [[Japan]]) → near-dormancy during WTO era (1995-2016) → revival under Trump for the [[China]] trade war (2018) → Biden's four-year review expanded rates into strategic sectors (EVs 100%, semis 50%) → and now, post-SCOTUS, the primary vehicle for replacing struck-down IEEPA tariffs.

The March 2026 expansion is the inflection. [[USTR]] launched Section 301 investigations into 76 economies (16 for excess capacity, 60 for forced labor) — covering virtually every major US trading partner. This is a radical departure from Section 301's history of targeting one country at a time. The constraint is procedural: investigations take ~12 months, meaning replacement tariffs can't land before early-to-mid 2027. The market is pricing a tariff gap between IEEPA's death (Feb 2026) and Section 301's replacement tariffs (~early 2027).

The legal foundation is solid. [[HMTX Industries v United States|Federal Circuit upheld]] Section 301 tariffs unanimously (Sep 2025). SCOTUS distinguished Section 301 from IEEPA precisely because Section 301 contains explicit tariff-delegation language. The four-year statutory review provides democratic accountability that IEEPA lacked. For the foreseeable future, Section 301 is the tariff authority that matters.

## Legal mechanics

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Statute | Trade Act of 1974, Title III (P.L. 93-618) |
| Codified | 19 U.S.C. §§2411-2420 |
| Authority | [[USTR]] (transferred from President in 1988) |
| Rate cap | None |
| Duration limit | None (but 4-year statutory review required) |
| Investigation required | Yes (~12 months non-trade-agreement; ~18 months trade-agreement) |
| Public comment | Yes (hearings + written submissions) |
| Country targeting | Yes (country-specific) |

### Actionable conduct

Four categories trigger Section 301:

1. Denial of US trade agreement rights — violations, benefit denials. Retaliation is mandatory if confirmed.
2. Unjustifiable acts — violate international legal rights (IP protection, national treatment, establishment rights). Retaliation mandatory if burdening US commerce.
3. Unreasonable acts — unfair/inequitable but not violating legal rights (export targeting, market access denial, forced technology transfer). Retaliation discretionary.
4. Discriminatory acts — denying national or MFN treatment.

### Investigation process

1. Initiation — private petition (USTR decides within 45 days) or USTR self-initiation under §302(b)
2. Consultation — USTR must request talks with the foreign government
3. WTO referral — if a WTO agreement is at issue and consultations fail by day 150, USTR must request WTO dispute settlement
4. Section 301 Committee investigation — staff-level body under Trade Policy Staff Committee (TPSC)
5. Public hearings and comment periods
6. Determination — within 12 months (non-trade-agreement) or 18 months (trade-agreement cases)
7. Four-year review — tariffs expire unless a domestic industry files for continuation (hard statutory requirement)

## Comparison of tariff authorities

| Feature | Section 301 | [[Section 232 semiconductor tariff|Section 232]] | IEEPA | Section 122 | Section 338 |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Statute | Trade Act 1974 | Trade Expansion Act 1962 | 50 USC 1701 | Trade Act 1974 | Tariff Act 1930 |
| Authority | USTR | President (Commerce rec.) | President | President | President |
| Trigger | Unfair trade practices | National security threat | National emergency | Balance-of-payments deficit | Foreign discrimination |
| Investigation | Yes (~12 mo.) | Yes (~9 mo.) | No | No | Ambiguous |
| Public comment | Yes | Yes | No | No | Unclear |
| Rate cap | None | None | N/A (struck down) | 15% | 50% |
| Duration | 4-year review | None | N/A | 150 days | None stated |
| Country targeting | Yes | Yes or global | Yes | Must be global | Yes |
| Historical usage | 130 cases since 1974 | Steel, aluminum, semis | Feb 2025–Feb 2026 | Invoked Feb 2026 | Never used |
| Post-SCOTUS status | Fully intact | Fully intact | Struck down | Available | Available |

The critical distinction: Section 301 and 232 contain explicit statutory language delegating tariff authority. IEEPA's "regulate...importation" language does not mention tariffs, duties, or taxes. The [[Supreme Court]] held tariffs are "a branch of the taxing power" reserved for Congress — IEEPA's nine operative verbs authorize freezes and prohibitions, not revenue generation.

## History

### Pre-China era (1975-2016)

130 total investigations since 1974. Heavy use in the 1980s (49 cases), primarily targeting [[Japan]] on [[Semiconductors|semiconductors]], auto parts, agriculture, telecommunications.

| Period | Use pattern |
|--------|------------|
| 1975-1979 | Early cases against European Community (citrus, soybeans, canned fruits) |
| 1980s | Peak usage — Reagan-era [[Japan]] market-opening campaigns |
| 1989-1990 | Super 301 — statutory requirement to identify "priority" unfair trading countries (expired 1991) |
| 1995-2016 | Near-dormancy — WTO Dispute Settlement Understanding provided multilateral forum |
| 2013 | Last pre-Trump investigation ([[Ukraine]] IPR practices) |

### Key legislative amendments

- 1979 (Trade Agreements Act): established investigation timetables
- 1984 (Omnibus Tariff and Trade Act): expanded to services, investment, IP; defined actionable conduct categories; granted USTR self-initiation
- 1988 (Omnibus Trade and Competitiveness Act): transferred authority from President to USTR; added mandatory retaliation; created Super 301 and Special 301
- 1994 (Uruguay Round Agreements Act): clarified WTO dispute settlement relationship

### Trump-era China investigation (2017-2020)

| Date | Action |
|------|--------|
| Aug 14, 2017 | USTR self-initiates investigation into [[China]]'s technology transfer, IP, innovation practices |
| Mar 22, 2018 | Determination: China's acts "unreasonable or discriminatory" |
| Jul 6, 2018 | List 1 — 25% on $34B (818 HTS lines, industrial/tech) |
| Aug 23, 2018 | List 2 — 25% on $16B (279 lines, semiconductors, chemicals) |
| Sep 24, 2018 | List 3 — 10% on $200B (5,745 lines); raised to 25% May 10, 2019 |

WTO challenge (DS543): [[China]] challenged at WTO. Panel found US tariffs inconsistent with GATT (Sep 15, 2020). US argued Article XX(a) public morals defense. No practical effect — US did not comply.

### Bessent timeline — April 2026

At the WSJ CEO Council on April 14, 2026, [[Scott Bessent]] said [[USTR]] Section 301 investigations would allow tariffs to be "back in place at the previous level" by the beginning of July 2026. He cited Section 301's durability: "survived more than 5,000 legal challenges." Bessent attributed the pre-ruling capex slowdown to legal uncertainty, arguing corporates "held up their capex because of the lawsuit." Post-ruling: "using Section 301, they will be certain of what the tariff policy is." He reported capex intentions and construction jobs now picking up, citing [[Boeing]] expanding the Charleston Dreamliner facility by 50% (1,000 manufacturing jobs, 400 construction jobs upfront).

The stated July 2026 timeline is faster than the ~12-month investigation schedule implied by the standard Section 301 process — suggesting expedited implementation or reliance on pre-existing determinations.

*Source: WSJ Opinion, Paul Gigot interview, 2026-04-14*

### Post-SCOTUS expansion (Mar 2026)

After the [[SCOTUS IEEPA ruling]] struck down IEEPA tariffs, [[USTR]] pivoted to Section 301 as the primary replacement:

| Date | Action | Scope |
|------|--------|-------|
| Mar 11, 2026 | Excess capacity investigations | 16 economies ([[China]], [[EU]], [[Singapore]], [[Switzerland]], [[Norway]], [[Indonesia]], [[Malaysia]], Cambodia, [[Thailand]], [[South Korea|Korea]], [[Vietnam]], [[Taiwan]], Bangladesh, [[Mexico]], [[Japan]], [[India]]) |
| Mar 12, 2026 | Forced labor investigations | 60 economies (virtually all major US trading partners) |

Excess capacity sectors: aluminum, automobiles, batteries, cement, chemicals, electronics, energy, glass, machine tools, machinery, paper, plastics, processed food, robotics, satellites, semiconductors, ships, solar modules, steel, transportation equipment.

Comment deadline: Apr 15, 2026 (excess capacity); hearing May 5, 2026.
Forced labor hearing: Apr 28, 2026.

This is unprecedented — Section 301 has never been used against this many economies simultaneously. The 12-month investigation timeline means replacement tariffs likely cannot take effect before early-to-mid 2027.

## Legal durability

### Federal Circuit: *HMTX Industries v. United States* (Sep 25, 2025)

Unanimous three-judge panel upheld Section 301 Lists 3 and 4A tariffs. Rejected:
- APA challenges
- Major questions doctrine arguments
- Constitutional delegation claims

Found Section 307(a)(1)(C) independently authorizes USTR to modify and escalate tariffs — the authority is statutory, not just delegated presidential power.

### SCOTUS: *Learning Resources Inc. v. Trump* (Feb 20, 2026)

6-3 ruling struck down IEEPA tariffs but explicitly distinguished Section 301:
- Section 301 contains explicit tariff-delegation language ("duties or other import restrictions")
- IEEPA's nine operative verbs + eleven transaction types authorize freezes/prohibitions, not revenue generation
- Section 301 has ~50 years of tariff use; IEEPA had never been used for tariffs before Feb 2025
- Section 301 requires formal investigation, public comment, and hearings — procedural safeguards IEEPA lacked
