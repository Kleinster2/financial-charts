# Iran conflict FX dynamics

FX market response to [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]] — how the dollar, euro, yen, and EM currencies are repricing around the war. The dollar initially rallied on safe-haven flows but the move is structurally fragile, sitting on top of the deepest short positioning since 2020. The longer the war runs, the more it accelerates [[De-dollarization|de-dollarization]] dynamics already in motion.

---

## Synthesis

The dollar's war rally is a positioning squeeze, not a conviction move. Traders have been net short the dollar since December — the currency is down ~12% since early 2025 — and when bombs dropped, those shorts covered. DXY jumped from ~97 to ~99.4 in four days. But Reuters' poll of 60 FX strategists (conducted during the first week of fighting) shows the consensus *hasn't changed*: EUR/USD at $1.18 by end-March, $1.20 by September, two Fed cuts still expected by year-end. The war bid is a tactical unwind, not a structural repricing.

The deeper story is that the conflict is accelerating the slow erosion of dollar dominance. The Guardian frames it well: this isn't about one currency replacing the dollar (the way the dollar replaced sterling post-WWII) but a more complex multipolar system emerging. Central bank dollar reserves have slipped from 71% (2001) to 57% (Q4 2025). The ECB is building repo lines as euro lender-of-last-resort infrastructure. BRICS are working on swap lines and interoperable CBDCs. Each US act of aggression — tariffs, sanctions, freezing assets, bombing Iran — makes the case for self-insurance stronger.

For positioning: the war creates a short-term dollar bid (short covering + oil inflation = delayed Fed cuts) but a medium-term dollar headwind (erosion of safe-haven credibility, accelerated de-dollarization). The pivot point is peace negotiations — when Iran's outreach hit the NYT on March 4, the dollar fell the most in a month. If the conflict resolves quickly (the equity market's base case), the dollar gives back the war premium and resumes its 2025-2026 downtrend. If it drags on, oil above $90 could push DXY above 102 (Morgan Stanley's extended war scenario) but at the cost of further structural damage to dollar hegemony.

## The positioning story

The dollar rally isn't typical safe-haven behavior. Key data points:

- Traders **net short USD since December** — longest sustained short positioning since 2020
- DXY down ~12% since start of 2025 before the conflict
- JP Morgan flows data showed deleveraging signals appearing *before* strikes began — some traders already sensing Iran risk
- **JP Morgan estimate:** if dollar shorts fully cover back to flat, that's worth +1.5-2% to DXY from current levels
- **Reuters poll (60 analysts, Mar 4):** medians unchanged — EUR/USD $1.18 end-March, $1.19 in 3 months, $1.20 in 6 months

Jane Foley (Rabobank head of FX strategy): "Is the dollar as safe as it used to be? Probably not, because if it was, we wouldn't be having this debate."

Only 5 of 45 currency strategists expect a reversal to net long dollar by end-March. 21 expect no change or increased shorts.

## EUR/USD: terms-of-trade shock

The euro is the primary victim of the energy channel:

- **EUR/USD hit $1.1528** on Mar 4 — three-month low, breaching 200-day MA ($1.1580) and 50-day MA ($1.1608)
- RSI dropped from overbought 66 (late Jan) to 42 — bearish momentum confirmed
- MACD histogram turned negative late February

**Why Europe gets hit harder:**
- Europe imports the vast majority of its energy; US is increasingly energy-independent
- [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure disrupts 20%+ of global oil supply — direct hit to European terms of trade
- [[ECB]] already cut to 2.00% deposit rate with limited room to ease further
- If oil sustains above $80, eurozone inflation could re-accelerate from ~2% toward 3%

**Analyst views:**
- [[Goldman Sachs]]: maintains $1.25 EUR/USD year-end target (implying war impact is transitory)
- [[JP Morgan]]: consolidation at $1.16-$1.18 near-term, floor at $1.15
- [[Morgan Stanley]]: extended war scenario = DXY above 102, EUR/USD toward $1.10-$1.12

The $1.15 level is critical. Below that would coincide with oil above $90 — the extended war scenario that equity markets are not pricing.

## Yen: caught between haven and energy

[[Japan]] faces contradictory forces:

- **Safe-haven bid** pulling yen stronger (lower USD/JPY)
- **Energy import shock** pulling yen weaker (higher USD/JPY)
- **BOJ rate hike expectations pushed out** to July from earlier timing
- Finance Minister Katayama explicitly threatening intervention if dollar breaks above ¥158

BOJ Governor Ueda acknowledged the paradox directly: higher oil could push down the price trend *or* boost inflation through elevated price expectations.

USD/JPY range: ¥156.85-158.00, with ¥158 as the intervention line in the sand.

Key divergence: oil *exporters* (MXN should benefit from higher crude but peso still weakened — risk-off sentiment overwhelming fundamentals) vs oil *importers* (INR, KRW getting hammered on terms-of-trade deterioration).

### India — the acute case (March 2026)

India is the most detailed case study of the oil-importer FX trap. The [[Reserve Bank of India]] has burned through >$20B in FX reserves in under three weeks defending the [[Indian rupee]], which hit a record low of ~93.2/USD on Mar 19. The RBI's net-short book (forward dollar sales) has grown to >$100B. Despite ₹1T ($10.7B) in government bond purchases in March alone, the 10Y yield has climbed ~0.2pp YTD to ~6.7%.

[[Goldman Sachs]] ranks India alongside [[Thailand]] and [[Philippines]] as the most vulnerable Asian economies to war fallout, forecasting INR at 95/USD over 12 months. Foreign portfolio investors have pulled ~$10B from Indian equities YTD (~$8B in March alone). [[Standard Chartered]] sees the current account deficit widening to 2.5% of GDP from a prior top-end estimate of ~1%, with import cover falling from 10 months toward the 9-month threshold where RBI appetite to defend wanes.

The Gulf dimension is uniquely punishing for India: the region is India's largest trading partner and the source of >$50B in annual remittances from millions of Indian workers — a crucial buffer for external accounts now at risk. See [[Indian rupee]] for the full RBI defense toolkit and analyst views.

*Source: FT, Mar 19 2026*

## De-dollarization acceleration

The conflict adds fuel to a structural shift already underway:

| Metric | Level | Trend |
|--------|-------|-------|
| USD share of global reserves | 57% (Q4 2025) | Down from 71% in 2001 |
| Trade-weighted dollar | -7% past year | Despite strong US growth |
| Gold in CB reserves | > [[Treasuries]] | First time since 1996 |
| Renminbi trade settlement | Rising | Beijing actively facilitating |

**New infrastructure being built:**
- [[ECB]] beefing up repo lines — euro lender-of-last-resort for future crises
- [[BRICS]] working on swap lines and interoperable CBDCs
- China building strategic oil reserves — reduces dependence on dollar-denominated spot market

The Guardian's Heather Stewart frames it as "weaponised interdependence" — each time the US leverages financial dominance (sanctions, asset freezes, tariffs, now military strikes in the Persian Gulf), the incentive for self-insurance grows. Mark Carney at Davos 2026: "Great powers have begun using economic integration as weapons, tariffs as leverage, financial infrastructure as coercion, supply chains as vulnerabilities to be exploited."

## Rate expectations repricing

| Cut timing | Pre-war | Post-war (Mar 4) | Shift |
|------------|---------|-------------------|-------|
| June Fed cut | Fully priced (as of Feb 10) | ~55% probability | Major pullback |
| Total 2026 cuts | ~3 cuts (75bp) | ~2 cuts (50bp) | -25bp |
| First cut | July | September | +2 months |
| ECB next move | Paused at 2.00% | Paused; inflation risk rising | Hawkish tilt |
| BOJ next hike | Earlier 2026 | July | Pushed out |

The energy-driven inflation repricing is working *against* the traditional war playbook. In past conflicts, flight-to-safety compressed yields and accelerated rate cuts. Here, oil pushing toward $80+ is *raising* inflation expectations, delaying cuts, and keeping the dollar bid via rate differentials — even as the structural safe-haven appeal erodes.
