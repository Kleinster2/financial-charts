# Nitrogen

Nitrogen fertilizer — the "N" in NPK. Nitrogen is the largest of the three primary nutrient segments at ~109M tonnes annual consumption (~58% of global fertilizer nutrient demand). Unlike [[Phosphate]] and [[Potash]], which come from mined rock and salt deposits, nitrogen fertilizer is *synthesized* from atmospheric N₂ via the [[Haber-Bosch]] process, making natural gas the primary feedstock. This fundamentally different supply chain — manufactured rather than extracted — shapes the market's geography, economics, and geopolitical exposure.

---

## Synopsis

Nitrogen is the manufactured nutrient. Phosphate and potash are dug out of the ground; nitrogen is made in factories that consume roughly 3-5% of the world's natural gas to split atmospheric N₂ into ammonia. That structural fact drives everything downstream: production concentrates wherever gas is cheapest ([[Qatar]], [[Saudi Arabia]], [[Russia]], [[Trinidad and Tobago]], [[United States|US]] Gulf Coast), capacity can be built in 3-5 years (vs. decades for potash mines), and the whole chain rises and falls with gas prices. The 2022 European gas spike shut down ~30% of EU nitrogen capacity almost overnight; the 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] crisis is the same story in reverse — Gulf gas is cheap but cargoes can't leave, creating the [[Nitrogen Trap]].

The product chain runs from [[Ammonia]] (the base molecule, NH₃) to three main finished products: [[urea]] (46% N, the dominant traded form at ~50% of nitrogen consumption), AN/CAN (ammonium nitrate/calcium ammonium nitrate, ~8%), UAN solutions, and DAP/MAP (which are phosphate products that also contain nitrogen). Direct-application ammonia accounts for ~3%. NPK blends consume another 17% of the nitrogen segment. The market is more liquid and more commoditized than phosphate or potash — urea trades on futures (CBOT, DCE, MATBA), spot prices are quoted daily at multiple benchmarks (Egypt fob, China fob, US Gulf fob, Brazil cfr), and re-routing capacity between regions is routine.

Supply is concentrated in gas-rich regions but spread across more countries than phosphate or potash. [[China]] is the largest producer (~40M mt urea capacity) but operates with swing-export behavior — shutting exports when domestic food security is tight, reopening under quotas. The Persian Gulf ([[Qatar]], [[Saudi Arabia]], [[UAE]], [[Iran]], [[Oman]]) is the single most concentrated export cluster, routing through [[Strait of Hormuz]]. [[Russia]] is ~15% of global trade. [[United States|US]] is self-sufficient via [[CF Industries]], Nutrien, and Koch. Europe is the structural loser — high gas costs since 2022 have made it a chronic importer.

Demand is growing but slower than phosphate or potash on a percentage basis, because nitrogen is closer to demographic saturation in major markets. Growth comes from [[India]], Africa, and Southeast Asia. The wild card is [[industrial nitrogen]] — [[DEF]] (diesel exhaust fluid, NOx control), mining explosives (AN), power plant NOx reduction, and low-carbon ammonia as a hydrogen carrier are all pulling capacity away from agriculture.

## Connection to the 2026 Nitrogen Trap

The [[Nitrogen Trap]] concept captures the 2026 crisis: Persian Gulf producers (~37% of urea trade, ~30% of ammonia trade) cannot ship through [[Strait of Hormuz]], European producers cannot restart without gas price relief, and [[China]] is simultaneously restricting exports. This triggered an unprecedented simultaneous squeeze where nitrogen, phosphate, and potash are all tight at once — but nitrogen is the tightest because of its manufacturing concentration in the Gulf and Europe's feedstock dependence. See [[Nitrogen Trap]] for the full crisis framework.
