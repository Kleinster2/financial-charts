# De-equitisation

De-equitisation — the structural shrinkage of public equity supply since 2003 through corporate buybacks, debt-financed M&A, private-equity take-privates, and a slowdown of new IPO/secondary issuance. The framework attributes a large share of the 2003-onwards bull market's persistence to a "de-equitisation put" — a structural support floor created when public-equity demand exceeded shrinking supply, regardless of valuation, rates, or geopolitics.

Coined and popularized by [[Robert Buckland]] (former chief global equity strategist at [[Citigroup]], now senior adviser at Engine AI and Investa). [[FT]] May 16 2026: Buckland argues the AI capex boom is finally breaking the dynamic.

---

## Mechanism — how supply has shrunk

The 2003 setup: post-dotcom-crash equities looked cheap vs bonds (S&P -50% from peak, 10Y UST at 3%) but institutional buyers stayed away because of bear-market scarring. Less traditional buyers filled the gap:

| Channel | Net effect on float |
|---|---|
| Listed-company buybacks | Reduces shares outstanding |
| Debt-financed M&A (cash takeovers of competitors) | Removes target's float; acquirer issues debt not equity |
| [[Private equity]] take-privates | Delists public companies entirely |
| Slowdown in IPOs, secondaries, equity-financed M&A | Suppresses new supply |

Result: the US and UK public equity markets have de-equitised in *each of the past four years* per Datastream. The US IT sector — usually the dominant issuance engine in tech-driven bull markets — has *shrunk* its equity base by 4% over the past four years.

For comparison, the 1998-2000 IT bull market saw the sector *expand* its equity base by 40% over two years. This is the cleanest evidence that 2003-onwards is structurally different from prior tech-driven equity cycles.

## The Mag 7 self-financing dynamic

Through 2025, the AI tech bull run was self-financed: [[Mag 7]] cap-lite business models generated FCF that funded buybacks, and the Mag 7's $230bn of 2025 buybacks more than absorbed new-issuance supply elsewhere in the sector. Buckland's framing: this bull market has been notably *less* capital-intensive than canals (1840s), railways (1840s), radio (1920s), or TMT (1990s) — all famously capex-heavy booms that needed *new* public equity to finance the buildouts.

That dynamic is breaking. Three forcing functions emerging in 2026:

| Mag 7 firm | 2026 capex commitment | 2026 buyback policy |
|---|---|---|
| [[Alphabet]] | Part of the $725B Big Tech aggregate | Paused |
| [[Amazon]] | Part of the $725B aggregate | — |
| [[Meta]] | Part of the $725B aggregate | Paused |
| [[Microsoft]] | Part of the $725B aggregate | — |
| [[Apple]] | Sitting out the AI capex boom | $100B buyback announced |

The combined Meta + Alphabet 2024 buyback program was $92B. With both paused, that supply absorber is now offline. Apple's $100B still buys back stock, but at Apple's $4.3T cap a $100B program is materially smaller in float-percentage terms than the same dollar program at lower caps.

## The 2026 IPO supply wave

Private-market AI funding has been substantial — [[OpenAI]] + [[Anthropic]] raised a combined $152B privately in 2026. Now the pipeline is moving toward public markets:

| IPO candidate | Target valuation | Status |
|---|---|---|
| [[SpaceX]] | $1.75T | June 2026 listing target ([[SpaceX IPO 2026]]) |
| [[OpenAI]] | ~$1T | H2 2026 |
| [[Anthropic]] | $900B (post-Series H) | Wilson Sonsini engaged |

Collective target: up to $4T in fresh public-market valuation. Not all of this hits float immediately — staged lockups, retention of insiders, indices absorbing passively — but earlier-stage private investors and employees will want at least partial liquidity, and that unsold stock can overhang public markets for years.

Buckland's calibration: $4T of new public-equity valuation amounts to roughly 6% expansion of the US public equity market. The last time the US saw a 6% equity-supply expansion was the late-1990s bubble. That is the framework's most concrete signal — the de-equitisation dynamic that has supported the bull market since 2003 may be turning over the next 12-24 months.

---

## Why this matters for the bull market

The de-equitisation put has insulated the bull market from valuation, rate, trade-war, and geopolitical shocks for two decades by ensuring net equity supply was always negative or flat. Buckland's read: previous bubbles all burst from equity-supply oversupply — *new* shares being issued faster than they could be absorbed. The 2003-2026 bull market has not had that condition. The AI boom is finally creating it.

Three implications:

1. The supply-anchor that has supported equities through prior macro stresses begins to weaken. Valuations no longer benefit from constantly shrinking float.
2. Specific AI mega-IPOs (SpaceX, OpenAI, Anthropic) will be the visible carriers of the shift. Their absorption tests are the real-time signals for whether the framework's turning point thesis is right.
3. The 6% equity-supply expansion mirroring the late-1990s sets a calibration test: if the public market absorbs $4T without a multiple-compression event, the de-equitisation put may persist longer than Buckland argues. If it doesn't, the framework's turning point is confirmed.

Buckland's closer, quoting an old equity capital markets banker: "If the ducks are quacking, feed them." In US big-cap tech, the ducks have been quacking for some time. It finally looks like they are about to be fed.
