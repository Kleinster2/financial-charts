PIPE financing (private investment in public equity) — a capital-raising transaction in which a publicly traded company sells newly issued shares (typically common stock, sometimes preferred or convertibles) to a limited group of institutional investors in a privately negotiated transaction that is then registered for resale. PIPEs sit between public equity offerings and private-placement debt: the issuer accesses private-market speed and certainty, but investors ultimately own registered public securities.

## Structure

- Investors commit capital based on a term sheet; closing is typically conditional on a shelf registration or subsequent resale registration statement being declared effective.
- Pricing is usually at a modest discount (0–10%) to recent public prices, with occasional premium PIPEs when investor demand is strong.
- Lead investor or group often anchors at a stated minimum commitment; placement agents source additional investors.
- Placement-agent fees typically range 3–5% of gross proceeds.

## Common use cases

- Biotech and emerging-growth companies bridging between public offerings.
- De-SPACed companies raising capital at or shortly after de-SPAC closing (PIPEs were structurally central to the 2020–2022 SPAC boom).
- Industrial-policy-backed companies raising matched private capital to satisfy federal funding conditions, e.g., [[USA Rare Earth]]'s $1.5B PIPE in January 2026 paired with the Commerce Department LOI.

## Risks

- Dilution is the defining feature — new shares meaningfully expand share count; warrants or adjustable-price PIPEs can increase dilution on later triggers.
- Anchor-investor risk: PIPE share prices can serve as a reference for subsequent trading, pinning the stock near the PIPE clearing level.
- Timing risk around registration: if resale registration stalls, investors are locked in longer than expected.
