# Commodity trading houses

The handful of private firms that move raw materials — crude oil, refined products, LNG, coal, metals, grains, cocoa, cotton — from producers to end users, extracting margin from the spread between where a barrel or tonne is and where it needs to be. The top tier includes [[Vitol]], [[Trafigura]], [[Glencore]], [[Mercuria Energy Group|Mercuria]], and [[Gunvor]] in energy; [[Cargill]], ADM, Bunge, and COFCO in agriculture; Glencore, Trafigura, and [[Darton Commodities]] in metals. They are almost entirely private, almost entirely Swiss-domiciled (Geneva, Zug, Baar), and operate through a mix of physical logistics (tanker fleets, terminals, pipelines, warehouses), trading arms that hedge inventory with futures, and increasingly specialized financing and market-making operations. [[Oliver Wyman]] publishes an annual industry P&L analysis — the primary public data source, since individual houses are private and disclose minimally.

## How they make money

Commodity trading houses do not make money on long-term directional views on oil or metal prices. They make money on basis — the gap between the price of a specific physical grade at a specific location on a specific date, and the exchange-traded benchmark futures contract. Every barrel of [[Nigeria]]n Bonny Light or Omani crude trades at a differential to [[Brent crude]] or Dubai; that differential is where traders live. Their edge is information — who has cargo, who needs cargo, what the exact freight and blending economics look like — plus physical optionality: terminals to store, blenders to mix, ships to reroute, refineries to feed. The futures hedge isolates the basis from the headline price, so a correctly constructed book is long a physical cargo and short an equivalent number of futures contracts. The P&L comes from the basis move, not the headline oil move.

Volatility is the friend, not the enemy. When prices are stable, basis is compressed and margins are thin; when prices are volatile and supply chains are dislocated, physical barrels trade at large premia or discounts to benchmark futures and the information advantage of the traders is maximized. This is why the industry earned record profits during the [[Russia-Ukraine war|2022 Russian invasion]] and why 2024 — a year of unusually low oil volatility — was the industry's weakest since 2021.

But volatility has a cost before it pays. Holding physical cargo requires working capital, and hedging that cargo with a short-futures position means posting variation margin as futures prices rise. A trader long a physical cargo and short a matching futures contract has economically zero price risk, but when the futures contract jumps $20/bbl, the trader has to wire $20/bbl of cash to the exchange overnight, before the physical P&L is realized at the delivery port weeks later. This is why the industry has repeatedly come to banks for enlarged credit lines in the opening days of supply shocks — the [[2022 energy crisis]], and again in March 2026 during the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]].

## Industry earnings cycle

[[Oliver Wyman]]'s annual report tracks industry earnings across the top ~30 commodity trading firms. The decade's trajectory captures the structural shift from pre-pandemic norm to post-Russia-invasion regime:

| Year | Industry earnings | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-pandemic norm | ~$35–45B | Stable prices, compressed basis |
| 2021 | ~$61B | Recovery + dislocation from Covid reopening |
| 2022 | ~$115B (peak) | Russia invasion; Urals discounts, LNG arbitrage, scrambled flows |
| 2023 | ~$105B (est.) | Elevated volatility continues |
| 2024 | $92B (lowest since 2021) | Oil vol collapsed; metals +20%, oil desks –15% |
| 2026 (Q1) | "Billions" of early-war losses | Iran war caught traders short |

Oliver Wyman's 2026 baseline forecast is $90–110B/year excluding geopolitical events. The pre-pandemic run-rate is unlikely to return — the post-2022 environment has seen more frequent dislocations (Red Sea shipping, Hormuz closure, LNG redirections) that sustain basis volatility even without a single dominant crisis. Concurrently, the industry has grown up: trading houses now run proprietary shipping fleets, refineries, midstream assets, and renewable power platforms, generating physical-asset income that smooths over pure-trading cycles.

The 2026 split within the industry is instructive. Oil desks were down 15% in 2024 because crude traded in an unusually narrow range; metals desks were up 20% because copper, aluminum, zinc, and nickel were moving on competing supply stories (Chinese demand, Indonesian nickel oversupply, [[Red Sea shipping disruption]] copper freight). The 2026 Iran war flipped the script again — oil volatility exploded and caught the consensus-short positioning.

## 2026 Iran war case study

The industry entered the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] positioned for falling oil. The February 2026 consensus (reflected in CFTC managed-money positioning) was that Brent would drift back to the $60s as US production kept growing and global demand softened. When [[US-Iran nuclear escalation February 2026|US-Israeli strikes on Iran]] triggered Iranian retaliation and the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure on March 2, Brent spiked from ~$70 to $112 in three weeks.

The loss mechanics per [[Oliver Wyman]]'s 2026 report:

1. Short futures hedges against physical cargo ownership marked-to-market against the trader. Even though the hedge was structurally correct, the interim cash drain forced margin calls well beyond normal working-capital buffers.
2. Pre-sold cargoes stranded inside the Gulf (100+ tankers trapped behind the Hormuz closure) had to be replaced at wartime prices — a trader committed to deliver 2M barrels at $70 in April had to buy the replacement at $100+ while the original cargo sat behind the strait.
3. Insurance costs for Gulf voyages rose more than sixfold, adding freight pass-through complications.
4. At least two [[Vitol]]-owned naphtha vessels were stranded and set on fire by Iranian forces on March 12, killing one crew member — a total physical loss on top of the paper loss.

Losses ran into the "billions" industrywide per [[Oliver Wyman]]'s Alexander Franke. Some were reversed by April as positions rolled off and the price curve stabilized, but the episode produced an immediate credit-facility expansion:

| Firm | March 2026 additional credit facility |
|---|---|
| [[Vitol]] | $3B |
| [[Trafigura]] | $3B |
| [[Gunvor]] | $1.5B |

The $7.5B aggregate was not distress financing — none of the firms disclosed losses threatening solvency — but a prepositioning of liquidity to handle margin calls in a regime where daily oil moves of $5–15/bbl became routine. Trafigura explicitly characterized its facility as a "liquidity buffer, if required during a period of heightened commodity price volatility."

The episode is a clean illustration of why the industry's baseline earnings are rising but so is its working-capital footprint. Bigger P&L swings require bigger balance sheets; the largest houses have responded by quietly becoming systemically important players in commodity finance.

## Structural trends per Oliver Wyman 2026

| Trend | Direction | Driver |
|---|---|---|
| "Seat cost" per trader | +30% since 2021 | Technology, data, compliance, talent cost inflation |
| Working-capital footprint | Up materially | Volatility regime; margin call frequency |
| Asset ownership (refineries, ships, renewables) | Up | Earnings smoothing; physical optionality |
| Metals / LNG / power share | Up | Diversification away from oil-heavy 2010s model |
| Private credit / structured finance arms | Up | Monetizing client relationships and flow |
| Geographic footprint (Asia, MENA) | Up | Following demand and supply growth |
| Disclosure | Still minimal | All top-5 energy traders remain private |

The single most important structural shift is that trading houses are no longer pure merchants. [[Glencore]] is the extreme case — a vertically integrated mining-and-trading conglomerate — but [[Vitol]] runs refineries and LNG terminals, [[Trafigura]] owns [[Nyrstar]] (zinc/lead smelters) and a minority of oil and gas assets, and [[Mercuria Energy Group|Mercuria]] has moved aggressively into energy-transition investments ($2B+ committed). The "trading" P&L that Oliver Wyman reports is increasingly a slice of a broader physical-assets business, which makes year-over-year comparability noisier than headline figures suggest.
