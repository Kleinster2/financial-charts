# US crude export controls

#concept #policy #energy #oil #usa

The live-decision framework for a policy option that currently exists on the menu but has not been exercised in the modern US energy-exporter era. A US ban on crude or refined-product exports is executable by the sitting president through Commerce Department authority and would invert in a matter of days the 15-year architecture built by the [[US Shale|shale revolution]] and the December 2015 repeal of the 1975 export ban. The question is not *whether* the option exists — it does — but at what oil price, for how sustained a duration, and against what alternative instruments a president would reach for it. [[Rapidan Energy Group]] flagged $150/bbl as the threshold on April 15, 2026.

## Synthesis

The single decision that would reprice the global oil market overnight is whether the US president orders a ban on US crude and/or refined-product exports. US exports currently run at a record 5.2 mb/d of crude plus ~7.5 mb/d of products — volume that is functioning as the marginal global supply during the 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure. Shutting that valve would invert three things simultaneously: the [[WTI]]-Brent spread (collapse WTI, spike [[Brent crude|Brent]]), the pump-price transmission (muted domestic relief because gasoline tracks global refined-product pricing, not US crude), and the [[US Energy Independence Six Countries]] framework that has anchored US energy posture since the 2015 ban repeal. [[Rapidan Energy Group]]'s $150/bbl call is the analytical pivot: their published note implies the White House is already discussing the option even as public messaging denies it, reprising the 1975 policy instrument created under the [[1973 oil crisis]]. The 2022 near-miss at ~$120 WTI — when the Biden administration rejected controls and drained 600 mn bbls from the [[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]] — establishes the precedent that SPR is the first-line instrument; export controls enter the menu only when SPR-type interventions visibly fail.

The April 15 data captures the moment of maximum US supply elasticity — US exports are absorbing global demand that can't reach Gulf producers because of the [[Strait of Hormuz]] blockade. This is the condition a ban would reverse.

## The trigger framework

Three elements need to align for a sitting president to execute an export ban:

1. Price level. [[Rapidan Energy Group]]'s $150/bbl WTI threshold. Below it, alternative instruments ([[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]] release, jawboning, tariff threats) are sufficient. Above it, the alternatives are exhausted or politically unpalatable.
2. Duration. A single-session spike is insufficient. Sustained pricing above the trigger for weeks — enough that pump gasoline breaches $5/gal and diesel blows through its ATH — is what converts the option from "contemplated" to "executable."
3. Political failure mode. The president's alternative actions must visibly fail to move prices. In 2022, SPR release was credited (rightly or not) with the fall from $120 to $76. If similar interventions fail in 2026 — either because the SPR is too depleted post-2022 release, or because the global supply loss from Hormuz exceeds what SPR can substitute — the export-ban option rises.

Rapidan's edge is not in price forecasting; it is in reading *when* an administration will break its own stated policy. Their published call implies McNally's White House network is already hearing the option discussed, even as public messaging denies it.

## Transmission math

A rough rule of thumb for US pump-gasoline response to WTI:

- Every $10/bbl WTI ≈ $0.25/gal pump gasoline (national average), given stable crack spreads
- $91 WTI → $4.10 pump (observed, April 15)
- $120 WTI → ~$4.85 pump (2022 peak reference)
- $150 WTI → ~$5.50-5.80 pump (breaches 2022 peak of $5.00)

The transmission is looser when refining capacity is strained (crack spreads blow out) or when regional disruptions dominate (West Coast, East Coast versus Gulf Coast). [[Diesel fuel|Diesel]] transmission is faster and more volatile — diesel passes through to every trucked good, so goods inflation accelerates before pump gasoline fully reflects the move.

## Activation mechanics

A US export ban does not require new legislation. The legal scaffolding exists:

- Commerce Department authority under the Export Control Reform Act (2018) — allows the Secretary of Commerce to restrict exports on national-security or short-supply grounds
- Presidential emergency authority under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)
- Precedent in the 1975-2015 ban — the infrastructure and legal framing can be reconstituted quickly

In practical terms: an executive order could impose restrictions within 24-48 hours. Implementation (licensing, enforcement, exceptions) would take longer, but markets would reprice on announcement.

## Bidirectional scenario map

### If executed (export ban activated)

| Variable | Reaction |
|----------|----------|
| [[WTI]] | Sharp decline — trapped domestic supply floods Gulf Coast storage |
| [[Brent crude|Brent]] | Sharp spike — ~5 mb/d of marginal global supply removed overnight |
| WTI-Brent spread | Blows out from current ~$3 to $20-30+ |
| US gasoline | Initial decline, then muted — global refined-product pricing dominates |
| Refiners | Mixed — Gulf Coast refiners gain cheap feedstock but lose export margins |
| E&P companies | Hit hard — US crude sells at growing discount to global peers |
| VLCC/tanker rates | Collapse on route side (US out-flow) |
| [[US Energy Independence Six Countries]] | Repriced — coastal import economies still import at world prices; Gulf Coast export economy dismantled |
| Ally relationships | Diplomatic rupture — Europe, Japan, South Korea, India cut off from US substitute supply mid-war |

### If not executed (status quo holds)

| Variable | Reaction |
|----------|----------|
| WTI | Continues to be shielded by US export substitution — the Apr 15 softness pattern persists |
| Brent/Dubai | Remains elevated on Gulf supply loss |
| Pump gasoline | Continues rising if Hormuz stays closed — political pressure grows monotonically |
| SPR | Second major drawdown likely (first was 2022, 600 mn bbls) |
| Administration posture | Jawboning, tariff threats, diplomatic moves — but not the structural intervention |

The bidirectional risk is genuinely binary. Positioning that assumes continued US export substitution must size the tail risk of a ban; positioning that anticipates a ban must accept carrying the trade through an indefinite window of administration denial.

## Trigger watchlist

Operational signals that the option is moving from contemplation to execution:

- Administration messaging shifts from "not contemplating" to "reviewing all options"
- White House convenes energy-industry executives (Rapidan-style pattern)
- Commerce Secretary publicly non-committal
- SPR releases announced in sizes comparable to 2022 (≥100 mn bbls), suggesting the first-line instrument is being exhausted
- Congressional energy-committee chairs endorse or oppose export controls publicly
- [[Rapidan Energy Group]] or similar shops move the probability higher in client notes

## Precedent and historical context

The US operated under a crude export ban from 1975 to 2015, covering 40 years and responding originally to the [[1973 oil crisis]]. The [[US Shale|shale revolution]] made the ban binding again in reverse (trapping US light sweet crude that domestic refineries couldn't absorb), and a December 2015 omnibus budget deal repealed it. The Biden administration considered reinstating controls in 2022 at ~$120 WTI during the Russia-Ukraine crisis but rejected the option and drained 600 mn bbls from the SPR instead.
