#concept #trading #quant #hft #finance

**HFT-hedge fund convergence** — Structural trend since ~2020 in which proprietary trading firms and quantitative hedge funds are expanding into each other's traditional territory. Prop firms are adding longer-duration "mid-frequency" strategies (1-5 day holds); hedge funds are pushing toward faster signals. The convergence zone, holding periods of hours to days, is becoming the industry's most competitive and potentially crowded battleground.

---

## Why it's happening

### Prop firms expanding outward

Post-Covid trading profits gave firms like [[Jane Street]], [[Hudson River Trading]], and [[Tower Research Capital]] excess capital that couldn't be deployed in traditional low-latency strategies (capacity-constrained by physics). Longer-duration trades absorb more capital.

| Driver | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Capital surplus | Covid-era profits created capital seeking deployment |
| Latency limits | Pure speed butting up against physics (picosecond-level) |
| Diminishing returns | Low-latency competition so intense one exec says "there is no alpha, it's all latency" |
| [[Jane Street]] precedent | $8.4bn net trading rev H1 2020 (2x [[Citadel Securities]]) showed profit potential of longer holds via ETF market-making |

### Hedge funds speeding up

Capacity constraints in existing strategies, plus the appeal of high-[[Sharpe ratio]] fast strategies to support lower-Sharpe longer-duration ones.

| Driver | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Capacity constraints | Top funds closed to new capital, need new strategy types |
| Sharpe barbell | Fast, low-capacity strategies boost fund-level Sharpe ratio |
| Signal compression | Longer-term premia crowded; capital migrates to middle |

As a senior multi-strategy hedge fund executive told FT Alphaville (Sep 2025): "We're in the early stages of a reorganisation of systematic trading."

## Who's doing what

### Prop firms moving slower

| Firm | Mid-frequency evolution |
|------|------------------------|
| [[Hudson River Trading]] | Prism unit: $2bn+ profits (2024), separate mid-freq operation |
| [[Tower Research Capital]] | Mid-freq now 25-30% of revenue (up from <10% 2-3 yrs prior) |
| [[Citadel Securities]] | Warehousing risk "up to weeks" depending on size, product, risk, liquidity (company spokesperson) |
| [[Jane Street]] | ETF market-making naturally creates multi-day positions |
| [[DRW]], [[Susquehanna]] | Part of "new HFT royalty" evolving beyond pure speed |

### Hedge funds moving faster

| Firm | Speed evolution |
|------|----------------|
| [[Qube Research & Technologies]] | Reportedly succeeding at faster signals |
| [[D.E. Shaw]] | Quant + discretionary hybrid, expanding systematic |
| [[Millennium]] | Poached two [[Jane Street]] traders (talent convergence signal) |

Prop firms have been more successful expanding into slower strategies than hedge funds have been at speeding up. As one quant hedge fund manager put it: "It's easier to go from building a Ferrari to building a Volkswagen, than from building a Volkswagen to a Ferrari."

## The convergence zone: mid-frequency (1-5 days)

The collision point is strategies with holding periods from hours to days. This was historically a gap between:
- HFTs (seconds to minutes, sometimes intraday)
- [[Statistical arbitrage]] hedge funds (days to weeks)

Both sides are now meeting in the middle, creating intensified competition for signals, higher fixed costs (research, compute), and crowding risk. [[Goldman Sachs]]' annual quant survey shows the estimated market footprint of prop traders expanding dramatically in recent years.

An estimated ~2/3 of US equity trading volume is now systematic/algorithmic, roughly 20x the total trading volume of the entire long-only investment fund industry.

---

## Implications

### 1. Talent convergence

Firms are competing for mid-career professionals, not just fresh graduates. [[Millennium]]'s poaching of two [[Jane Street]] traders is a harbinger. Hedge funds can offer $100mn PM packages; prop firms counter with partnership economics and equity upside.

### 2. Prop firms as prime brokerage clients

As prop firms hold positions longer, they need financing, drawing them into the bank [[prime brokerage]] ecosystem historically reserved for hedge funds. [[Goldman Sachs]]' [[Ashok Varadhan]] acknowledged the tension: clients and competitors simultaneously, requiring "maturity" to manage.

Historically, prop trading firms needed banks only for execution and trading fees. Now they need the full suite of financing and services.

### 3. External capital

[[Citadel Securities]], [[Hudson River Trading]], and [[Jane Street]] have all tapped debt markets to boost firepower. [[Tower Research Capital]] has talked to investors about more traditional fund-like vehicles. Several industry insiders predict HFT-powered long-short equity funds will eventually emerge.

At the same time, some stat-arb hedge funds are internalizing their best strategies: [[Renaissance Technologies]]' Medallion is entirely employee money, and [[D.E. Shaw]]'s Valence fund is heading in that direction.

### 4. Crowding risk

The July 2025 "quant quiver" may have been an early signal. With ~2/3 of US equity trading volume systematic, correlated drawdowns could be amplified. As one trading firm head noted: "It's unclear how much of it is due to this, but there's a lot of money chasing similar strategies and signals in similar instruments."
