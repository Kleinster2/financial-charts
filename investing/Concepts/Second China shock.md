# Second China shock

The "second China shock" is a contested but increasingly standard framing for the 2020s wave of Chinese export pressure on the rest of the world — qualitatively different from the first [[China trade|China shock]] of 2001-2011. Where the first wave displaced US labor-intensive manufacturing via cheap labor and [[WTO]]-enabled market access, the second wave hits capital-intensive and technology-intensive sectors via scale, subsidized capital, and process engineering. [[Europe]] — not the US — is now the primary target, because the US has assembled a multi-layer defensive stack (tariffs, IRA, [[CHIPS Act]], export controls, USMCA content rules) while Europe has not.

The term is invoked most prominently by [[Michael Pettis]], [[Richard Baldwin]], [[Brad Setser]], [[Beatrice Weder di Mauro]], [[Jeromin Zettelmeyer]], and [[Adam Tooze]] in the 2024-2026 debate over global imbalances. It sits inside the larger [[Global imbalances|imbalances]] conversation but operates at a more specific level: the composition and geography of the shock, not just its aggregate scale.

---

## The first shock — what the framing descends from

The original "China shock" is specifically the [[David Autor]] / [[David Dorn]] / [[Gordon Hanson]] 2013 AER paper "The China Syndrome" and its follow-ups. They documented that US commuting zones most exposed to Chinese import competition from 2001 forward experienced:

| Effect | Finding |
|---|---|
| Manufacturing employment | Persistent job losses, not offset by other sector growth |
| Wages | Lower for workers who kept jobs, especially non-college |
| Welfare dependence | Sharp rise in disability and income-support program enrollment |
| Adjustment speed | Did *not* self-correct on classical trade-theory timelines |
| Political effects | Later follow-ups linked China-exposed districts to Trump 2016 voting shifts and similar UK patterns |

The paper is why "free trade helps everyone on average, but the losers are concentrated and don't adjust" became mainstream macro, displacing the earlier Ricardian consensus. It is the empirical anchor for the second-shock framing.

## First shock vs second shock — what changed

| Dimension | First shock (2001-2011) | Second shock (2020s) |
|---|---|---|
| Goods composition | Labor-intensive: textiles, furniture, toys, basic electronics | Capital and tech-intensive: EVs, batteries, solar, wind, electrolyzers, mature chips, industrial machinery, chemicals, shipbuilding |
| Enabling event | China [[WTO]] accession (Dec 2001), PNTR with US | Xi's post-2020 industrial-policy pivot: accelerated [[Made in China 2025]], dual-circulation strategy, "new productive forces" doctrine |
| Competitive edge | Cheap labor + scale | Scale + process engineering + subsidized capital (labor costs now above [[Mexico]]) |
| Primary target | US Midwest manufacturing, then Western European low-end | [[Europe]] much more than US |
| Defensive architecture | None in either region | US has one (tariffs, [[CHIPS Act]], [[Inflation Reduction Act|IRA]], export controls, CFIUS, USMCA); Europe has fragments |
| Political legacy expected | Already produced: Brexit, Trump 1.0, trade-consensus collapse | Rising far-right in [[Germany]] (AfD), [[France]] (RN), [[Italy]] (FdI); European fiscal/industrial-policy revisionism |

## Why Europe is exposed where the US is not

**US defensive stack assembled (in order of political salience):**

1. **Tariffs** — Section 301 and 232 duties carried forward from Trump 1.0, expanded under Biden (EV tariff at 100%, batteries, solar, semis), and compounded again under Trump 2.0.
2. **[[Inflation Reduction Act|IRA]]** — massive subsidy for US-located clean-tech manufacturing, with local content rules that exclude Chinese inputs from the highest credit tiers.
3. **[[CHIPS Act]]** — $52B semiconductor subsidy for US fab capacity, paired with a growing [[US-China chip export controls|export control]] apparatus.
4. **USMCA content rules** — North American auto content requirements that block Mexican-assembled Chinese-component vehicles from tariff-free US entry.
5. **[[CFIUS]]** — inbound-investment screening hardened across 2018-2024, blocking Chinese equity positions in strategic US assets.
6. **Outbound investment rules** — 2024 Treasury rules restricting US capital into Chinese AI, quantum, and advanced semis.

**Europe's partial versions:**

| Tool | EU status |
|---|---|
| EV countervailing duties | ~17-36% on Chinese EVs (Oct 2024); politically contested by [[Germany]] |
| State aid framework | Temporary Crisis and Transition Framework extended, but each member funds separately — creates intra-EU asymmetry |
| Foreign Subsidies Regulation | In force, but enforcement is slow and case-by-case |
| Critical Raw Materials Act | Framework only; funding insufficient vs [[IRA]] scale |
| CBAM (carbon border adjustment) | Phases in 2026; fights the symptom not the cause |
| Outbound investment screening | Proposed, not adopted |

The consolidated picture is that Europe has partial versions of most US tools but none of them at US scale or speed, and the EU-level political economy makes a coherent state-aid-plus-tariff-plus-export-control response structurally difficult: [[Germany]]'s auto-industry exposure to Chinese demand makes Berlin soft on [[China]] retaliation, which freezes Brussels.

## The sectors where the second shock is concentrated

**Auto / EV:** [[BYD]], Geely, SAIC, Chery, Nio have collectively moved from irrelevance to threatening the German-premium and French-mass-market price points. [[Volkswagen]], [[Stellantis]], [[Renault]] face real pricing pressure. Chinese EV export volume roughly 5x 2020 levels by 2024. The auto sector is also ~7% of EU GDP directly and ~14% with supply chains, so the political gravity is enormous.

**Solar panels:** Already lost in the first round (2011-2015 — EU tried anti-dumping, abandoned it under German pressure). Cited by Tooze and others as the precedent: Europe has *already* demonstrated that its political economy can lose an entire clean-tech sector without responding coherently.

**Batteries:** [[CATL]] and [[BYD|BYD battery unit]] dominate global production. European cell-manufacturing initiatives (Northvolt, ACC, Verkor, Italvolt) have struggled; [[Northvolt]] filed for bankruptcy in late 2024. China controls 60%+ of global lithium refining and even higher shares for cathodes and anodes.

**Wind turbines:** Goldwind, Envision, Mingyang are climbing the value chain. [[Vestas]], [[Siemens Energy]], [[GE Vernova]] still lead on offshore and complex onshore, but Chinese price competition is starting to hit installed cost in [[Brazil]], [[India]], [[Türkiye|Turkey]], and Middle East markets where European firms had relied on volume.

**[[Electrolyzers]] and heat pumps:** Both sectors Europe claimed as strategic in [[European Green Deal]] documents. Chinese firms (Sungrow, Longi, Midea) are already undercutting European OEMs on price by 30-50%. Chinese alkaline electrolyzers sell at roughly a third of Western prices, and the first two EU Hydrogen Bank auctions cleared disproportionately on Chinese-equipment bids — the [[Electrolyzers]] note carries the full sector-specific detail.

**Mature chips (28nm and above):** [[SMIC]] and peer Chinese foundries now have substantial mature-node capacity that competes with [[TSMC]], [[UMC]], and [[GlobalFoundries]] in the fastest-growing volume segment (automotive, industrial, IoT). This is the slower-moving piece, but it threatens the US and European "design, not fabricate" assumption.

**Industrial machinery, shipbuilding, chemicals:** All German export-surplus pillars. The gradient here is longer but the direction is consistent.

## Why this matters for macro and markets

The second shock is the physical-economy expression of the imbalances story. China runs a ~$1.2T trade surplus (2024, reportedly understated in IMF data per Tooze) because its domestic consumption share remains structurally compressed and its investment share structurally elevated — the Pettis framing. That surplus has to land somewhere. In a world where the US has closed most of its intake valve, it lands in Europe and in the global south.

The market consequences:

| Channel | Effect |
|---|---|
| European manufacturing earnings | Structural compression, especially autos and capital goods |
| Euro | Weaker than rate-differentials suggest (Tooze point) |
| European fiscal | Pressure to spend US-style to defend sectors; breaks Stability and Growth Pact logic |
| German-bund spreads | Narrow then widen episodically as fiscal credibility reprices |
| EM surplus countries | Caught between Chinese goods competition and dollar-funding tightness |
| US asset prices | Supported by the defensive architecture; AI-infrastructure premium layered on top |

## The Tooze framing specifically

[[Adam Tooze]] uses the second shock in [[Adam Tooze#Chartbook 442 Global imbalances a new cocktail in old bottles Apr 22 2026|Chartbook 442]] (Apr 22, 2026) as the stress-test for European state capacity. His argument: the imbalances accounting of 2026 looks familiar, but the political institutions that used to absorb such shocks are eroding. Debating textbook macro-policy responses while the decision-making architecture in Washington disintegrates is escapism. His test case: can Europe build US-equivalent state capacity to absorb the second shock fast enough? Tentative answer: not yet.

The Pettis and Baldwin versions are more structurally pessimistic about China's ability to rebalance internally without political disruption, which means the shock continues regardless of Western response. The Setser version focuses more tightly on the balance-of-payments accounting and the mismeasurement problem.

## What the second-shock frame is competing with

**Optimistic alternative:** Some economists ([[Paul Krugman]], some at [[Peterson Institute]]) argue the first-shock framing exaggerates the persistence of adjustment failure, and that Chinese overcapacity is a self-liquidating problem. The second-shock frame is too alarmist.

**Different frame — "weaponized interdependence":** [[Henry Farrell]] and [[Abraham Newman]] argue the real story is strategic leverage through chokepoints, not aggregate trade flows. China's dominance in rare earths and lithium refining matters more than its surplus in consumer electronics.

**Purely domestic China explanation:** Some argue the surplus is essentially a symptom of [[China|Chinese]] domestic deflation and property-sector collapse, and will resolve as China's real-estate drag unwinds. The second-shock frame overreads a cyclical dynamic as structural.

The second-shock frame has nonetheless become the dominant reference point in European policy circles and in analyses from Tooze, Pettis, Baldwin, and Setser through 2024-2026.
