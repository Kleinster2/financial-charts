**Coal** — The crisis fuel. Dirty, abundant, and the recurring beneficiary of every major energy supply shock since the 1970s. ~27% of global primary energy, ~36% of global electricity generation. Largest producers: [[China]] (~52%), [[India]] (~10%), [[Indonesia]] (~8%), [[United States]] (~6%), [[Australia]] (~5%).

---

## Synthesis

Three energy supply shocks in five decades have produced the same response: coal capacity expansion. The 1970s buildout (+50% in a decade) was meant to be temporary and ran for 40 years. The 2022 European coal restart was meant to be temporary and most plants did re-close. The 2026 Asian switching is being framed as temporary. [[Fatih Birol]] predicts a sustained buildout anyway — and the IEA head's own track record suggests he knows what he's forecasting against, since the agency's decarbonization scenarios require coal to fall ~70% by 2035. The investment question is which "temporary" this one turns out to be: the 2022 kind (brief, reversed) or the 1973 kind (locked in for decades). The answer probably depends on whether the [[Strait of Hormuz]] crisis resolves in weeks or months. Asia's switching happened within days; the capital commitments that make it permanent take quarters.

## The crisis pattern

Coal's role in the energy system follows a ratchet: each oil/gas supply shock sends countries back to coal, and the resulting capacity stays online longer than the crisis that justified it.

| Crisis | Coal response | Legacy |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| [[1973 oil crisis]] | Global coal power capacity +50% in 10 years ([[Fatih Birol]]/[[IEA]], Apr 2026) | Locked in decades of coal baseload |
| 1979 Iranian revolution | Further coal buildout, especially US and Germany | US coal generation peaked in 2007 |
| [[Russia]]-[[Ukraine]] 2022 | European coal resurgence — [[Germany]] restarted mothballed plants, [[Netherlands]] lifted coal cap | Most plants re-closed by 2024, but demonstrated the reflex |
| [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] | Asian coal switching within weeks (see below) | TBD — Birol predicts sustained buildout |

The pattern is structural: coal is the only energy source with enough spare capacity to respond to a shock within weeks (not years). Gas plants need fuel deliveries; nuclear takes a decade to build; renewables need grid integration. Coal plants sit idle and can restart.

## 2026 Hormuz crisis — Asian coal switching

The [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure triggered immediate coal reversion across Asia. Countries that could switch from gas to coal did so within days.

| Country | Action | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| [[Thailand]] | Ordered coal plants to full capacity | Fortune (Mar 22) |
| [[Bangladesh]] | Boosted coal consumption; [[LPG]] rationing considered | FT, Carnegie |
| [[South Korea]] | Fuel price cap (first in 30 years); coal plant caps lifted | Fortune (Mar 21) |
| [[Taiwan]] | Preparing coal reversion | [[Bloomberg]] |
| [[India]] | Record coal burn expected summer 2026; coal already ~55% of primary energy | India Energy data |

Henning Gloystein ([[Eurasia Group]]): *"Asia is in full price competition, with any country that can switch from gas to coal doing so."*

The semiconductor dimension: [[South Korea]] and [[Taiwan]] produce the majority of the world's advanced semiconductors. Their energy base is shifting toward coal dependence during the crisis — adding supply chain risk if coal stocks deplete or emissions constraints bind. See [[Iran conflict semiconductor energy risk]].

## Birol prediction: coal as the big winner (Apr 2026)

[[Fatih Birol]]/[[IEA]] (FT, Apr 7) explicitly predicted a repeat of the 1970s coal buildout: *"The oil shocks in the 1970s set off a wave of investment in coal-fired power generation. In the 10 years after 1973, coal power capacity around the world rose by 50 per cent. This time, we may well see some countries increasing the use of coal again, at least temporarily, especially in emerging economies in [[Asia]] but also in [[Europe]] and [[North America]]."*

The qualifier "at least temporarily" is doing heavy lifting. The 1970s coal buildout was also meant to be temporary. Plants built in response to the 1973 shock ran for 30-40 years.

## The tension

Coal's crisis utility runs directly against decarbonization commitments. The IEA itself projects coal-fired generation must fall ~70% by 2035 under Net Zero pathways. Yet every supply shock proves coal's indispensability as a fallback. The result is a wedge between stated policy and revealed preference — countries sign phase-out pledges in calm periods and restart coal plants during crises.

[[China]] embodies the contradiction: simultaneously the world's largest renewable energy investor and the world's largest coal consumer, adding ~100 GW of new coal capacity permits in 2023 alone while pledging carbon neutrality by 2060.
