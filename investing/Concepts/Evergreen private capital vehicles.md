#concept #privateequity #privatemarkets #wealth

**Evergreen private capital vehicles** — Perpetual-life funds that hold private equity, private credit, or private real estate, sold primarily to accredited investors and high-net-worth wealth channels. Unlike traditional closed-end private capital funds (10-12 year locked structure with one capital-call period and exits at fund termination), evergreen vehicles accept monthly subscriptions, mark to NAV monthly, and offer periodic NAV-based redemptions, typically capped at ~5% of NAV per quarter. The format is the primary distribution channel through which $14tn-to-$24tn private capital growth has been packaged for wealth investors, and the structural overhang behind the [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026|2026 private credit retail liquidity crisis]].

---

## Synthesis

Evergreen vehicles solve a real distribution problem: institutional PE economics (locked capital, 10-year horizon, capital calls on a schedule, no liquidity until exits) are operationally and emotionally incompatible with wealth-channel investors. Evergreens reformat the asset class around monthly subscriptions, monthly NAVs, periodic redemptions, and quarterly distributions — keeping the underlying private-asset exposure while resembling a mutual fund on the surface. The format has worked brilliantly for issuers: by 2025, evergreen fundraising had become the dominant growth driver for [[Blackstone]], [[Apollo]], [[Ares Management]], [[KKR]], [[Brookfield]], [[Blue Owl]], and [[StepStone Group]]. But the format embeds a liquidity mismatch the underlying assets cannot meaningfully bridge. The 2026 stress events ([[Apollo]] ADS BDC capping redemptions at 46% fulfillment, [[Cliffwater]]'s 14% redemption request rate, [[Morgan Stanley]] interval fund stress) confirmed what was structurally obvious: subscribers can ask for cash faster than the underlying private credit or private equity portfolio can produce it. The same pattern is building in evergreen secondaries vehicles, which lean on the discount-to-NAV mark-up mechanism described in [[Private equity secondaries#The mark-to-NAV problem|PE secondaries]] to deliver high reported early returns to subscribers who may not understand the decay trajectory.

## How evergreens differ from closed-end PE funds

| Feature | Closed-end PE fund | Evergreen vehicle |
|---|---|---|
| Investor type | Institutional (pension, SWF, endowment, family office) | Accredited, HNW, wealth advisor, RIA channels |
| Fund life | 10-12 years + extensions | Perpetual |
| Capital call | Multiple calls over 3-5 year investment period | Single subscription at purchase |
| Liquidity | None until distributions / fund termination | Monthly subscription, quarterly (capped) redemptions |
| NAV cadence | Quarterly NAV, less consequential | Monthly NAV; determines subscription/redemption price |
| Distributions | Lumpy (deal-by-deal exits) | Quarterly target distributions |
| Investment period | Defined window | Continuous deployment |
| Minimum | $1mn+ typical | $25k-$50k typical |
| Reporting cadence | Quarterly | Monthly |
| Tax | K-1 (partnership) | 1099 typical (REIT or RIC for many products) |
| Fees | 1.5-2% mgmt + 20% carry | 1.25-1.5% mgmt + 12.5-15% performance, often with hurdle |

The "evergreen" naming comes from the perpetual fund life — there is no termination date.

## Structural building blocks

Most evergreens are built on top of one of three regulatory wrappers, each with slightly different liquidity and reporting mechanics.

| Wrapper | Examples | Liquidity feature |
|---|---|---|
| Non-traded BDC | [[Blackstone]] BCRED, [[Blue Owl]] OCRED, [[Apollo]] ADS, [[Ares Management]] ASIF | Quarterly tender offers, typically 5% of NAV cap |
| Non-traded REIT | [[Blackstone]] BREIT, [[Starwood]] SREIT, [[KKR]] KREST | Monthly or quarterly repurchase plan, typically 2% monthly / 5% quarterly cap |
| Interval fund / tender offer fund | [[Cliffwater]] CCLFX, [[Morgan Stanley]] PCRX, [[StepStone Group]] STPEX, [[Hamilton Lane]] PE evergreen | Quarterly repurchase, 5% of NAV typical |
| Luxembourg ELTIF / SICAV (Europe) | [[Apollo]] European product, [[BlackRock]] private markets ELTIFs | Varies by product, monthly NAV typical |
| Private LP feeders | Various | Often combined with one of the above for international access |

The 5% quarterly cap (~20% annual) is the most common gate. When subscriptions exceed redemptions, the cap is irrelevant; when redemptions exceed the cap, the vehicle prorates fulfillment and queues the balance.

## Why issuers love the format

| Driver | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Perpetual fee base | No fundraising cycle; AUM grows with subscriptions until redemptions match |
| Scale through wealth channel | $80T global retail/HNW wealth, vs ~$30T institutional — much larger pool |
| Margin profile | Wealth-channel fees roughly match institutional management fees but distribute through advisor platforms |
| Cross-sell into wealth platforms | Wirehouse, RIA, and private-bank shelves give long-term distribution moat |
| Capital deploys faster | Subscriptions are dollar amounts to deploy, not commitments to call |
| Vintage diversification | Continuous deployment averages entry timing across years |

[[Hamilton Lane]] estimated in 2025 that the wealth channel could ultimately match institutional in size for private markets allocation. The fundraising data through 2026 supports the trajectory — [[KKR]]'s K-Series private wealth raised $16bn in 2025 alone, [[StepStone Group]]'s SPWS hit $10.2bn AUM by mid-2025, and [[Blackstone]] reports private wealth as its fastest-growing channel.

## Why secondaries are operationally critical inside evergreens

Subscriptions arrive in cash. Cash sitting on the balance sheet drags the fund's reported return below the underlying asset return. Issuers therefore need deployable strategies that can absorb new cash faster than primary buyout fund commitments (which take 3-5 years to fully invest).

[[Private equity secondaries|Secondaries]] are the natural fit:

| Mechanism | How it serves evergreens |
|---|---|
| Existing portfolios | Cash deploys into positions immediately, not over years |
| Closer to exits | Distributions cycle back faster, refilling subscribers' redemption-funding needs |
| Diversification | Each transaction adds exposure to many underlying companies, supporting marketing claims |
| Mark-to-NAV gains | Discount-to-NAV purchases produce high early reported IRR, attracting new subscriptions |
| Operational fit | Smaller deal size + faster diligence cycle than buyout origination |

The secondaries-evergreen marriage is consequential enough that the FT (Apr 2026) cited it as the primary structural driver behind elite-firm secondaries consolidation ([[EQT]]–[[Coller Capital]], [[KKR]]–[[Arctos]], [[Ares Management]]–Landmark, [[CVC Capital Partners|CVC]]–Glendower, [[Apollo]] S3 build).

| Evergreen vehicle | Manager | Secondaries allocation | Vehicle scale |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[BXPE]] | [[Blackstone]] | ~5% | Largest PE evergreen by AUM |
| [[StepStone Group\|STPEX]] | [[StepStone Group]] | High | $750mn+ at Nov 2025 launch |
| Hamilton Lane PE evergreen | [[Hamilton Lane]] | ~50% | Significant |
| Flagship US private wealth fund | [[Partners Group]] | ~17% | Multi-billion |
| Apollo Global Wealth PE | [[Apollo]] | Material | New product, scaling |
| Ares Wealth Solutions | [[Ares Management]] | Material | Multi-billion |

## Stress events and the liquidity-mismatch problem

The format embeds a known and unavoidable risk: subscribers can request liquidity faster than the underlying assets can be sold without taking discounts. The 2026 stress events made it visible in private credit and are building in private equity.

| Vehicle | Date | Event | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Cliffwater]] | Q1 2026 | ~14% redemption request rate | Capped at 5% NAV; ~36% fulfillment |
| [[Apollo]] ADS BDC | Q1 2026 | $1.6bn redemption requests (11.2% of NAV) | Honored ~$730mn (~46%); worst fulfillment among tracked managers |
| [[Morgan Stanley]] PCRX | Q1 2026 | ~11% request rate | Cap engaged |
| [[Blackstone]] BREIT | 2022-23 | Redemption gating | First high-profile case; eventually normalized after Saudi PIF backstop |
| [[Starwood]] SREIT | 2024 | Persistent gating | Operating at cap quarter after quarter |

The full landscape is documented in [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]]. The private equity evergreen channel has not yet experienced an analogous stress event, but the structural conditions are present:

- Same subscription/redemption asymmetry
- Same NAV-based pricing where marks lag market
- Same retail investor base with shorter time horizons
- Same dependence on a functioning exit environment for the underlying portfolio
- Greater illiquidity in the underlying assets than private credit (which has loan-level cash flows)

## The mark-to-NAV problem for retail investors

Evergreen secondaries vehicles report high early IRRs partly because of the mechanical mark-to-NAV gain when buying at a discount. This is documented in detail at [[Private equity secondaries#The mark-to-NAV problem|PE secondaries — mark-to-NAV problem]].

The retail-investor risk is that high early reported IRRs attract subscriptions from individuals who:
- Believe the high return is what they will personally experience
- Are subscribing in year 3+ after the mark-up gain has already accrued to earlier subscribers
- Hold for a shorter window than the discount-decay period
- Hold for a period during which underlying-asset realization will dominate

[[Partners Capital]] partner Euan Finlay (Apr 2026 FT): if early returns "had been driven by buying secondaries at a discount and marking them up, you will not benefit from that uplift" as a later investor.

The institutional LP base understands this. The wealth-channel base — accessing through advisors who themselves may not fully understand the structure — may not.

## Regulatory landscape

US:
- BDCs governed by 1940 Act (Investment Company Act), with disclosure and concentration rules
- Non-traded REITs governed by 1940 Act + state-level blue-sky rules
- Interval funds governed by 1940 Act with explicit periodic-repurchase mechanics
- SEC adviser-led secondaries rules (Aug 2023) vacated by Fifth Circuit (Jun 2024) — see [[Continuation fund]]
- Accredited investor threshold: $200K income or $1mn net worth — not adjusted for inflation since 1982
- DOL retirement plan rules (in flux) on whether private capital can be defaulted into target-date funds

Europe:
- ELTIF 2.0 framework (Jan 2024) liberalized retail access to private capital
- AIFMD II tightened disclosure requirements on illiquid funds with retail access
- MiFID II suitability rules apply to wealth-channel sales

The regulatory direction has been broadly toward wider retail access, not narrower. The 2026 redemption-cap events did not produce regulatory tightening because no covered fund actually broke gates or sold below NAV — the cap mechanisms worked as designed.
