# Precise mass

Doctrinal term coined by Michael Horowitz ([[Council on Foreign Relations]]) for the production-side inversion of the precision-strike paradigm: where precision once meant a handful of Tomahawks or stealth bombers, it now means cheap one-way drones built from commercial parts and launched in swarms. Precision plus mass. The thesis is that this combination becomes "a regular feature of warfare, just like machine guns or tanks" (per Horowitz, cited in Zakaria/WashPost March 20, 2026).

The investable read: precise mass is the structural bull case for the attritable cohort ([[AeroVironment]], [[Kratos]], [[Anduril]], [[Shield AI]], [[Helsing]], [[Stark]]) and the bear case for incumbent prime-contractor unit economics that depend on every platform surviving every mission. The [[Russian sovereign drone ecosystem]] is the leading empirical instantiation of the production curve precise mass requires.

## Synthesis

The thesis matters because it changes the unit of analysis in defense procurement from platform survivability to cost per effect. Once you accept that a $500 FPV drone destroying a $5M armored vehicle is the dominant exchange ratio rather than an exotic edge case, the procurement budget logically migrates from a small number of exquisite manned platforms toward large numbers of attritable autonomous systems. That migration is what the [[Replicator Initiative]] / [[Defense Autonomous Warfare Group]] FY2027 budget signal is operationalizing on the US side and what the [[NATO Drone Wall Initiative]] is funding on the European side.

The bull case for capital allocation is concentration in the proven attritable cohort plus the AI-autonomy stack ([[Anduril]] Lattice, [[Shield AI]] Hivemind, [[Auterion]] OS) plus the counter-drone economics ([[Counter-UAS]]). The bear case is that the same cost asymmetry that makes drone offense cheap also commoditizes the airframe layer over time, leaving software and production-curve scale as the durable moats. Russia's centralized champion-picking model and Ukraine's ~50,000 FPV/month decentralized model are the two empirical proof points that the production curve can be built — the question is whether the US and European institutional structures can build it without fragmenting procurement across too many programs.

## Cost asymmetry

| Platform | Unit cost | Philosophy |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| [[F-35]] | ~$80M | Must survive every mission |
| MQ-9 Reaper | ~$30M | Expensive, limited numbers |
| XQ-58 Valkyrie | ~$3M | Designed to be lost |
| [[LUCAS]] | ~$35K | One-way attack, reverse-engineered Shahed |
| Switchblade 600 | ~$50K | One-use, anti-armor |
| FPV drone ([[Ukraine]]) | ~$500 | Disposable, mass-produced |

For the cost of one [[F-35]]: 26 Valkyries, 1,600 Switchblades, or 160,000 FPV drones. Quantity has a quality all its own.

## Production-curve evidence

- [[Ukraine]] ~50,000 FPV drones/month domestic production
- [[Russia]] 404 Shahed-type drones/day, targeting 1,000/day; state targets of 130,000 large UAS/yr by 2030 and 350,000/yr by 2035 ([[Russian sovereign drone ecosystem]])
- US [[Lockheed Martin]] Patriot interceptor capacity ~600 in 2025, targeting 2,000 by 2027 — the production asymmetry is the strategic problem, not the technology gap
