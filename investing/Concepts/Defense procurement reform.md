# Defense procurement reform

US [[Defense]] procurement reform campaign by the second [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration to invert the Army's purchasing mix from roughly 90% purpose-built military solutions to 90% commercial off-the-shelf, with the explicit goal that at least one of the [[Defense Primes]] is out of business within two years. Architected by Army Secretary [[Dan Driscoll]], codified at the cabinet level by [[Defense]] Secretary [[Pete Hegseth]]'s November 2025 procurement strategy, and enforced through [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s January 8, 2026 [[Trump defense budget|executive order]] that ties capital-return restrictions to production-capacity investment.

The reform is the most aggressive challenge to the prime-contractor model since the post-Cold War "Last Supper" of 1993, and the inverse direction — that consolidation went from 51 primes to 5; this campaign aims to break the 5 by routing demand around them rather than through them.

---

## Synthesis

The story is not simply that the Pentagon wants cheaper procurement. It is a bid to rebuild dual-use industrial capacity by routing more demand toward commercial manufacturers and software-first insurgents, while using budget growth and capital-return pressure to force the [[Defense Primes]] to change. The market question is whether this becomes a genuine multi-year sourcing shift or remains a political threat layered on top of the same prime-controlled programs of record.

---

## The structural claim

The argument the reform is built on, drawing from the analytical case already in [[Defense supply chain]] and [[Defense Primes]]:

- In 1989 (Berlin Wall still standing), 94% of US weapons spending went to dual-purpose companies that also made consumer products; 6% went to pure-play defense specialists
- By 2026 those shares have inverted: 86% goes to specialists, six total primes remain (five ex-[[Boeing]])
- The dual-use industrial base — Chrysler making both Minuteman ICBMs and minivans, [[Ford]] building satellites until 1990, General Mills building torpedoes and inertial guidance off grain-processing R&D — was lost in the consolidation
- WWII mobilization took 18 months from a base of existing factories; today's base does not exist at the same depth, and the [[Defense supply chain]] note documents the 8-day munitions inventory vs roughly 800 days needed in a [[China]] scenario

[[Dan Driscoll]] frames this as the primes having "conned the American people and the Pentagon and the Army into thinking that it needed military specific solutions, when in reality, a lot of these commercial solutions are equal to or better."

[[Shyam Sankar]] of [[Palantir]] frames the same problem as: "When we lost the American industrial base and got a defense industrial base, you lose volume. You lose the R&D stimulus to come up with creative ideas."

The reform's structural answer is to rebuild a dual-use industrial base by buying off-the-shelf from commercial manufacturers and software-first insurgents like [[Anduril]] and [[Palantir]], rather than asking the primes to expand their specialist capacity.

## The 90/10 inversion

The Army's stated target mix, as articulated by [[Dan Driscoll]]:

| Procurement category | Today (Army) | Driscoll target |
|----------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Purpose-built military solutions | ~90% | ~10% |
| Commercial off-the-shelf | ~10% | ~90% |

The inversion is the durable framework. Specific contracts and policy moves should be read as steps along this trajectory, not as discrete events.

## What the reform asks of commercial manufacturers

The April 2026 [[Pentagon]] outreach to [[General Motors]] and [[Ford]] is the operational pattern. Per the FT and WSJ reporting:

- "Wide-ranging" preliminary talks at the senior-Pentagon level with [[General Motors]] and [[Ford]] CEOs
- Focus is on producing components or parts for existing weapons systems, not whole platforms
- Advanced-manufacturing emphasis (3D printing) to break supply-chain bottlenecks the [[Defense Primes]] cannot clear
- Goal explicitly framed as helping the existing defense contractors, not displacing them on whole-system contracts — but the structural effect, if scaled, is to create alternative suppliers who can be substituted in the next contract cycle
- The talks pre-date the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]], so this is not an emergency-mobilization story; it is the planned campaign hitting its operational phase

[[General Motors|GM]] is the more immediately relevant actor — it already runs a GM Defense-style subsidiary working on autonomous vehicles capable of transporting wounded personnel and is part of the [[Artemis]] lunar-rover consortium whose battery and off-road capabilities have direct military read-through. [[Ford]] does not have a dedicated defense subsidiary, which makes its inclusion the more interesting structural signal — the Pentagon is recruiting commercial capacity that has not historically had a defense business at all.

| Sector-level | [[Defense Autonomous Warfare Group]] (DAWG, ~$55B FY27 request) is the autonomous-systems funding vehicle the reform routes through | Generalist [[Defense Primes]] basket faces a multi-year overhang on terminal-margin assumptions |

The market reaction in [[Trump defense budget]] (primes +6.7% on the $1.5T headline, then -4.8% on the executive order the next day) is the template: budget growth is the carrot, prime-specific friction is the stick, and net-net the rerating depends on which side investors weigh more heavily over the multi-quarter campaign.

## Tensions and constraints

- Capital-return mechanics: [[Defense Primes]] are slow-growth, average-margin businesses whose investor base is built around buybacks and dividends. Restricting capital returns and capping executive pay until production capacity is built is a structurally hostile policy for the standing equity holder. [[Bloomberg]] Intelligence analysts Will Lee and George Ferguson flagged this in the [[Trump defense budget]] note.
- Enforceability: The government does not own equity in the primes, has no board seats, and the executive order has no statutory backing for the capital restrictions. Legal challenges are likely.
- Pentagon execution: Even if the budget passes and the reform direction holds, the [[Pentagon]] cannot redirect contracting at speed; the talks with [[General Motors]] and [[Ford]] are explicitly preliminary.
- Commercial-manufacturer interest: [[General Motors]] is in the EV pullback already documented in its actor note; whether senior management views defense as a margin-accretive use of idled capacity, or a political distraction, will determine how seriously the talks scale. [[Ford]] has even less defense muscle memory.
- Prime defense capacity: Even at the inversion target, the primes still hold the program-of-record contracts, the qualified-supplier ecosystems, and the security clearances. The reform is a multi-year reconfiguration, not an overnight substitution.
