#concept #accounting #data #china #intangibles

**Data as an asset** — the question of whether the data a company holds should be recognized, measured, and reported as an asset on its balance sheet, rather than treated as a costless byproduct of operations. [[China]] has moved furthest: it designated data a "factor of production" in 2020 and, from January 2024, lets enterprises book data resources as either inventory or intangible assets under formal accounting rules. The [[United States]] generates an estimated $200bn-a-year data-broker market and treats customer data as the most valuable asset in bankruptcies, yet recognizes none of it under [[GAAP]]. The gap is between economic reality — where data is the feedstock for AI — and accounting treatment, where for most firms it does not exist.

---

## Why it matters

Accounting recognition is not bookkeeping arcana; it determines what gets measured, protected, financed against, and disclosed. An asset that appears nowhere on the balance sheet is an asset no one is required to value, secure, or report when it is breached or sold. As data becomes the essential input to AI systems, the inability to measure it leaves a structural blind spot in corporate valuation, M&A, bankruptcy, and national data policy. The live tension, argued by [[Mike Kuiken]] in a February 2026 FT op-ed, is strategic intent versus strategic indifference: China is experimenting with data valuation as state policy while the US has not engaged the question at the standard-setting level at all. Whether that experiment produces durable value or, as the [[US-China Economic and Security Review Commission]] flagged, "creative accounting in service of a debt crisis," is unresolved.

## China's framework

China has built data valuation in three layers — economic doctrine, accounting machinery, and market infrastructure.

### Data as the fifth factor of production (2020)

In March 2020 the Communist Party Central Committee and State Council issued "Opinions on Building a More Complete System for Market-Based Allocation of Factors of Production," formally adding data as a fifth market factor alongside land, labour, capital, and technology. This was a doctrinal declaration — it placed data in the same conceptual category as the classical inputs to production and triggered a cascade of national and provincial industrial policy treating data allocation as a policy objective. See [[National Data Administration]] for the bureaucracy built on top of it.

### The 2024 accounting rule

China's Ministry of Finance circulated the "Interim Provisions on the Accounting Treatment for Enterprise Data Resources" on 1 August 2023, effective 1 January 2024. The rule is more precise than the shorthand "data as intangible assets" suggests: it provides for two recognition paths depending on how the data is used.

| Use of data resource | Balance-sheet classification | Standard analogue |
|---|---|---|
| Held for sale in the ordinary course of business | Inventory | inventory accounting |
| Used internally, sold as a service, or licensed | Intangible asset | intangible-asset accounting |

For internally generated data, development-phase costs may be capitalized only if the enterprise can demonstrate technical feasibility, intent and ability to use or sell, probable future economic benefit, adequate resources, and reliable cost measurement — the same hurdle that governs internally developed intangibles. This is what makes China first-of-kind: not that it values data philosophically, but that it created a formal, auditable path onto the balance sheet.

Adoption has lagged the rhetoric. As of end-April 2025, only about 2% of A-share listed companies — roughly 100 firms — had disclosed any data-resource recognition on the balance sheet. The framework exists; the take-up is thin, and the actual market value of recognized data assets remains, in the USCC's words, "difficult to determine."

### The marquee example: China Unicom

[[China Unicom]], one of the world's largest mobile operators, reported Rmb204mn (~$29mn) in data assets in its first filing under the new rules (per the FT), drawing on the data generated by its ~345mn mobile subscribers. The figure is small relative to the company's ~Rmb671bn total assets — illustrating both the novelty of the recognition and how conservatively it is being applied.

### Data exchanges and the debt motive

The [[National Data Administration]] oversees dozens of data exchanges (Shanghai, Shenzhen, Guiyang/GBDEx and others) intended to give traded data a price. A less philosophical driver sits underneath: local government financing vehicles ([[LGFV]]), the off-balance-sheet entities Chinese municipalities use to fund infrastructure and which are heavily indebted, have in some cases used data as collateral for fresh loans. The USCC's 2024 report flagged this — recognizing data as a balance-sheet asset creates new collateral against which strained borrowers can lend, even where the asset's true market value is unverifiable. Recognition and leverage are linked.

## The US gap

The United States knows data has value and refuses to say so on the balance sheet.

- The data-broker industry generates an estimated $200bn+ annually trading information that appears on no corporate balance sheet (Kuiken's figure; broker-market estimates vary widely and are contested).
- Courts already treat customer data as a company's most valuable asset in distress: the [[FTC]] intervened in the Toysmart bankruptcy (2000) to stop customer data being sold; RadioShack's 2015 bankruptcy produced a legal fight over its ~117mn customer records.
- The 2015 Office of Personnel Management breach compromised the security-clearance data of 21.5mn Americans — an asset valued at zero on any ledger, and protected accordingly.

Under [[GAAP]], internally generated data is expensed as incurred, not capitalized — the same treatment that keeps most internally developed intangibles off the balance sheet (contrast [[ASC 350]], which governs only acquired intangibles and goodwill). The [[FASB]] has no project to recognize data assets; the [[SEC]] has no disclosure requirement for material data holdings. Federal agencies — sitting on agricultural, geological, and anonymized health data — have no systematic valuation framework either.

## Why it matters for markets

Three read-throughs:

1. AI feedstock. Data is the essential input to model training. A regime that cannot measure data cannot measure the most strategic input to the highest-growth technology sector — a blind spot in valuing AI-exposed companies.
2. Hidden value in distress and M&A. Data already drives bankruptcy outcomes and acquisition premia despite being invisible on the balance sheet; recognition would move that value from footnote and courtroom into the financials.
3. Precedent exists. Governments have measured and auctioned the electromagnetic spectrum — rights to invisible frequencies — for decades. The argument is that data is the same kind of problem: invisible, valuable, and measurable once the will exists.

Kuiken's proposed remedies: FASB should open a data-asset recognition project; the SEC should study disclosure requirements for material data holdings; Congress should mandate federal agencies assess the value of their data; state and local governments should do the same. None requires adopting China's exchange-led model wholesale.

## Synthesis

Data is the clearest case of value that everyone transacts and no one books. The accounting question — does data go on the balance sheet — is really a question about who gets to measure, secure, finance against, and disclose the most strategic input to the AI economy. [[China]] answered it top-down: data became a factor of production by doctrine (2020), then a recognizable asset by rule ([[National Data Administration]]-coordinated MOF provisions, 2024), wired into [[LGFV|municipal balance sheets]] as collateral against a debt overhang. The mechanism is real but the take-up is thin — ~2% of A-shares — so the policy is still mostly intent. The [[United States]] sits at the opposite pole: deeper, more liquid markets for data (the $200bn broker industry, data as the prize asset in the [[FTC]]'s Toysmart fight and RadioShack's bankruptcy) but zero standard-setting engagement, because [[GAAP]] expenses internally generated intangibles and [[FASB]] has no project. The asymmetry [[Mike Kuiken]] names — strategic intent versus strategic indifference — is the read-through: the side that learns to measure data shapes how AI-era enterprise value is valued, taxed, secured, and disclosed. Recognition is downstream of who decides data is worth counting.
