# May 16, 2026 — The AI-capex regime cracks the de-equitisation put while Iran reprices the global long end

## SpaceX, Anthropic, and the supply story that replaces the buyback story

[[SpaceX]] locked in its IPO calendar across Friday: June 12 listing on Nasdaq under ticker SPCX, a 5-for-1 share split completing May 22, the public S-1 filing as soon as Wednesday, and a valuation now above $2 trillion — revised intraday from the FT's $1.75T morning print. The bank syndicate has grown beyond the original five-name alphabetical structure, with [[Michael Grimes]] as central deal-runner. [[Anthropic]] separately agreed terms for a $30B Series H at $900B — leapfrogging [[OpenAI]]'s recent $852B mark, with WSJ sources putting the run-rate revenue trajectory at $50B by end of Q2 (vs $9B at end of 2025).

The same WSJ piece pulls [[PitchBook]]'s structural number: in Q1 2026, five names — OpenAI, Anthropic, [[xAI]], [[Waymo]], [[Databricks]] — absorbed roughly 75% of total venture investment, a concentration the firm calls without modern precedent. Six firms on the GP side raised $36.4B in Q1, more than 75% of all LP commits. See [[Venture capital#Q1 2026 capital concentration]].

[[Robert Buckland]]'s Saturday FT column names what these data points are doing collectively. The [[De-equitisation|de-equitisation put]] — the structural shrinkage of public equity supply since 2003 through buybacks, debt-financed M&A, and PE take-privates — has been the load-bearing support beam through valuation, rate, and geopolitical shocks. Big Tech 2026 AI capex of $725B (per BofA) has now forced [[Meta]] and [[Alphabet]] to pause buybacks (vs $92B combined in 2024); the $4T SpaceX + OpenAI + Anthropic IPO cohort represents roughly 6% public-equity expansion, a level last seen in the late-1990s bubble.

The tension: the same VC concentration that funded the IPO cohort is now also the cohort that needs the public market to clear it. The Q1 GP-LP-portfolio quasi-closed system makes the absorption test a structural liquidity test for the whole cycle.

## The day Iran started repricing the global long end as stagflation

Friday's bond rout was the moment the war's economic transmission completed. US 30-year Treasury at 5.12% — highest closing yield since July 2007. JGB 30-year above 4% for the first time in history. UK 30-year gilts at 5.85% — highest since 1998, with the [[Andy Burnham|Burnham]] leadership-challenge premium adding political risk on top. Brent crude $109.26, up 50% since the US-Israeli strike on Iran. The OIS swaps curve now prices a full 25 bp Fed hike by the March 2026 meeting (vs Feb 27's curve which still priced cuts). G7 finance chiefs are convening on the selloff. See [[2026 Iran conflict market impact#May 15, 2026 — global long-end repricing]].

The structural read is [[Priya Misra]]'s: "the market starts to move from 'is this just inflationary?' to 'is this stagflationary?'" The physical-market evidence is in [[LNG]]: [[Mubadala Energy]] + [[CPP Investments]] + [[BlackRock]] + [[Ares Management]] just closed $9.75B of project financing for Commonwealth LNG in Cameron Parish — a $13B export plant joining [[Venture Global]]'s adjacent $8.6B CP2 expansion. Rapidan's Alex Munton: the war "has knocked Qatar and UAE's proposed LNG expansions sideways and removed about 80mn tonnes from the market... that leaves only the US to provide growth at scale." Middle East→US capital pivot showing up as physical infrastructure, not just bond yields.

The other side of the same coin: [[BofA]] private clients ($4.5T AUM) hold a record 65.7% in equities and record-low 9.8% in cash, while $5B+ has flowed into covered-call and buffer ETFs in the past month. The retail extremes and the defensive flows are happening in the same allocation accounts.

## Berkshire's first 13F under Greg Abel: rotation, not deployment

[[Berkshire Hathaway]]'s Q1 13F is the first under [[Greg Abel]]'s 94% discretion (residual 6% with [[Ted Weschler]]; [[Todd Combs]] left for JPMorgan in December). The signature trade is [[Delta Air Lines]]: a new $2.65B / 6.1% / 39.8M-share position that explicitly reverses Buffett's April 2020 airline exit ("the world had changed"). Reversing a six-year-old Buffett decision in your first quarter is how a new CEO claims portfolio identity.

[[Alphabet]] tripled to $16.6B; [[Macy's]] new at $55M; [[New York Times]] doubled to 9.4%. Exits: [[Amazon]], [[UnitedHealth]], [[Visa]], [[Mastercard]], [[Domino's Pizza]], [[Aon]], [[Pool Corp]]. [[Chevron]] trimmed ~$8B (35%) at a $182.59 VWAP into the March oil-spike high — Berkshire's 2020 entry was at $65, 2022 add at $124, this trim at $182.59. Net Q1: $15.94B buys, $24.09B sells, -$8.15B disposition into a $288B book with the $397B cash pile held in reserve. Abel is tightening grip on the portfolio without changing its size — rotation, not deployment.

## Three probes hit the AI and private-credit cycles simultaneously

Three Bloomberg and FT pieces converged Friday on the same observation: the regulatory state is now investigating the structures the AI and private-credit cycles depend on. [[Arm Holdings]] faces parallel antitrust probes in the US (FTC), EU (Commission, on Qualcomm's 2024 complaint), and South Korea (Seoul office inspections last year) — the question is whether Arm will degrade CPU-blueprint licenses as it ramps its own $15B-by-2030 chip-design business. [[BlackRock]]'s TCP Capital Corp is under SDNY criminal investigation for valuation practices, with executives questioned. [[Jay Clayton]] (SDNY): "if people are mismarking in order to generate fees, that's always been a no-no." [[HSBC]] separately paused its own $4B private credit allocation after a $400M Q1 charge on back-leverage to an [[Apollo Global Management|Apollo]] unit with [[Market Financial Solutions]] exposure.

The three probes hit different layers of the same AI/credit infrastructure: Arm's licensing is the foundation under everyone's chip-design ambitions, BlackRock's BDC marks are templates for $1.8T in private-credit valuations, HSBC's pause is the bank-channel stepping back from the same market the DOJ is criminally investigating.
