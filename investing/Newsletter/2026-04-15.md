# 2026-04-15 — Hyperscalers buy the physical layer; BlackRock's alt engine compounds

## Amazon lands Globalstar at $11.6B — three satellite stacks, one strategy

Definitive agreement at $14/share, ~$11.6B cash — well above the ~$9B range the FT floated on Apr 2. [[Apple]]'s 85% capacity commitment for iPhone Emergency SOS stays in place through close; Apple's 20% equity converts to cash at deal price. FCC plus 12 foreign jurisdictions — targeted 2026-Q4 close.

Direct-to-device is the category at stake. Phones connect to satellites without a ground terminal, using the handset's own antenna against spectrum licensed for that purpose. The technical bar is high — it requires globally coordinated spectrum, a satellite constellation sized for ubiquitous coverage, and regulatory clearance in each country. Before today, three independents had pieces of the stack: [[Globalstar]] (L/S-band spectrum + operational LEO constellation, Apple's current host), [[AST SpaceMobile]] (large-aperture satellites built to work with standard cellular bands), and SpaceX (Starlink Direct-to-Cell, leveraging the scale of the existing Starlink fleet).

With this deal, [[Amazon]] owns three distinct satellite stacks: Kuiper (LEO broadband, the [[Starlink]] analog), Globalstar (direct-to-device plus the Apple relationship that comes with it), and Band 53 IoT (licensed spectrum for low-bandwidth device telemetry). Different orbits, different spectrum, different use cases — combined, a full-stack connectivity play at a scope only SpaceX can match. Amazon Leo's direct-to-device roadmap compresses from multi-year to "acquired." [[AST SpaceMobile]] becomes the only independent direct-to-cell pure-play — meaning the only one not captured by a hyperscaler, which matters if major carriers prefer to route traffic through a neutral third party rather than a cloud competitor.

Open question: does Apple extend capacity commitment beyond the current window once Amazon controls the host, or migrate Emergency SOS to AST or SpaceX? That decision sets the ceiling on Globalstar's post-acquisition earnings power. GSAT trading ~$92 against the $96 deal price implies ~4% spread to deal value — that's regulatory-close risk being priced, not deal-break risk.

## Oracle commits to ~2.8 GW of Bloom fuel cells — first hyperscaler bypass at GW scale

Multi-year agreement across OCI data centers. At ~2x [[Bloom Energy]]'s currently deployed 1.5 GW base, the deal requires Bloom's planned 2 GW/year manufacturing capacity (coming online end-2026) running at full tilt.

A solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) is a ceramic electrochemical generator: natural gas in, electricity plus high-quality heat out, no combustion. Efficiency runs 50-60% as pure electric, 85%+ if the heat is captured — well above a gas peaker's 35-40%. It sits next to the data center, consumes pipeline gas or hydrogen, and produces grid-independent baseload. The acronym that matters here is BYOP (bring-your-own-power): the hyperscaler generates its own electricity on-site and skips the utility grid queue entirely. This is the step beyond a standard PPA.

The speed number is the punchline. SOFC units deploy in under 50 days. Compare: a gas peaker is 12-18 months; a combined-cycle gas plant is ~3 years; a nuclear PPA with a new build is 10+ years; a grid-interconnect queue in the major data-center markets (Northern Virginia, Phoenix, Dallas) is currently quoting 3-7 years. For [[Oracle]], which has committed to $50B FY26 capex and is building capacity for [[Stargate]] and [[OpenAI]], power lead time is the binding constraint, not capital.

This is the first hyperscaler committing to fuel cells at multi-GW scale. It confirms the SemiAnalysis 44 GW power gap thesis — the gap between projected hyperscaler compute demand and the grid capacity that can actually be delivered — is being resolved through private infrastructure, not through the grid. The readthrough matrix follows from that logic:

- [[GE Vernova]] and [[Caterpillar]]: gensets remain the short-duration peak/backup layer. Not displaced by SOFC, but no longer the primary baseload play they were pre-deal.
- [[Constellation Energy]]: nuclear PPAs remain the premium baseload tier — higher capacity factor, carbon-free, but measured in 5-10 year lead times. Still the structural winner on scale, now on a longer clock.
- [[NextEra Energy]]: the BYOG (bring-your-own-generation) renewables-plus-storage model gets category validation. Oracle chose gas; someone else will choose firmed renewables.
- [[Bloom Energy]] itself: order book now heavily concentrated in [[Brookfield]] + Oracle. Manufacturing execution is the binding constraint — the 2 GW/year capacity is committed before it exists.

## BlackRock prints the structural alt-fee story in one quarter

Q1 2026: AUM $13.9T (+20% YoY), record Q1 long-term net inflows $135.9B, revenue $6.7B (+27% YoY), GAAP EPS $14.06 (+46% YoY), record 13% organic base fee growth. Stock opened higher.

The last number deserves a gloss. Organic base fee growth measures the rate at which the fee-generating asset base is compounding from inflows alone, stripping out both market appreciation and M&A additions. It answers: if markets had been flat and [[BlackRock]] had bought nothing, how fast would the fee base have grown? A durable 5-7% range is considered good for a scaled asset manager; double-digits is rare without a deliberate mix shift.

The mix shift is the story. [[BlackRock]]'s GIP acquisition ([[Global Infrastructure Partners]], ~$170B private-infrastructure AUM, closed Oct 2024) and HPS acquisition ([[HPS Investment Partners]], ~$150B private-credit AUM, closed Dec 2024) did more than add AUM — they shifted the fee-pool composition toward alternatives. Private infrastructure and private credit earn base fees of roughly 60-100 bps versus iShares ETFs at under 10 bps. The same dollar of AUM in alts produces 6-10x the fee dollars it does in passive. 13% organic base fee growth says this rebalancing is now compounding structurally, not cyclically.

This confirms the thesis [[Larry Fink]]'s annual letter laid out: alternatives are a permanent share of the fee pool, not a cyclical tailwind. Peer divergence is the trade to watch. [[T. Rowe Price]], [[Franklin Resources]], and [[State Street]] report later this week. They carry less alts exposure and less M&A-driven mix shift. If their organic base fee growth lines land in the 3-7% range against BLK's 13%, the P/E discount peers already trade at against BlackRock widens — the market pays up for the rerating optionality BLK has delivered and peers have not.

## Johnson & Johnson beats, raises FY26 on faster-than-feared biosimilar absorption

Q1 2026 revenue $24.1B (+9.9% YoY, +5.8% operational). Innovative Medicine $15.2B led by DARZALEX, [[TREMFYA]], CARVYKTI; MedTech $8.9B. Adj EPS $2.77 beat. FY26 guide raised to $100.3-101.3B. Stock +3-4% early trade.

Biosimilar cliff mechanics are the subtext. When a biologic drug's patent expires, generic-equivalent molecules (biosimilars) enter at 30-60% discounts. The historical erosion curve runs roughly 30-50% of branded revenue lost in year 1, 60-80% by year 3 — steeper than small-molecule generics because hospital formularies reprice quickly. STELARA ([[Johnson & Johnson]]'s ~$10B immunology franchise) hit its US patent cliff in Jan 2025, with global markets staggering through 2026. Analyst models had loaded steep erosion assumptions into 2026. A beat here means the erosion curve is flatter than consensus had pencilled in — STELARA prescriptions are defending better, and TREMFYA is absorbing the switching.

[[TREMFYA]] taking share from [[AbbVie]]'s [[Skyrizi]] and [[Humira]] is the offsetting engine. The ~$400M tariff headwind management reiterated is a known drag; the FY26 guide raise means the underlying franchise growth is running ahead of it. For the pharma season read, the relevant logic is concentration risk. [[Johnson & Johnson|JNJ]] has the lowest concentration of any large-cap pharma — four franchises each $5B+, plus MedTech as a separate leg. That diversification cushions biosimilar hits in a way more concentrated peers can't replicate. [[Eli Lilly]] runs ~60% of growth through Mounjaro/Zepbound; [[Merck]] is Keytruda-concentrated ahead of its own 2028 cliff; [[Pfizer]] is still post-COVID rebasing. If JNJ — with the most diversified cushion in the cohort — is barely absorbing the cliff with a raise, the more concentrated pharmas reporting later have less margin for guide integrity. [[Medtronic]] and [[Boston Scientific]] play on a different axis entirely (device cycles, not patent cliffs), but the MedTech read-across says consumer and elective procedure volume held.
