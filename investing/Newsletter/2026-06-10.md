# 2026-06-10 — The market stopped doubting AI demand and started invoicing it

## Oracle proved the orders; the tape repriced the bill

[[Oracle]] delivered the print the morning briefing called the first hard test of AI-cloud conversion: revenue $19.18B (+21%), [[Oracle Cloud Infrastructure|OCI]] accelerating to +93% from +84%, and [[Remaining performance obligations|RPO]] growing $85B in a single quarter to $638B — with a new disclosure that $75B of the big AI contracts is prepaid or customer-supplied hardware. The conversion test was answered with more accumulation, and the stock sold off anyway: -7%+ after hours off the $204.58 close, recovering to roughly -1% to -3% by late evening. The reason sat in the same release — FY2026 capex of $55.7B against ~$50B guided, free cash flow of -$23.7B, $43B of debt plus $5B of equity raised this year, and ~$40B more financing penciled for FY2027.

[[Super Micro Computer]] ran the same trade in smaller, messier form: $39B of AI-server orders from 20+ customers — about a year of revenue, arriving at once — funded by a $7B equity and convertible raise that cost the stock 26% in two sessions. Demand is no longer the contested variable anywhere in the AI stack. The funding path is. Every backlog now comes with an invoice attached, and the market is reading the invoice first.

## A hot print and a hot strait split the rest of the tape

May CPI at +4.2% YoY — fastest since May 2023, in line but hot — killed the residual July-cut case, while the [[Strait of Hormuz]] exchange escalated past April's ceasefire: a downed US Apache, retaliatory strikes, Iranian missiles on US bases in Jordan, Kuwait, and Bahrain. WTI sits at $95. The two shocks sorted the day cleanly: [[Devon Energy]] +6.6% on war premium plus its first post-[[Devon-Coterra merger February 2026|Coterra]] outlook (1.38M boe/d, $8B buyback), [[US Bancorp]] +2.7% on rate repricing, and everything long-duration-AI sold. [[Mike Wilson]] holds ~8,000 on the S&P but says inflation isn't returning to 2% soon; [[Ed Yardeni]] raised to 8,250 calling the pullback healthy. Same tape, opposite clocks.

## The AI-power map now has mathematics under it

![[ai-power-ipp-cluster-dendrogram-1y.png|The IPPs cut as one clean block; regulated utilities, fuel, and equipment all separate.]]

Three validation runs gave the AI-power space its statistical map in one evening. The merchant generators — [[Vistra]], [[Constellation Energy]], [[NRG Energy]], [[Talen Energy]] — are a genuine factor: 0.773 intra-correlation, 83% single-factor variance, permutation floor p-values against both random and vol-matched nulls, and a birthday — the factor formed in 2025 (intra-correlation 0.51 → 0.81) when the PPA wave hit. The cohort is registered with a June 10 definition date, so its out-of-sample ledger starts now — and the first datapoint arrived same-day, with [[NRG Energy]] -6.9% in the risk-off despite an oil-up tape. The equipment bloc that fell together (ETN -6.3%, GNRC -8.4%, PWR -6.4%) failed the same test: [[Power equipment complex]] co-moves at floor significance but never separates from CAT, Cummins, and XLI itself at any threshold — an equipment basket is leveraged industrials, and [[Generac]] turns out to be a statistical singleton that merely fell with the headline. [[Solaris Energy Infrastructure]], deepdived today from stub to full note (the xAI-anchored turbine landlord, a nine-bagger since its September 2024 pivot, 88% single-customer concentration, a Morpheus short file on the record), is the third shape: a real business with no peer factor at all.

## The AI-macro debate got its accounting

The FT backfill closed the loop on the economics layer. The [[Citrini 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis|Citrini GIC]] scenario — the February memo that knocked 822 points off the Dow — now sits filed with its institutional critics ([[Evercore]]'s Krishna Guha: the macro requires "a set of extreme and improbable conditions"), and the mechanism lives in [[AI disintermediation]]: agents competing away the oligopoly rents embedded in friction. Against it, the hard data cuts both ways: the Stanford Canaries study ([[Erik Brynjolfsson]]/[[Bharat Chandar]]/[[Ruyu Chen]]) shows a 16% relative employment decline for 22-25-year-olds in AI-exposed occupations, concentrated where AI automates rather than augments — while Brynjolfsson's own take-off claim has US productivity at ~2.7% in 2025 against a 1.4% prior-decade average. Displacement and productivity are both showing up in the data at once; firm-level rent compression and macro outcomes remain separate claims, and the gap between them is where 2026's whole AI argument lives.
