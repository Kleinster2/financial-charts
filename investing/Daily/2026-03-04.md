## Synthesis

The Pentagon-Anthropic dispute entered its most consequential phase yet. The story is no longer about a $200M contract — it's about whether the US government can effectively ban a domestic AI company from the defense ecosystem by executive fiat, and whether the industry will let it. The ITI letter (signed by NVIDIA, Amazon, Apple, OpenAI among others) is the first organized industry pushback. OpenAI is simultaneously pursuing NATO contracts AND publicly defending Anthropic's right to exist in the market — a remarkable split-screen. The legal scholars are near-unanimous that the supply-chain risk designation won't survive judicial review, but the damage is already being done: Palantir has to rebuild Maven, Lockheed is complying preemptively, and the State Department is switching to OpenAI. The most important number from today: Anthropic's revenue run rate hit ~$19B, up from $14B just weeks ago. The Pentagon standoff is accelerating consumer adoption faster than any marketing campaign ever could.

Meanwhile, NVIDIA is quietly closing the circle on its AI lab investments — $30B in OpenAI (down from $100B), $10B in Anthropic, both likely the last before IPOs. Jensen Huang framing these as the end of an era rather than the beginning. Both labs going public in 2026 changes the power dynamic: public shareholders don't tolerate the same opacity that private investors accepted.

Broadcom's Q1 was the strongest AI infrastructure signal of the week: AI revenue doubled to $8.4B, Q2 guide smashed consensus by $1.5B. This isn't a company benefiting from AI hype — it's the picks-and-shovels supplier confirming that hyperscaler capex ($630B+ this year) is translating into real orders. Intel's 18A reversal is the subplot: Tan's willingness to reconsider external foundry customers signals either genuine yield progress or desperation. Either way, it matters for the foundry competitive landscape.

The White House energy pledge is bigger in substance than the initial reports suggested. The transcript reveals company-specific commitments that go beyond a press-release pledge: Google has 7,800 MW contracted in Texas alone and is closing Intersect Power; xAI committed 1.2 GW per data center and dropped the orbital data center bombshell (Gwynne Shotwell: space-based DCs powered by the sun, freeing terrestrial plants for communities). Sacks's $650B figure — 2% of GDP — frames the AI buildout as macro-relevant infrastructure spending, not a tech-sector curiosity. The workforce dimension is real: Google training 100K electrical workers, Meta launching guaranteed-job fiber tech programs. The structural critique stands (paying doesn't build faster), but the political function is immediate: CEO signatures give local governments cover to approve data center projects. Indiana filing a rate *reduction* is the early proof point.

China AI: Alibaba lost three senior Qwen executives in two months despite the app surging from 31M to 203M MAU. The brain drain at the top while the product goes mainstream is a concerning signal — either the commercial direction is diverging from the research vision, or competitors (DeepSeek, ByteDance) are poaching.

## Energy pledge at White House (Mar 4)

[[Google]], [[Microsoft]], [[Meta]], [[Amazon]], [[Oracle]], [[xAI]], and [[OpenAI]] signed the [[Ratepayer Protection Pledge]] at the White House. Five commitments: pay for all AI power generation, cover grid upgrades, negotiate separate utility rates (paying even for unused capacity), invest in local workforce, provide backup power to grids. First announced in Trump's State of the Union. Full event note: [[Ratepayer Protection Pledge]].

Key numbers from signing ceremony: [[Google]] contracted 7,800 MW new energy in Texas, closing [[Intersect Power]] acquisition; [[xAI]] committing 1.2 GW per data center + announced orbital data centers; [[Meta]] claims $650M Louisiana ratepayer savings over 15 years; [[David Sacks]] cited $650B AI infrastructure investment this year (2% GDP tailwind); Google training 100,000 electrical workers + 30,000 apprentices. [[Lee Zeldin]] issuing permits in 2-3 weeks vs 20+ years historically. Indiana first state filing rate reduction. Jon Gordon critique still stands: paying for generation doesn't build it faster.
