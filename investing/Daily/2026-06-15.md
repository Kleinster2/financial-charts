## Synthesis

Lithium is the third commodity-beta cohort, and it is the one that maps the law's boundary. Copper and gold both showed that a commodity's listed miners cohere into a real factor that a single ETF replicates; lithium confirms the pattern a third time — the four names clear the vol-matched null at p 0.0003, so the cohesion is genuinely lithium-specific and not an artifact of their enormous shared volatility — but it does so at the lowest cohesion in the family (weekly 0.62 against gold's 0.86 and copper's 0.71), and the reasons why are the lesson. Uranium closes the commodity-beta family at four and extends it on the one axis the first three left implicit: how far the equity sits from the metal. The year made it concrete — the uranium equities returned +45-156% while the metal returned +8.5%, the widest equity-over-metal gap the family has recorded. With four metals validated, the family now reads as one law with two dials: cohesion set by the cleanliness of the price regime (gold and uranium tight in sustained bulls, copper and lithium loose in chop), and distance-from-metal set by the cohort's developer content — both forward-testable, a chop in uranium should loosen this 0.77 cohort toward copper's 0.71, and a wave of uranium developers reaching production should pull the equity back toward spot.

Oil & gas majors are the fifth commodity-beta cohort and the one that tests whether business model can rescue a commodity equity from being its ETF. It cannot. The integrated supermajors carry refining and chemicals that should partly hedge crude — refining margins widen when crude falls — yet [[ExxonMobil]] and [[Chevron]] trade indistinguishably from the pure E&P names [[ConocoPhillips]] and [[EOG Resources]] (the integrated and E&P sub-pairs merge at 0.22, correlation ~0.78), because the upstream crude exposure dominates the stock's factor and the downstream offset is second-order. The result is the family's purest verdict: validated, exceptionally durable cohesion (0.80 intra, 85% PC1, every null at the floor, holdout 0.97) that is nonetheless only +0.04 distinct from XLE — the cohort literally contains its benchmark, with the energy ETF sitting inside the XOM/CVX sub-branch on the dendrogram. The campaign now has the full commodity-beta wedge spectrum, and it scales with how much of a cohort is forward-thesis rather than spot exposure: uranium's +0.18 (half the names pre-production developers pricing a supply deficit), gold's +0.07, oil's +0.04 (mature producers uniformly levered to a deep, liquid price). The constructive find is one step down the value chain: oilfield services (SLB/HAL/BKR) is the single genuinely distinct energy factor (+0.34, its own dendrogram branch), because services trade on the drilling/capex activity cycle — rig counts and E&P budgets — not directly on spot crude. So "energy" is two equity factors after all, but the split is producers-vs-services (price vs activity), not integrated-vs-E&P (business model) — the same lesson the campaign keeps finding: cohorts split by dominant driver, never by org chart. The placement also exercised the doc rule written minutes earlier: with no existing note owning the oil-equity cohort identity ([[Oil]] is the commodity, [[Energy and Utilities]] a broad mixed hub), the finding earned a standalone [[Oil and gas equity beta]] concept — the new-factor-concept case, parallel to its commodity siblings.

Managed care is the campaign's cleanest "sometimes-factor" — a cohort whose existence depends on the regime. The question it was built to answer is whether the 2024–25 health-insurer crisis ([[UnitedHealth]]'s DOJ probe, the [[Medicare Advantage]] rate squeeze, the medical-cost spike that blew through every loss ratio) drove the names apart on idiosyncratic stories or bound them into one object. The rolling history answers sequentially: they fragmented to 0.40 in 2024–25 as the problems surfaced unevenly, then re-cohered to 0.59 in 2026 as the market began trading the whole group as one regulatory-and-medical-cost trade — so the shared shock did bind them, but only after first pulling them apart, and the holdout's negative loadings correlation says the factor's internal structure reshuffled in the process. What it is not is a clean basket: the health-providers ETF IHF sits inside the cluster, so the managed-care factor is the ETF, and [[Cigna]] — whose Express Scripts PBM dominates its mix — trades as an outlier rather than a core insurer. The constructive cut is an end-market split that mirrors casinos and sports betting: the diversified commercial-plus-Medicare insurers (= IHF) and the Medicaid MCOs (Centene, Molina) are two distinct factors, because Medicaid names live on rate notices and redeterminations while the diversified names live on the commercial-and-Medicare-Advantage cycle. Set against the day's commodity work, managed care is the opposite kind of cohort: oil majors are a permanent factor that is always the ETF; managed care is a conditional factor that is the ETF only when a sector-wide shock dominates — durable structure versus regime-contingent structure, both ETF-replicable for different reasons.

The capstone closes the campaign by asking the one question no individual validation could: after this many shots on goal, are the verdicts real or is the campaign a multiple-testing artifact? They are real. Across the 83 cohorts that carry a permutation p-value, 71 survive Benjamini-Hochberg FDR control and the survivor count is identical to the uncorrected count — so correcting for ~80 tests removes essentially nothing, and the dozen cohorts that fail are precisely the ones the campaign had already falsified by hand ([[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1]], [[Theatrical exhibition]], [[DTC Telehealth]], [[Ad-tech]], the IoT-module trio). The statistics and the judgment agree, which is the strongest evidence the hand-built verdicts were measuring something. The out-of-sample layer does the work in-sample tests cannot: of the 33 cohorts old enough to grade on post-definition data, 29 hold or strengthen, and the three that do not — [[Mag 7]], [[Boutique advisory consolidation|boutique advisory]], and the edge control-plane basket — are now flagged for downgrade, the last of them the textbook overfit catch (clean in-sample, intra-correlation collapsing to zero on unseen days). But the capstone's most useful output is a caveat, not a count: the whole correction runs on the random-basket null, which certifies co-movement, not distinctness — so "71 survive" must be read as 71 real cohorts, not 71 tradeable factors, because the campaign's hardest-won finding is that most validated cohorts (the entire commodity family, the [[Mega banks basket|mega banks]], the [[Regulated utilities]]) ARE their sector ETF, and that verdict lives in the threshold scan the FDR never sees. The number to remember is not 71 or 43 but the distinction itself: cohesion is cheap and correctable-for; distinctness from the ETF is the rare and valuable thing, and it is a separate test. Full writeup in [[2026-06-15-cluster-validation-capstone]].

Online travel completes the travel-cyclical set and, in the process, delivered the campaign's cleanest lesson on data integrity. The cohort itself is the third distinct travel factor: the OTAs ([[Booking Holdings]], [[Airbnb]], [[Expedia]]) cohere (intra 0.585, weekly 0.635, PC1 72%) and stand apart from airlines, cruises, hotels, and the market alike (positive intra-advantage against every one), so "travel" resolves into at least three separable equity factors split by trip-economics role — seat-and-fuel ([[Airlines]]), berth-and-leverage ([[Cruise lines]]), and booking-take-rate (OTAs). What makes OTAs different from the day's other validated cohorts is that they are not ETF-replicable: oil is XLE, managed care is IHF, but there is no pure online-travel ETF, so the OTA factor exists only as the three names — a rare case where the basket is the only expression. It is also the loosest and most eroding of the travel trio (fragile threshold, weakened holdout, a sharp 2024 dip), fitting platform businesses with idiosyncratic regulatory and turnaround stories. The deeper result, though, is the data-integrity catch underneath it. The first run falsified the cohort — intra-corr 0.21, Booking a 367%-volatility uncorrelated singleton — and the verdict was entirely an artifact: Booking's stored prices carried an un-back-adjusted 25-for-1 split, a fake −96% return that decorrelated it from everything. The framework's own diagnostics surfaced the impossibility, the fix re-fetched split-adjusted data, and the verdict inverted to validated. The follow-on scan of all 2,158 tickers confirmed the damage was contained — only Booking reached a cohort, every other flagged cohort name was a real catalyst move, and the broader 2025-12-24 mis-adjustment touched only ETF and foreign series no cohort uses — but the episode is the campaign's clearest proof of the rule it has repeated throughout: a clean, dramatic statistical result is a reason to check the inputs, not to trust the output. The validation is only ever as good as the data underneath it.

Pharma majors closes the day's healthcare arc and completes a clean triptych of falsification by dominant-driver divergence. After [[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1]] (the obesity drugmakers) and [[Medtech]] (the device makers), big pharma is the third healthcare label that turns out not to be a factor: intra-corr 0.412, weekly dropping to 0.327, and a shatter that is diagnostic of the cause. [[Eli Lilly]] — the GLP-1 growth winner — clusters with biotech and the health ETFs rather than its pharma peers, [[Pfizer]] and [[Bristol-Myers Squibb]] pair only because they share a post-patent-cliff decline, and [[Merck]] and [[AbbVie]] each trade on their own franchise clock. The intra-advantage tells the whole story: ~zero versus biotech and negative versus the sector ETF, so the cohort clears the random-basket null only on shared healthcare beta — grade-2 in the campaign's taxonomy, the same as Medtech. The contrast with the day's managed-care run sharpens it: both are healthcare, both are ETF-adjacent, but managed care is a real (if regime-dependent) factor that a shared regulatory-and-medical-cost shock binds, while big pharma has no shared shock — each name lives on its own pipeline and patent calendar, so the label never resolves into a factor at all. The investable read is the cleanest kind: own XLV for healthcare exposure and [[Eli Lilly]] (or the GLP-1 names) for the growth trade; the five-name "big pharma basket" is not a thing to hold.
