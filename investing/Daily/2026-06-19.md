## Synthesis

"Refresh the WFE note" turned into a two-layer job: a routine re-validation of the campaign's tightness ceiling, and a data-integrity catch that would have silently inverted it. The validation confirmed what the cohort has always shown — [[WFE]] (ASML/AMAT/KLAC/LRCX) is the tightest, most durable factor in the vault (intra 0.82, PC1 86.5%, 0.82-0.91 every year since 2020) — and re-confirmed it is also the cleanest "real but embedded" case: WFE is so large a weight in SMH/SOXX that the cohort never separates from the semis ETFs (threshold width 0.00, intra-advantage only +0.077), so the right expression is the ETF, not a bespoke four-name basket. Same verdict shape as [[Mega banks basket|mega banks]] = XLF and the commodity-equity cohorts = their sector ETF: tight, real, durable, but ETF-replicable.

The more consequential lesson is the recurring pipeline bug. For the third time in the campaign (after the BKNG 25:1 catch and the earlier 12-series batch), an un-back-adjusted split inverted a cluster verdict — KLA's June 10:1 split made the tightest cohort in the vault look like its loosest (0.42), purely because a lookback price update appended post-split prices onto unadjusted history. The signature is now unmistakable (a single-day discontinuity at a clean split ratio with a sustained level shift), and the fix is a full auto-adjust re-fetch when the data vendor has the split, or a manual factor adjustment when it does not — as with Offerpad, where Yahoo still has not applied the reverse split. The sibling scan is the right reflex after any split-driven catch: KLA's recent split made other June splitters likely, and it caught Offerpad. The standing risk is structural — `update_market_data.py --lookback N` cannot back-adjust history for a split that lands inside its window, so every recent splitter is a latent corruption until a full re-fetch runs. A periodic split-discontinuity scan (the one written today) is the cheapest guard.

Oilfield services answers the question the oil-majors run teed up: does energy fracture by position in the value chain? It does. Services ([[Schlumberger|SLB]]/[[Halliburton|HAL]]/[[Baker Hughes|BKR]]/[[NOV]]/[[TechnipFMC|FTI]]/[[Weatherford|WFRD]]) validates as its own factor — distinct from the crude pole (+0.254 vs the majors, a separate dendrogram cluster) — because the services names are paid on customer capex (rigs, fracs, completions) and ride the drilling cycle rather than spot crude. That is the energy instance of the value-chain-split law, the same shape as towers vs data-center REITs ([[Tower REITs]]): one macro theme, two distinct equity factors split by where in the chain a name sits. But the split is ETF-bounded on both sides — the majors are XLE ([[Oil and gas equity beta]]), services are OIH (inside the cohort from the tightest cut) — so the actionable read is "own XLE for crude beta, OIH for the capex cycle," not a bespoke basket. And unlike the structurally permanent crude pole (holdout 0.97), the services factor is conditional on the cycle: it tightened into the 2024–25 spending wave (0.83) and is eroding in 2026 (0.66, holdout 0.74) as activity plateaus and the names diverge on backlog. A real factor, distinct from crude, but rented from the capex cycle and rentable via OIH. The energy value chain now has two validated, ETF-bounded poles; refiners and midstream remain the open legs.

Refiners close the asymmetry the value-chain split implied but hadn't proven. The two downstream-adjacent legs behave oppositely: oilfield services decouples from crude (its own cluster, +0.254 — the capex cycle is not the price), but refiners do NOT — they cluster with the majors and crude (merge at ~0.49, only +0.193 distinct), because the crack-spread offset that should hedge crude is only a partial wedge in the tape. So the energy chain has three poles but two behaviors: a crude-price factor (majors = XLE) that refiners ride with a dampener (= CRAK), and a separate activity-cycle factor (services = OIH). All three are ETF-bounded — XLE, CRAK, OIH — so the campaign's energy chapter is the commodity-beta law extended down the value chain: business position changes WHICH ETF you own, not WHETHER you can avoid one. Refiners are the durable-but-undistinct case (holdout 0.88, steadiest of the three, yet least separable from crude); services the distinct-but-eroding case (holdout 0.74, the only real decoupling). [[Midstream and pipelines|Midstream]] — fee-based throughput-and-yield, structurally the most crude-decoupled leg — is the last open test of whether any energy equity escapes its sector ETF.

Midstream closes the chain, and the answer to its own test is no — no energy equity leg escapes its sector ETF. Midstream is the most crude-decoupled leg, exactly as the fee-based thesis predicts: it forms its own cluster, separate from crude (majors+XLE) AND from utilities (XLU), so the bond-like distribution does not make it a rate proxy and the toll-revenue model does break the crude tie. But the decoupled factor is AMLP — the MLP ETF sits inside the cohort — so "distinct from crude" still resolves to "own the ETF," just a different one. And the decoupling comes at a cost: midstream is the loosest (intra 0.56) and least durable (holdout 0.68, eroding to 0.53 in 2026) of the four legs, because once you leave the single exogenous crude price, what binds the names is a grab-bag of idiosyncratic fee/distribution/structure dynamics (C-corp vs MLP, gas vs NGL vs crude mix, MPLX's Marathon tie) that decohere easily. So the full energy value chain reads as one clean law: moving down-chain from crude (majors → refiners → services → midstream), the names progressively decouple from spot oil but lose internal cohesion and durability — and at every step the factor collapses to a sector ETF (XLE → CRAK → OIH → AMLP). Business position changes WHICH ETF you own; it never lets you avoid one. The campaign's energy chapter is closed: four legs, four ETFs, one law.

Automakers, the first fresh sector after energy, is the opposite outcome — a clean falsification — and the contrast is instructive. Energy's legs all cohered (they share crude, or a crack spread, or a capex cycle, or a fee base); "automakers" does not, because the five names have no shared driver at all. TSLA is an AI/autonomy/robotaxi story that trades as a Mag-7 name; GM and Ford are a legacy truck/credit-cycle pair; Rivian and Lucid are cash-burn survival options. The label is industrial classification, not factor structure — intra 0.325, PC1 46%, negative intra-advantage vs the ETFs, decohering every year since the 2022 EV bubble. It is the driver-divergence law in its purest form (the [[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1]]/[[Ad-tech]] family): when the members of a "sector" are paid on genuinely different things, there is no factor to own, and even the cap-weighted sector ETF (CARZ) collapses to its dominant name (TSLA) plus market. The campaign's two recurring outcomes, side by side in one day: a cohesive sector resolves to its ETF (energy's four legs), and an incoherent one resolves to nothing — you own the names separately or not at all.

Packaged food/beverages is a third outcome, between those two: real cohesion that is neither distinct nor cleanly its own cohort. The staples names co-move (they reject the nulls — a genuine low-beta defensive bid) but only +0.042 more with each other than with household products, and XLP sits inside them — so the factor that exists is the broad consumer-staples complex (= XLP), not "packaged food." And the carve-out splits: the mega-cap branded blue-chips (KO/PEP/PG/CL, the XLP core) cluster together while the mid-cap food names (MDLZ/GIS/KHC) drift off on their own GLP-1-volume and private-label pressures, with Hershey an outlier. That completes the defensive trio and sharpens its one real finding: of the three classic defensives, only [[Tobacco majors basket|tobacco]] is a genuinely distinct factor (a real nicotine-industry cohesion that beats the vol-matched null and is NOT its sector ETF); [[Regulated utilities|utilities]] and staples are both just their sector ETFs (XLU, XLP). "Defensive" is not one trade — it is XLU plus XLP plus the one idiosyncratic survivor, tobacco.

Hotels complete the travel cyclicals and supply the same shape on the cyclical side that staples did on the defensive side: a "sector" that is really beta to a broader complex. The campaign had validated three travel cyclicals as distinct sub-factors — airlines (fuel-inverse, market-neutral), cruise lines (+0.178, the demand channel), online travel (+0.178, no pure ETF) — so hotels looked like a fourth. They are not: the global upscale big-3 (Marriott/Hilton/Hyatt) cluster with the airlines, cruises, XLY, and the leisure ETF PEJ, so they are travel-demand/discretionary beta, not a separable lodging factor, and the cohort fractures between those global brands and the domestic economy franchisors (Wyndham/Choice), which trade as a fee-stable pair. The lesson across today's two fresh sectors (staples, hotels): a "sector" earns a distinct factor only when its members share a driver the broad ETF doesn't already price — tobacco's regulation/litigation/secular-decline, airlines' fuel, OTAs' agency economics. Where they don't (big hotels = travel demand = XLY; packaged food = defensive bid = XLP), the label is GICS classification and the trade is the ETF. Of today's six fresh cohorts, only the energy legs and tobacco (from earlier) own anything an ETF doesn't — and even the energy legs are each just a different ETF.

Railroads, the seventh fresh cohort, is the exception that proves the rule — and the cleanest counter-example of the day. It is the one sector today that genuinely does NOT collapse to its ETF: the five Class I rails form their own separable cluster (threshold robust [0.45-0.55]), distinct from the transports ETF IYT (+0.203), which sits with trucking/parcel instead. The rail oligopoly has a driver IYT averages away — carload volumes, PSR operating ratios, the consolidation wave — exactly the "shared driver the broad ETF doesn't price" test that hotels and staples failed. So the day's full ledger reads cleanly: a sector is a distinct, ownable factor only when its members share a driver no liquid ETF already captures, and that is rare — across seven fresh cohorts (energy's four legs, autos, staples, hotels) plus the WFE refresh, only railroads and tobacco clear it as genuinely non-ETF-replicable; the energy legs each just trade one sector ETF for another, and autos/hotels/staples are broad-complex beta. The merger-arb footnote sharpens it: even within the one distinct cohort, the tightest pair (UNP+NSC, 0.095) is tight for a non-fundamental reason — the pending transcontinental merger — a reminder that correlation structure picks up corporate actions, not just shared economics.

Biotech, the eighth cohort, closes the day on the other end of the spectrum — and completes the healthcare picture. It is the purest non-factor: nothing clusters at 0.5 (the tightest pair joins at 0.518), it barely beats random via shared healthcare beta, and it carries a negative intra-advantage vs IBB/XBI/XLV — own the ETF, every name is its own pipeline. With pharma, GLP-1, medtech, and DTC telehealth, that makes the entire healthcare sector a clean sweep of falsifications: no healthcare sub-sector is a distinct equity factor, because drug/clinical/patent-cliff outcomes are idiosyncratic by construction — the anti-cluster. So the day's two poles are now both anchored: railroads (and tobacco) at the "distinct, ownable factor" end, where members share a driver no ETF prices; healthcare at the "no factor at all" end, where members share nothing but sector beta. Everything else — energy, autos, hotels, staples — lands in between as "real cohesion that still resolves to a sector ETF." The campaign's one-line conclusion after eight cohorts in a day: distinctness is rare and must be earned by a shared, non-ETF-priced driver; cohesion alone (even strong cohesion) almost always just means "buy the ETF."
