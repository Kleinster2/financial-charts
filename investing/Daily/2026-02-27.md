## Synthesis

Three threads tonight, one connecting tissue: **AI is reshaping capital allocation at every level** — which chips get built, which software survives, which countries attract investment, and which security risks emerge.

**Memory supercycle pricing in.** [[Samsung]] confirmed the pricing power thesis with [[HBM|HBM4]] at $700/unit — 30% above HBM3E, 50-60% operating margins. [[SK Hynix]] may raise to match. Kospi +34% YTD, world's best market. The [[Memory shortage 2025-2026|memory shortage]] isn't easing — it's getting monetized at the premium end. Combined memory maker market cap: $1.8T. Hyperscaler capex heading to $650B.

**The great rotation: long hardware, short software.** [[Polar Capital]]'s Nick Evans — $12B fund, 99th percentile 1yr — has dumped all application software ([[SAP]], [[ServiceNow]], [[Adobe]], [[HubSpot]]) and gone 7/10 top holdings into semis. His framing: app software = newspapers in the 2000s. Infrastructure software ([[Cloudflare]], [[Datadog]], [[Snowflake]]) is different — it benefits from AI traffic. SOX vs software ETF gap: ~77pp. [[Workday]] earnings illustrate the tension: beats on numbers ($2.53B revenue, +14.5%), but stock is -50% from 2024 highs on terminal value uncertainty from [[Agentic AI]].

**AI safety pressure building on [[Anthropic]].** [[Claude]] was jailbroken to hack [[Mexico|Mexican]] government agencies — 150 GB stolen, 195M taxpayer records. Second known AI-orchestrated cyber campaign involving Claude. This lands while Anthropic is in a [[Pentagon AI access dispute 2026|Pentagon standoff]] over military use restrictions with a Friday deadline. The timing is brutal: Anthropic is simultaneously arguing it's too responsible for unrestricted military deployment while its model is being used to breach a sovereign government.

**The macro backdrop.** The [[Sell America trade]] has a $7T hole in it. US companies generated $6.7T more revenue than European peers, invested nearly double in R&D. Europe's edge is valuation and yield — but the negative feedback loop of underinvestment eroding fundamentals is the structural bear case. [[BlackRock]] and [[UBS]] still expect higher *returns* from Europe, but the fundamental trajectory favors the US.

**Stock dispersion at 2009 extremes.** [[Citadel Securities]] data shows single-stock dispersion at its highest since the GFC aftermath. More than one-fifth of S&P 500 constituents have moved ±20% YTD — risers outnumber fallers ~2:1 — while the index itself is flat. The [[Mag 7]] are down 5.6% since January, but 7/11 sectors are up and 60%+ of stocks are beating the index (broadest in decades). Result: >50% of large-cap active funds outperforming their benchmark — highest in almost 20 years ([[Goldman Sachs]]). Underneath the subdued index: revenue growing 9%, Q4 earnings +12% YoY (4th straight double-digit quarter), 7/11 sectors seeing margin expansion. [[Société Générale|SocGen]]'s Manish Kabra: "The macro message underlying the index is actually boom." [[Barclays]]' Venu Krishna points to "violent rotations" as investors flee tech for consumer durables and other sectors. This is an earnings-driven market that suppresses index returns but creates "phenomenal returns" in individual names — the exact environment where thesis-driven stockpicking should outperform. See [[Sector rotation]], [[Breadth divergence]].

The winners (memory makers, infrastructure software, US equities broadly) and losers (application software, consumer electronics OEMs, European growth) are getting more distinct by the week.
