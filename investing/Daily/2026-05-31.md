## Synthesis

The default `/news` set was a durable-change batch, not a headline batch. The through-line is that the AI/China/energy shock is showing up simultaneously in supply chains, strategic posture, macro reaction functions, export prices, AI chip design, and data-center siting.

Nexperia China saying production and deliveries are recovering matters because operational continuity and legal control are now separate questions: [[Wingtech]] and the Dutch authorities can be fighting over ownership and governance while the Chinese operating base keeps serving customers. The Reuters China launch-pad story is best read as survivability infrastructure around fixed ICBM fields, not as a simple new-warhead-count update. That is why the full treatment belongs in geopolitics, with the investing note only carrying the cross-vault signal.

The macro layer is the same pattern in prices. [[Jeffrey Schmid]]'s Fed speech pushes against casually treating oil shocks as transitory, which connects [[2026 Iran conflict market impact]], [[Inflation]], and [[Rate expectations]]. The Bloomberg China export-price item adds a separate pass-through channel: war-linked commodities and AI/semiconductor demand are interrupting the prior China export-price deflation story captured in [[China PPI]] and [[Second China shock]].

The technology layer is the highest-conviction investing signal. The [[ByteDance]] / [[InnoStar Semiconductor]] item suggests Chinese hyperscalers are trying to move from buying constrained GPUs toward workload-specific ASICs and ReRAM-style inference hardware. That pulls together [[Groq]], [[NVIDIA alternatives]], [[Export controls]], and [[China token economy]]: constraint is becoming architecture. [[SoftBank]]'s France plan is the physical-infrastructure analog - AI data centers are following available low-carbon baseload, grid partners, and state industrial-policy wrappers, not just cheap land.

The add-on #5 and #9 items sharpen that same constraint theme. On EU-China, the new point is not that Brussels dislikes Chinese overcapacity -- the vault already had that -- but that Beijing is now threatening anti-discrimination and supply-chain-security investigations against the EU's possible overcapacity instrument. That turns the [[EU-China trade]] file from ordinary trade defense into a retaliation-risk problem for European companies with China exposure, and links the [[EU Made in Europe Tech Rules 2026]] thesis to [[China retaliatory toolkit]].

On enterprise AI, the WSJ / Hindustan Times story is not "AI demand is fading." It is the moment AI spend becomes budget-governed. Companies are rationing premium tools, routing routine tasks to cheaper models, and trying to connect token burn to shipped output. Google's I/O token numbers and Anthropic's $965B Series H confirm demand is still huge; the new constraint is who captures the margin when enterprises learn to route work across model tiers. That is why the durable home is [[Enterprise AI adoption]], [[Inference economics]], and [[Model lab economics]], not a standalone headline note.

Skipped lower-priority items were either partly covered already, softer signal, or mostly market-tape noise. The vault split was: investing for market and industrial effects, geopolitics for China nuclear posture and EU-China statecraft, and technologies for ReRAM / ASIC / data-center architecture plus token-budget-aware agents.

The later FT Citadel Securities ingest (item 3 of the same /news sweep) extends the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact]] map rather than opening a new thread. The vault already documented the war's volatility losers — the [[Goldman Sachs]] rates desk wrongfooted in Q1, macro hedge funds down 6-15% in March as the [[Steepener trade]] broke. What was missing was the win leg: the private market-making cohort is structurally long volatility, and Q1 2026 handed it two engines at once — Iran-war swings in oil and Treasury rates, plus AI-driven equity volatility as software business models got repriced. [[Citadel Securities]] ($4.3bn, record), [[Jane Street]] ($16.1bn, record, beat JPMorgan and Goldman that quarter), and [[Hudson River Trading]] ($6.4bn) booked ~$27bn combined. The durable read is twofold: the same supply shock splits Wall Street P&L by mandate (directional desks lose, spread-capture desks win), and Citadel Securities is recycling the windfall into competitive encroachment on the banks via high-touch equities — so this is a market-structure story, not a one-quarter profit print. Kept as actor-note results plus an event-note subsection; no standalone note, since a single quarter's revenue is a data point, not a durable entity.

Item 4 (FT, May 28) is the Korean won's "DRam dollars" puzzle — and a process note worth recording honestly. I first misattributed it to the Taiwan dollar off a generic homepage teaser, created notes with fabricated specifics, then while cleaning up wrongly deleted the pre-existing real [[BNP Paribas]] note; I caught both on reading the actual article and checking git, reverted via git, and redid it against verified data. The substance: South Korea has arguably the cleanest positive fundamentals in Asian FX (record $73.8bn Q1 current-account surplus; [[Samsung]] and [[SK Hynix]] at $1tn caps on the AI-memory boom) yet the [[Korean won]] sits near ₩1,500, a level last seen in the 2008-09 crisis. The resolution is flows, not fundamentals — two of them. First, the [[Kospi]] doubled, so passive/benchmarked global managers must sell Korean equities to avoid going overweight (foreigners net-sold a record $79bn), and those proceeds leave the won. Second, exporters park earnings in dollars rather than repatriating, which Setser frames as "DRam dollars" — a manufacturing-surplus analog to petrodollars. The Iran-war energy shock and a weak [[Japanese yen]] are secondary drags, not the core. The asymmetry worth holding: if the won turns, a repatriation rush could snap it sharply higher (RBC), and Konrad pairs the won with the [[Taiwan dollar]] on the same logic. Won levels were cross-checked against USDKRW=X (DB, 2026 range ~1,443–1,516) and a USD/KRW chart is embedded in [[Korean won]]. The failure mode was anchoring on a headline teaser instead of the article; the catch was the standing rule to verify against the primary source and the database before trusting framing — the won facts grounded cleanly, the Taiwan version never did because it was built on an assumption.
