## Synthesis

The one entity gap this fills is a person who sits exactly on the vault's most active AI fault line — the open-vs-proprietary / [[US-China AI race]] split — and whose name keeps surfacing as the attribution for the "~80% on Chinese open models" claim. Recording him as an actor does two things: it gives that recurring figure a single home so the estimate is always traceable to its source (an a16z partner's framing, not a measurement), and it ties the investing vault's deep China-open-models coverage ([[Chinese open models]], [[Open-weight models]], [[Open source commoditization]], [[Inference economics]]) to the human making the loudest market-and-policy case for that thesis. The read-through: Casado is the investor-side voice of the same diffusion argument the USCC "Two Loops" report makes from the government side and Jeffrey Ding makes from the academic side — open models as the real competitive axis, with US export controls aimed at the wrong layer. His anti-regulation / "little tech" stance ([[California]] SB 1047 opposition) is the policy corollary worth watching, because a16z's lobbying weight makes him a swing voice on whether US AI policy tilts open or closed — which is itself a market variable for the open-weight cohort.

[[Martín Escobari]] fills the parallel human-node gap on the Brazil/LatAm growth-equity side: [[General Atlantic]] the firm was already in the vault, but the person who actually routed it into [[XP Inc]] — and who now chairs GA's global investment committee — was not. He is the connective tissue between US growth capital and the Brazilian fintech/edtech cohort ([[XP Inc]], dLocal, Arco, [[Nubank]]), which makes him a recurring name behind LatAm-tech ownership and a useful node for any XP / Brazil-fintech read-through. Both notes today are the same move — turning a recurring person referenced only through their firm into a traceable actor — on the two themes the vault is densest in: the AI open-vs-proprietary split (Casado) and Brazil/LatAm fintech (Escobari).

The Ashkenazy stub is graph hygiene with analytical bite: it keeps the Saks emergence note from flattening all retail real estate into one outcome. The contrast between selling Beverly Hills and retaining [[611 Fifth Avenue]] sharpens the read-through that the Manhattan flagship was not just excess real estate; it was the collateral anchor that let the reorganized company survive with less debt.

ICF adds the energy-infrastructure version of the same durable-node move: the AI-power story is less about one national demand forecast than about where load can actually be served. The numbers make PJM the cleanest US stress case, because +43% demand growth by 2035 meets thin reserve margins and slow interconnection. That pushes the investable edge back toward grid contractors, turbines, storage, utility capex, and hyperscaler BYOP deals rather than treating aggregate 445 GW generation additions as an automatic solution.

The [[EU]] cloud-DMA item adds the regulatory version of that same AI-infrastructure story. Hyperscalers are not just buyers of power and data-center capacity; they are becoming the gateways through which AI demand, enterprise procurement, and software distribution pass. Brussels is now saying that the lock-in and AI-procurement pull around [[AWS]] and [[Microsoft Azure]] are sufficient to justify gatekeeper treatment even without the normal DMA quantitative-threshold story. The market reaction matters: AMZN and MSFT sold off sharply versus [[QQQ]] on the event day, suggesting investors understood it as cloud-specific regulatory risk rather than generic tech weakness.
