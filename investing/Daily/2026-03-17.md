## Synthesis

The main story today is the Chinese tech beachhead in Brazilian food delivery — [[Meituan]]'s [[Keeta]] and [[Didi]]'s [[99]] are throwing R$7.6B combined at cracking [[iFood]]'s ~80% monopoly in a R$100B market. The corporate espionage angle (500 LinkedIn approaches, police raids, sealed court documents) elevates this beyond a competitive story into geopolitical territory: [[Lula]]'s government celebrated the Chinese investment but now faces awkward questions about foreign consultancies extracting Brazilian IP. For [[Prosus]], iFood is the LatAm crown jewel — if the Chinese can break the network effect with subsidies, the playbook applies to every market Prosus operates in. The broader thread: China's domestic tech saturation ([[Meituan]] losing money to [[Alibaba]]'s Ele.me, [[Didi]] still recovering from the delisting crackdown) is forcing internationalization into EM markets, and Brazil is the proving ground.

## UK fusion goes commercial — STEP reactor opens to private capital

[[UK Fusion Energy]] (formerly UKIFS), the [[UK]] government's fusion development arm, is being restructured as a commercial entity to attract private investment. A subsidiary of the [[UK Atomic Energy Authority]], the company is responsible for the STEP spherical tokamak reactor at a former coal-fired power station in Nottinghamshire. Government has pledged £1.3bn; 2024 tender estimated up to £20bn total cost. Target: electricity to grid by 2040 — no fusion reactor has achieved this anywhere.

The restructuring reflects intensifying global fusion competition: US startups raised $1.6bn privately in 2025 ([[PitchBook]]); [[China]] has invested $6.5-10bn since 2022 (Special Competitive Studies Project, [[Eric Schmidt]]-chaired). The AI power demand thesis is a stated driver of tech investor interest.

Updated [[Long nuclear]] thesis — added UK Fusion Energy to fusion row and evidence log.

Source: FT (Mar 16)
