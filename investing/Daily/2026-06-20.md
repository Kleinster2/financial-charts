## Synthesis

Athletic footwear/apparel is the brand-momentum analogue of the campaign's driver-divergence law, and it fails on two independent axes at once. Cross-sectionally it IS the retail ETF: the six athletic names ([[Nike|NKE]], [[Lululemon|LULU]], [[Deckers|DECK]], [[On Running|ONON]], [[Under Armour|UAA]], [[Crocs|CROX]]) carry a negative −0.053 intra-advantage versus [[XRT]]/[[XLY]] — they correlate with the retail ETF more than with each other — and a negative −0.024 versus broad apparel ([[Ralph Lauren|RL]]/[[Tapestry|TPR]]/[[VF Corp|VFC]]), so there is no separable athleisure factor, just retail/discretionary beta. Temporally the cohesion isn't even stable: the 2Y holdout reads REGIME-DEPENDENT (ratio 0.60, loadings corr 0.22), oscillating between ~0.60 when a common consumer/rates shock synchronizes the names (2023, 2025, holdout train 0.62) and ~0.33 when brand-specific product cycles take over (2024, 2026, holdout test 0.37) — NKE's China/wholesale reset, LULU's North-American deceleration, DECK's HOKA hypergrowth, ONON's ascent, UAA's turnaround, CROX's HEYDUDE drag each pull a different way. That regime-dependence is the new wrinkle versus durably-loose [[Biotech]] (consistently ~0.42): biotech is always noise, apparel is noise punctuated by shared-shock windows the ETF already prices. The one constructive sub-structure is [[Deckers|DECK]]+[[On Running|ONON]] (premium performance running, HOKA + On), the only pair under the 0.5 cut at 0.493. Campaign read-through: this is another consumer-brand falsification, joining automakers, restaurants, and the healthcare set — and it keeps the standing scoreboard intact, where across the fresh cohorts only [[Railroads]] and [[Tobacco majors basket|tobacco]] have escaped their sector ETF as genuinely distinct factors; everything brand- or pipeline-driven resolves to the ETF. Separately, the split scan surfaced a real cross-thread data-integrity risk: a FUBO 1:12 reverse split (and a FISV move) sitting un-back-adjusted inside cluster-config series, which would invert whatever cohort reads them — worth an authorized re-fetch/back-adjust pass (the same KLA/OPAD failure mode caught during the WFE refresh). That pass is now done: of the seven LIKELY flags only FUBO actually corrupted a cohort (its strz control group), now re-fetched and continuous; OPAD restored via manual back-adjust; the remaining five plus the two REVIEW items (FISV, SIVE.ST) verified as real crashes/rallies with no yfinance split, so the scanner's price-pattern heuristic — not the data — produced them. The lasting lesson: a LIKELY flag is a candidate, not a verdict; cross-check yfinance's recorded splits before adjusting, because real −44% earnings crashes (FISV) and 10x rallies (SIVE.ST) pattern-match a split ratio.

[[Building products]] extends the campaign's "real cohesion is not distinctness" line in a new way: it is the first fresh cohort to shatter into TWO different sector ETFs rather than one. The nine GICS building-products makers beat the random and vol-matched nulls decisively (p 0.0001) — they genuinely co-move — but the cohesion is shared late-cycle construction/rate beta, not a building-products factor, and it splits cleanly by end-market into an HVAC pole that resolves to [[XLI]] (commercial/industrial capex, electrification, data-center cooling) and a housing-products pole that resolves to [[XHB]] (residential new-build and repair-and-remodel). The negative −0.056 intra-advantage vs the ETFs is the tell, and the two poles only bridge at 0.557, above the cut. It is the [[Packaged food and beverages|packaged-food]] shape — real, ETF-replicable, and it splits — but where packaged food collapsed to one XLP, building products collapses to two ETFs, one per demand driver. The standing scoreboard holds: across the fresh cohorts only [[Railroads]] and [[Tobacco majors basket|tobacco]] are genuinely distinct non-ETF factors; everything else — brand-driven (apparel), pipeline-driven (biotech), or end-market-split (building products) — resolves to a sector ETF.

The fresh-cohort batch ([[Solid waste]], [[Life science tools]], [[Trucking and LTL]], [[Industrial distributors]]) was the campaign's best yield yet: three genuinely distinct non-ETF factors plus one ETF-replicable. The decisive diagnostic across all four is the intra-advantage versus the sector ETF: [[Solid waste|waste]] (+0.424, the campaign's largest — [[EVX]] is too diluted to capture the oligopoly), [[Trucking and LTL|trucking]] (+0.203, separable from [[IYT]] at tight thresholds), and [[Life science tools|life-science tools]] (+0.172, no liquid pure-tools ETF exists) each clear it and form separable clusters; [[Industrial distributors|distributors]] (+0.062) do not — they are [[XLI]] beta, with [[Watsco|WSO]] defecting to the HVAC pole. This lifts the count of genuinely distinct, non-ETF-replicable factors in the campaign to five — [[Railroads]], [[Tobacco majors basket|tobacco]], [[Solid waste|waste]], [[Life science tools|life-science tools]], [[Trucking and LTL|trucking]]. The pattern is now sharp: the distinct factors are defensive/oligopolistic (waste, rail, tobacco) or single-driver picks-and-shovels (tools), where a liquid ETF either does not exist or is too diluted to capture the names; the ETF-replicable ones are broad cyclical complexes (distributors, building products, apparel) a sector ETF already prices. Distinctness is earned where the basket out-coheres every tradeable ETF — and that happens at concentrated oligopolies and specialized supply chains, not at diffuse cyclical labels.

[[Auto parts retail]] adds a new shape: distinctness concentrated in a pair. The five-name aftermarket label is loose (intra 0.471) and never clusters cleanly, yet it is genuinely distinct from cyclical retail (+0.157 vs [[XRT]]/[[XLY]]) — and that distinctness is overwhelmingly the premium [[O'Reilly Automotive|ORLY]]+[[AutoZone|AZO]] duopoly, which carries a +0.599 intra-advantage over the retail ETFs (one of the campaign's largest) and the widest stable threshold band in the sub-cohort sweep (0.45). The ownable factor is a robust two-name duopoly, not a sector basket: the high-quality DIY/DIFM model decouples from cyclical retail, while the laggards (turnaround [[Advance Auto Parts|AAP]], industrial-mixed [[Genuine Parts|GPC]], salvage/Europe [[LKQ Corporation|LKQ]]) each run their own story. It refines the campaign law — distinctness is earned by a shared driver no liquid ETF prices — to note that the unit of distinctness can be a pair, not a whole sector.

[[Drug distributors]] caps the distribution arc (industrial, auto-parts, healthcare) with the campaign's most ETF-distinct result. The big-3 oligopoly ([[McKesson|MCK]]/[[Cencora|COR]]/[[Cardinal Health|CAH]]) carries a +0.502 intra-advantage over the healthcare/medtech ETFs — the largest margin recorded — the widest separable threshold band (0.30), and a STABLE holdout. It is the cleanest illustration of the campaign's law: a low-margin, regulated, fee-based logistics oligopoly whose drivers (generic deflation, distribution-fee contracts, opioid-settlement cash, new-modality volume) are orthogonal to the drug-development cycle [[XLV]] prices, so no ETF can replicate it. The distribution arc itself splits cleanly by structure — the concentrated oligopolies (drug big-3; the ORLY/AZO auto duopoly) are distinct factors, the diffuse one (industrial MRO distributors) is sector-ETF beta. Distinctness tracks concentration, not the "distribution" label. The campaign now stands at six genuinely distinct non-ETF factors — [[Railroads]], [[Tobacco majors basket|tobacco]], [[Solid waste]], [[Life science tools]], [[Trucking and LTL]], [[Drug distributors]] — every one a concentrated oligopoly or specialized supply chain.

[[Tankers]] is the campaign's eighth genuinely distinct non-ETF factor, and the cleanest separation yet of a shared driver from the equity factor it drives. The five crude/product tanker owners ([[Frontline|FRO]]/[[Scorpio Tankers|STNG]]/[[International Seaways|INSW]]/[[DHT Holdings|DHT]]/[[Teekay Tankers|TNK]]) cohere at intra 0.795 — second-tightest of any distinct cohort behind [[Residential REITs]] — with a +0.483 ETF intra-advantage second only to [[Drug distributors]], a WIDE separable band [0.30–0.45], all nulls rejected at the 0.0001 floor, and a STABLE holdout (0.95, loadings corr 0.93). What makes it the cleanest case in the whole campaign: the shared driver — the tanker spot rate — is itself directly tradeable, via the [[BWET]] freight-futures commodity pool (the best-performing US ETF of 2026, +836% on the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]]). But BWET prices the rate, not the levered equities, which carry balance-sheet, fleet/NAV, dividend, and equity-beta characteristics an FFA lacks — so the rate has a liquid instrument and the equity factor does not, and the broad-shipping equity ETFs [[BOAT]]/[[SEA]] only approximate it (diluted with dry bulk/container/LNG, contaminating the cluster solely at the loose 0.50 cut, never [[XLE]] or the market). The cohort's internal structure matches its physics: the crude names form the tight core (INSW+TNK 0.112, FRO+DHT 0.154) while the product-tanker name [[Scorpio Tankers|STNG]] — clean rates run a partly different cycle from dirty crude — joins last at 0.255. The [[Cluster campaign synthesis|capstone]] formalized seven Tier-1 factors (adding [[Residential REITs]] to the running six above); tankers makes eight, slotting near the top by robustness. It is the law in its sharpest form: distinctness is a shared driver no liquid equity ETF prices — visible here precisely because the driver itself does have an ETF, and the equities still escape it.
