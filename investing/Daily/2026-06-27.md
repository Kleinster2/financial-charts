## Synthesis

The FT ingestion closes the vault's June 17-19 central-bank resolution gap. The vault had been tracking the BoE's June 18 MPC, the ECB's next move after Wunsch's May 20 signal, and the BOJ's expected June hike to 1% as forward-looking "next decision" items — but none of the actual outcomes had landed in the actor notes. The FT article is the gap-closing source for all three, plus the cross-CB synthesis that ties them together.

The durable insight is that the [[US-Iran framework deal June 2026|peace deal]] did not invert the inflation-policy arc the way the June 15 peace-dividend tape priced. Spot oil fell toward $76, but energy futures stayed above pre-war levels; fertiliser, metals, and food-supply chains continue propagating; El Niño adds a weather-side risk; and damaged oil/gas infrastructure delays a return to prewar production. The four major central banks validated the persistent-inflation read within an eight-day window: the [[Federal Reserve|Fed]] held with a hawkish dot plot flip (Jun 17), the [[Bank of England|BoE]] held with ~two dozen second-round-effects references in its minutes (Jun 18), the [[ECB]] had already hiked +25bp to 2.25% the week before and dropped its "well anchored" expectations language, and the [[BOJ]] lifted to ~1% (highest since 1996) and signaled further tightening. The cross-CB consensus is captured in [[Luke Bartholomew]]'s "not ready to call the all-clear" framing and [[David Wilcox]]'s "we've been trying to tell that story now for quite a while and it hasn't come true" — the persistent-inflation camp's cleanest answer to the peace-dividend disinflation trade. The vault-level implication: the [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium|risk-premium]] arc, the [[Steepener trade|steepener]], and the [[Iran conflict economic disruption#Central bank bind|central-bank bind]] do not cleanly reverse with the spot oil unwind; the second-round-effects channel is now the binding watch item across G3+.

The daily-scan follow-through changes the [[OpenAI]] IPO read from "2026 is still the base case, just cooler than the May 21 spike" to "no 2026 IPO is now the base case, with venue disagreement." That matters because OpenAI's IPO is not just an exit event; in the vault's financing-risk map it is part of the runway extension for the $600B pre-2030 burn and the compute contracts behind [[Project Stargate]]. The nuance is important: the conditional valuation ladder still centers around $1.21T if the IPO happens, so the market is not mainly saying "OpenAI cannot be a trillion-dollar public company." It is saying the timing window is less likely to open in 2026, which raises absorption risk for the broader trophy-IPO queue and for counterparties depending on OpenAI's access to public capital.

The Germany drug-pricing ingestion is the vault's first capture of a doctrinal expansion in US trade law: Section 301 — historically aimed at tariffs, market access, IP theft, forced tech transfer, and subsidies — is now being trained on an ally's sovereign health-reimbursement regime. The [[US Section 301 investigation of Germany drug pricing (2026)|Jun 18 probe]] reframes Germany's [[AMNOG]] reference-pricing system as an "unreasonable or discriminatory" trade practice that shifts R&D costs onto US patients (the ~3.9× branded-drug price gap is the load-bearing number). The structural frame — captured in [[Pharma R&D burden-sharing]] — is durable across administrations: pharmaceutical R&D is a global public good priced into nationally administered reimbursement systems, so the burden-sharing fight is the unavoidable consequence of that mismatch. The [[Trump II]] contribution is the instrument stack: domestic MFN reference pricing + Section 301/232 tariffs + bilateral deals ([[US-UK pharma pricing deal (2026)|UK template]]) closing the US-foreign gap from both sides. The market read (verified against canonical prices) is that the *investigation opening* was a non-event for pharma — mean abnormal ~-0.4% vs XLV across the seven large names, all within noise — because (a) it is a ~12-month process with tariffs a 2027 event, and (b) the cohort already repriced the MFN/Section 232 stack in April–May. The binding near-term event for pharma names is the Jul 31 / Sep 29 Section 232 tariff effective dates, not this probe. What to watch: whether Merz's "national jurisdiction" line holds (routing the dispute to the slower EU-US channel vs a UK-style bilateral) and whether a successful Germany route becomes the template for NICE/PBAC/PMPRB — a read-across that would make the burden-sharing doctrine the default US approach to every reference-pricing regime.
