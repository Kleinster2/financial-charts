### Memory shortage — consumer build-level confirmation (DDR5 + used-GPU prices)

Folded a ground-level pricing datapoint into [[Memory shortage 2025-2026]] (Events, expanded) — a new "## Jun 25, 2026 — consumer build-level confirmation" section. Verified US street prices on 2026-06-25: 128 GB DDR5 (2x64) ~$1,100 (up from ~$400 earlier in the year, peaked ~$1,864 on Mar 18), 64 GB ~$500-600, 32 GB ~$350 (from ~$100-200 in Oct 2025) — the consumer tier of the same squeeze the note tracks at the OEM/HBM level, and the [[Micron]] Crucial line the note already flags as discontinued. Added the used-GPU spillover: a four-year-old RTX 3090 still ~$900, a used RTX 5090 within 5% of new MSRP — GPUs barely depreciating because AI demand revalues them as near-cash. Sourced (Tom's Hardware RAM index, Pangoly, Best Value GPU); cross-linked to the personal-vault machine-ops PC-build decision that surfaced the prices.

## Synthesis

The memory super-cycle the vault has tracked at the wafer / HBM / OEM level showed up today at the most granular tier there is — a single consumer pricing a PC build. The confirmation is clean: 128 GB of DDR5 has roughly tripled to ~$1,100 in months, putting memory on par with a flagship GPU as the biggest line item in a high-end build, which is the [[Memory shortage victims|consumer-pays-more]] leg of the thesis made concrete below the OEM layer the note had documented. The newer signal is the used-GPU spillover: AI demand has propped resale values so hard that a four-year-old RTX 3090 still fetches ~$900 and a used RTX 5090 sells within 5% of new MSRP — the same GDDR / [[HBM]] allocation pressure, now visible as an asset-price effect that turns used cards into near-cash. The behavioral read-through ties back to the thesis's own mechanism: the only way to free constrained supply in a market that refuses to ration is demand destruction via price ([[Dylan Patel]]'s "double or triple from here"), and the rational individual response to that — stage the RAM buy, buy depreciation-proof used GPUs and flip them — is household-level demand deferral, the micro mirror of the OEM build cuts the note tracks. The datapoint is anecdotal by construction, but it triangulates the thesis from the one direction the institutional sources cannot: the buyer at the end of the chain.
