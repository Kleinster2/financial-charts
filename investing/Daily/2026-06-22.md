## Synthesis

This is the continued 2026-06-21 session — see [[2026-06-21]] for the first three FT pickups and the unifying read (AI capability meeting the institutions — markets, then regulators — that price and police it). The fourth pickup, ingested under the 2026-06-22 date, completes the supply/demand picture: where #1 (the [[Jefferies]] narrative-lifecycle note) flagged Datacenter AI Capex as euphoria "at risk of cracks," #4 is the demand-side reckoning that could crack it — enterprises capping token spend, agents multiplying compute 10–1,000x per worker, and cheaper, more efficient Chinese models ([[OpenRouter]]) undercutting US labs' premium-token economics just as [[Anthropic]] and [[OpenAI]] head toward near-trillion-dollar IPOs. The bull capex case ([[Goldman Sachs]] 24x tokens by 2030 → chip shortage) and the bear efficiency case (compute-efficiency replacement) are the same fact read two ways — the [[Jevons Paradox]] against the [[Narrative lifecycle investing|euphoria-cracks]] tension, now with named corporate budgets behind it.

The fifth pickup (an FT Asset Management newsletter) widens the frame to capital flows. The defence-tech VC rush ($12.3bn YTD, ~2x last year) is the private-capital echo of the [[Defense procurement reform|software-first procurement]] shift — but the rising multiples are the now-familiar late-cycle tell, a private mirror of the public primes' overhang. The [[Thoma Bravo]] / [[Medallia]] $5bn write-off is the same AI-disruption thread from #1 and #4 landing as a realized loss: a 2021 PE-software take-private (customer-service chatbots) financed by [[Private credit]], now worth 60–70c because the growth didn't come and AI is eating the product. And [[Kevin Warsh]]'s first-meeting hawkish turn — futures pricing an October hike, the dollar reversing the carry trade into [[Brazil]]'s real — is the rate-side of the same story: a chair-validated "hike-bias hold" that tightens financing for everything from AI-capex issuance to the near-trillion-dollar AI-lab IPOs. Capability, capital, and policy keep converging on one question — whether the AI-and-defence spending boom is durable demand or a multiple the next entrant overpays for.

The sixth pickup steps outside the AI/defence spine to India — but rhymes on the same two threads. The [[Jio Platforms]] filing (India's largest-ever IPO, ~$160bn implied) plus the [[NSE India]] float mark an India IPO-market revival, extending the [[2026 IPO pipeline]] absorption test from the US trophy trio (SpaceX/OpenAI/Anthropic) to Mumbai — and Jio's pitch leans on the same AI narrative ([[Nvidia]]/[[Meta]]/[[Google]] partnerships) running through pickups #1 and #4. The macro tie is cleaner still: the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran-war oil shock]] the BoE is now stress-testing for (#3), and that lifted the dollar and reversed the carry (#5), is the same force that briefly downturned India's IPO market and knocked [[Reliance Industries]]'s refining (−16% YTD, enough for [[Adani Group|Adani]] to overtake [[Mukesh Ambani]] as Asia's richest). Six pickups in, the session has traced one crude-and-compute shock through US equities, regulators, private capital, FX, and now an Indian mega-IPO.
