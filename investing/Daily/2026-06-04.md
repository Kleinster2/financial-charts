## Synthesis

The BoE/Bailey queue item is not mainly a "rates up or rates down" story. Bailey is drawing a boundary around how the UK should treat an energy-driven inflation overshoot from the Iran/Gulf shock. The May 29 Reykjavik speech says the BoE can look through direct energy effects because interest rates cannot create energy supply or reopen disrupted transit. But that discretion is constrained: once the shock starts moving wages, expectations, or broad price-setting, the Bank has to defend the 2% anchor.

The June 2 House of Lords session sharpened the political signal. [[Reuters]] reported Bailey rejecting a shift to a 3% inflation target and saying households need confidence that the target is real. That makes the market read narrower than April's simple rate-hike repricing: Bank Rate is 3.75%, the next MPC decision is June 18, and the investable question is whether the overshoot stays first-round. If inflation expectations and wage-setting calm, the BoE can wait. If the shock embeds, the target forces a tighter stance even with weak UK activity.

For the vault, the durable link is between [[Bank of England]], [[Andrew Bailey]], [[UK]], and [[Inflation]]. UK asset risk now has two interacting channels: fiscal/political credibility in the gilt market, and monetary credibility around whether the 2% target survives a sequence of energy shocks and public pressure. No sibling-vault write is necessary yet; this is an investing/macro-policy update rather than a new geopolitics primitive.

The BIS/NVIDIA queue item is a chip-control mechanism update rather than just another Nvidia headline. The May 31 guidance makes clear that non-China geography is not enough if the buyer is China/Macau-headquartered or ultimately parented there. That tightens the clean legal version of the Southeast Asia workaround, raises diligence pressure on NVIDIA, AMD, server integrators, data-center operators, and foundries, and keeps the strategic read two-sided: US controls narrow legal frontier-chip access, while China gets stronger incentives to harden overseas compute structures and accelerate Huawei/Ascend substitution.

The market-close mover check added a second kind of AI signal: expectations risk. [[Broadcom]] and [[CrowdStrike]] both reported strong operational evidence for the AI infrastructure/security thesis, but the tape punished or pressured them because the market wanted faster upward revisions after very sharp rallies. Broadcom's Q2 showed AI semiconductor revenue already at $10.8B and Q3 AI guidance of $16.0B, yet the unchanged $100B 2027 long-range AI-chip revenue target became the disappointment. CrowdStrike's Q1 showed record Q1 net new ARR and raised AI-security language, yet the stock reaction shows the same valuation/revision bar. The causal read-through is that AI winners are no longer moving simply on "AI growth"; they have to keep expanding the forward slope.
