### Reuters Graphics — China's coal-based urea buffer tightens the ex-China market (Apr 20, 2026)

Expanded:
- [[urea]]
- [[Fortress China]]

Main takeaway:
The key insight is that [[China]] was not just another fertilizer exporter caught in the [[Strait of Hormuz]] shock. Its coal-based urea system insulated the domestic market while the rest of the world was repricing gas-linked nitrogen scarcity. That same insulation then tightened the global market because Beijing restricted exports to protect local supply, pushing more pain onto import-dependent buyers such as [[Brazil]], [[India]], and Southeast Asia.

### Chartbook / WSJ — the AI manufacturing boom is real, but it still runs through imports (Apr 20, 2026)

Expanded:
- [[Adam Tooze]]
- [[Manufacturing reshoring]]
- [[AI capex arms race]]

Main takeaway:
The important shift is not just that some US factory activity is reviving. It is that the AI-led manufacturing upturn does not look like classic reshoring. In the key computer-and-electronics bucket, domestic production is rising alongside even faster imports, because the buildout still depends on foreign semiconductors and equipment. That pushes the macro question beyond "can America build more?" to "how does the rest of the world fund recurring payments to US AI owners if Washington also wants to choke off their export earnings?" The AI boom increasingly looks like an external-balance and trade-regime story, not only a capex story.

### FT / Ruchir Sharma, EM comeback narrows into North Asian AI leverage (Apr 20, 2026)

Expanded:
- [[Emerging markets]]
- [[Ruchir Sharma]]
- [[South Korea]]
- [[Taiwan]]

Main takeaway:
The important shift is not just that EMs are back. It is that the current rerating is narrow and hardware-led. [[South Korea]] and [[Taiwan]] are working less like generic high-beta EMs and more like liquid international access to the memory and foundry earnings created by the US AI capex cycle. That also explains the split inside the asset class: equities could recover past pre-war levels even while EM currencies stayed weak on oil and the dollar. This is a real EM rotation, but it is not yet a broad macro all-clear.

Analyst / newsletter sweep:
I did not find a fresh 48-hour specialist newsletter or named-analyst note that added a cleaner country-level frame than Sharma's FT column. Search-based checks for Ideas in Development and FT Alphaville did not surface relevant new posts, and the closest sell-side overlap was older Goldman material favoring North Asia tech over more oil-sensitive Asian peers.

Earnings check:
The earnings bridge supports the thesis. Reuters reported on Apr 16 that [[TSMC]]'s first-quarter profit rose 58% to a record T$572.5 billion and that the company raised its 2026 revenue outlook while calling AI demand "extremely robust." Reuters also reported on Apr 8 that Samsung's blowout preliminary profit had already lifted expectations for [[SK Hynix]]'s January-March results later that month. So the EM rerating is still anchored in live semiconductor earnings, not just a factor-flow story.
