## Synthesis

The link closes a loop. The Risk Parity vault has tracked AOR as its lone static benchmark for a while but treated "60/40" abstractly in its comparison theory. Tying it to the investing-vault suite note supplies the real instrument behind the abstraction, the three sibling rungs that show 60/40 is just one point on a capital-weighted equity-risk gradient, and the empirical equity-bull premium (AOR 8.4% vs AOA 10.6% CAGR over 17 years) that the vault's regime tables predict only qualitatively. The reusable point is structural: the entire iShares ladder lives on the capital-weighted axis that risk parity deliberately steps off — so AOR is not a midpoint between AOK and a risk-parity fund, it is a point on a different line. No new investing-vault notes today; this is a graph-completion pass plus the commit of the 2026-06-26 ladder work.

The sleeve charts are the first concrete layer of the risk-parity merge scoped earlier: rather than fold 463 machine-generated tracking notes into the investing vault, the integration moves the data into market_data.db and the analysis into the hub. The three charts already earn their place — managed futures show why risk parity carries them (uncorrelated crisis alpha, ~+6.5% CAGR while lagging a +15% S&P), capital-efficient shows the 2022 bond-overlay drag (US NTSX trailing plain SPY) alongside GDE's gold-leverage outlier (+170%), and tail/defensive shows the raw cost of convexity (TAIL −39% over 7.5 years). Each makes a structural risk-parity claim legible on live tickers rather than backtest tables.

The CXMT deepdive sharpens the memory squeeze read-through. The old binary was wrong in both directions: CXMT is not a fake supplier that can be ignored, and it is not a clean Chinese flood of cheap DRAM that breaks the global oligopoly in 2026. The better read is policy-gated validation. CXMT has enough conventional DRAM capability for Apple and PC OEMs to test China-local sourcing, but buyer approvals, 1260H status, possible Entity List escalation, yield/die-size gaps, and HBM qualification keep it from being frictionless global relief. That makes it a wedge supplier: bullish for China self-reliance and a medium-term risk to incumbents, but not yet a cycle-ending force for [[Samsung]], [[SK Hynix]], or [[Micron]].
