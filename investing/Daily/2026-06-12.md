## Synthesis

The Lionsgate note was wrong on its single most important fact — the corporate structure. The vault still described a pre-2025 conglomerate (LGF.A/LGF.B dual class, Starz inside, ~$2B cap), when in reality the studio has been a standalone single-class NYSE company (LION, ~$4.0B cap) since the May 2025 separation. This is a reminder that entity notes go stale precisely on the structural facts that matter most for valuation.

The deeper read is that Lionsgate is now an optionality play. The entire two-step separation — de-SPAC, then Starz split — was engineered to produce a clean, separately valued, acquirable pure-play studio. FY2026 shows why that matters: a $181.8M full-year net loss alongside the best studio quarter ($165.4M OIBDA) in twelve years, all on a ~$1.6B net-debt balance sheet. The investment case is the asymmetry — a ~$1B/yr library annuity plus a single hit (Q4's $190.4M FCF) can flip the cash picture fast, and that same profile makes Lionsgate a standing takeout target (Legendary reportedly circled in Aug 2025). Feltheimer's extension to 2031 is the counterweight signaling near-term independence.

Two structural notes for the vault: (1) the [[Runway]] AI deal — Lionsgate took an equity stake in June 2026, making it the clearest studio test of generative AI as a cost lever ("tens of millions" claimed, but reported model problems); worth watching as a [[AI Video Generation]] proof point. (2) Data hygiene — de-SPAC tickers carry shell-company history; Alpha Vantage stitched [[Screaming Eagle Acquisition Corp]]'s pre-merger junk onto LION's annual series, so the note's financials are anchored on the official release, not the DB annual table. Fundamentals/Sankey charts were deliberately skipped for this reason (plus a net-loss year and pre-FY26 Starz consolidation make a clean series impossible).

The Starz deepdive completes the picture: the two former halves of the old conglomerate are now both consolidation candidates, but for different reasons. Lionsgate is a leveraged library-and-franchise studio that is a *latent* takeout target (Legendary circling). Starz is an *active* one — a ~$475M micro-cap with a named aggressor (Byron Allen, via the stake he bought from Mnuchin) openly campaigning for control, a poison pill in place, and a stock that doubled off his $13.86 entry to price deal probability. The shared lesson the separation taught: breaking a conglomerate into pure-plays doesn't just unlock valuation, it removes the takeover-defense of scale — each clean, cheap pure-play becomes individually acquirable. Both also hit the same de-SPAC/consolidated-history data-pollution trap in Alpha Vantage; both notes are anchored on official releases, and fundamentals/Sankey charts were skipped for both.

The cluster work turned the LION/STRZ deepdives into a structural finding about the whole sector. Three runs (LION-primary studios, STRZ-primary premium-subscription, and a 22-name universe sweep at 1Y/2Y/3Y) converge on one answer: nothing in US-listed media clusters at a tradeable level except share-class arithmetic (FOX/FOXA), market beta wearing a media costume ([[Roku]]+SPY+XLC), and the ad-cycle plumbing ([[Trade Desk]]+[[Magnite]] at the 3Y boundary). The AMC correction is the day's data-integrity lesson and a direct payoff of the price-verification gate: a mover entry in [[AMC Networks]] carried [[AMC Entertainment]]'s tape (+8.7% to $1.12 — verified as AMC's exact Apr 2 close path; AMCX closed $7.17) welded onto AMC Networks' genuine debt-exchange news. Both notes now carry correction trails, the alias is gone, and the rule is promoted to docs/vault-note-guide.md: aliases must never include another listed company's ticker; name-collision pairs get disjoint aliases and mutual "not to be confused with" lines.

The Tang ingestion gives the capex-financability debate its missing denominator. The vault already had the cost side (~$35-60B/GW build, $10-13B/GW rental, Epoch's ~$8.5B/yr annualized hurdle) and the spending side ($700-725B Big-4 2026, the FCF trough, the equity raises); what it lacked was a clean cross-stack comparison of what a GW actually earns. Tang's scatter supplies it: rental infrastructure barely clears the hurdle (hence 10-20% core margins and the software-attach imperative — AWS's 17%→40% decade is the canonical arc), model providers monetize inference-allocated capacity at 2-3x the infrastructure layer with 50-70% estimated gross margins, and the ad platforms still tower over everything at $41-57B/GW. The structural claim worth carrying: the system-level ROIC is cleared by the layers above raw rental, so the load-bearing variables are the inference ARR slope (OpenAI's disclosed $10.5B ARR/GW blended is the benchmark), the training share of footprints, and depreciation honesty. Jensen's Q4 FY26 "profitable tokens" line is the supplier-side version of the same claim — and the vault's existing counterweights (enterprise token rationing, the GPU deployment bottleneck, financing risk, the FCF trough) are exactly the falsifiers the framework would fail on.

Two hygiene wins came out of the same pass: the Altimeter duplicate pair (crossing aliases, ambiguous link resolution since Feb) is consolidated into [[Altimeter Capital]], and the alias gap behind a long-standing dead link ([[Aurora Innovation]] → [[Aurora]]) is fixed at the source.
