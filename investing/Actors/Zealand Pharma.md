# Zealand Pharma

Danish biotech specializing in peptide-based medicines, partnered with [[Boehringer Ingelheim]] on survodutide (dual GLP-1/glucagon, Phase 3 for obesity and MASH) and with [[Roche]] on petrelintide (amylin analog, Phase 2 for obesity, licensed for up to $5.3B). One of the few mid-cap biotechs with a serious shot at the [[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1]] market currently dominated by [[Novo Nordisk]] and [[Eli Lilly]].

## Synopsis

Zealand Pharma has built a 25-year peptide engineering platform that enables the design of novel peptide therapeutics targeting metabolic diseases. The crown jewel is survodutide (BI 456906), partnered with [[Boehringer Ingelheim]], which showed 18-19% body weight loss in Phase 2 obesity trials — competitive with [[Eli Lilly]]'s tirzepatide and [[Novo Nordisk]]'s semaglutide. The dual GLP-1/glucagon mechanism offers potential differentiation: the glucagon component may drive greater fat loss and liver fat reduction, making survodutide particularly relevant for MASH (metabolic-associated steatohepatitis), a liver disease with no approved treatments in most markets.

The partnership with [[Boehringer Ingelheim]] is critical — BI funds development and holds global commercial rights, paying Zealand milestones and royalties. This de-risks Zealand's balance sheet but also caps the upside: if survodutide becomes a blockbuster ($5B+ peak sales potential in the GLP-1/obesity class), Zealand captures a fraction of the economics. The company's second major asset is petrelintide (amylin analog for obesity), licensed to [[Roche]] in 2025 for up to $5.3B in milestones — one of the largest obesity deals in the sector. But petrelintide's Phase 2 readout on March 6, 2026 was a setback: 10.7% body weight loss after 42 weeks fell well short of the ~13% threshold [[Barclays]] analysts considered competitive, and far below the 20.1% upper-end result from [[Eli Lilly]]'s rival amylin drug eloralintide. Zealand fell 34% on the day; [[Roche]] fell over 3%. The safety profile was clean — no vomiting, no GI-related discontinuation at max dose, side effects similar to placebo — but in a market where [[Novo Nordisk]] and [[Eli Lilly]] deliver up to 25% weight loss, tolerability alone does not compensate for inferior efficacy. The drug is not expected on market before 2029 even if Phase 3 proceeds. Zealand trades in Copenhagen (ZEAL) with a market cap that fluctuates heavily around clinical data readouts — a typical biotech dynamic amplified by the enormous TAM of the [[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1]] obesity market.

## Petrelintide Phase 2 (March 6, 2026)

Phase 2 trial results for petrelintide, an amylin analog that works by making patients feel full faster — a distinct mechanism from [[GLP-1 receptor agonists|GLP-1 drugs]] which suppress appetite.

| Metric | Result |
|--------|--------|
| Weight loss | 10.7% average after 42 weeks |
| Primary endpoint | Met |
| GI side effects | Similar to placebo; no vomiting at max dose |
| Discontinuation (GI) | Zero at max dose |
| Competitive threshold | ~13% (per [[Barclays]]) — missed |
| Market launch | 2029 at earliest if Phase 3 proceeds |

[[Roche]] licensed petrelintide from Zealand in 2025 for up to $5.3B if all milestones are met. Zealand fell 34% in Copenhagen on the readout; [[Roche]] fell over 3% in Zurich.

[[Barclays]] analysts described the data as "disappointing" — it fell short of the 13% reduction that could have made it competitive. The market leaders ([[Novo Nordisk]]'s Wegovy/Ozempic, [[Eli Lilly]]'s Mounjaro/Zepbound) deliver weight reduction of up to 25%. [[Eli Lilly]]'s own amylin competitor eloralintide achieved 20.1% weight loss at the upper end in its Phase 2 trial, nearly double petrelintide's result.

The amylin class as a whole offers a potential safety advantage over GLP-1 drugs — fewer gastrointestinal side effects — but petrelintide's efficacy gap raises questions about whether it can command premium pricing even with a cleaner tolerability profile.

*Source: FT, March 6, 2026*
