The **DAX** (Deutscher Aktienindex) is [[Germany]]'s blue-chip stock index, comprising the 40 largest companies on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange by market capitalization and order book volume. Expanded from 30 to 40 constituents in September 2021 following the [[Wirecard]] scandal, which exposed selection criteria weaknesses. Key members include [[SAP]], [[Siemens]], [[Allianz]], [[Deutsche Telekom]], and [[ASML]] (added as a foreign listing).

## Synopsis

The DAX is a performance index (total return, including dividends), which distinguishes it from most global benchmarks like the [[S&P 500]] that are typically quoted as price-return indices. This means DAX levels incorporate reinvested dividends, making headline comparisons to the S&P 500 structurally misleading — the DAX appears to outperform on a headline basis because it includes a return component other indices exclude. The index is heavily weighted toward industrials, autos, and financials, giving it cyclical exposure that amplifies moves in global manufacturing PMIs.

The 2021 expansion from 30 to 40 constituents was triggered by the [[Wirecard]] collapse in 2020 — a DAX 30 member that turned out to be a fraud with EUR 1.9B in fictitious cash. The embarrassment forced [[Deutsche Boerse]] (the exchange operator and index calculator) to tighten inclusion criteria, adding profitability requirements and expanding the index to reduce single-name concentration. As of early 2026, [[SAP]] alone represents ~15% of the index, creating a de facto technology bet within what is nominally a diversified industrial benchmark. For global investors, the DAX functions as a proxy for European industrial and export strength — it rallies on strong Chinese PMI data and weakens on trade friction, making it a high-beta expression of global manufacturing sentiment.
