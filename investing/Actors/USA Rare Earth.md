USA Rare Earth (USAR) — American rare earth mining, processing, and magnet-manufacturing company pursuing a vertically integrated "mine-to-magnet" supply chain for neodymium–praseodymium–[[Dysprosium|dysprosium]]–[[Terbium|terbium]] magnets used in defense, electric vehicles, and robotics. Based in Stillwater, Oklahoma with its anchor resource at the Round Top deposit in [[Texas]]. Came public in March 2025 via business combination with [[Inflection Point Acquisition Corp II]]. Led by [[Barbara Humpton]] (CEO since October 1, 2025), formerly head of [[Siemens|Siemens USA]].

## Synopsis

USAR's 2026 story is about three intertwined events that effectively re-priced the company: the January 26 [[CHIPS Program Office]] letter of intent for up to $1.6B in government funding plus a 10% federal equity stake, the January 29 $1.5B [[PIPE financing]] placed by [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] and [[Moelis]] that actually funded the build-out, and the April 20 ~$3B acquisition of [[Serra Verde]] that gave USAR Western access to a producing heavy-rare-earth mine in [[Brazil]]'s Goiás state. Together these moves took the company from a US-focused development-stage project with ~$343M of cash to a vertically integrated Western producer with ~$3.5B of available capital and operating mine supply, positioning it as the US government's favored non-Chinese rare earth champion alongside [[MP Materials]].

That positioning brought with it a [[Lutnick family conflicts of interest|conflict-of-interest controversy]]. Commerce Secretary [[Howard Lutnick]] architected the industrial policy that made USAR eligible for the government funding, while Cantor Fitzgerald — run by his son [[Brandon Lutnick]] after Howard's divestiture — earned lead-placement-agent fees on the $1.5B PIPE that the LOI explicitly required USAR to raise. Senators [[Elizabeth Warren]], [[Chris Van Hollen]], and [[Ron Wyden]] sent a letter on February 26, 2026, alleging that the Commerce Department "created demand" for Cantor's services, and Rep. [[Zoe Lofgren]] (ranking member, House Science Committee) followed with a March 19 letter on the equity stake. This is the second Lutnick-era controversy involving Cantor, after the [[Tariff refund claims market]] trading.

USAR's equity recovered to $23.05 by April 23, 2026, modestly above the $21.50 PIPE clearing price, while Cantor Fitzgerald's own research desk carries a $35 price target.

## Evolution

### Pre-public era
- Round Top origin — The project was originally owned by Texas Mineral Resources Corp. USAR acquired the Round Top deposit in [[Texas]], a heavy-rare-earth-enriched rhyolite body containing 15 of 17 rare earth elements plus lithium, uranium, and beryllium.
- Thor Mining joint venture and exit — Earlier partnership structures were unwound as USAR consolidated control.
- Magnet line — USAR acquired magnet-manufacturing equipment and IP from Hitachi Metals' former North Carolina sintered-magnet operation, relocating it to Stillwater.

### Public-market era (2025–)
- March 2025 — de-SPAC: Combined with [[Inflection Point Acquisition Corp II]] and listed on NASDAQ under USAR. Warrants trade as USARW.
- October 1, 2025 — CEO transition: [[Barbara Humpton]] (ex-[[Siemens|Siemens USA]] CEO, ~7 years) joined as CEO. Mark Smith moved to executive chair.

### 2026 inflection year
- January 26, 2026 — Commerce LOI: The [[CHIPS Program Office]] within the [[Commerce Department]] issued a letter of intent for up to $1.6B of total federal support — up to $277M in direct grant funding plus up to $1.3B in loans — for two rare earth mining and magnet projects. The package included a 10% federal equity stake in USAR and required USAR to secure at least $500M in concurrent private financing. Trump administration framing positioned the deal as the model for post-[[Inflation Reduction Act|IRA]] industrial policy targeting [[Rare earth leverage|Chinese rare earth dominance]].
- January 29, 2026 — $1.5B PIPE closes: USAR issued 69.8M common shares at $21.50/share for ~$1.5B gross proceeds. Anchored by [[Inflection Point Acquisition Corp II]] affiliates (the former SPAC sponsor). [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] acted as lead placement agent; [[Moelis]] as co-placement agent. [[White & Case]] advised USAR; [[DLA Piper]] advised the placement agents. Combined with the existing ~$343M cash and pending CHIPS funding, total capital access reached ~$3.5B.
- January 30, 2026 — Cantor price-target raise: Cantor Fitzgerald's research desk raised the USAR price target to $35 from $28, citing the LOI and the $3.5B capital position. The fact that Cantor's research arm upgraded the same transaction for which its investment-banking arm had just earned lead-placement-agent fees drew immediate press attention.
- February 26, 2026 — Senate conflict letter: Senators [[Elizabeth Warren]] (D-MA), [[Chris Van Hollen]] (D-MD), and [[Ron Wyden]] (D-OR) sent a letter to Commerce Secretary [[Howard Lutnick]] arguing that the Commerce Department "created demand" for [[Cantor Fitzgerald]]'s placement-agent services by conditioning the LOI on the $500M+ private raise. They requested written answers and records of communications with [[Brandon Lutnick]] by March 11, 2026. Howard Lutnick had transferred his Cantor stake to his adult sons per his ethics agreement, but senators argued the arrangement failed to neutralize the conflict.
- March 19, 2026 — House Science letter: Ranking Member [[Zoe Lofgren]] (D-CA) of the House Science, Space, and Technology Committee sent Secretary Lutnick a follow-up letter focused on the 10% federal equity stake — its valuation mechanics, governance rights, and the precedent it set for later [[Project Vault]]-style industrial-policy deals.
- April 20, 2026 — Serra Verde acquisition: USAR announced an agreement to acquire [[Serra Verde]] Group, operator of [[Brazil]]'s Pela Ema ionic-clay rare earth mine in Goiás state, for ~$3B (~$2.5B in USAR stock + $300M cash). The acquisition gives USAR access to a producing mine of all four magnetic rare earths (Nd, Pr, Dy, Tb) plus yttrium, moving USAR from development-stage to operating producer in heavy rare earths. CEO [[Barbara Humpton]] framed the deal as "USA Rare Earth is now the global champion in rare earths." Serra Verde's Thras Moraitis characterized the combined entity as "the defining Western player."

## Operations

### Round Top (Texas)
- Heavy-rare-earth-enriched rhyolite deposit in Hudspeth County, [[Texas]]
- Contains 15 of 17 rare earth elements, concentrated in heavy rare earths ([[Dysprosium|Dy]], [[Terbium|Tb]])
- Also contains lithium, uranium, beryllium, and other critical minerals
- Permitting-stage as of 2026; USAR targeting development funding from the CHIPS LOI

### Serra Verde / Pela Ema (Brazil)
- Ionic-clay rare earth deposit in Goiás state, [[Brazil]]
- One of the few operating non-Chinese sources of [[Dysprosium|dysprosium]] and [[Terbium|terbium]]
- Previous equity backing from Denham Capital and others; absorbed into USAR via Apr 2026 transaction
- Production target ~5,000 tonnes rare earth oxide (REO) per year

### Stillwater magnet facility (Oklahoma)
- Sintered neodymium-iron-boron (Nd-Fe-B) magnet manufacturing
- Equipment and IP originally from Hitachi Metals' former North Carolina operation
- Targets defense, EV, robotics, and industrial motor applications

## Government and policy context

USAR is positioned as one of two Trump-administration "national champions" for non-Chinese rare earths, alongside [[MP Materials]]. The January 26, 2026 LOI extended the pattern of [[Project Vault]] industrial-policy deals — direct federal equity in strategically important domestic producers, conditioned on matched private capital. See [[Rare earth leverage]] for the broader policy context and [[Critical minerals]] for the supply-chain framing.

The 10% federal equity stake is the first of its kind for a post-[[Inflation Reduction Act|IRA]] critical minerals deal and sets a template for subsequent [[Commerce Department]] agreements. Governance rights, valuation mechanics, and exit provisions for the federal stake remain the subject of [[Zoe Lofgren|Lofgren]]'s ongoing Science Committee inquiry.

## Conflict-of-interest controversy

See [[Lutnick family conflicts of interest]] for the full pattern. In brief:
- [[Howard Lutnick]] architected the Trump administration industrial-policy framework that created the USAR LOI.
- The LOI required USAR to raise ≥$500M in private financing — essentially mandating placement-agent mandates.
- USAR hired [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] (run by Howard's son [[Brandon Lutnick]]) as lead placement agent for the resulting $1.5B PIPE, with [[Moelis]] as co-agent.
- Howard Lutnick had transferred his Cantor stake to his sons per his ethics divestiture, but his family's economic interest in Cantor's fees was not addressed.
- The [[Tariff refund claims market]] episode (mid-2025) established an earlier version of the same pattern: Cantor trading special situations created by Howard Lutnick's tariff policy.
- Senators [[Elizabeth Warren]], [[Chris Van Hollen]], [[Ron Wyden]], and Rep. [[Zoe Lofgren]] have open inquiries as of April 2026.

Specific fee disclosure: Cantor's compensation for the PIPE has not been publicly itemized; Senate letter noted Cantor "acted as a compensated broker" and PIPE placement-agent fees typically run 3–5% of proceeds, implying ~$45–75M gross on $1.5B.
