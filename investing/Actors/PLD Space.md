**PLD Space** is a Spanish launch company developing the MIURA 5 small-satellite launcher. The investing-vault reason to track it is not that it can displace [[SpaceX]] directly. It is that European, Japanese, and public-sector capital are trying to turn PLD into another non-US launch node at a time when satellite operators are mapping launch-provider concentration risk.

Operationally, PLD is still a pre-orbital execution story. Its usefulness in the vault is as an early indicator of whether Europe can create credible launch redundancy through private launchers, public financing, and Kourou-based infrastructure before satellite demand outruns the US-led launch stack.

## 2026 financing and European launch hedge

PLD closed a EUR180M Series C in March 2026 led by Mitsubishi Electric Corporation, with Spanish public capital and Nazca Capital also participating. In April, the European Investment Bank added EUR30M of venture debt for MIURA 5, taking 2026 secured financing to EUR210M and lifetime funding above EUR380M by PLD/EIB accounting.

The Journal of Space Commerce framed the round as a supply-chain hedge rather than a narrow small-launch story. The useful read is that a vertically integrated European launcher, based outside the US export-control and tariff surface, gives satellite operators another procurement option if US-heavy launch dependency becomes operationally or politically expensive.

The important constraints remain execution and cadence. MIURA 5 is still pre-orbital; the planned 2026 test flight and 2027 commercial launch window have to convert funding and factory buildout into successful missions. The upside case is not "European SpaceX." It is a credible second-order launch option for small satellites, European institutional demand, and operators that need non-US redundancy.

## Evolution

PLD has moved from Spain's experimental launch capability around MIURA 1 toward a full small-launch industrialization plan around MIURA 5. The 2026 financing package is the inflection to watch: it gives PLD the balance-sheet runway to attempt flight qualification and commercial launch scale-up, but the company still has to cross the first-flight and cadence threshold that separates launch startups from durable infrastructure.

## MIURA 5 positioning

| Dimension | Read-through |
|-----------|--------------|
| Payload class | Small-satellite launch, with EIB describing the vehicle as a way to reduce scheduling risk for small payloads on medium/heavy vehicles |
| Industrial model | Vertical integration around propulsion, launch infrastructure, and in-house systems |
| Strategic buyer logic | European autonomous access to space, civil/commercial/defense payloads, and customer diversification away from US launch concentration |
| Cadence ambition | EIB/PLD materials point to up to 30 missions per year from multiple spaceports after scale-up |
| Main risk | First-flight, qualification, insurance, and conversion from booked/targeted launches into repeatable operations |
