#actor #person #semiconductor

**Jim Keller** is the most consequential chip architect of his generation — the designer behind AMD's K8 and Zen, Apple's A4/A5, and Tesla's FSD chip — now leading [[Tenstorrent]] as CEO with a bet that open-source RISC-V can break [[NVIDIA]]'s AI silicon monopoly.

## Synopsis

Keller's career reads like a highlight reel of semiconductor inflection points. At [[AMD]], he designed the K8 architecture (the Opteron/Athlon 64 that gave AMD its brief 2003-2006 lead over [[Intel]]) and returned a decade later to architect Zen (the design that rescued AMD from near-bankruptcy and enabled the Ryzen/EPYC renaissance under [[Lisa Su]]). Between AMD stints, he designed Apple's A4 and A5 processors (powering the first iPad and iPhone 4S, launching Apple's custom silicon era), then led Tesla's Full Self-Driving chip team (replacing [[NVIDIA]] hardware with a custom ASIC that cut inference costs by ~5x). A brief and reportedly tumultuous stint at [[Intel]] (2018-2020) ended abruptly, reportedly over frustration with Intel's bureaucratic culture.

Keller joined [[Tenstorrent]] as CEO in late 2022, turning a small Toronto-based AI chip startup into the most prominent RISC-V alternative to [[NVIDIA]]'s CUDA-locked ecosystem. The thesis is that AI workloads will eventually fragment away from NVIDIA's monolithic GPU model toward specialized, licensable architectures — and that RISC-V (open-source instruction set) plus Tenstorrent's Tensix cores can provide that alternative. His arrival prompted [[Taalas]] founder Ljubisa Bajic to leave and start a competing venture, suggesting the Tenstorrent team was split on strategic direction. The company raised $693M at a $2.6B valuation in late 2024 (investors include [[Samsung]], [[Hyundai]], and [[LG]]). The bull case is that Keller is the one person with the track record to design a genuinely competitive AI architecture; the bear case is that CUDA's software moat makes hardware specs irrelevant.
