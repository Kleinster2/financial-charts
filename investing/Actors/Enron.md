# Enron

Enron was a [[Houston]]-based energy company whose December 2001 bankruptcy was the largest in US history at the time (~$63.4B in assets). The accounting fraud — which used off-balance-sheet special purpose entities to hide debt and inflate earnings — destroyed $74B in shareholder value and led to the dissolution of [[Arthur Andersen]], then one of the Big Five accounting firms. CEO Jeff Skilling was sentenced to 24 years (later reduced to 14), and CFO Andrew Fastow received 6 years.

## Legacy

Enron's collapse catalyzed the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002), which imposed CEO/CFO certification of financials, created the PCAOB, and significantly increased corporate governance requirements for public companies. The [[SEC]] penalties from the Enron era remained a benchmark for corporate fraud enforcement until [[Allianz]]'s $6B Structured Alpha settlement in 2022 — the largest fraud-related fine against a financial institution. Enron's name has become shorthand for corporate fraud, and its failure pattern (off-balance-sheet leverage, mark-to-market abuse, auditor capture) recurs in subsequent scandals from Wirecard to Luckin Coffee.
