#actor #person #politician #eu #greece

**Apostolos Tzitzikostas** is the European Commissioner for Sustainable Transport and Tourism since December 2024 (von der Leyen II Commission). Greek politician; previously Governor of Central Macedonia (2014-2024) and President of the European Committee of the Regions (2020-2022). Mid-50s.

The investing-vault relevance comes from his transport portfolio, which routes through several of the largest live threads in the vault: [[EU]] aviation safety regulation under [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]] airspace disruption (see [[Iran conflict airline disruption]]), maritime traffic policy on Hormuz alternatives (see [[Strait of Hormuz]]), and EU port-infrastructure / supply-chain rules. He surfaced in the vault on May 17, 2026 as the first sitting EU commissioner to publicly raise world recession risk in connection with the [[Iran]] war.

## Iran war recession warning — Athens (May 14-17, 2026)

At an [[Financial Times|FT]] conference in Athens on Thursday May 14, 2026, reported in the FT's May 17 peak-summer-crunch piece, Tzitzikostas said: if the Middle East conflict "does not end in the coming weeks and we don't have the reopening of the [[Strait of Hormuz|Hormuz strait]], I'm afraid a world recession could be on the table."

This is the first time a sitting EU commissioner has invoked global recession in connection with the [[Operation Epic Fury|2026 Iran conflict]]. Three things make the statement analytically distinct from earlier EU policy framing:

1. Recession word used explicitly — earlier EU framings (e.g., the March commissioner-level discussion of [[LNG]] storage targets via [[Dan Jørgensen]]) focused on supply security, not recession risk.
2. The trigger is geographically specific — Hormuz reopening, not generic ceasefire. Locating recession risk on a specific infrastructure variable narrows what diplomatic success has to look like to avoid the downside scenario.
3. The portfolio fit is precise — Tzitzikostas's portfolio touches every cross-border consequence of Hormuz closure (aviation reroutes, maritime traffic, port congestion, tourism collapse). He is closer to the data than most commissioners would be.
