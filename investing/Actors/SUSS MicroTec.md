# SUSS MicroTec

German semiconductor equipment company headquartered in Garching bei München. Near-monopoly positions in two critical [[Advanced packaging|advanced packaging]] processes: ~90% share in photomask cleaning at [[TSMC]] and ~50-65% share in temporary wafer bonding/debonding (TBDB) used by [[Samsung]] and [[Micron]] for [[HBM]] production. Around 70% of revenue is tied to AI-driven semiconductor demand. Market cap ~€1.1B. One of the most overlooked pure-play advanced packaging equipment names globally — almost no US analyst coverage.

The investment case is structural: as [[CoWoS]] and [[HBM]] capacity scales from ~70-75K wafers/month (end-2025) to ~90-100K (2026), SUSS's monopoly positions mean near-guaranteed equipment pull. The risk is the 2026 "transition year" management flagged — AI bonder orders pausing and muted China photomask demand create a near-term revenue dip before the next capacity wave. The stock has rallied from lows but trades at ~€57, down from peak levels, reflecting this uncertainty.

## Products and market position

### Temporary bonding/debonding (TBDB)

The critical process for [[HBM]] manufacturing: wafers must be temporarily bonded to carrier substrates during thinning (to <40µm) and via-reveal steps, then cleanly debonded and stacked. SUSS supplies:
- 100% of [[TSMC]]'s [[CoWoS]]-L temporary bonders
- ~50-65% share for [[Samsung]] and [[Micron]] HBM temporary bonding
- Sole-source or dominant position at all three major HBM/advanced packaging customers

As HBM stacks increase from 8-Hi to 12-Hi to 16-Hi, each additional layer requires more bonding/debonding cycles, driving equipment demand faster than wafer starts alone would suggest.

### Photomask cleaning

~90% market share at [[TSMC]] for high-end photomask cleaning systems. Photomasks are the master templates used in lithography — keeping them contamination-free is essential for yield at advanced nodes. SUSS's cleaning systems operate at the interface between [[ASML]]'s EUV lithography tools and the fab process.

### Other products

- Mask aligners and UV lithography (legacy business, lower growth)
- Full-field UV scanning systems (~100% market share)
- Expanding into hybrid bonding (the next frontier beyond temporary bonding, relevant for chip-to-wafer and wafer-to-wafer integration at <10µm pitch)

## Evolution

1949: Founded as Karl Süss GmbH in Garching bei München — originally a precision optics and microscope repair business. The name is literally that of the founder, Karl Süss.

1960s-1990s: Pivoted into semiconductor lithography equipment, building mask aligners for contact and proximity printing. Became a standard tool in MEMS, LED, and compound semiconductor fabs. Not competing with [[ASML]] or [[Canon]] on leading-edge scanners — instead occupying adjacent niches that the giants didn't pursue.

1999: Renamed to SUSS MicroTec, listed on the Neuer Markt (Frankfurt). Expanded into wafer bonding, photomask cleaning, and other specialty equipment.

2000s-2010s: Survived the dot-com bust and multiple semiconductor downturns by staying in niche monopoly positions. Revenue oscillated between €120-180M — a small, profitable, boring equipment maker that nobody outside Germany covered.

2020-2023: The AI infrastructure buildout changed everything. [[TSMC]]'s [[CoWoS]] expansion and [[Samsung]]/[[Micron]]'s HBM production ramps created massive demand for exactly the two processes where SUSS holds near-monopoly positions: temporary bonding (for HBM stacking) and photomask cleaning (for EUV lithography yield). Revenue went from €252M (2020) to €304M (2023), with the real acceleration still ahead.

2024: Breakout year — €446M revenue (+47%), operating margin nearly doubled to 18.6%. Product mix shifted heavily toward high-ASP AI bonders and cleaners. Opened new cleanroom facility in Tsubei ([[Japan]]) to be closer to key customers. Capital Markets Day in November laid out a 2030 growth path.

2025-2026: Management guided 2026 as a "transition year" with AI bonder pausing and muted China photomask demand. But the structural drivers — HBM scaling from 8-Hi to 12-Hi to 16-Hi, CoWoS capacity doubling, US onshoring raising the premium on proven tools — remain intact. The question is timing, not direction.
