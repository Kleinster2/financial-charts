**Philippines** is a Southeast Asian archipelago economy. Remittance-dependent, oil importer, vulnerable to Gulf energy disruption. Inbound remittances reached ~$35B in 2025 (>7% of national GDP) from an overseas workforce of ~15-20M Filipinos employed as domestic helpers, cruise crew, factory workers, and skilled professionals across the Middle East, Korea, Japan, and the Anglosphere. The remittance corridor is the country's most consequential source of household FX and now a key proving ground for stablecoin-rail cross-border payments — [[Coins.ph]] (CEO [[Wei Zhou]]) anchors the local off-ramp, with corridor partners including [[Remitly]] and other digital senders running a [[Stablecoin sandwich]] (USD → USDC → PHP) at costs targeting 20-30 bps versus historical 8% correspondent banking.

## Remittance corridor

The Philippine remittance corridor is now one of the cleanest stablecoin-rail test cases globally. Three structural features make it work:

1. Receivers want dollar-denominated financial services — overseas workers paid in USD want to deliver USD-equivalent purchasing power home, even though the local economy runs in pesos
2. Concentrated sender geographies — Middle East GCC states, Korea, Japan, US — match well to existing stablecoin issuer compliance frameworks
3. Local off-ramp infrastructure via [[Coins.ph]] (18M users, BSP licenses) integrates the sandwich into QRPh, Philippines' national QR payments standard

[[Wei Zhou]]'s stated cost compression path: 8% (10 years ago, traditional correspondent banking) → 2-4% (digital remitters) → 0.5% (stablecoin sandwich) → 20-30 bps target. On $40B of annual flow each 1% saved is ~$400M retained by workers and their families.

The Philippine bank that first banked crypto exchanges in the country moved from roughly top 7 to top 3 by size — an early signal of how stablecoin-rail integration redraws domestic banking league tables.

Source: Bloomberg TV, "Stablecoins Are Quietly Changing How the World Sends Money" (May 2026).
