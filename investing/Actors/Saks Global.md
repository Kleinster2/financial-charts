**Exemplar Luxury Group (ELG)** — Luxury department store holding company (renamed from Saks Global on June 26, 2026). Owns Saks Fifth Avenue, [[Neiman Marcus]], Bergdorf Goodman. Emerged from Chapter 11 on June 26, 2026 with ~75% debt reduction and 49 core luxury stores.

The renamed ELG is now a creditor-owned test of whether luxury department stores can survive when the capital structure is cleaned up and the discount / real-estate leverage strategy is abandoned. The investable read-through is not public equity upside, but the creditor-control outcome, vendor normalization, and implications for [[Department store decline]], luxury retail landlords, and minority investors such as [[Amazon]].

The crown jewel asset — worth more than the entire company was bought for. See [[611 Fifth Avenue]] for full building details.

|--------|-------|
| Building | [[611 Fifth Avenue]] (1924 store) + [[623 Fifth Avenue]] (1990 tower) |

| Retail size | ~650,000 sq ft |
| Architect | Starrett & van Vleck |
| Style | Neo-Renaissance palazzo, Indiana limestone |
| Landmark status | NYC designated 1984 |

### The valuation rollercoaster

| Year | Appraisal | Key driver |
|------|-----------|------------|
| 2014 | $3.7B | Peak Fifth Ave rents, retail optimism |
| 2019 | $1.6B | E-commerce disruption, rent collapse (-57%) |
| 2024 | $3.6B | Luxury buying spree, tourism recovery (+125%) |

Context: HBC bought all of Saks for $2.9B in 2013. The flagship alone has consistently been worth more than that entire acquisition.

### 100-year history

1924 — The dream store:
Horace Saks and Bernard Gimbel (cousins, rival Herald Square merchants) opened the flagship on Sept 15, 1924. First large retailer to locate in what was then residential Fifth Avenue. Won Fifth Avenue Association gold medal for "best new building." Horace Saks died suddenly in 1926 (age ~34).

Ownership carousel:

| Year | Owner | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| 1923 | Gimbel Brothers | Merger |
| 1973 | Brown & Williamson | Tobacco conglomerate |
| 1990 | Investcorp | PE; 36-story tower added |
| 1998 | Proffitt's/Saks Inc. | Public company |
| 2013 | HBC / [[Richard Baker]] | $2.9B for entire chain |

2014 — "Most valuable retail building in the world":
- $3.7B appraisal
- HBC originated $1.25B [[Ground lease]] mortgage (not assumed existing debt)
  - Collateral: Land + ground lease only (NOT the building)
  - Terms: 20-year, interest-only, fixed <4.40%
  - Lenders: BofA, [[Morgan Stanley]], [[Goldman Sachs]], [[Bank of Nova Scotia]]
  - Use of proceeds: Paid down $1.2B floating-rate corporate debt (4.75%)
- Annual rent: $108M
- Baker: "This valuation seems to indicate that it's the single most-valuable retail building in the world."

2019 — [[Retail apocalypse]]:
- $1.6B appraisal (-57%)
- Annual rent capacity dropped to $84M (-22%)
- [[Fifth Avenue luxury corridor]] rents down 18% from 2015 peak
- CBRE scenarios: as-is $1.6B, vacated $250M, subdivided $1.18B

2024 — Recovery:
- $3.6B appraisal (back to within 3% of 2014 peak)
- Driven by [[Kering]], [[Prada]], [[LVMH]] buying Fifth Ave properties
- Fifth Ave rents hit $2,500/sq ft (world's most expensive)

### Bankruptcy collateral

In bankruptcy, the flagship became central collateral for $1.75B [[DIP financing]] — subordinating [[Amazon]]'s $475M equity stake behind bondholders. See [[Saks bankruptcy]] for the courtroom fight. The June 2026 emergence resolved this: the flagship survived as ongoing collateral, [[Amazon]] equity confirmed wiped, and the building was not liquidated.

## Emergence (June 26, 2026)

The company formally exited Chapter 11 on June 26, 2026, renamed to Exemplar Luxury Group (ELG). See [[Saks bankruptcy emergence 2026]] for the full event note.

Key terms:
- Debt cut ~75% ($5B → ~$1.25B)
- $500M new exit financing
- 148 → 49 stores (Saks OFF 5th discount format being wound down)
- Saks Fifth Avenue, [[Neiman Marcus]], [[Bergdorf Goodman]] brands retained
- [[Geoffroy van Raemdonck]] retained as CEO
- [[611 Fifth Avenue]] flagship survived as ongoing collateral, not liquidated
- [[Amazon]]'s $475M equity confirmed wiped

Strategy pivot: pure luxury / white-glove service. The Baker era of real-estate-as-leverage is over; the discount-format era is being ended. The three surviving brands each date back over a century (Saks 1924, Neiman 1907, Bergdorf 1899).

## Investment implications

#### Post-emergence (Jun 2026)
- Bonds resolved via debt-for-equity swap; original bondholders (incl. [[Pentwater Capital]], [[Bracebridge Capital]]) now equity holders
- [[Amazon]] equity ($475M) confirmed wiped
- Vendor relationships with [[Chanel]], [[Kering]], [[LVMH]] restart with cleaner balance sheet
- $9B sales target cited for the new entity
- Survival — not liquidation — validates that a consolidated luxury department store model can work with the right capital structure

#### Indirect
- [[Department store decline]] thesis gets a survival data point, not just failure
- [[Macy's]] still a beneficiary of the disruption but now faces a deleveraged competitor
- [[Luxury]] sector stress signal partially resolved
