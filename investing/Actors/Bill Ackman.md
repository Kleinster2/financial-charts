## Mortgage policy proposals (Jan 2026)

**Prepayment penalty idea:**
- Add prepayment penalties to new mortgages
- Could shave **~65bps** off rates (6.16% → ~5.5%)
- Rationale: MBS investors demand spread for prepay optionality
- Customizable lockouts (5yr, 10yr, etc.)

**Ackman:**
> "Locking in the 65 bps savings upfront over the life of the mortgage may be the difference between the borrower being able to afford the home and not."

**Pushback — Kevin Jackson ([[Wells Fargo]]):**
> "There is no empirical evidence whatsoever that suggests more prepayment penalty originations correspond to significantly lower mortgage rates. A terrible position to put the borrower into."

**Policy access:** Ackman has pitched ideas directly to Trump, Bessent, Lutnick, and Pulte.

## Gotham Partners (1993-2003)

Founded March 1993 with David Berkowitz (HBS classmate). Initial capital: $3M from family and friends — Ackman cold-called 100+ Forbes 400 members, 4 invested. Strategy: deep-value with catalysts, influenced by Seth Klarman's Margin of Safety.

Early returns were strong (~20% annualized through the late 1990s). AUM peaked at $568M in 2000. Key early trades included Circa Pharmaceuticals (83% return in months) and the 1995 Rockefeller Center bid with Leucadia National (failed but raised the firm's profile).

The fund shifted toward illiquid controlling stakes — First Union Real Estate (first hostile REIT takeover, Ackman became chairman) and Gotham Golf Corporation (25 courses, losing $20M+/year). When Ackman tried to merge Gotham Golf with First Union — effectively bailing out the golf business with REIT cash — a court blocked it.

Redemptions hit: $108M in 2000, then a quarter of the fund after the lawsuit. AUM fell to ~$300M. Liquidation announced December 27, 2002. Eliot Spitzer investigated Ackman for publishing a buy recommendation on Pre-Paid Legal Services while selling shares during wind-down. No charges filed.

The Hallwood Realty sale to [[Carl Icahn]] in March 2003 included "schmuck insurance" (50% of profits above 10% annual return if sold within 3 years). When Hallwood was acquired 13 months later at $136/share (vs $80 sale price), Icahn refused to pay. Courts sided with Ackman in 2005; Icahn paid ~$9M in 2011. This was the origin of the Ackman-Icahn feud.

[[Pershing Square]] launched January 1, 2004 with $54M — $50M from Leucadia National, the rest from Ackman personally.

## MBIA short (2002-2009)

Ackman's defining trade. In December 2002, while still running Gotham, he published a 66-page report ("Is MBIA Triple-A?") arguing that the nation's largest muni bond insurer had dangerously expanded into guaranteeing subprime-backed CDOs through an off-balance-sheet subsidiary (LaCrosse Financial Products), that its reserves were the lowest of any non-government financial institution, and that its AAA rating was fraudulent.

He shorted MBIA stock and bought CDS on its debt. Cost: ~$64M in premiums and short-sale costs over six years.

MBIA counterattacked aggressively. CEO Jay Brown told Ackman "we have friends in high places." MBIA convinced Eliot Spitzer to investigate Ackman for "short and distort." The SEC opened a parallel probe. Both investigations cleared Ackman — no charges — but the reputational damage nearly destroyed his ability to raise capital for Pershing Square.

Ackman was vindicated by the 2008 financial crisis. MBIA lost its AAA rating in stages (Fitch Apr 2008, S&P Jun 2008, Moody's to junk Jun 2009). The stock collapsed from ~$76 (Jan 2007) to ~$2.17 (Mar 2009) — down 97%. In January 2007, the SEC fined MBIA $75M for securities fraud related to the sham reinsurance transactions Ackman had identified five years earlier.

Ackman closed the position on January 16, 2009. Total profit: ~$1.1B on ~$64M invested — an 18x return. Documented in Christine Richard's 2010 book, Confidence Game.

## Target proxy fight (2007-2011)

Ackman's thesis: [[Target]]'s ~1,740 stores sat on enormously valuable real estate the market gave no credit for. He proposed spinning the land into a REIT ("TIP REIT") while Target leased it back under a 75-year master lease.

He created a dedicated single-stock fund (Pershing Square IV) raising $2B, and accumulated a ~10% stake at ~$58/share (~$4.2B including derivatives). The position was heavily levered through options.

Target rejected the REIT proposal twice (Sep 2008, Nov 2008). In March 2009, Ackman launched a proxy fight for 5 of 12 board seats. ISS recommended shareholders elect 2 of his 5 nominees. At the May 28, 2009 vote, all incumbents got 70%+ and none of Ackman's nominees got above 22%. Terrible timing — proposing a REIT spinoff in the worst real estate downturn in decades.

Target fell from ~$58 to ~$25 (early March 2009). With leverage, PSIV lost ~90% of its original capital. Total loss: ~$1.8B. Ackman personally contributed $25M to compensate PSIV investors and waived future incentive fees until they recovered. He sold remaining shares in Q1 2011.

## J.C. Penney (2010-2013)

Bought an 18% stake (39.7M shares) starting October 2010 at ~$22/share. Thesis: undervalued department store needing better management. Gained a board seat and pushed for a CEO change.

In November 2011, J.C. Penney hired Ron Johnson (creator of Apple Stores) as CEO. Johnson launched "Fair and Square" pricing — eliminating 590 annual sales promotions and all coupons. The strategy was catastrophic. JCP's core coupon-driven shoppers were completely alienated.

FY2012: revenue down 24% ($17.3B → $13B), net loss of $985M, Q4 same-store sales down 32%. Over 40,000 employees lost their jobs. Johnson's refusal to test before rolling out chain-wide — "We didn't test at Apple" — became a cautionary tale.

Johnson was fired April 8, 2013 after 17 months. Mike Ullman (the CEO Ackman had pushed out) was brought back. Ackman fought with the board, leaked a letter demanding Ullman's removal, then resigned from the board on August 13, 2013. Sold all shares to Citigroup on August 26, 2013 at $12.90/share. Total loss: ~$500M. J.C. Penney filed for bankruptcy in May 2020.

## Canadian Pacific Railway (2011-2016)

Ackman's cleanest activist win. Thesis: CP was the worst-run Class I railroad in North America — operating ratio of 81.3% (worst in the industry) vs CN Railway at ~63%. The gap was management, not structure.

Accumulated a 14.2% stake (~$1.4B) starting September 2011 at ~$44/share. Recruited E. Hunter Harrison, the legendary former CN CEO who had invented precision scheduled railroading (PSR). The CP board dismissed Ackman's targets as "a shot in the dark."

At the May 2012 vote, six directors resigned before the meeting and CEO Fred Green stepped down. Ackman's seven nominees won 83-93% of votes. Harrison was appointed CEO on June 29, 2012.

Harrison's transformation was extraordinary:

| Year | Operating ratio |
|------|----------------|
| 2011 (pre-Harrison) | 81.3% |
| 2012 Q4 | 74.8% |
| 2013 Q3 | 65.9% |
| 2014 | 59.8% (beat CN's 60.7%) |

Ackman exited by August 2016 at ~$147/share. Total profit: ~$2.6B. Compounded annual return: 45.39%. Harrison later left CP for CSX but died in December 2017 at age 73.

## Herbalife short (2012-2018)

On December 19, 2012, Ackman disclosed a $1B short position in Herbalife at $42.50/share and delivered a 342-slide, 3.5-hour presentation calling it "the best-managed pyramid scheme in the history of the world." He also spent $50M on a PR campaign targeting Latino communities where Herbalife recruited most heavily.

On January 25, 2013, the most famous moment in financial television: Ackman was on CNBC when [[Carl Icahn]] called in. For 30 minutes they hurled insults on live TV, bringing NYSE floor trading to a standstill. Icahn built a 16.5% long position. Dan Loeb's [[Third Point]] also went long. The stock recovered above $60 by 2014.

The FTC settled in July 2016: Herbalife paid $200M and agreed to restructure its compensation — but the FTC did not label it a "pyramid scheme." The market read it as Herbalife surviving. Ackman covered his direct short in November 2017, replaced it with puts, then fully exited February 28, 2018. HLF jumped 9% to an all-time high on the news.

Total loss: ~$1B (including $50M PR campaign). Icahn made ~$1.3B on the other side.

## Valeant (2014-2017)

Ackman partnered with Valeant CEO Mike Pearson on a ~$47B hostile bid for Allergan in 2014. Pershing Square built a 9.7% Allergan stake before the bid went public — earning ~$1B in a single day when the stock jumped. (Later settled insider trading claims for $290M.) Allergan was ultimately acquired by Actavis instead.

In February 2015, Ackman bought directly into Valeant at ~$161/share, accumulating a 5.7% stake (~$3.9B). Valeant was ~25% of the fund. The company's model: acquire pharma companies, slash R&D, raise drug prices (66% average increase in 2015, 5x the closest peer).

On October 19, 2015, the Philidor pharmacy scandal broke — Valeant had secretly controlled a specialty pharmacy (Philidor Rx), with Valeant employees working there under fake names. Short-seller Andrew Left (Citron Research) compared Valeant to Enron. The stock dropped 30% in three days.

Stock collapse: $262 peak (Aug 2015) → under $27 by March 2016 (congressional hearings, financial restatements, a guidance "typo" overstating projections by $600M). Ackman sold March 13, 2017 at ~$11/share — down 93%. Total loss: ~$4B, or ~$7.7M per trading day. Valeant renamed itself Bausch Health in 2018.

## Chipotle (2016-2025)

Bought a 9.9% stake (~$1.1B) in Q3 2016 at ~$375-440/share after E. coli and norovirus outbreaks had wiped ~$11B in market value. Gained board seats in December 2016. Pushed out founder Steve Ells and installed Brian Niccol (ex-Taco Bell CEO) as CEO in March 2018.

Under Niccol, Chipotle rebuilt consumer trust, invested in digital ordering, and drove comp-store growth. The stock more than quadrupled. Chipotle executed a 50-for-1 split in June 2024. Ackman fully exited by end of 2025, rotating proceeds into [[Amazon]]. Total profit: ~$3B+ on $1.1B invested. Niccol left for Starbucks in August 2024.

## COVID hedges (Feb-Mar 2020)

In late February 2020, Pershing Square spent $27M on credit default swaps on investment-grade and high-yield corporate bond indices (CDX IG, iTraxx Main, CDX HY) with ~$65-75B notional exposure — 0.3% of the fund's $8B AUM. IG spreads were near all-time tights (~50bp).

On March 18, 2020, Ackman called into CNBC and said "hell is coming," that Hilton was "going to zero along with every other hotel company," and called on Trump to shut down the country for 30 days. The S&P fell another ~4% that day.

By March 23 (the exact market bottom, also the day the Fed announced unlimited QE), Ackman had closed all CDS positions. Total profit: $2.6B on $27M — a 96x return (9,526%). He had already sold roughly half before the CNBC appearance.

That same day, he deployed $2.3B into equities at the bottom — including Hilton (the stock he'd said was going to zero five days earlier), Lowe's, Starbucks, and Berkshire Hathaway. Full-year 2020 return: +70.2%.

Critics accused him of market manipulation. No SEC action. Ackman's defense: by the time of the interview, Pershing was net long equities and would benefit from a recovery, not further decline.

## Pershing Square IPO (Mar 10, 2026)

Filed for combined dual listing on NYSE — [[Pershing Square]] management company (PS) and new closed-end fund Pershing Square USA (PSUS). PSUS targeting $5-10B at $50/share, no performance fees, 12-15 concentrated holdings. Sweetener: PSUS buyers get free PS shares (20 per 100 IPO shares, 30 per 100 private placement). $2.8B already committed. Building a "modern-day [[Berkshire Hathaway]]" permanent capital vehicle.
