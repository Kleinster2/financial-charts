Bob Iger — CEO of [[Disney]] for nearly two decades (2005-2020), returned November 2022, and stepped down as CEO a second time shortly before the FT's June 20 2026 profile. The handover to [[Josh D'Amaro]] was announced February 2026; Iger remains on the Disney board through December 2026 and an employee through December 31, 2026. Defined by an acquisition spree: [[Pixar]] ($7B, 2006), [[Marvel Entertainment]] ($4B, 2009), [[Lucasfilm]] ($4B, 2012), and [[21st Century Fox]] ($71B, 2019; ~$54B net).

## Career before Disney

Iger started as a weatherman in Ithaca in the early 1970s. As president of ABC Entertainment in the late 1980s he backed the original Twin Peaks. [[Michael Eisner]] brought him to Disney as chief operating officer around five years before his 2005 elevation to CEO, at a moment when the board wanted a new broom and some doubted Iger as a "suit" who could not make movies. [[Steven Spielberg]], a friend, told the FT Iger "takes an awfully good picture" and recalled Disney under Eisner as a backwater for anything but G-rated fare.

---

## First tenure (2005-2020): the acquisition spree

Iger set three priorities on arrival — quality content, technology, and theme parks — and ran an aggressive M&A playbook totaling roughly $90bn, plus close to $100bn committed to the parks business. Revenues tripled and, from 2005 to 2020, Disney's share price increased fivefold, outpacing the S&P 500.

The [[Pixar]] repair was foundational. The Disney-Pixar relationship had disintegrated under [[Michael Eisner]] and [[Steve Jobs]] had declared it over. Iger's first call led to Disney content on the first video iPod, reframing Disney in Jobs's eyes; at a Pixar meeting Jobs listed every reason a deal would fail on a whiteboard, then conceded that a few great pros outweighed the cons. Disney bought Pixar in 2006.

Iger then worked down a target list — Marvel, Star Wars, James Bond — buying [[Marvel Entertainment]] ($4B, 2009) and [[Lucasfilm]] ($4B, 2012). Only [[James Bond]] escaped, later landing at [[Amazon]]. The fourth megadeal, [[21st Century Fox]] in 2019 ($71bn headline, ~$54bn net after disposals including the Sky stake), drew criticism as too expensive but gave Disney the content volume and executives — including [[Dana Walden]] — to scale Disney+ to what Iger called "number two to [[Netflix]] globally."

[[Steven Spielberg]] told the FT there has been "no one in the history of the entertainment business who has had such back-to-back-to-back success."

## Deals that did not happen

Iger told the FT he was close to buying [[Twitter]] from [[Jack Dorsey]] "at a very attractive price" to use as a global distribution platform, but pulled out the morning of the deal fearing "a horrible distraction." He also held exploratory talks about merging Disney with [[Apple]] — a combination he called "truly transformational and equal" — but "Apple didn't show that much interest."

## The Chapek interregnum and return (2020-2022)

On the eve of Covid, Iger stepped back to executive chairman while [[Bob Chapek]] ran as CEO — a structure Iger modeled on [[Mark Parker]]'s move at [[Nike]]. The arrangement "quickly went bad," Iger said, worsened by the pandemic; he left the company in December 2021 and the two Bobs barely spoke. After "10 months and three weeks," chair [[Susan Arnold]] called Iger on a Friday to ask him back; by Monday he was in his old office. Iger describes the crisis he returned to as "significantly" worse than the one he inherited in 2005. [[Blair Effron]] of [[Centerview Partners]], who spent time with Iger during the gap, said Disney had "lost its culture" and needed "emergency room fixing."

Iger reversed Chapek's changes and launched a $6bn cost-cutting plan that cut 7,000 jobs. He attracted criticism during the 2023 Hollywood strikes for calling writers' and actors' demands "not realistic."

## Second tenure: survival and the handover

Iger framed his second stint as survival rather than the front-foot ambition of the first. Disney topped the 2025 global studio rankings with $6.58bn at the box office (still below pre-Covid), entering 2026 with a sequel-heavy slate (The Devil Wears Prada 2, Toy Story 5, Avengers: Doomsday). He signed off [[Josh D'Amaro]]'s $60bn parks investment plan in 2023.

The handover itself was muted — no parties, at Iger's request, and "Thank you Bob" staff badges. He told D'Amaro a week before his last day that the office was his, and pledged not to impose: "if you want advice, you call me... I'm not going to call to give you advice."

---

## Political-pressure saga (2025-26)

Disney's ownership of [[ABC]] put it in the crosshairs of the Trump administration. After [[Jimmy Kimmel]] joked about the shooting of rightwing figure [[Charlie Kirk]], Disney took Kimmel off the air and asked him to apologize; Iger said the move was about taste, not politics, and came hours after an FCC threat. When Kimmel later joked about "Lady Trump," [[Donald Trump]] demanded ABC fire him and the FCC ordered Disney into an early ABC licence-renewal process. Disney stood firm; Iger said he "wholeheartedly" endorsed the stance.

## Legacy and post-Disney

Iger considers the opening of [[Shanghai Disneyland]] among his greatest achievements and planned to attend its 10th anniversary in June 2026. He has taken an advisory role at [[Thrive Capital]] (Joshua Kushner's firm) but does not expect many more official positions. With his wife Willow he owns a women's soccer team, and he owns the 213-foot superyacht Aquarius. His license-plate holder reads: "Is there life after Disney?"

Iger told the FT the exit is a "huge emotional shift... It's just this... It's an ending," and conceded he was "not more successful in selling [Disney] to the street" — a regret he attributes to the conglomerate's complexity for investors. Parks now make up about 60 per cent of Disney's profits; Iger says Disney needs to lift streaming profitability to rebalance.
