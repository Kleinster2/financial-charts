# Michael Pettis

Michael Pettis is a [[Beijing]]-based finance professor at [[Peking University]]'s Guanghua School of Management and a senior fellow at [[Carnegie Endowment for International Peace]]. He is the leading contemporary theorist of [[China]]'s chronic underconsumption and the [[Second China shock|overcapacity]] framework that it implies. His core argument — that Chinese household consumption as a share of GDP is structurally suppressed by the financial-repression architecture, forcing excess savings into investment and then into export surplus — anchors most modern accounts of [[Global imbalances]].

## The Pettis framework

Pettis's central claim is an accounting identity with political-economy consequences: in a country with repressed household consumption, the savings side of the national accounts must flow into either domestic investment or a current-account surplus. If domestic investment already runs at unsustainable levels (as in [[China]]), then the excess must land abroad as a trade surplus — and someone else's deficit.

This makes [[China]]'s trade surplus not primarily a competitiveness story but an internal-distribution story. Rebalancing requires transferring income from local governments, state firms, and elites to households — which is politically hard and structurally slow.

Pettis extends the same frame to analyze:
- [[Germany]] and eurozone imbalances (same underconsumption logic)
- US deficits as the "reluctant absorber" of global excess savings
- Why tariffs alone cannot resolve imbalances without changing the underlying domestic-distribution arithmetic
- Why the [[Second China shock]] continues regardless of Western trade-policy response

## Why he matters to the vault

Pettis is cited as a foundational reference in [[Second China shock]], [[Adam Tooze]]'s imbalances analysis, and [[Brad Setser]]'s trade-data work. He represents the most systematic structural-accounts framework for thinking about Chinese export pressure. Whenever a vault note invokes Chinese "overcapacity" or "rebalancing failure," the Pettis framework is the implicit reference.
