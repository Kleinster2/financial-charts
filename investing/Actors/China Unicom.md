#actor #china #telecom #soe

**China Unicom** — the smallest of [[China]]'s three state-owned mobile network operators (behind [[China Mobile]] and [[China Telecom]]), with ~356mn mobile subscribers and ~1.25bn total connections. A regulated SOE pivoting from a flat connectivity business toward computing power, data centers, and AI services. Notable in this vault as the first Chinese enterprise to put data on its balance sheet under the 2024 [[Data as an asset|data-asset accounting rule]] — RMB204mn drawn from its subscriber base.

One-line read: China Unicom is the laggard of a tight, state-driven China-telecom bloc that has re-rated 2–4x since 2021 on the [[中特估|SOE value-up theme]], with the forward bet being whether the AI/computing pivot (AI revenue +140% in 2025) can turn a flat top line into a growth story before the dividend-driven re-rating exhausts itself.

## Dual-listed structure

China Unicom's corporate structure is a three-layer chain that confuses headline comparisons:

| Entity | Listing | Role |
|---|---|---|
| China United Network Communications Group Co. | unlisted (SASAC) | ultimate state parent |
| China United Network Communications Ltd | 600050.SS (Shanghai, 2002) | A-share arm; site of the 2017 mixed-ownership reform |
| China Unicom (Hong Kong) Ltd | 0762.HK (HK, 2000) | the H-share entity holding the operating telecom business |

The financial figures in this note are the consolidated operating results reported by the Hong Kong entity (0762.HK). The A-share 600050.SS reports on the same operating business but with different minority-interest accounting; the two should not be compared line-for-line. Instrument-level detail (H-share vs A-share vs the delisted ADR) lives in [[China Unicom securities note]].

*Source: company reporting via StockAnalysis. The story is margin expansion on a flat top line — revenue grew just +0.7% in 2025 while net income compounded ~9.7%/yr 2021–2025 and net margin widened from 4.38% to 5.31%.*

Two structural features:
- Net income exceeds operating income in most years (e.g., FY2025 RMB20.8bn net vs RMB15.9bn operating). The gap is below-the-line: share of profit from associates — principally the [[China Tower]] stake the three telecoms jointly own — plus interest income on a large net-cash balance. The equity is a regulated operator plus a tower annuity plus a cash pile.
- FY2025 free cash flow RMB36.0bn (+28.5% YoY) with capex falling to 16% of service revenue — the post-5G-buildout capex cliff is the source of the dividend/value-up case.

## Strategic pivot: from connectivity to computing

China Unicom's growth has migrated entirely to "strategic emerging industries," which were >86% of revenue in 2025. The connectivity base is flat; the marginal growth is computing:

| FY2025 segment | Revenue | Growth |
|---|---:|---:|
| Computing power business | >15% of total | +1.1pp share YoY |
| Data center | RMB 28.1bn | +8.5% |
| AI | n/a (disclosed as growth) | +140% |
| 5G private networks | RMB 12.3bn | +50% |
| International | RMB 13.6bn | +9% |

Intelligent computing capacity reached 45 EFLOPS; 5G-Advanced base stations are deployed in 330+ cities. This is the same pivot [[China Mobile]] and [[China Telecom]] are running — state-directed buildout of domestic AI/computing infrastructure ("[[东数西算|East Data West Compute]]"-style capacity), using the telecoms' land, power, and fiber footprint. Unicom Cloud and the "computing power network" are the vehicles. Whether AI/IDC revenue (small base, fast growth) can offset the flat connectivity core is the operating question.

## Data on the balance sheet

China Unicom was the first Chinese enterprise to recognize data as a balance-sheet asset under the Ministry of Finance's 2024 [[Data as an asset|data-resource accounting rule]] — RMB204mn (~$29mn), derived from the data generated by its ~345mn mobile subscribers. The figure is tiny relative to the company's ~RMB671bn total assets, reflecting both the novelty and the conservative application of the rule. The move is more significant as a policy signal — a flagship SOE validating the [[National Data Administration]]'s data-as-factor-of-production agenda — than as a material asset. See [[Data as an asset]] for the accounting mechanics and the China-US contrast.

## Mixed-ownership reform (2017)

China Unicom was the marquee test of China's SOE mixed-ownership reform (混合所有制改革). In August 2017, a consortium of private tech and state investors bought 35.2% of the Shanghai-listed arm (600050.SS) for ~RMB78bn (~$11.7bn):

| Investor | Stake |
|---|---:|
| China Life Insurance | 10.2% |
| [[Tencent]] | 5.2% |
| [[Baidu]] | 3.3% |
| [[JD.com]] | 2.4% |
| [[Alibaba]] | 2.0% |

The deal was held up as proof that Beijing could bring private-sector capital and discipline into a state monopoly. In practice the strategic-cooperation synergies (cloud, retail channels, content) were modest, and state control was never in question — but the capital and the cap-table signal were real, and the BATJ stakes remain.

## NYSE delisting (2021)

China Unicom's ADR (CHU) was removed from the New York Stock Exchange in 2021, alongside [[China Mobile]] (CHL) and [[China Telecom]] (CHA), under [[Executive Order 13959]] — the November 2020 Trump order barring US persons from holding securities of "Communist Chinese military companies." The NYSE reversed itself twice in early January 2021 before proceeding; clearing services (NSCC) were withdrawn as of January 11, 2021, and the formal Form 25-NSE delisting followed. The three telecoms now trade only in Hong Kong and on mainland exchanges — a clean early example of US-China capital-markets decoupling. Mechanics in [[China Unicom securities note]].

## What to watch

- AI / computing revenue mix — computing was >15% of revenue in 2025 and AI revenue grew +140%. If the computing/IDC/AI stack keeps compounding while connectivity stays flat, the equity re-rates from utility to growth. A stall here leaves only the dividend case.
- Capex discipline — capex fell to 16% of service revenue and FCF jumped +28.5%. Rising capex (a new AI-infrastructure buildout cycle) would pressure the FCF/dividend story that underpins the SOE re-rating.
- Dividend / payout policy — the [[中特估]] re-rating is dividend-driven. Payout-ratio increases sustain it; a pause breaks it.
- The bloc, not the name — given PC1 at ~78%, China Unicom mostly trades the China-telecom factor. Watch [[China Mobile]] and [[China Telecom]] policy/dividend signals as much as Unicom's own.
- Data-asset accounting — whether the RMB204mn data-asset line grows materially, and whether peers follow, signals if [[Data as an asset|the MOF rule]] is becoming real or stays symbolic.

## Analysis

China Unicom is best understood not as a standalone company but as the third member of a tightly co-moving state bloc. The cluster work is unusually clean (PC1 ~78%, durable since 2020): the three Chinese telecoms trade as one factor, and that factor is neither "global telecom" (0.05 correlation to AT&T/Verizon) nor "China beta" (0.19 to FXI/KWEB). It is its own thing — Chinese telecom regulation plus SOE dividend/value-up policy plus state ownership. For a portfolio, that means Unicom is a vehicle for a policy view, not a stock-picking opportunity; the idiosyncratic surface (it is the laggard and the highest-vol member) is small relative to the common factor.

The fundamental tension is the one every flat-growth utility pivoting to AI faces: the connectivity core does not grow (revenue +0.7% in 2025), so the entire equity story rests on (a) margin expansion and capex discipline funding a rising dividend — the realized 2021–2025 path, net margin 4.38%→5.31%, FCF +28.5% — and (b) the computing/AI stack (AI +140%, computing >15% of revenue) eventually being large enough to matter. The first is a value/income story already substantially priced by the 中特估 re-rating; the second is an option that is still small. The data-asset filing is a microcosm — RMB204mn is immaterial financially, but it is China Unicom volunteering to be the state's proof-of-concept, which is the more durable read on what this company is: an instrument of policy that happens to be listed.
