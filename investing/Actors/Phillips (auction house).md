# Phillips

Third-largest Western auction house ($927M in 2025, +10%). Focused on 20th/21st-century art, design, watches, and jewelry — younger-skewing than [[Christie's]] or [[Sotheby's]]. Owned by Mercury Group, a private investment vehicle of Russian-origin businessmen Leonid Friedland and Leonid Strunin. Founded 1796 in London.

The smallest of the Big 3 Western auction houses, Phillips occupies a deliberate niche: contemporary art, design, and watches aimed at younger collectors. At $927M in 2025 (+10%), it's a fraction of [[Christie's]] ($6.2B) or [[Sotheby's]] ($7.1B), but its trajectory tells a more interesting structural story. Private sales surged 66% to $202M (now 22% of total, up from 14%), suggesting Phillips is evolving from pure auctioneer toward a hybrid dealer-broker model — a shift that reduces auction-cycle volatility and mirrors what the larger houses are pursuing at scale.

Ownership by Mercury Group (Leonid Friedland and Leonid Strunin, Russian-born, London-based) creates an opaque capital structure with no public financials and potential sanctions/reputational overhang depending on the geopolitical environment, though neither principal has been sanctioned. Switzerland (+19%) is the strongest regional market, driven by the watches segment, while the UK (-14%) and [[Hong Kong]] (-6%) are contracting.

## Evolution

The story of Phillips is the story of a house that nearly died twice — bankrupted by a luxury conglomerate's hubris, then resurrected by Russian money and a watches bet that nobody saw coming.

- 1796-1840: Harry Phillips, a former senior clerk at [[Christie's]], opened his own auction rooms in London in 1796. He held twelve auctions in his first year and introduced elaborate evening receptions before sales — a practice the industry still follows. The house quickly attracted elite consignors: paintings from the estate of Queen Marie Antoinette, household items from Napoleon Bonaparte, sales for Beau Brummell. Phillips remains the only auction house to have held a sale inside Buckingham Palace. Harry Phillips died in 1840.

- 1840-1939: The business passed to Harry's son William Augustus Phillips, becoming Phillips & Son (1879), then Phillips, Son & Neale (1882). For nearly a century, the house operated as a mid-market London specialist — respected but not in the same league as [[Christie's]] or [[Sotheby's]]. In 1939, the New Bond Street headquarters (100+ years) was destroyed by fire; a new salesroom established across the street. Control passed out of the Phillips family in the 1930s.

- 1970s-1998: Christopher Weston acquired the business in the early 1970s and modernized, dropping "Son & Neale" to rebrand as Phillips. First expansion beyond London in nearly 200 years — New York, Sydney, Zurich. In January 1998, Weston sold his 96% stake as part of a merger with Foster & Cranfield, with venture capital firm 3i becoming majority shareholder. This put Phillips in play.

- 1999-2002: The [[LVMH]] disaster. Bernard Arnault, having failed to acquire [[Sotheby's]], bought Phillips in November 1999 to create a credible third force. In December 2000, Arnault merged Phillips with de Pury & Luxembourg, a Geneva dealership founded by Simon de Pury (former Chairman of [[Sotheby's]] Europe) and Daniella Luxembourg. The new entity tried to buy its way to relevance through aggressive financial guarantees — and went catastrophically wrong. The Heinz Berggruen collection was guaranteed ~$100M but brought $71M. The Smooke Collection carried a guarantee of at least $150M (reportedly the largest auction guarantee in history at that point) but realized just $86M. Total losses reached approximately $400M. In 2001, Arnault sold the UK regional operations to [[Bonhams]]. In 2002, he sold 70% of his remaining stake to de Pury, funded largely by the personal fortune of de Pury's partner Louise MacBain. One of Arnault's worst deals — a $400M+ lesson in why auction market share cannot be bought with guarantees alone.

- 2002-2008: Stripped to a lean contemporary art operation in New York and London, Phillips de Pury & Company operated as de Pury's personal stage — charismatic, media-savvy, but small and money-losing. Daniella Luxembourg departed in 2004. The house carved a niche in post-war and contemporary art but lacked the capital to challenge the duopoly.

- 2008: Mercury Group closes its acquisition on October 6, 2008 — three weeks after Lehman collapsed — for approximately $60M. Mercury, founded 1993 by Leonid Friedland and Leonid Strunin, is Russia's largest luxury retail company: TSUM department store in Moscow (acquired ~$60M in 2002), DLT in Saint Petersburg, Tretyakovsky Proezd. They had built early franchise rights for Dolce & Gabbana, Gucci, and Prada in the former Soviet Union. Phillips was a brand play — Western prestige to complement luxury retail. De Pury stayed as chairman; sold his remaining shares to Mercury late 2012 and stepped down. House reverted to simply Phillips in 2013.

- 2014-2021: Ed Dolman, a 27-year [[Christie's]] veteran, joined as CEO in July 2014. The most consequential strategic pivot in Phillips' 220-year history: (1) Launched Phillips in Association with Bacs & Russo (November 2014), a watch auction partnership with Aurel Bacs (formerly [[Christie's]]) that became a market-defining franchise. (2) Combined modern, post-war, and contemporary art into a single department targeting younger collectors. (3) Expanded into [[Hong Kong]] (2015), new 55K sq ft global HQ at 432 Park Avenue (2021). By 2018, annual sales had reached $916M (129% increase from 2014). In 2021, Phillips crossed $1B for the first time — $1.2B total, with watches alone generating $209M.

- 2022: Peak at $1.3B. The $85M sale of a Basquiat from collector Yusaku Maezawa became the highest-value lot in Phillips' history. But the market was peaking.

- 2023-2024: First-half 2023 sales plunged 39% from the same period in 2022. CEO Stephen Brooks (September 2021) stepped down January 2024 for personal reasons. Dolman returned as operational head.

- 2025: Recovery to $927M (+10%). The structural shift: private sales surged 66% to $202M (22% of total, up from 14%). Watches in Association with Bacs & Russo hit its highest annual auction total at $290M in its 10th anniversary year; combined with Phillips Perpetual (retail/private watches, +150% in [[Hong Kong]]) and private sales, the watches franchise generated ~$370M total. 88% sell-through rate, seven white-glove auctions, 110+ world auction records. Ed Dolman stepped down as executive chairman December 2024; Martin Wilson (art lawyer, former CLO, board member since 2019) appointed CEO.
