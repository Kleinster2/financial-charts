# Goldman Sachs Global Institute

**Goldman Sachs Global Institute** - [[Goldman Sachs]] client-facing advisory, convening, and insights platform focused on the intersection of geopolitics, technology, and global markets.

The deeper story is that Goldman is turning geopolitics into a repeatable senior-client product. The Institute is not just research or public affairs; it is a way to help CEOs, boards, sovereign investors, and sponsors make capital-allocation decisions when AI, war risk, state-directed capital, sanctions, energy constraints, and supply-chain chokepoints all collide.

The Cohen/Lee pairing is the core design. [[Jared Cohen]] supplies the statecraft frame: diplomacy, information networks, sovereign power, and the political logic of technology. [[George Lee (Goldman Sachs)]] supplies the Goldman-native translation layer: TMT banking, internal technology execution, AI capex, enterprise adoption, and what those issues mean for clients, financing, deals, and market structure.

## Why it matters

The institute is Goldman's clearest institutional sign that geopolitics has become a client product. It is not just research; it is a senior-client platform for boards, sovereigns, executives, and investors trying to make decisions when AI, state capital, supply-chain fragmentation, and military conflict all affect where capital can move.

The difference from normal sell-side research is audience and actionability. A research note can tell investors what happened; the Institute is built for clients asking what to build, where to finance, whom to partner with, what risks can be insured or hedged, and where politics changes the permissible map of capital. That makes it closer to a hybrid of strategy consulting, investment banking, and geopolitical intelligence than to a standard research vertical.

Goldman has three assets that make this credible:

| Asset | Why it matters |
|-------|----------------|
| Client franchise | Access to CEOs, boards, sovereigns, asset owners, and sponsors |
| Markets business | Ability to translate shock risk into funding, hedging, liquidity, and positioning |
| Banking franchise | Ability to turn the same themes into M&A, financing, capital raising, and partnerships |

The Institute matters when it converts a theme from "macro risk" into "transaction or allocation question." AI power availability, critical minerals, stablecoin dollarization, Gulf sovereign capital, and sanctions exposure all become monetizable Goldman conversations once clients need advice, financing, or counterparties.

## Operating model

Goldman presents the Institute around three verbs: insights, partnerships, and convening. That phrasing matters because it describes a business model, not just a content shop.

| Format | What it does | Goldman readthrough |
|--------|--------------|--------------------|
| Insights | Frames inflection points in AI, capital, conflict, and markets | Creates the vocabulary clients use before calling bankers or markets desks |
| Partnerships | Helps clients solve cross-border, technology, or policy-linked problems | Turns geopolitical complexity into advisory mandates and strategic relationships |
| Convening | Brings executives, policymakers, investors, and experts together | Gives Goldman privileged demand sensing across public and private power centers |

The convening layer is especially important. The public writeups show Goldman assembling prime ministers, intelligence leaders, former diplomats, executives, policy specialists, and market participants. That is not just branding. It is a relationship engine for clients who need to understand where governments, capital providers, and technology operators are actually moving.

## Core story

### 1. Geopolitics moved from background risk to capital allocation

For most of the post-Cold War period, corporate strategy treated geopolitics as an exception: sanctions, wars, tariffs, or elections mattered, but they were often handled as one-off disruptions around a globalization baseline. The Institute is built for the opposite world. Politics is now part of the base case for where factories, data centers, payment rails, chips, ports, and capital pools can exist.

This is why the same platform covers [[AI Race]], [[Critical minerals]], [[Stablecoins]], [[Trade]], [[Russia]] / [[Ukraine]], [[Iran]], and Gulf sovereign capital. They look like different topics, but they all answer the same client question: where can capital still move, scale, and compound when governments are actively shaping the rules?

### 2. AI is the wedge product

AI is the cleanest wedge because it forces every Institute theme into one problem. It is a technology race, an energy and data-center buildout, a semiconductor supply-chain issue, a national-security problem, a productivity debate, and a financing opportunity. Cohen frames AI as power and diplomacy; Lee frames it as capex, costs, adoption, and ROI.

That combination is why Goldman's AI work keeps returning to power, chips, data, models, enterprise adoption, and sovereign capacity. The question is not simply which model is best. It is which countries and companies can assemble the full stack: capital, energy, compute, customers, rules, and trusted partners.

### 3. State capital is becoming a protagonist

The "instrumental capital" frame from Cohen and Lee is a big tell. It says state-directed capital is not just another funding source; it is a tool of state power. [[Sovereign Wealth Funds]], national champions, government-backed investors, and public-private capital are now part of strategic competition.

That maps directly onto the vault's [[Saudi PIF]], [[QIA]], [[UAE]], and [[Qatar]] work. Gulf capital can be financial return, AI infrastructure funding, energy strategy, diplomatic leverage, sports/media soft power, and economic diversification all at once. The Institute gives Goldman a language for advising both the sovereign capital itself and the companies trying to partner with it.

### 4. Chokepoints are becoming the new risk map

The 2026 Global Institute dialogues on economic chokepoints and [[Critical minerals]] show the platform moving into the infrastructure of coercion. The important question is no longer just "what country has GDP growth?" but "who controls the scarce node that everyone else needs?"

Those nodes can be physical, like [[Rare earths|rare-earth]] processing and permanent magnets; digital, like cloud services and models; financial, like dollar settlement and stablecoin rails; or geographic, like cables and energy routes. For Goldman clients, chokepoint analysis becomes a practical checklist: supplier exposure, sanctions exposure, insurance costs, capital access, inventory strategy, and where new redundancy needs financing.

### 5. Convening is intelligence gathering by another name

The public summit writeups are useful because they show Goldman building a listening post. A room with heads of state, intelligence figures, diplomats, CEOs, investors, and technology executives is not just a panel series. It is a way to learn what powerful actors are worried about before those worries become consensus.

That matters for Goldman commercially. The first bank to understand that a sovereign wants AI infrastructure, a board needs [[China]] redundancy, or an asset owner wants exposure to a supply-chain bottleneck can turn that into advisory work, financing, markets flow, or long-term relationship depth.

## Goldman readthrough

For [[Goldman Sachs]], the Institute is not likely to show up as a standalone revenue line. Its value is more subtle: it protects and expands the senior-client franchise at a time when the hardest client questions are no longer purely financial. A client asking about AI infrastructure may need financing, power sourcing, partners, M&A advice, hedges, and geopolitical judgment. A sovereign wealth fund may need deployment ideas, Western counterparties, and legitimacy. A board may need to decide whether [[China]] exposure is a margin advantage or a future liability.

The commercial logic is that the Institute gets Goldman into the room earlier. If the firm helps define the problem, it has a better chance of shaping the transaction, financing, hedge, or partnership that follows.

The risk is that the Institute becomes branding without enough mandate conversion. The signal to watch is whether Global Institute themes keep appearing inside concrete Goldman activity: AI infrastructure financing, Gulf sovereign deals, cross-border restructuring, supply-chain advisory, risk hedging, and board-level strategic mandates.
