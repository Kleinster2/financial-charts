**Petrobras** — [[Brazil]]'s state-controlled oil giant and Latin America's largest company. World-class pre-salt deepwater assets, top-5 global oil producer, perennial tension between shareholder returns and political interference.

## Synopsis

Petrobras produces 2.7M boe/d (81% from pre-salt), holds 11.4B boe in proven reserves (13.2-year life, 154% replacement ratio in 2024), and generated US$23.3B in free cash flow on US$38B operating cash flow in FY2024. Pre-salt lifting costs of ~$6.50/boe make it one of the cheapest barrels on Earth — competitive with [[Saudi Aramco]] ($3.50) and far below US shale (~$8-9) or Canadian oil sands (~$23-25). The stock (PBR) trades at 7.4x forward earnings with a ~5.9% dividend yield — among the highest of any major oil company globally.

The investment case is straightforward: world-class reserves, low-cost production, massive FCF, high yield. The risk is equally straightforward: the Brazilian government owns 37% of total capital (50.26% of voting shares) and has a documented history of destroying shareholder value through fuel price controls, dividend raids, and management churn. The Dilma era (2011-2016) cost the refining division R$59B+ in below-market fuel sales and turned Petrobras into the world's most indebted oil company. [[Lava Jato]] then exposed US$2B+ in kickbacks, triggering a 90% stock collapse and US$3B in class-action settlements. The recovery since 2016 — PPI pricing, debt reduction, pre-salt ramp — vindicated the asset base, but each election cycle reopens the question of whether the government will again sacrifice the balance sheet for politics. The current test: [[Brazil oil shock trilemma March 2026|the March 2026 oil shock]], where Brent >$100 and no transparent pricing rule put Petrobras at the center of a fiscal windfall vs. inflation conundrum.

## Evolution

The story of Petrobras is the story of the best rocks in the Western Hemisphere trapped inside the worst governance structure — and whether the assets can generate enough cash to survive the politics.

- 1953: President Getúlio Vargas created Petrobras as a state monopoly under the "O petróleo é nosso" (the oil is ours) nationalist campaign. The monopoly held for 44 years. For decades, Petrobras was a competent but unremarkable national oil company producing modest volumes from mature onshore and shallow-water fields.

- 1997: The Petroleum Law (9478/97) broke the state monopoly, opened [[Brazil]]'s upstream to foreign companies, and created the ANP (National Petroleum Agency) as regulator. Petrobras retained preferential access to existing production but now had to compete for new blocks. This set the stage for the deepwater exploration push.

- 2006-2007: Geologists confirmed massive sub-salt carbonate reservoirs in the Santos Basin — the pre-salt. Tupi (later renamed Lula), discovered at the Tupi prospect in 2006, held an estimated 8B boe in place, the largest deepwater find in the Western Hemisphere. The geology was extraordinary: Cretaceous-age lacustrine microbialites trapped beneath 2,000+ meters of salt, in 2,000+ meters of water depth. Total depth to reservoir: 5,000-7,000m. Nobody had drilled through this much salt before. Petrobras had.

- 2008-2010: The pre-salt discovery triggered euphoria. PBR hit an all-time high of ~US$72+ per share (pre-split US$18.64 split-adjusted, May 21, 2008). The government designed a new regulatory framework: production-sharing contracts for pre-salt, with Petrobras as mandatory operator (minimum 30% stake). In 2010, Petrobras conducted the largest share offering in history — US$70B — to fund the pre-salt ramp. The dilution was massive, but the assets justified the ambition.

- 2011-2014 (Dilma era — the destruction): President [[Dilma Rousseff]] forced Petrobras to import refined products and sell domestically below international prices to suppress inflation. The refining division lost over R$59B (~US$24B). Petrobras became the most indebted oil company in the world. Simultaneously, Dilma's allies were running the biggest corruption scheme in Latin American history through Petrobras' infrastructure contracts — kickbacks of 1-5% on inflated costs, funneled to politicians. Total documented bribes: US$2B+. Total write-downs: US$17B. The stock began a five-year, 90% collapse.

- 2014-2016 ([[Lava Jato]]): Operation Car Wash, launched March 2014, exposed the full scale. 361 convictions. Petrobras settled with the DOJ/SEC for US$853.2M and with shareholders for US$3B (the largest US class-action settlement against a foreign issuer, 5th largest all-time). PBR hit a trough of ~US$2-3 in early 2016. Dilma was impeached in August 2016 — not directly for Petrobras, but the corruption scandal destroyed her political base.

- 2016-2019 (recovery): President Temer appointed Pedro Parente as CEO, who introduced PPI (Preço de Paridade Internacional) in October 2016 — import parity pricing that ended the subsidy regime. Financial discipline returned. Debt began declining. Pre-salt production ramped aggressively. Under Bolsonaro (2019-2022), the dividend policy was formalized and generous: up to 60% of operating cash flow minus capex. Petrobras went from financial basket case to the highest-yielding major oil company in the world.

- 2023-2024 (Lula III — the test): Lula returned and immediately abolished PPI (May 16, 2023), replacing it with an opaque "commercial strategy" using domestic market references. Gasoline fell 17.5%, diesel 27.2% — partly oil-driven, partly below-parity pricing. First CEO Jean Paul Prates was fired in May 2024 after Lula accused him of paying too much in dividends instead of investing. [[Magda Chambriard]] replaced him — a career Petrobras engineer and former ANP director, signaling a shift toward operational investment. The dividend formula was cut from 60% to 45% of FCF. Despite the policy uncertainty, the stock surged +58% YTD in early 2026 as pre-salt production hit records and the Hormuz crisis drove oil above $100.

- 2025-2029 (current strategic plan): US$111B total capex — US$77.3B in E&P, US$19.6B in refining (+17% vs prior plan), US$16.3B in energy transition (+42%). Production target: 3.2M boe/d. 10 new FPSOs by 2029. Refining capacity from 1.813M to 2.105M bbl/d. The plan assumes US$28/bbl Brent breakeven and projects US$45-55B in ordinary dividends over five years.

## Pre-salt assets

The pre-salt province — Santos and Campos Basins — is the crown jewel. Sub-salt carbonate reservoirs (lacustrine microbialites) trapped beneath 2,000m of salt in 2,000m water depth. Lifting costs of ~$6.50/boe put pre-salt among the cheapest producing barrels globally.

| Field | Partners | Production (2024) | Capacity target |
|-------|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Búzios (formerly Franco) | Petrobras 92.6%, [[CNOOC]] 3.7%, CNODC 3.7% | 800,000+ bbl/d (record) | 1.5M bbl/d by 2030 (11 FPSOs) |
| Tupi/Lula | Petrobras 65%, [[Shell]] 25%, Petrogal 10% | Large (original 2006 discovery) | Mature, stable |
| Mero (Libra block) | Production sharing | ~650,000 bbl/d | 4 FPSOs total |
| Sapinhoá | Petrobras 45%, Shell 30%, Repsol Sinopec 25% | — | — |
| Sépia | Petrobras + partners | Producing since 2021 | P-85 FPSO late 2029 (225,000 bbl/d) |

Pre-salt went from first oil (2008-2009) to 2.2M boe/d own production and 3.2M boe/d operated by 2024 — 81% of Petrobras total. Búzios alone produces more than many national oil companies.

| Era | Policy | Impact |
|-----|--------|--------|
| Pre-2002 | Government-set prices | Chronic underinvestment in refining |
| 2002-2010 | Informal parity, periodic adjustments | Manageable, pre-salt discovery era |
| 2011-2014 (Dilma) | Forced below-market sales | R$59B+ refining losses, world's most indebted oil co |

The current absence of a transparent pricing rule is the core investor uncertainty. Each oil spike reopens the question of whether the government will force below-market domestic prices. See [[Brazil oil shock trilemma March 2026]] for the live test.

## Oil shock pricing conundrum (March 2026)

Brent breaching $100 on the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran conflict]] and [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure put Petrobras at the center of a trilemma. Diesel defasagem already massive — same city showing R$9/liter vs R$5/liter at the pump. No transparent pricing rule exists since PPI was abolished.

If Petrobras holds prices below international parity: importers stop importing, private refineries cut production, market disorganizes. If Petrobras passes through: inflation spike in an election year, [[Banco Central do Brasil|BCB]] holds [[Selic]] at 15%.

[[BTG Pactual]] estimates the fiscal windfall at R$55B (R$45B taxes/royalties + R$10B Petrobras dividends to Treasury) at BRL 5.20/USD and Brent $100 — enough to zero [[Brazil]]'s primary deficit. But that windfall requires pass-through, which triggers the inflation the government wants to avoid.

[[Sidônio Palmeira]], the government's marketing secretary, is emerging as de facto arbiter of the pricing decision — electoral calculus overriding technocratic policy. Panelists explicitly warned of a Dilma-era rerun.

See [[Brazil oil shock trilemma March 2026]] for the full analysis.

## Investment case

Bull:
- Pre-salt = low-cost (~$6.50/boe), long-life (13.2 years), high-quality reserves with 154% replacement
- US$23.3B FCF on US$38B operating cash flow (FY2024)
- ~5.9% dividend yield, highest among major oil companies
- Production growth trajectory: 2.7M boe/d → 3.2M target by 2029
- Gross financial debt at US$23.2B — lowest since 2008
- Breakeven at US$28/bbl Brent — resilient across oil price scenarios

Bear:
- Government owns 50.26% of voting shares — pricing, dividends, capex all subject to political interference
- No transparent fuel pricing rule since PPI abolished (May 2023) — each oil spike reopens subsidy risk
- CEO turnover with political cycles — Prates fired after 16 months for being too shareholder-friendly
- Election year 2026 increases probability of below-market pricing
- BRL currency risk on USD-denominated earnings
- [[Lava Jato]] governance scars — institutional checks improved but not tested under sustained political pressure
- Capex ramp (+37% YoY) could compress FCF if projects underperform
