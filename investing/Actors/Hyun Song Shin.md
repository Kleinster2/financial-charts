#actor #economist #policy

**Hyun Song Shin** (Korean: 신현송, b. 1959) — South Korean economist and 2026 governor of the [[Bank of Korea]], after serving as Economic Adviser and Head of Research at the [[Bank for International Settlements]]. Leading thinker on financial stability, global banking, and endogenous risk.

## Bank of Korea governorship

Shin's first major 2026 policy signal as governor was a hawkish hold. On May 28, the [[Bank of Korea]] kept the policy rate at 2.50%, but Shin framed future increases as likely once the board had more data on inflation pressure, domestic recovery, and financial-stability risks. The macro bridge matched his research background: Korea was absorbing an external energy shock from the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]], but the domestic growth impulse from the AI-memory export cycle was larger.

The numbers matter because they turn the Korea equity story into central-bank arithmetic. The BoK's May 2026 outlook put the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran-war]] drag at -0.4 percentage points of GDP, versus +0.7 percentage points from stronger semiconductor and IT exports, plus +0.2 from the supplementary budget and +0.1 from stock-market effects. That is the policy version of the same tape seen in [[Kospi]] and [[Korea Memory]]: AI earnings duration is offsetting a near-term imported-energy shock, while inflation, [[Korean won]] volatility, Seoul-area housing, and household debt keep the rate path biased upward.

*Sources: [[Financial Times|FT]], "AI boom outweighs Iran war pain, Korean central bank chief says," May 28 2026: https://www.ft.com/content/2d1c6dbb-aef5-4f08-bc5a-20c0aa3c1149; Bank of Korea, "Monetary Policy Decision & Opening Remarks to the Press Conference (May 28, 2026)": https://www.bok.or.kr/eng/bbs/E0000634/view.do?depth=400069&menuNo=400069&nttId=10098190&oldMenuNo=400007&programType=newsDataEng&relate=Y.*

## Key concepts

Endogenous risk: Market volatility isn't just external shocks — it's generated by participants' own behavior. When everyone de-risks simultaneously, fire sales create the very crash they're trying to avoid.

Global dollar banking: Even non-US banks depend heavily on USD funding. Dollar strength → tighter global credit conditions, regardless of local central bank policy.

Dollar as credit supply (not just competitiveness): Traditional view says strong dollar → cheaper imports. Shin shows strong dollar → tight dollar credit → [[Global Value Chains|GVCs]] contract. The credit channel dominates competitiveness, especially for trade between non-US countries.

## International commerce research

Shin's work reframes global trade as a financial phenomenon. Key insight: ~80% of trade finance is dollar-denominated, so GVC length depends on dollar credit conditions.

| Paper | Co-authors | Finding |
|-------|------------|---------|
| Theory of supply chains (WP 1070, 2023) | Se-Jik Kim | GVC length = f(financing costs). Easy credit → longer chains; tight credit → reshoring |
| Dollar and Exports (WP 819, 2019) | Valentina Bruno | Exporters reliant on dollar credit suffer when dollar strengthens — opposite of textbook |
| Exchange rates and working capital channel (WP 694, 2018) | Bruno, Kim | Trade finance 80% USD. Strong dollar tightens this credit globally |
| Dollar invoicing and GVCs (WP 860, 2020) | Cook, Patel | Dollar appreciation hits direct bilateral trade harder than GVC trade serving US demand |
| Mapping GVC realignment (Bulletin 78, 2023) | Qiu, Zhang | Post-COVID supply chain restructuring patterns |

Implication: The broad dollar index is a proxy for global financial conditions. Fed policy transmits to world trade through the working capital channel, not just exchange rates.
