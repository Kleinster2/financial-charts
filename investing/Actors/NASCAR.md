# NASCAR

Premier US stock car racing series and the dominant American motorsport franchise, with ~$425M annual revenue and ~2.8M average TV viewers per race. Still leads [[Formula 1]] in US television ratings but declining from its 2000s peak while F1 grows aggressively in the American market under [[Liberty Media]]'s ownership.

## Synopsis

NASCAR generates revenue through a combination of media rights, race sanctioning fees, licensing, and track operations (the organization owns several major speedways through its subsidiary International Speedway Corporation, merged back into NASCAR in 2019). The current media rights deal with [[Fox]] and NBC runs through 2024, with a new $7.7B, seven-year deal (2025-2031) split across Fox, NBC, [[Amazon]] (Prime Video), and [[Warner Bros. Discovery]] (TNT). The Amazon and Warner additions reflect NASCAR's push to reach younger audiences beyond its traditional cable TV demographic, while the total deal value ($1.1B/year) represents a significant step up from the prior ~$820M/year arrangement.

The investment relevance is as a [[Liberty Media]] peer comparison and a case study in sports-media economics. NASCAR's challenge is structural: its fanbase skews older, whiter, and more geographically concentrated (Southeast US) than F1's rapidly diversifying global audience. Average viewership peaked at ~8M in the mid-2000s and has declined to ~2.8M, while [[Formula 1]] US viewership has surged from ~500K to 1.2M+ since Liberty Media's acquisition. The France family (which has owned NASCAR since its founding by Bill France Sr. in 1948) explored a partial sale in 2023, reportedly engaging bankers to value the business at $15-20B, but no deal materialized. The question is whether the new media deal stabilizes the franchise's trajectory or merely prices in a declining asset at peak media-rights inflation.
