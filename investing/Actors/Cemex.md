**Cemex** is the world's third-largest cement producer by installed capacity, operating in 50+ countries with a strategic tilt toward infrastructure-heavy emerging markets.

## Synopsis

Cemex built its global empire through aggressive M&A in the 1990s-2000s — [[Southdown]] ($2.8B, 2000), RMC ($5.8B, 2005), [[Rinker]] ($14.2B, 2007) — becoming the world's largest building materials company by revenue at its peak. The [[Rinker]] deal, closed months before the 2008 financial crisis, nearly killed the company: net debt hit $23B, the stock fell 95% from its 2007 high, and Cemex spent the next decade in survival mode — asset sales, debt-for-equity swaps, and operational austerity. The balance sheet repair has been the defining corporate narrative. Net debt fell from $23B (2008) to ~$4.5B by 2025, and the company regained investment-grade rating from [[Fitch]] in 2024 for the first time in 15 years.

The investment case now centers on nearshoring demand in [[Mexico]] and the $1.2T [[IIJA]] infrastructure bill in the [[United States]]. Cemex is the largest cement producer in Mexico and a top-five producer in the US — both markets are seeing structural demand growth from reshoring (semiconductor fabs, battery plants, logistics hubs) and federal infrastructure spending. The risk is execution: Cemex has historically been a better acquirer than operator, and margins still trail [[Holcim]] and [[HeidelbergCement]]. The company's Operation Resilience restructuring has improved EBITDA margins to ~20%, but capital allocation discipline remains the open question.
