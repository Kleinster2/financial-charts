## Synopsis

Bally's is a leveraged bet on US casino expansion, assembled through an acquisition spree and renamed from the unglamorous Twin River Worldwide Holdings to capture the iconic Bally's brand (bought from [[Caesars Entertainment]] for just $20M in 2020). [[Standard General]]'s Soo Kim took 74% control in February 2025 via a $4.6B deal ($18.25/share, 71% premium), simultaneously merging in The Queen Casino & Entertainment. The company then sold its international interactive business (Jackpotjoy, Virgin Games, Monopoly Casino) to [[Intralot]] for EUR 2.7B ($3.12B), receiving $1.77B cash plus 58% of Intralot's equity. At least $1B of after-tax proceeds went to debt reduction.

The debt is staggering — ~$6.7B total, 11.3x debt/EBITDA — making Bally's the most leveraged major US casino operator. The bet is that three development projects will justify the balance sheet: Bally's Bronx (NYC casino license, $4B, won December 2025), Bally's Chicago (permanent casino, $1.7B, targeting late 2026-2027), and Bally's Las Vegas (new integrated resort on the Tropicana site adjacent to the Oakland A's new ballpark, $1.19B, targeting 2030). Each is a multi-year, capital-intensive build. If all three execute, Bally's transforms from a regional operator into a major multi-market casino company. If any stumbles, the leverage becomes existential.

The flagship remains Bally's Lincoln (Rhode Island) — 190 acres, 165,000 sq ft casino, 3,900 slots, $490M+ GGR in 2025. It's the reliable cash engine funding everything else.

## Evolution

The story of Bally's Corporation is the story of a regional casino operator that decided to grow by acquisition, borrowed aggressively to do it, and is now racing to build out three major projects before the debt catches up.

- **2004-2019**: Originally founded as BLB Investors by [[Starwood Capital]], [[Kerzner International]], and Waterford Group. Evolved through Twin River Management Group into Twin River Worldwide Holdings. Operated a small portfolio of New England casino properties — reliable, unglamorous, cash-generative.

- **2020**: The reinvention. Acquired the Bally's brand and Bally's Atlantic City from [[Caesars Entertainment]] for just $45M total ($20M for the brand, $25M for the property). Renamed to Bally's Corporation, ticker changed to BALY (November 2020). The Bally's name — once one of the most recognizable in casino gaming — had been parked as a secondary asset inside Caesars. Twin River saw the arbitrage.

- **2021**: The acquisition spree began. Bought MontBleu (Nevada), Jumer's Casino (Illinois), and Tropicana Evansville (Indiana) for a combined $275M. Then the big one: acquired [[Gamesys Group]] (UK online gaming — Jackpotjoy, Virgin Games, Monopoly Casino, Rainbow Riches Casino) for $2.7B. This was supposed to be the digital transformation play, giving Bally's a major international iGaming platform. It added enormous debt instead.

- **2022**: Acquired the Tropicana Las Vegas for $148M from [[Gaming and Leisure Properties]] (GLPI) and [[Penn Entertainment]] — buying the operations of one of the Strip's most storied properties. The plan: demolish it and build a new integrated resort.

- **2023-2024**: Bally's Chicago temporary casino opened at Medinah Temple (September 9, 2023, $50M renovation) — 1.3M visitors in the first year. Standard General began its takeover bid.

- **February 2025**: Standard General deal closed. Soo Kim took 74% controlling stake at $18.25/share ($4.6B total deal including Queen Casino merger). Financed with $500M senior secured notes from [[Apollo]]. Bally's remains publicly listed.

- **October 2025**: Sold International Interactive business (UK brands) to [[Intralot]] for EUR 2.7B ($3.12B). Received $1.77B cash + $1.31B in Intralot shares (58% ownership of Intralot). At least $1B allocated to debt reduction.

- **December 2025**: Won one of three NYC downstate casino licenses for the Bronx (Ferry Point Park, 15-year license). Joined [[Resorts World NYC]] and [[Metropolitan Park]] as the three winners. Manhattan bids ([[SL Green]]/[[Caesars Entertainment]], [[Silverstein Properties]], [[Soloviev Group]]) all failed community advisory committee votes.
