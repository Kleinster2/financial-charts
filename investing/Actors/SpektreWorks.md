# SpektreWorks

**SpektreWorks** is a Phoenix-based drone startup that reverse-engineered [[Iran]]'s [[Shahed-136]] into the [[LUCAS]] one-way attack drone — the first US combat deployment of a long-range loitering munition, used in [[Operation Epic Fury]] on February 28, 2026.

---

## Synopsis

SpektreWorks went from a ~$2M/year engineering services firm to the manufacturer of the most consequential new US weapon system in a generation. The company reverse-engineered a captured [[Shahed-136]] into the FLM-136 target drone, then weaponized it into [[LUCAS]] — a $35,000 one-way attack drone that went from public unveiling to combat use in 43 weeks. That timeline is the story: 18 months from concept to deployment vs. the Pentagon's typical 5-10 year acquisition cycle. The vehicle was the APFIT rapid procurement pathway and [[Pete Hegseth]]'s Drone Dominance directive.

The catch is that SpektreWorks doesn't own what it built. The US government holds the [[LUCAS]] design IP, deliberately structured for multi-vendor production across up to 20 manufacturers simultaneously — the Liberty Ship model applied to drones. SpektreWorks holds the first $30M production contract and the first-mover credibility, but the architecture is open. The company has 5-15 employees and raised only $109K in disclosed funding. Whether SpektreWorks becomes a major defense contractor or gets absorbed by the production ecosystem it enabled depends on whether first-mover advantage translates into manufacturing scale before larger competitors gear up.

## Evolution

The story of SpektreWorks is the story of three drone engineers who reverse-engineered a captured enemy weapon and got it into combat faster than the Pentagon thought possible.

- 2015: Daniel Laks leaves [[3D Robotics]] after seven months absorbing commercial drone autonomy. He'd spent the prior seven years at [[Orbital Sciences]] building satellite guidance systems — the combination of mil-spec GN&C with commercial drone agility becomes SpektreWorks' founding DNA. Laks, Brad Golding (also ex-3D Robotics, composites specialist), and Jeremy Wagoner incorporate in Arizona on July 16, 2015. The company starts as an engineering services firm — revenue stays at ~$2M/year. They build target drones and UAS subsystems, invisible to the defense establishment

- Pre-2025 (exact date unclear): The US captures a [[Shahed-136]] — battlefield recoveries from [[Ukraine war|Ukraine]] and Middle East interceptions provide the airframes. SpektreWorks reverse-engineers it. Laks' autopilot background lets the team replicate and improve the guidance stack; Golding's composites expertise replicates the airframe at 59% of the weight (81.5 kg vs 200 kg). The result is the FLM-136, marketed as a "Flexible Loitering Munition" for "authentic threat emulation" — a training target that faithfully mimics the Shahed for counter-UAS exercises. The Pentagon now has a domestically produced Shahed analog

- June-July 2025 (the acceleration): President Trump signs Executive Order 14307, "Unleashing American Drone Dominance" on June 6. Defense Secretary [[Pete Hegseth]] follows with the "Unleashing U.S. Military Drone Dominance" memorandum on July 10, reclassifying Group 1/2 UAS as "consumable commodities" instead of durable property — a procurement framework change that lets the Pentagon buy drones like ammunition. Six days later, Hegseth hosts SpektreWorks and 12+ other drone companies in the Pentagon courtyard. SpektreWorks publicly debuts [[LUCAS]]. Eight days after the Hegseth memo, the company receives a $30M APFIT contract through Army Contracting Command — their first major defense award

- December 2025 (deployment): CENTCOM announces [[LUCAS]] deployment to the Middle East on December 3, under Task Force Scorpion Strike — a first-of-its-kind one-way attack drone squadron under US Special Operations Command-Central. On December 16, the USS Santa Barbara (LCS-32) conducts the first-ever shipborne [[LUCAS]] launch in the Persian Gulf (Task Force 59). The drone is now operational across ground and sea launch platforms

- February 28, 2026 (combat): CENTCOM confirms the first official combat use of [[LUCAS]] during [[Operation Epic Fury]] against [[Iran]]. Task Force Scorpion Strike launches ground-based LUCAS against IRGC command/control nodes, air defense assets, missile/drone launch sites, and Shahed manufacturing sites. Public debut to combat: 43 weeks. Concept to deployment: ~18 months. Admiral Brad Cooper calls LUCAS "indispensable" (March 5)

- March 2026 (scaling question): Owen West, Senior Advisor for Drone Dominance and DIU director, testifies before the Senate Armed Services Committee: "Just a few days ago, America used one-way attack drones for the first time in combat in Iran, with Task Force Scorpion Strike firing the LUCAS drone, a reverse-engineered Shahed-136." The government owns the LUCAS design IP. The plan is multi-vendor production across up to 20 manufacturers — SpektreWorks is currently the sole producer, but the architecture is deliberately open. Production capacity is the binding constraint: a 5-15 person company that just went from $2M revenue to a $30M contract. Whether SpektreWorks scales to meet the moment or gets left behind as larger manufacturers adopt the design is the open question

## Drone Dominance Program context

[[LUCAS]] is the flagship system of the Pentagon's Drone Dominance Program (DDP):

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Total program | $1.1B, four-phase framework |
| Target | 200,000+ one-way attack drones fielded by Jan 2028 (300,000-340,000 total drones) |
| Phase I (Gauntlet I) | $150M for 30,000 FPV drones — different class than LUCAS |
| LUCAS position | First $30M production award under DDP. Larger, longer-range than Gauntlet FPV systems |
| IP model | Government-owned design, multi-vendor production (up to 20 simultaneous manufacturers) |
| Program lead | Owen West, Senior Advisor for Drone Dominance / DIU director |
| Production target | 100 units/day (aspirational; current throughput unknown) |

SpektreWorks won the production contract, but the DDP is designed so that SpektreWorks is not necessary for the production to scale. Col. Nicholas Law (Director of Experimentation, OSD R&E): "We want to produce a lot of these in a rapid fashion. It's not a single manufacturer: it's designed to go to multiple manufacturers to be built in mass quantities."

## Controversy

[[Iran]]'s FM Abbas Araghchi claimed (March 15, 2026) that LUCAS drones are designed to "fully mimic" the [[Shahed-136]] for false-flag attacks on Arab nations to frame Iran. Ebrahim Zolfaghari (Khatam al-Anbiya Central HQ) called it a "diabolical plot." The visual similarity is by design — it's a reverse-engineered copy — but the false-flag narrative plays on that resemblance.

Senator Mark Kelly (AZ) raised AI autonomy concerns at the March 5 SASC hearing — no statutory framework governs AI in lethal military operations. Maj. Gen. Steven Marks deflected to a classified briefing.
