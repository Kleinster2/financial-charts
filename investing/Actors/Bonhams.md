# Bonhams

Fourth-largest global auction house ($970M in 2025, -2%). Acquisitive under PE ownership — bought Skinner (US, 2022), Bukowskis (Nordics), Cornette de Saint Cyr (France). Operating in 24 countries. Owned by Pemberton Asset Management (UK private credit) since October 2025, after a debt-for-equity swap from Epiris (PE, owned 2018-2025). Epiris pursued a buy-and-build strategy — Skinner (US), Bukowskis (Nordics), Bruun Rasmussen (Denmark), Cornette de Saint Cyr (France) — fueled by GBP 207M in Pemberton debt. The ~$1B exit never materialized; the lender took the keys. Founded 1793 in London.

Bonhams is a case study in PE-fueled auction house consolidation — and what happens when the exit doesn't come. Epiris (formerly Electra Partners) acquired Bonhams in 2018 and executed four acquisitions in 2022, expanding to 24 countries and 14 salerooms. Sales crossed $1B in 2022 and peaked at $1.14B in 2023. But JPMorgan's sale process at ~$1B collapsed as the art market turned. Vantage Bidco (the holding company) posted a GBP 213M pre-tax loss in 2024, including GBP 153M in impairment charges. In October 2025, Pemberton converted its GBP 207.3M debt into equity and took control.

The question now is whether the geographic scale built through acquisitions — 24 countries, broader reach than [[Phillips (auction house)|Phillips]] despite similar revenue ($970M vs $927M) — is an asset or a liability under new ownership. [[Hong Kong]]'s performance (+21% combined, best since 2007 launch) runs counter to the contraction narrative at other houses. Pemberton is not a natural long-term owner; another exit (likely trade sale) is expected.

## Evolution

The story of Bonhams is the story of a Georgian-era London auction house that survived two centuries of quiet family stewardship, then underwent more ownership changes in seven years than in its prior 225 — a PE-fueled acquisition spree that briefly vaulted it past $1B in sales before debt mechanics handed it to its own lender.

- 1793-1850s: Thomas Dodd, a print dealer and engraver, established the business in 1793 at Tavistock Street, Covent Garden, specializing in books and prints alongside Walter Bonham. Dodd went bankrupt in 1817; the firm passed to his apprentice George Jones. Jones partnered with George Bonham in the 1850s, broadening into fine art, furniture, porcelain, jewelry, arms, armor, and wine. By 1879 the firm had evolved into Messrs W & FC Bonham.

- 1956: Leonard Bonham built a purpose-built saleroom on Montpelier Street, Knightsbridge. In 1958, opened additional premises on Lots Road, Chelsea, for furniture and household sales (400-500 lots/week). Dual-site model: Knightsbridge for higher-end, Chelsea for volume.

- 1989-2000: Robert Brooks — youngest-ever auctioneer at [[Christie's]] (director at 27, main board at 30) — left in 1989 to found Brooks, a specialist motor car auction house backed by Dutch collector Evert Louwman. In 2000, Brooks acquired family-owned Bonhams and renamed it Bonhams & Brooks. A motor car specialist swallowing a generalist house — unusual in auction M&A, but it gave Brooks a full-category platform and Bonhams capital it had lacked.

- 2001: Bonhams & Brooks merged with Phillips Son & Neale's UK regional operations — the pieces [[LVMH]] shed after its disastrous Phillips acquisition. The deal gave Bonhams a 50.1% stake (with Louwman), [[LVMH]] retained 49.9%. Crucially, it came with Phillips' prestigious 101 New Bond Street headquarters. Renamed simply Bonhams. By 2003: 700+ annual sales generating $304M in revenue.

- 2002: Acquired Butterfields (California) from eBay for $21.8M — a fraction of the $260M eBay paid for it in 1999. Funded entirely from own resources. Salerooms in San Francisco and Los Angeles; New York followed 2005 (Madison Avenue 2008), [[Hong Kong]] 2007.

- 2013: Invested GBP 30M in a complete rebuild of 101 New Bond Street. Opened October 24 by then-Mayor Boris Johnson. Three large, fully flexible auction rooms, climate-controlled galleries. Bonhams staking a physical claim to Mayfair parity with [[Christie's]] and [[Sotheby's]].

- 2018: Epiris Fund II (formerly Electra Partners, rebranded December 2016) acquired Bonhams from Robert Brooks and Evert Louwman for undisclosed terms. Epiris: London-based PE firm founded 1976, targeting UK-centric businesses at £75-500M EV, deploying £40-150M equity per deal. At acquisition, Bonhams' 2017 sales exceeded £450M (~50K lots). PE thesis: install professional management, invest in digital, pursue acquisitive expansion for an eventual $1B exit. Bruno Vinciguerra (ex-COO of [[Sotheby's]] for eight years) joined as Executive Chairman; moved to Global CEO February 2020.

- 2022: Four acquisitions in rapid succession: Bukowskis (Sweden/Finland, leading Nordic house, founded 1870, $21.7M turnover); Skinner (Boston, New England institution); Bruun Rasmussen (Copenhagen, Denmark's leading house, ~DKK 400M valuation per Danish media, ~13.4x EV/EBITDA); Cornette de Saint Cyr (Paris/Brussels, founded 1973). Result: 14 salerooms worldwide, 34 offices, 60+ departments. Revenue split: North America 33%, Continental Europe 33%, UK 19%, Asia-Pacific 16%.

- 2022-2023: Acquisitions plus the post-pandemic art market surge pushed Bonhams past $1B for the first time in 2022 ($1.0B, +27%). In 2023, climbed to $1.14B (+14%), the best result in 230 years. Online sales volume +132% in H1 2023.

- 2023: Failed exit. Epiris engaged JPMorgan Chase to explore a sale at ~$1B valuation. The process collapsed — the art market was turning, and the valuation proved optimistic.

- 2024: Vantage Bidco Ltd (holding company) filed consolidated accounts showing a pre-tax loss of GBP 213M ($286M), with revenue down 9% to GBP 176M. Of that loss, GBP 153M ($205M) was impairment charges — Epiris writing down their investment. The accounts revealed Bonhams owed GBP 207.3M to Pemberton Asset Management at 6% above SONIA.

- October 2025: Pemberton Asset Management converted its GBP 207.3M debt into equity, acquiring Bonhams from Epiris in a debt-for-equity swap. The lender took the keys. Leadership overhauled: CEO Chabi Nouri (appointed August 2024, formerly CEO of Piaget under [[Richemont]]) and CCO Celine Assimon both departed. Seth Johnson (formerly CEO of IPGL) became CEO. Two Pemberton partners took board seats. Pemberton is not a natural long-term owner — another exit (likely trade sale) is expected once stable profitability is demonstrated.

- 2025 performance: $970M in global sales — down 15% from peak but reflecting scale built through acquisitions. [[Hong Kong]] record year at $104M. February 2026: new 42K sq ft US flagship at Steinway Hall, 111 West 57th Street (landmarked 1925 Warren & Wetmore building). 91% of lots sold online by volume, 46% of total value transacted digitally.
