# Christie's

World's second-largest auction house by total sales ($6.2B in 2025, +6%). Owned by the Pinault family through Groupe Artémis — the same family holding company that controls [[Kering]] (Gucci, Saint Laurent, Balenciaga). No direct investable exposure, but Christie's performance is a window into Pinault family capital allocation and an indirect read on [[Kering]]'s strategic direction. Founded 1766 in London.

The world's second-largest auction house by revenue, Christie's is a 260-year-old institution that functions as a luxury-market barometer despite offering no direct investable exposure. Owned since 1998 by the Pinault family's Groupe Artémis — the same holding company that controls [[Kering]] — its $6.2B in 2025 sales represent a 6% recovery from the 2024 trough but remain well below the $8.4B 2022 peak. The real signal is structural: 46% of new buyers are Gen Z or millennials, 81% of bids are placed online, and the luxury segment drives 38% of new buyer acquisition — the same demographic and channel shifts reshaping every luxury business.

As a competitive read, Christie's trails [[Sotheby's]] ($7.1B) for the first time in recent years, with the US market (55% of auction sales, +15% YoY) masking weakness in [[Hong Kong]] (-5%) and France (-3%). The Pinault family connection means Christie's competes with [[LVMH]]-affiliated interests on the cultural prestige front — Arnault family are major collectors — creating an unusual luxury-conglomerate rivalry that plays out through consignments and cultural positioning rather than P&L.

## Evolution

The story of Christie's is the story of how a Georgian auctioneer's gift for showmanship became an institution that survived wars, scandals, and ownership changes by continuously positioning itself at the intersection of wealth, taste, and spectacle — and how a French luxury billionaire turned a 230-year-old British house into a private strategic asset.

- 1766: James Christie conducts his first sale on December 5 at rooms on Pall Mall, London — bed sheets, pillowcases, chamber pots, four Indian works on glass. His first painting sale (March 1767) raises a meagre GBP 244 18s. But Christie, born in Perth in 1730 and a former Royal Navy midshipman, cultivates friendships with Thomas Gainsborough and Sir Joshua Reynolds (first president of the Royal Academy), positioning himself as a trusted intermediary for the cultural elite. By the 1770s, his rooms on Pall Mall sit adjacent to the Royal Academy and the studios of the leading painters of the day.

- 1778-1803: Christie establishes the house's template — handling the collections of the powerful as their fortunes shift. In 1778, he brokers the private-treaty sale of Robert Walpole's paintings from Houghton Hall to Catherine the Great for GBP 40,000 — the prototype for the mega-consignment model. The French Revolution becomes a windfall: from the 1790s onward, Christie capitalizes on art fleeing Paris as French aristocrats liquidate to survive. In 1795, he auctions the jewels of Madame du Barry (guillotined 1793) for GBP 8,791. By James Christie's death in 1803, he is the leading auctioneer in London.

- 1823: Move to 8 King Street, St. James's — Christie's address for the next two centuries. The 19th century brings a surge in estate sales as British aristocratic collections turn over with each generation.

- 1859: Restructures as Christie, Manson & Woods. In 1889, the last Christie family member retires. That same year, the firm conducts Britain's first auction of Impressionist art — following money into new categories rather than clinging to Old Master orthodoxy.

- 1941-1953: Incendiary bombs during the Blitz destroy 8 King Street (April 16-17, 1941). The firm decamps to temporary quarters. Auctions continue throughout the war, but Christie's cannot return to King Street until 1953 — a 12-year exile.

- 1958-1977: After nearly two centuries as a London-only operation, Christie's opens in Rome (1958), Geneva (1968), Tokyo (1969). Goes public on the London Stock Exchange in 1973 (pretax profits grew 8x from GBP 139K in 1968 to GBP 1.1M in 1972). In 1977, first US sale at the Delmonico Hotel ballroom, Park Avenue — transforming from British institution to global duopoly competitor with [[Sotheby's]].

- 1998: François Pinault takes Christie's private. On May 2, 1998, Pinault announces he has acquired 29.5% by purchasing Joe Lewis's block. Within a week, he secures SPO's 9% stake. On May 18, he issues an agreed offer at 396p/share, valuing Christie's at GBP 721M (~$1.2B). Delisted from the LSE after 25 years. Pinault, who already controls Gucci Group (later [[Kering]]) through Artémis, treats Christie's as a prestige platform — a cultural institution that confers legitimacy in the luxury world.

- 2000-2002: The price-fixing scandal. In January 2000, Christie's approaches the DOJ with documents revealing that Christie's and [[Sotheby's]] had been colluding on seller commission rates since at least 1993. Christie's cooperates in exchange for amnesty. [[Sotheby's]] CEO Diana Brooks pleads guilty to federal price-fixing charges. [[Sotheby's]] pays a $45M criminal fine. Chairman A. Alfred Taubman is convicted at trial (2002) and serves 10 months in prison. A $512M civil settlement follows — Christie's and [[Sotheby's]] each pay $206M. Christie's escapes criminal charges entirely through its cooperation.

- 2017: [[Sotheby's|Salvator Mundi]] sells for $450.3M (November 15) — shattering the record for any artwork at public auction. Christie's stages the sale at Rockefeller Center with a marketing campaign pitched at a general audience. The auction as global media event.

- 2020-2021: Total sales drop 25% to $4.4B in 2020. Online-only sales surge 262% to GBP 243M. By 2021, total sales rebound to $7.1B — a record — boosted by $150M in NFT sales including Beeple's *Everydays* ($69.3M, March 11, 2021, first purely digital NFT sold by a major house).

- 2022: The $8.4B peak — all-time record. Driver: the Paul Allen collection ($1.6B in a single-owner sale, five works over $100M individually, highest-grossing single-owner auction in history). But Allen is a one-off windfall masking what becomes a market contraction.

- 2023-2024: Total sales drop to ~$6.0B (-26%), then $5.8B (-6%). A May 2024 cyberattack by RansomHub — claiming access to data on 500K clients — forces the website offline during an $840M auction week. Auctions proceed by phone and in person, but Christie's faces a class-action lawsuit.

- 2025: Recovery to $6.2B (+6%). 273 sales (lowest in six years) but higher average lot values. US share rises to 55% (up 7pp); [[Hong Kong]] falls to 15% (down 2pp). CEO Guillaume Cerutti steps down, replaced by Bonnie Brennan (13-year Christie's veteran). Dedicated NFT department shuttered. The house is running leaner and more US-centric under private ownership that prioritizes stability over growth.

Wholly owned by Groupe Artémis, the Pinault family holding company controlled by François-Henri Pinault (also CEO of [[Kering]]). Artémis acquired Christie's in 1999. The holding sits alongside [[Kering]], Stade Rennais FC, and other Pinault assets.

The Pinault connection means Christie's competes with [[LVMH]]-affiliated interests (Arnault family are major collectors; [[LVMH]]'s [[Tiffany]] and [[Louis Vuitton]] overlap in ultra-luxury). No indication of an IPO or sale — the family treats Christie's as a prestige/cultural asset rather than a financial one.
