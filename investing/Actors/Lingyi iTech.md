# Lingyi iTech

**Lingyi iTech** is a [[China]]-based high-precision intelligent-manufacturing platform for electronic devices that supplies functional components, modules and one-stop assembly to [[Apple]], [[Huawei]], [[Samsung]] and [[Honor]], is Shenzhen-listed (002600) since 2018, and completed an A+H [[Hong Kong]] listing (1688.HK) on 26 June 2026 to fund a pivot into AI-server hardware, humanoid-robot components and AI optical-communications infrastructure.

Run by founder-chair [[Zeng Fangqin]], the group did ¥51.4B of revenue at a ~4% net margin in FY2025 — consumer-electronics contract-manufacturing economics — and used its US$1.06B Hong Kong raise, priced at the top of the range and at a ~44% discount to its A-shares, to brand a phone-parts business as an AI-hardware and "physical AI" supplier. Whether the cash actually lifts margins off the floor is the open question.

## One-line read

## Synopsis

Founded in 2006 by [[Zeng Fangqin]] (曾芳勤), Lingyi iTech manufactures high-precision functional parts — structural and shielding components, thermal modules, charging and connectivity parts — plus modules and one-stop assembly for consumer electronics. Per Frost & Sullivan, it ranked first globally by 2025 revenue in high-precision functional components for AI terminal devices. Its anchor customer is [[Apple]], with [[Huawei]], [[Samsung]] and [[Honor]] among other clients; automotive and new-energy components are the fastest-growing newer lines.

The group reached ¥51.4B revenue and ¥2.29B net income in FY2025, but operates at consumer-electronics contract-manufacturing economics: gross margin around 16% and net margin around 4%. Growth has come from scale and overseas expansion rather than margin gains — gross margin has compressed from ~21% (2022) to ~16% (2025) as the revenue mix shifted toward lower-margin assembly and automotive work.

The company has traded in Shenzhen under 002600 since 2018. On 26 June 2026 it added a Hong Kong primary-share listing (1688.HK), raising about US$1.06B at HK$10.18 per share — the top of the marketed range — to fund capacity upgrades and the AI/robotics pivot. The H-shares priced at roughly a 44% discount to the Shenzhen A-shares, the standard A+H gap that frames both the day-one premium question and any A-H arbitrage. See [[Asia IPO pipeline 2026]].

## A-share performance and the A-H discount

The Shenzhen A-shares (002600) closed at ¥16.73 on 26 June 2026. Verified moves against actual closing data: +8% YTD, +100% over the trailing twelve months (the stock roughly doubled on the AI-hardware narrative), and +83% over five years (adjusted closes). A widely circulated secondary-source "+293% over five years" figure does not reconcile with the price record and is not used here.

The H-shares priced at HK$10.18. At an HKD/CNY rate near 0.915 that is about ¥9.3 — roughly 44% below the A-share's ¥16.73. A double-digit A-H discount is normal (mainland and HK pools are partly segmented and price the same equity differently), but a ~44% gap is wide, which is why the GuruFocus framing led on it. The discount sets up two things to watch on debut: a possible first-day pop toward the A-share, and a persistent A-H spread that southbound flow ([[Stock Connect]]) tends to compress only partially.

## Evolution

Lingyi began in 2006 as a precision functional-components maker for consumer electronics and grew into a top [[Apple]] supply-chain name. It became Shenzhen-listed under 002600 in 2018. Through 2022–2025 revenue scaled from ¥34.5B to ¥51.4B while gross margin compressed from ~21% to ~16% as the mix shifted toward lower-margin assembly and automotive work. In 2026 it marked its 20th anniversary with an A+H [[Hong Kong]] listing, repositioning around AI-server hardware, humanoid robotics and AI optical-communications — the "physical AI" pivot funded by the IPO.

---

## Customers and business mix

Lingyi sits in the [[Apple]] supply chain as a functional-components and module supplier — structural parts, EMI shielding, thermal management, charging and connectivity — rather than a final-device assembler like [[Foxconn]]. Apple is the concentration risk and the franchise. [[Huawei]], [[Samsung]] and [[Honor]] broaden the smartphone base; automotive and new-energy components are the growth edge the company highlights, part of a broader [[China+1]] and diversification dynamic across the cluster. The Frost & Sullivan "#1 globally in AI-terminal high-precision functional parts" claim is the positioning the IPO leans on to reframe a phone-parts supplier as an AI-hardware name.

---

## AI and robotics pivot

The investment story the listing sells is migration up the value chain into "physical AI": high-density AI-server hardware (structural, thermal and power components for compute racks), humanoid-robot hardware (precision actuators, structural and thermal parts — see [[Humanoid robotics]] and the [[Hangzhou Robotics Cluster]] supply ecosystem), and AI optical-communications infrastructure (components for AI networking, adjacent to [[Zhongji Innolight]]'s optical-module space). The ~HK$1.71B three-year earmark is real money against these lines, but they are early-stage relative to the ¥51B phone-and-consumer-electronics core. The question the pivot raises is margin: AI-server and robotics content can carry richer economics than phone functional parts, which is the only credible path off the ~4% net-margin floor.

## What to watch

- Day-one trade vs the A-share — whether the ~44% H-share discount narrows on debut (pop) or persists (lasting A-H spread).
- Net margin trajectory — any move off the ~4% floor is the only evidence the AI/robotics mix shift is actually repricing the business; flat margins mean it is still a phone-parts supplier.
- AI-server and humanoid-robot revenue disclosure — first reported contribution from the ~HK$1.71B earmarked lines; until it shows in segments, the pivot is narrative.
- Apple revenue concentration — direction of the single-customer dependence; diversification into Huawei/Honor/automotive is the de-risk, deeper Apple reliance is the opposite.
- Tariff / supply-chain-diversification exposure — US-China measures and customer "China+1" sourcing pressure on the core book.
- Use-of-proceeds M&A — what the "industry consolidation" acquisitions actually buy (margin-accretive AI/robotics content vs more commodity capacity).

## Analysis

The deal is a textbook narrative re-rate: a ¥51B-revenue, ~4%-net-margin precision-EMS supplier raising US$1.06B in Hong Kong by branding itself the world's #1 in "AI-terminal" components and pointing proceeds at AI servers, humanoid robots and optical networking. The financials underneath are sound but unglamorous — revenue compounding low-teens, net income lagging revenue, gross margin eroding from ~21% to ~16% as the mix moved toward assembly and automotive. The tension is exactly there: the equity is being priced on AI-hardware optionality while the cash flows are still phone-parts economics.

Two structural facts frame the trade. First, the ~44% A-H discount means the HK line is the cheap pool by construction; the interesting read-through is less about valuation level than about whether southbound demand and the AI story close the gap. Second, the only durable validation of the pivot is margin — capacity and revenue from AI/robotics lines can grow without changing the franchise's economics, but a net margin that lifts off 4% would prove genuine value-chain migration. Cornerstone composition (customer [[Honor]] and peer [[Sunny Optical]] alongside funds) signals strategic conviction, but cornerstones lock up, not validate. Until the segment disclosures show AI-server and humanoid content at richer margins, this trades as an Apple-supply-chain cyclical wearing an AI-hardware label.
