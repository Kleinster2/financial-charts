Parag Agrawal is the founder and CEO of [[Parallel Web Systems]] and the former chief executive of [[Twitter]], where he served 11 months before [[Elon Musk]] closed his $44B acquisition in October 2022 and fired him on day one. A Stanford computer-science PhD who spent a decade building Twitter's ranking and machine-learning infrastructure, Agrawal was an inside-engineer choice to succeed [[Jack Dorsey]] in 2021 — relatively unknown publicly, deeply technical, and quickly caught in the crossfire of Musk's takeover. Since 2023 he has been building Parallel as web-search-and-research infrastructure for AI agents, a bet that leans directly on the web-scale relevance systems he ran at Twitter.

## Twitter CEO tenure and the Musk ouster

Agrawal was elevated from CTO to CEO in November 2021 when Dorsey stepped down, an inside-engineer succession that surprised observers who expected a higher-profile external hire. His compensation as CEO was a $1M salary plus roughly $12.5M in stock. The tenure was consumed almost immediately by Musk's bid: Musk agreed to buy Twitter in April 2022, spent months trying to terminate the deal, and was forced to close it in October 2022. On the day the deal closed, Musk fired Agrawal along with CFO Ned Segal, legal head Vijaya Gadde, and general counsel Sean Edgett.

In March 2024 Agrawal and three other ousted executives — CFO Ned Segal, chief legal officer Vijaya Gadde, and general counsel Sean Edgett — sued Musk and [[Twitter]] (by then X Corp) for roughly $128M in unpaid severance ($57M of it Agrawal's, each claiming a year's salary plus stock), alleging Musk fired them "for cause" to avoid the payments as "revenge" for being forced to close the deal. The case settled in October 2025 on undisclosed terms. The dispute is part of the seed of his post-Twitter independence: the severance fight, not a fresh raise, bridged the gap before [[Parallel Web Systems]].

## Parallel Web Systems

See [[Parallel Web Systems]] for the company. Agrawal's pitch is a direct extension of his Twitter work: he built web-scale relevance and ranking systems for human users, and argues AI agents are the new users that need a purpose-built web layer. That founder-thesis fit is explicitly part of what investors ([[Sequoia Capital]], [[Kleiner Perkins]], [[Index Ventures]], [[Khosla Ventures]]) are underwriting at the company's $2B valuation, on undisclosed revenue.

## Analysis

Agrawal is a clean instance of the technical-founder-as-asset pattern: the company's premium valuation rests as much on his operating record as on current metrics, because the product (agent web infrastructure) is exactly the domain he ran at scale. The risk that mirrors the bet is profile — he was a deep engineer thrust into a contested public-company CEO seat and removed before he could establish an independent track record as a chief executive, so the founder-market-fit case is strong on the engineering axis and unproven on the durable-CEO axis. The Musk severance litigation also keeps a residual public-conflict thread alive around an otherwise low-profile operator who prefers to build rather than perform.
