**UniCredit** — Italy's second-largest bank and a pan-European banking group headquartered in Milan. Under CEO [[Andrea Orcel]] (since April 2021), UniCredit has undergone a radical transformation: aggressive cost-cutting, €16B+ in buybacks, and a return to acquisitive growth. The bank operates across 13 countries with significant presences in Germany (via HypoVereinsbank), Austria (Bank Austria), and Central/Eastern Europe. Market cap ~€65B (early 2026).

## Synopsis

UniCredit's current significance centers on two M&A stories. First, the unsolicited €10.1B bid for [[Banco BPM]] (launched November 2024), which Banco BPM's board rejected as undervaluing the bank — creating a three-way dynamic with [[Crédit Agricole]] (19.8% Banco BPM shareholder) that remains unresolved. Second, Orcel's surprise accumulation of a ~26% stake in [[Commerzbank]] (plus ~4% through derivatives), [[Germany]]'s second-largest bank, which triggered political backlash from the German government (a 13% shareholder) and [[ECB]] regulatory scrutiny. Both deals reflect Orcel's vision of building a true pan-European banking champion, but face political resistance in both Italy and Germany.

In March 2026, UniCredit launched a formal €35B unsolicited offer for [[Commerzbank]] at €30.8/share — a miserly 4% premium designed not to win acceptances but to clear a legal threshold. Under German takeover rules, once UniCredit crosses 30% ownership (achievable even if only a handful of shareholders accept), it can buy freely in the open market without triggering another mandatory bid. The playbook mirrors [[Media for Europe]]'s 2025 acquisition of [[ProSiebenSat.1]] — a low-ball offer followed by quiet market accumulation that took MFE from 29% to 76% within months. [[Commerzbank]]'s board response was predictably chilly, but German law obliges them to review any formal offer, giving Orcel the audience — with the board, with [[Friedrich Merz]]'s government, with trade unions — that he has struggled to secure.

Orcel's track record justifies investor attention: UniCredit shares have roughly quadrupled since he took over, driven by 19.2% return on tangible equity that rivals the best US banks. His 2025 compensation (€16M) exceeded [[Ana Botín]]'s (€15M) at [[Santander]] for the first time. The paradox is that Orcel, the former king of European financial sector M&A (architect of the [[ABN Amro]] three-way break-up in 2007 among others), has completed zero deals as UniCredit CEO — three putative attempts ([[Monte dei Paschi]], [[Banco BPM]], [[Commerzbank]]), none successful so far. Stock +12% over the past year, underperforming [[Santander]]'s +44%. The question is whether M&A ambition derails the capital return story — or whether the Commerzbank offer's parsimonious structure limits the downside. [[Commerzbank]] stock is up 2.5x since UniCredit first revealed its stakebuilding in 2024, so even a failed bid leaves UniCredit with a profitable position.

[[Mediobanca]] analysts estimate €0.7B in after-tax synergies from combining [[Commerzbank]] with HVB, worth ~€7B in lump sum — enough to offer remaining shareholders a ~30% premium if Orcel chose to. The question is whether he will need to.
