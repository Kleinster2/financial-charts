**Axar Capital** — NYC-based opportunistic credit investor focused on U.S. middle-market distressed and stressed situations. $3.7B AUM. Founded 2015 by [[Andrew Axelrod]] and Timothy Parrott. Seeded by [[Louis Bacon]] ([[Moore Capital]]) and [[Julian Robertson]] ([[Tiger Management]]) with $200M combined.

---

## Synopsis

Axar runs a distressed-to-control playbook that is unusually aggressive for its size. The firm enters as creditor — typically buying fulcrum securities in $250M-$1B capital structures — then navigates restructurings to take operational control of the underlying business. Since inception, Axar has generated ~$1B in net P&L across evergreen vehicles, private funds, and insurance portfolios. The strategy produces a highly concentrated book with minimal overlap with other funds, and returns driven by company-specific catalysts rather than market beta.

What makes Axar distinctive is the breadth of its platform build-out. Beyond the core distressed credit hedge fund, Axelrod has assembled a collection of permanent-capital operating businesses: [[Everstory Partners]] (463 cemetery/funeral locations), [[Pavonia Life Insurance]] (350K policyholders, $900M+ in benefits), [[Terra Capital Partners]] (real estate credit), [[Morning Axe Management]] (CRE lending JV, ~$250M at launch), [[J.G. Wentworth]] (structured settlement purchasing, $10.5B+ historically), and consumer finance via [[Applied Data Finance]] ($250M forward flow). This is a firm that uses distressed credit as the entry point but thinks in terms of platforms and recurring cash flows.

The key risk is concentration — both in positions and in Axelrod's personal involvement. He sits on boards across nearly every portfolio company, and the firm has ~19 employees managing $3.7B across a sprawling set of operating businesses. The StoneMor and Avant Gardner situations both drew litigation alleging overreach, though both were resolved in Axar's favor.

## Strategy

Axar describes itself as "an opportunistic corporate credit investor focused on the U.S. middle market." Three pillars:

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Stressed credit | Solvent companies under industry/macro pressure with dislocations from corporate events, regulatory changes, or cyclical headwinds |
| Distressed credit | Fulcrum securities where restructuring is anticipated or in progress |
| Structured capital solutions | Privately originated hybrid structures combining fixed income with equity upside |

Targets $250M-$1B capital structures. Expected hold: 3-5 years. Initiates through secondary market debt, seeks significant influence or control. Team has experience in 80+ restructurings across U.S. and Canada. Highly concentrated portfolio.

## Evolution

The story of Axar Capital is the story of a distressed credit investor systematically converting restructuring expertise into a permanent-capital empire.

- 2015 — Launch with blue-chip backing. [[Andrew Axelrod]] left [[Mount Kellett Capital]] — where he'd risen to Partner and Co-Head of North America Investments over six years — to co-found Axar with Timothy Parrott. The $200M seed from [[Louis Bacon]]'s and [[Julian Robertson]]'s family offices was a strong signal: two of the most respected allocators in the industry backing a 33-year-old first-time fund launcher. Parrott brought operational depth from stints at Lehman Brothers (ED, Head of European Futures), Lyxor Asset Management, and his own fund Tactica Capital.

- 2016 — First major plays establish the template. Axar entered SFX Entertainment (Robert Sillerman's EDM conglomerate) as a senior secured creditor during its Feb 2016 Chapter 11 ($345M in debts). Alongside [[Allianz]], Axar took control, slashed $400M in debt, rebranded to LiveStyle, and Axelrod became Chairman. The same year, Axar participated in Seventy Seven Energy's prepackaged bankruptcy ($1.1B in debt, oilfield services spinoff from Chesapeake Energy) and American Gilsonite's restructuring as both Ad Hoc Noteholder and DIP lender. Three restructurings in year one, each producing a board seat. The playbook was clear.

- 2018 — Real estate credit platform begins. Axar made a strategic investment in [[Terra Capital Partners]] (Feb 2018), a NYC-based real estate credit manager with a 15-year track record and 300+ properties financed. By November 2018, Terra was wholly owned by an Axar affiliate. This was the first move toward permanent capital — not a trade to exit, but infrastructure to build on.

- 2019 — Consumer finance and retail. Axar acquired [[J.G. Wentworth]] (Aug 2019), the largest purchaser of secondary market structured settlements in the U.S. — $10.5B+ in payment streams purchased historically, 64 asset-backed securitizations totaling $6.5B+. Separately, Axar and [[Alden Global Capital]] acquired Payless out of its second bankruptcy, pivoting the shuttered 2,100-store chain to e-commerce. Axelrod also took a board seat at [[JAKKS Pacific]] after initiating a position at $0.71-$1.09/share.

- 2021 — LiveStyle monetization. Axar sold ID&T (Mysteryland, Awakenings, Sensation, Defqon.1) to [[Superstruct Entertainment]] for an estimated $150M-$200M, monetizing the core asset from the SFX restructuring five years earlier. A textbook distressed-to-control-to-exit cycle.

- 2022 — Insurance and deathcare platforms. Two major acquisitions cemented Axar's shift toward permanent capital. In September, Axar acquired [[Pavonia Life Insurance]] Company of Michigan from rehabilitation — licensed in 49 states + DC + Canada, protecting 350K policyholders with $900M+ in benefits. In November, the StoneMor take-private closed ($3.50/share, 54.2% premium) after Axar accumulated ~75% of common stock. StoneMor (302 cemeteries, 74 funeral homes, 23 states + Puerto Rico) rebranded as [[Everstory Partners]] in April 2023, now operating 463 locations serving 65K+ families annually. Axelrod became Chairman. Former stockholders challenged the deal in Delaware Chancery Court, alleging Axar exploited COVID to force an underpriced buyout — Vice Chancellor Laster dismissed the case.

- 2023 — CRE lending JV and aviation credit. Axar and Morning Calm Management launched [[Morning Axe Management]] (May 2023) with ~$250M of capital — a dedicated CRE lending platform targeting senior and mezzanine loans across industrial, multifamily, retail, self storage, and office. Combined Axar + Morning Calm deployment: $5B+ since respective inceptions. In August, Axar extended $35M in Senior Secured Notes at 15% to [[Global Crossing Airlines]] (GlobalX), receiving 10M warrants at $1.00 strike plus a board seat. Another $5M followed in December 2023.

- 2024 — Consumer finance and cannabis. A 5-year, $250M forward flow agreement with [[Applied Data Finance]] (June 2024) moved Axar into tech-enabled consumer lending — installment loans originated by ADF's bank partner for non-prime borrowers. In September, Axar and [[Mavik Capital]] took [[XS Financial]] private (C$0.05265/share), acquiring a CAPEX financing platform for U.S. cannabis companies.

- 2025 — Avant Gardner and CRE expansion. The Avant Gardner / Brooklyn Mirage situation became Axar's most visible deal. Axar had been secured creditor since late 2023; when Avant Gardner filed Chapter 11 in August 2025 (~$155M in secured debt to Axar), Axar provided $45.8M in DIP financing and credit-bid $110M+ for the assets (Brooklyn Mirage, Great Hall, Kings Hall — 80K sq ft total). The UCC alleged Axar had "taken control incrementally by installing associates into management." Axar brokered a sale/lease to FIVE Holdings (Pacha brand, Dubai-based) — creditors initially pulled support, alleging the deal was arranged "under the cover of darkness." Settlement: $6.8M+ minimum to unsecured creditors. Pacha New York opening June 2026. Separately, Axar made a strategic investment in [[ACRES Capital]] (July 2025), a private CRE lender — deals ranging $15M-$100M across multifamily, student housing, hospitality, office, and industrial. [[Houlihan Lokey]] served as placement agent.

## Litigation

StoneMor take-private (Delaware Chancery): Former stockholders alleged Axar exploited COVID to force underpriced buyout and planned to use cemetery trust funds to rescue other portfolio companies. Vice Chancellor J. Travis Laster dismissed the case.

Avant Gardner (D. Del.): UCC alleged Axar incrementally seized control by installing associates, directed unsafe permit submissions, and structured the FIVE Holdings transaction to eliminate CVR payouts. Settled for $6.8M+ minimum to unsecured creditors ($3.3M cash + 15% CVR). Judge Mary F. Walrath approved.
