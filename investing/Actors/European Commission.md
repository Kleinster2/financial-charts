#actor #government #eu

**European Commission** — executive branch of the [[European Union]], responsible for proposing legislation, enforcing EU law, and managing the day-to-day administration of the bloc. Headquartered in Brussels; led by President [[Ursula von der Leyen]] (second term began Dec 2024). The Commission was the victim of a March 2026 [[ShinyHunters]] data breach in which the threat actor claimed exfiltration of more than 350 GB of data — the largest publicly-claimed European-government cyber incident attributed to The Com / [[Scattered Lapsus$ Hunters]]. CERT-EU subsequently confirmed PII exposure for 42 internal clients and at least 29 EU entities and attributed the breach to ShinyHunters. Leaked content claimed by the attacker includes email backups, contractual documents, full SSO user directory, DKIM signing keys, AWS configuration snapshots, NextCloud / Athena data, and internal admin URLs. The breach raises potential GDPR enforcement implications across the EU institutional perimeter and is one of the most consequential government cybersecurity incidents in the Western European public sector.

## EU-China trade-defense mandate

At the June 18 2026 leaders' summit, von der Leyen used the goods deficit with [[China]] - described in FT coverage as roughly EUR1bn per day - to push for stronger EU trade-defense and industrial-policy instruments. Leaders did not authorize immediate confrontation, but they did give the Commission a dual mandate: keep dialogue with [[China|Beijing]] open and develop additional tools if talks fail to produce results.

The Commission's constraint is member-state unity. [[Germany]], [[France]], [[Italy]], [[Poland]], and the [[Netherlands]] wanted new instruments after Chinese rare-earth export restrictions, while [[Spain]] opposed new measures and several diplomats argued that Europe should reduce dependencies first to absorb retaliation risk. See [[EU-China trade]] and [[China retaliatory toolkit]].

*Source: [[Financial Times]], Andy Bounds and Henry Foy, Jun 18 2026. Article text supplied by user.*

## DMA cloud gatekeeper push

On Jun 25 2026, the Commission reached a preliminary position that [[Amazon]] and [[Microsoft]] should be designated as [[Digital Markets Act]] gatekeepers for [[AWS]] and [[Microsoft Azure]]. The move extends the Commission's digital-market enforcement frame from consumer-facing platform chokepoints into [[Cloud computing]] infrastructure, with AI procurement, switching costs, and ecosystem lock-in explicitly part of the theory. See [[EU cloud DMA preliminary findings 2026]].
