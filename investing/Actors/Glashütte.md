**Glashutte** is a small town in Saxony, [[Germany]] (population ~7,000), located near Dresden, that has served as the historic center of German watchmaking since Ferdinand Adolph Lange established the industry there in 1845. Lange brought watchmaking to the economically depressed mining town as a deliberate act of industrial policy, training local craftsmen and creating a manufacturing cluster that survived both World Wars and Soviet occupation.

The town's watchmaking heritage was nearly destroyed during the Soviet era — after 1945, all private watch companies were nationalized into the state-owned VEB Glashutte Uhrenbetriebe (GUB). After German reunification in 1990, the industry was rebuilt by Walter Lange (Ferdinand's great-grandson), who refounded [[A. Lange & Sohne]] in 1990, now part of [[Richemont]]. Today Glashutte is home to four significant manufacturers: A. Lange & Sohne (high horology, [[Richemont]]), Glashutte Original ([[Swatch Group]]), NOMOS Glashutte (independent, Bauhaus-influenced), and [[Wempe]] Chronometerwerke. Wempe also operates the German Watchmaking Museum and the Glashutte observatory, which still certifies chronometers.

German law protects the "Glashutte" appellation: at least 50% of a watch movement's value must be manufactured in the town to carry the designation. This makes Glashutte the German equivalent of "Swiss Made" — a legally protected geographic indicator of watchmaking quality.
