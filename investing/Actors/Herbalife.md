**Herbalife** — Multi-level marketing company selling nutrition products (shakes, supplements, teas) through a network of ~2M independent distributors in 90+ countries. Revenue ~$5B. Subject of the most famous short-selling campaign in history — [[Bill Ackman]]'s $1B bet that it was a "pyramid scheme" (2012-2018), which [[Carl Icahn]] took the other side of, turning it into the [[Ackman-Icahn Herbalife feud]]. The FTC investigated and settled in July 2016 for $200M — restructured compensation but did not label it a pyramid scheme.

## Business model

Herbalife sells nutrition products — primarily Formula 1 meal replacement shakes, Herbalife24 sports nutrition, and herbal teas — exclusively through independent distributors, not retail stores. Distributors buy products at wholesale and sell at retail, earning the margin. They also recruit new distributors below them, earning commissions on their recruits' purchases ("downline").

The MLM structure is the central controversy. Critics argue the compensation plan rewards recruitment over retail sales — most distributor income comes from commissions on downline purchases, not from selling products to end consumers. Ackman's thesis was that the bottom 90% of distributors lose money on average, subsidizing the top 1%.

Herbalife nutrition clubs — storefront locations run by distributors, common in Latino communities — became a focus of the debate. They were either proof of genuine retail demand (Herbalife's argument) or disguised recruiting centers where distributors consumed their own inventory (Ackman's argument).

## Founding and early history

Mark Hughes founded Herbalife in 1980, selling weight-loss products out of his car trunk in Los Angeles. The company grew rapidly through MLM distribution, going public in 1986. Hughes died in 2000 at age 44 from an accidental overdose. Michael Johnson, a former Disney executive, became CEO in 2003 and professionalized operations.

## FTC investigation and settlement (2016)

The FTC opened a formal investigation in March 2014, triggered partly by Ackman's lobbying and research campaign. The investigation examined whether Herbalife's compensation structure was an illegal pyramid scheme.

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Settlement date | July 15, 2016 |
| Fine | $200M (consumer redress) |
| Label | Not called a "pyramid scheme" |
| Restructuring | Required to compensate based on retail sales, not recruitment |
| Monitoring | Independent compliance auditor for 7 years |

The FTC's language was carefully calibrated — it required "fundamental restructuring" and found that distributors "often incurred significant expenses pursuing the business opportunity" — but never used the word "pyramid." This ambiguity was why the stock rallied on the settlement: the market read survival, not vindication.

## The short war (2012-2018)

See [[Ackman-Icahn Herbalife feud]] for full details.

[[Bill Ackman]] shorted $1B at $42.50 (Dec 2012), calling it a pyramid scheme. [[Carl Icahn]] went long 16.5% to squeeze him. [[Third Point]] (Dan Loeb) also went long. The FTC settlement in July 2016 did not kill the company. Ackman exited in February 2018 at a ~$1B total loss; Icahn made ~$1.3B on the other side.

---

## Post-settlement

After the FTC settlement, Herbalife restructured its compensation plan and reported improved retail sales metrics. Revenue peaked at ~$5.8B in 2021, boosted by pandemic-era health consciousness. Since then, revenue has declined — $5.1B in 2024 — as distributor recruitment slowed and GLP-1 weight-loss drugs ([[Ozempic]], [[Wegovy]]) began disrupting the nutrition supplement market.

Icahn gradually reduced his stake from 16.5% to ~7% by 2024. The stock trades well below its 2018 peaks.
