# Raghuram Rajan

Professor at University of Chicago Booth School of Business. Former Governor of the [[Reserve Bank of India]] (2013-2016). Former Chief Economist at the IMF (2003-2006). Co-author (with [[Rohit Lamba]]) of Breaking the Mold — a template for India's service-led growth model. One of the few economists who warned about financial system fragility before the 2008 crisis.

## Key arguments (March 2026)

### India's service-led model is more resilient than critics assume

The doom narrative around AI and Indian services is too simple. India missed the manufacturing bus in the 80s/90s (education gaps, logistics, bureaucracy), but built a creditable 6% growth path through two channels:
1. **High-tech services for export** — IT, software, GCCs (evolved from back-office to front-office)
2. **Moderate-skilled domestic services** — construction, plumbing, carpentry for urbanizing population

The cost arbitrage survives AI: Indian MBA at $50K vs US MBA at $250K. When both are equipped with AI, the differential persists. The zero-sum framing ("if India gets the job, the US loses it") is wrong — development is positive-sum.

### AI platform economics are uncertain

Skeptical that any AI platform can recover training costs. "Classic high fixed cost, low marginal cost industry" — if platforms can't differentiate, they all charge marginal cost and nobody recoups. End users are the likely winners. Dismisses singularity narratives: "there are no secrets in industry."

### Policy priorities for service-led growth

1. **Human capital** — fix malnutrition (still high), improve early schooling, build Tsinghua-level research universities (India creates 3 colleges/day but lacks quality). Indian pharma as cautionary tale: almost no fundamental research, just generics.
2. **Digital divide** — broad AI access, not confined to elites. Tamil Nadu giving laptops with [[Perplexity]] access to college entrants. But needs handholding and curriculum integration.
3. **Diaspora leverage** — China's "thousand turtles" program as model. India has deep talent bench abroad.

### AI + human > AI alone

MOOCs had <10% completion without human support. Nursing, agricultural extension, legal discovery — all benefit from human-AI pairing. Moderate-skilled services (plumbing, carpentry) are insulated because every situation requires physical adaptation.

## India AI policy: producer, not tenant (May 2026)

Rajan and [[Rohit Lamba]] sharpened the March service-led-growth argument into a sovereign-AI critique. Their May 2026 essay accepts that data centers and AI diffusion matter, but argues that [[India]] cannot stop there. [[Google Cloud]]'s Visakhapatnam hub is useful infrastructure; it is not proof that India owns the intelligence layer if the model weights, advanced silicon, and frontier research remain foreign.

The key update to Rajan's earlier position is the shift from "AI can enhance Indian services" to "India also needs to produce intelligence." Diffusion is necessary but insufficient. The missing layers are research universities, deep-tech capital, patient industrial finance, training-data infrastructure, and foreign deal terms that expand Indian capability rather than only renting Indian power and land.

For the vault, this creates a live tension inside [[India AI]]: the domestic adoption story and the sovereign capability story are not the same trade.
