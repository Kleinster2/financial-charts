**Gazprom Neft** — Russian oil major, majority-owned subsidiary (95.68%) of [[Gazprom]]. Operates upstream production across Western Siberia and beyond, plus downstream refining at the [[Moscow Oil Refinery]] (Russia's capital region's largest refining facility), the Omsk Refinery, and other domestic complexes. Trades on the Moscow Exchange under SIBN. Subject to Western sanctions since 2014 (Crimea-related, with later expansions); upstream production roughly 1.5-1.7 mboe/d. The Ukrainian drone strike on the Moscow Oil Refinery on May 17, 2026 — described by [[Volodymyr Zelenskyy]] as "entirely justified" retaliation for Russia's Wednesday Kyiv barrage — put Gazprom Neft at the centre of the [[Ukraine war]] refinery-targeting thread.

The investing-vault relevance routes through three channels: (1) Russian refining capacity vulnerability under Ukraine's "oil industry, military production" targeting doctrine; (2) [[Gazprom]] consolidated financial exposure to refinery damage and reconstruction costs; (3) Russian crude-export pricing dynamics — Gazprom Neft's upstream production must find export markets that the shadow fleet can absorb at acceptable discounts.

## Why it matters for the Ukraine-war thread

Gazprom Neft sits at the structural intersection of three Ukraine-war transmission channels:

1. **Strike target** — Moscow Oil Refinery is among the most strategically symbolic Russian refining assets (capital region) and economically meaningful (240 kb/d capacity = ~4-5% of Russia's ~5.5 mb/d total refining)
2. **State-aligned ownership** — as a [[Gazprom]] subsidiary, damage to Gazprom Neft assets compounds the parent's financial stress and pressures the Russian state budget through dividend / tax flows
3. **Export channel** — when domestic refining is disrupted, Gazprom Neft upstream output must find shadow-fleet export markets at increased discounts, compressing margins and revenues

Repair times for major refinery damage at modern complexes (catalytic crackers, hydrotreaters) typically run 3-9 months minimum; major fire damage can extend to 1-2 years. The cumulative effect of sustained Ukrainian targeting through 2024-26 has been a meaningful reduction in Russian effective refining capacity (~5 mb/d effective vs ~5.5 mb/d design).

## Sanctions context

Gazprom Neft has been on the OFAC SDN list since 2014 (Crimea-related sanctions). The 2022 sanctions expansion further constrained financing access and Western technology imports. Practical impact:

- Western shareholders / counterparties prohibited from transacting
- Russian-side substitution for previously-Western technology in refining/upstream is incomplete
- Export sales depend on shadow-fleet infrastructure (~978 tankers per [[Russia#Shadow fleet]]) routing crude to discount-buyer markets

The shadow-fleet escape valve is what makes Russian crude exports resilient despite sanctions — but it doesn't restore the lost refining capacity when drones damage it.
