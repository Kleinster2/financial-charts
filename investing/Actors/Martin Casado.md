**Martin Casado** — General Partner at [[Andreessen Horowitz]] (a16z) since 2016; software-defined-networking pioneer (OpenFlow at [[Stanford]]); co-founder and CTO of Nicira (acquired by [[VMware]] for $1.26B in 2012). Now one of the most prominent investor-voices on AI infrastructure, open-source AI, inference economics, and the US-China open-vs-proprietary split — the source of the widely cited "~80% of US AI startups build on Chinese open models" estimate.

Casado is the rare operator-turned-investor whose technical credibility — he built a foundational piece of cloud-era networking — gives weight to his market calls. At [[a16z]] he has become the firm's loudest voice on AI infrastructure economics and policy, arguing that open-source models, increasingly out of [[China]], are the real diffusion engine of AI, and that US regulation and chip export controls are aimed at the wrong layer of the stack. That stance puts him squarely on the [[US-China AI race]] map and makes his widely-quoted estimates (notably the ~80% figure) recurring inputs to the open-vs-proprietary debate.

## Evolution

- 2007 — Turns a [[Stanford]] PhD on OpenFlow into Nicira, commercializing software-defined networking.
- 2012 — Nicira sells to [[VMware]] for $1.26B; Casado runs the networking/security unit to a ~$600M run-rate.
- 2016 — Joins [[Andreessen Horowitz]] as its 9th GP, pivoting from operator to enterprise/infrastructure investor.
- 2024 — Emerges as a leading opponent of [[California]] SB 1047 and a public advocate against heavy [[AI regulation]].
- 2025-2026 — Becomes a central voice on open-source AI and the [[US-China AI race]]; his ~80% "US startups on Chinese open models" estimate circulates widely, cited alongside the USCC Two Loops report.
