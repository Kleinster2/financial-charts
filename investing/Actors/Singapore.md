**Singapore** — City-state and major Asian financial hub. [[Southeast Asia]]n gateway for global capital, wealth management ($4.7T AUM), and semiconductor manufacturing. Home to sovereign wealth funds [[GIC]] and [[Temasek]], with combined estimated assets exceeding $1.1T.

---

## Synopsis

Singapore punches absurdly above its weight. A 734 km2 city-state with 5.9M people generates $547B in GDP (2024), ranking it among the world's highest per-capita incomes at ~$68K nominal and ~$133K PPP. The economy grew 4.4% in 2024 and accelerated to 4.8% in 2025, driven by AI-related electronics demand, wholesale trade, and finance. AAA/Aaa credit ratings from all three agencies. The [[Straits Times Index]] crossed 5,000 in February 2026 for the first time, up ~28% in the prior 12 months, with the three domestic banks ([[DBS]], [[OCBC]], [[UOB]]) accounting for roughly half the index weight.

The investment case for Singapore rests on three pillars. First, wealth management: $4.7T in AUM as of 2024, with the family office population exploding from 400 to over 2,000 between 2020 and 2024 (a 400% increase), fueled by Chinese, Indian, and Southeast Asian UHNW capital seeking political stability, English common law, and zero capital gains tax. Second, semiconductor manufacturing: [[GlobalFoundries]], [[Micron]], [[UMC]], and [[STMicroelectronics]] together account for ~45% of the local chip market, with $4B+ in new fab investments coming online. Third, its role as the preeminent [[ASEAN]] logistics node — the world's second-busiest container port and a top-10 air cargo hub via [[Changi Airport]], with plans to build the world's largest fully automated port by 2040.

The structural risk is geopolitical: [[China]] is Singapore's largest trading partner ($150B+ bilateral trade, 2024), while the [[United States]] is its key security partner. Singapore's hedging strategy ("not choosing sides") has worked for decades, but the margin for diplomatic maneuvering narrows as US-China decoupling accelerates. [[MAS]]'s managed float of the SGD serves as the primary monetary policy tool (Singapore uses the exchange rate, not interest rates, as its policy instrument), and the currency's strength reflects confidence in the model — but also means Singapore imports global monetary tightening directly.

## Evolution

The story of Singapore is the story of a city-state that manufactured every advantage it has — geographic location excepted.

- 1965: Expelled from [[Malaysia]], Singapore became independent under [[Lee Kuan Yew]] with no natural resources, no hinterland, and a largely unskilled population of ~1.9M. Lee's People's Action Party (PAP) began building the institutional foundation: English as the language of business and government, zero tolerance for corruption, rule of law modeled on British common law, and compulsory national service. The deliberate choice of English over Malay or Mandarin as the working language was arguably the single most consequential economic policy decision — it made Singapore legible to global capital from day one.

- 1968-1980: Industrialization via foreign direct investment. Singapore targeted [[Semiconductors]] and electronics manufacturing early, offering tax holidays and purpose-built industrial parks. [[Texas Instruments]] and National Semiconductor established fabs in the 1960s-70s. The Jurong Industrial Estate became a model for purpose-built manufacturing zones. GDP per capita went from ~$500 at independence to ~$4,800 by 1980.

- 1971: The Monetary Authority of Singapore ([[MAS]]) was established — serving simultaneously as central bank, financial regulator, and national financial development agency. This unified structure gave Singapore unusual policy coherence: the same institution that sets exchange rate policy also licenses banks and approves fintech experiments.

- 1981: [[GIC]] (Government of Singapore Investment Corporation) founded to manage the country's foreign reserves for long-term returns. Unlike typical central bank reserve management (short-duration, low-risk), GIC was designed as a true sovereign wealth fund with a global equity and alternatives mandate. Today estimated at ~$800B by SWFI, though Singapore deliberately does not disclose the exact figure — revealing GIC's AUM would, combined with [[Temasek]] and MAS reserves, expose the full size of national reserves, which Singapore considers a strategic secret.

- 1974-2000s: [[Temasek]] Holdings, originally formed in 1974 to hold the government's stakes in state-linked companies (Singapore Airlines, DBS, Singtel), evolved into a global investor with ~$630B in assets. The two SWFs operate with different mandates: GIC manages reserves across asset classes globally; Temasek is more concentrated, growth-oriented, and takes active ownership stakes. Combined, they deploy over $1.1T — an extraordinary sum for a sub-6M population.

- 2004: [[Lee Hsien Loong]] (Lee Kuan Yew's eldest son) became PM, overseeing the financialization era. Singapore's wealth management AUM grew from under $500B to over $4T during his two-decade tenure. The integrated resorts (casinos) opened in 2010, the Variable Capital Company (VCC) fund structure launched in 2020, and the family office tax incentive regime attracted a global wave of UHNW relocations.

- 2020-2024: The family office boom. Driven by [[China]]'s regulatory crackdowns, [[Hong Kong]]'s political uncertainty, and global tax optimization, single-family offices in Singapore grew from ~400 to over 2,000 — a 400% increase. The AUM surge tracks Chinese billionaires (post-[[Ant Group]], post-tech crackdown), Indian industrialists, and Indonesian tycoons consolidating assets in Singapore's zero capital gains tax, common law jurisdiction. [[MAS]] tightened requirements in 2023 (minimum $20M AUM, local investment requirements) to filter out shell structures.

- May 2024: [[Lawrence Wong]] succeeded [[Lee Hsien Loong]] as PM — the first leadership transition outside the Lee family in Singapore's history. Wong, an economist by training (former MAS official), has prioritized the Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone with [[Malaysia]] (offering tax incentives and $1B+ in infrastructure funding), AI adoption, and enhanced social safety nets including conditional unemployment insurance. The PAP won the 2025 general election with 65.6% — reduced from historical supermajorities but still commanding.

- 2025-2026: The semiconductor buildout accelerates. [[GlobalFoundries]] investing $4B in a new 28nm specialty fab (reaching capacity by late 2025), [[UMC]] opening a 22nm fab with first-phase volume production in 2026, and [[Micron]] expanding advanced packaging. Singapore accounts for ~5% of global wafer fabrication capacity. Simultaneously, [[MAS]] finalized the DTSP licensing regime (June 2025) for crypto regulation, and will trial tokenized government bills settled via wholesale CBDC in 2026. The dual bet: stay relevant in physical manufacturing while leading digital asset regulation.

## Geopolitical positioning

Singapore's strategy is deliberate diversification rather than strict neutrality. The numbers tell the story: [[China]] is the largest trading partner ($150B+ bilateral, 2024), but Singapore signed $8.2B in new agreements with China in 2024 while simultaneously deepening defense cooperation with the [[United States]] and expanding [[EU]] trade ties. The country hosts US naval logistics (Changi Naval Base) and conducts joint exercises with the PLA Navy. This works because Singapore is small enough that neither great power demands exclusive alignment — but 62% of Singaporeans recognize the space for this balance is shrinking (2025 survey).

The [[Trump II]] tariff regime adds a new wrinkle: Singapore's effective tariff rate climbed 1.1 percentage points under new US tariffs, which the trade ministry is engaging Washington to clarify. For a trade-dependent economy (trade-to-GDP ratio exceeds 300%), even small tariff shifts have outsized impact.
