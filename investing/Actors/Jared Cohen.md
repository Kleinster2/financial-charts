# Jared Cohen

**Jared Cohen** - President of Global Affairs and co-head of the [[Goldman Sachs Global Institute]]. Former founder/CEO of [[Jigsaw]], former Google Ideas lead, and one of [[Goldman Sachs]]' clearest public voices on the overlap between geopolitics, technology, capital markets, and state power.

Cohen matters because he is not a classic banker, economist, or sell-side strategist. He is the person Goldman brought in from [[Alphabet]] to convert geopolitical and technological disruption into a client advisory product. That makes him a useful sensor for how Goldman wants large clients to think about [[AI Race|AI competition]], sovereign capital, supply-chain fragmentation, energy constraints, and war risk as investable facts rather than background noise.

The strategic logic is easy to miss if Cohen is treated as a public-affairs hire. Goldman hired him in 2022 as a partner, Management Committee member, and president of Global Affairs; in 2023, the firm launched the [[Goldman Sachs Global Institute]] with Cohen and [[George Lee (Goldman Sachs)|George Lee]] as leaders. The institute gives Goldman a house view at the intersection of geopolitics, technology, and markets. It is part thought-leadership platform, part senior-client convening arm, and part advisory wedge for boards and sovereigns trying to decide where capital can still move safely.

His recurring frame is that power is moving through nontraditional channels: AI capability, compute and energy access, [[Submarine Cables|subsea cables]], sovereign wealth funds, national champions, public-private partnerships, and countries that can sit between the [[United States]] and [[China]] without fully aligning with either. For the vault, Cohen is worth tracking when his commentary changes the vocabulary Goldman uses with clients: "instrumental capital," "state-directed finance," AI diplomacy, geopolitical swing states, and market-shock transmission are all signs that Goldman sees political risk becoming a balance-sheet and allocation problem.

## Goldman role

Cohen's Goldman role sits between client strategy and geopolitical intelligence. The [[Goldman Sachs Global Institute]] says it provides strategic advice and partnerships at the intersection of geopolitics, technology, and markets. That is a different product from normal sell-side research: it is aimed at board-level and sovereign-level clients trying to make capital-allocation decisions when politics, technology, and national security are entangled.

The useful readthrough is that Goldman is institutionalizing geopolitics as a client franchise. Cohen's background gives the platform three ingredients Goldman did not historically package together: Silicon Valley credibility from [[Google]] and [[Jigsaw]], diplomatic credibility from the [[State Department]], and Goldman distribution into CEOs, asset owners, governments, and sponsors. The product is less "what is GDP next quarter?" and more "where can a company or allocator still build, finance, buy, and partner without getting trapped by technology controls, war risk, or state-capital politics?"

This matters most for four market channels:

| Channel | Why Cohen is relevant |
|---------|-----------------------|
| AI infrastructure | Frames AI as compute, energy, talent, chips, and diplomacy rather than only models |
| Sovereign capital | Treats state-directed funds as strategic asset owners, not passive pools |
| Supply chains | Focuses on chokepoints, friendshoring, and countries with leverage between great powers |
| Geopolitical shocks | Connects wars, blockades, cables, and sanctions to market repricing and client decisions |

## Evolution

The story of Jared Cohen is the story of a policy-and-technology operator becoming a Wall Street geopolitical product. His career arc runs from diplomacy to Google-era internet freedom to Goldman-era statecraft-as-capital-allocation.

- 2006-2010 - State Department policy planning. Cohen served on the [[State Department]] Policy Planning Staff and advised Condoleezza Rice and Hillary Clinton. The durable thread is not party politics; it is the idea that information networks, youth movements, technology platforms, and foreign policy were becoming inseparable. That worldview later carried directly into Google Ideas and Jigsaw.

- 2010-2016 - Google Ideas. Cohen became Google's first director of Ideas and a chief advisor to [[Eric Schmidt]]. Google Ideas was built as a "think/do" effort around technology, censorship, extremism, cyber threats, and political instability. It gave Cohen a rare combination of policy access and platform-company resources at the moment when internet freedom and national-security debates were moving from NGOs into Big Tech.

- 2016-2022 - Jigsaw. Google Ideas became [[Jigsaw]] under [[Alphabet]] in 2016, with Cohen as founder and CEO. The mission was to use technology against geopolitical and digital-security problems: online censorship, violent extremism, harassment, disinformation, and cyber attacks. The key market readthrough is that Cohen learned how to turn geopolitical abstractions into software products and partnerships, a pattern Goldman later repackaged for boards and investors.

- 2022 - Goldman hire. [[David Solomon]] hired Cohen into [[Goldman Sachs]] as partner, Management Committee member, and president of Global Affairs. The initial platform was the [[Office of Applied Innovation]], co-led with [[George Lee (Goldman Sachs)|George Lee]], meant to identify commercial opportunities at the intersection of changing markets, geopolitics, and technology. Goldman was not just hiring a public intellectual; it was adding a senior operator who could help turn "technology plus geopolitics" into client revenue and senior relationships.

- 2023 - Global Institute launch. Goldman launched the [[Goldman Sachs Global Institute]] in October 2023, led by Cohen and [[George Lee (Goldman Sachs)|George Lee]], to advise clients on topics such as the U.S.-China relationship, [[Europe]] and Middle East conflicts, and shifting supply chains. The timing matters: after [[Russia]]'s invasion of [[Ukraine]], U.S.-China technology controls, and the return of Middle East war risk, geopolitics had become too central to leave as a side input to normal research.

- 2025-2026 - Public frameworks harden. Cohen's most useful recent public work has centered on three frameworks: AI competition is not a single U.S.-China leaderboard; sovereign wealth funds and public-private capital are becoming instruments of state power; and geopolitical shocks now transmit through energy, infrastructure, supply chains, and market positioning faster than old board processes can handle.

## Core frameworks

### AI competition is not a single race

Cohen's February 2026 TIME/Goldman piece argued that asking whether the [[United States]] or [[China]] will "win" AI is too simple. The better question is who leads in which layer: frontier models, open-source diffusion, chips, energy, cloud, talent, applications, and allied supply chains. The [[DeepSeek]] moment made that frame more useful because it showed that model capability, deployment economics, and national strategy can move on different clocks.

For market work, this points away from one-stock or one-country AI framing. AI leadership can be U.S. in advanced chips and capital formation, Chinese in rapid application diffusion, Gulf-led in capital and energy-enabled data-center buildouts, Taiwanese in semiconductor manufacturing, Indian in talent and deployment, and European in pieces of the industrial or regulatory stack. Cohen's view is basically a map of where AI bottlenecks become allocation opportunities.

### Instrumental capital

In "The New Wealth of Nations," Cohen and [[George Lee (Goldman Sachs)|George Lee]] describe "instrumental capital" as state-directed capital that pursues both financial return and state power. That includes [[Sovereign Wealth Funds]], national champions, public-private partnerships, and government-backed investment vehicles. The point is not that states are suddenly investing; it is that state capital is increasingly explicit about geopolitical objectives.

This is directly relevant to [[Saudi PIF]], [[QIA]], and [[UAE]]-linked capital. Their capital is patient, politically aligned, and often willing to fund infrastructure or strategic sectors that private capital alone cannot or will not finance. Cohen's framework helps explain why a sovereign wealth fund allocation to AI infrastructure, defense, ports, sports, or media may be both a return-seeking investment and an instrument of national positioning.

### Geopolitical shocks as market structure

Cohen's Global Institute work repeatedly treats geopolitical shocks as market-structure events, not exogenous headlines. War, sanctions, export controls, cable risk, energy chokepoints, and state financing choices all change the investable map. That overlaps with the vault's treatment of the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]], [[2026 Iran conflict market impact]], [[AI infrastructure financing]], and supply-chain fragmentation.

The key indicator to watch is when Goldman moves a geopolitical topic from "risk" language into "capital" language. Once a topic becomes about where money can be raised, insured, routed, sanctioned, blocked, or subsidized, it stops being only a policy question and starts affecting valuations.

### The two Middle Easts

At Goldman's 2025 Global Institute summit, Cohen framed the Middle East as two simultaneous stories: instability and conflict on one side, and Gulf economic transformation on the other. That distinction matters because it is how Goldman can remain constructive on Gulf capital deployment while acknowledging war risk. The same region can be a source of oil shock, security risk, AI infrastructure funding, sovereign M&A, and client growth.

For the vault, this is a useful lens on [[Saudi PIF]], [[QIA]], [[UAE]], AI data-center financing, and the Gulf's attempt to convert energy and sovereign balance sheets into technology leverage.
