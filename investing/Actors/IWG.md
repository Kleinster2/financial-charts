**IWG** is the world's largest flexible workspace provider by location count (~3,500+ centers across 120 countries), operating the Regus, Spaces, HQ, and Signature brands — the cockroach that survived while [[WeWork]] burned.

## Synopsis

IWG's story is the anti-WeWork: founder Mark Dixon started Regus in Brussels in 1989, grew it through cycles of expansion and near-death (the company entered administration after the dot-com bust in 2003, restructured, and resumed growing), and built a global footprint through patient, capital-light franchise expansion rather than lease-stacking. While [[WeWork]] grabbed headlines and a $47B valuation by taking long-term leases and offering short-term desks at a loss, IWG was quietly profitable, cash-generative, and boring. When WeWork imploded, IWG inherited the demand — hybrid work acceleration post-COVID meant corporations needed flexible space, and IWG had 3x the locations. The company has since shifted aggressively toward a franchise/management model (targeting 80%+ of new locations as franchised), which converts the business from a real estate leaseholder into a platform company with higher returns on capital.

The stock (LSE: IWG) has underperformed expectations despite the structural tailwind because the market doubts the franchise transition will deliver the margin expansion IWG promises. Revenue is ~GBP 3.5B, but EBITDA margins remain compressed at ~12-15% as the company invests in converting owned locations to franchised ones and absorbs the cost of legacy leases. Dixon (who owns ~25% of the company) has explored taking IWG private multiple times, and there have been persistent rumors of PE interest. The valuation (~5-6x EBITDA) reflects the market treating IWG as a real estate company; if the franchise pivot succeeds, it should trade as a services/platform business at 10-15x. That gap is the bull case.
