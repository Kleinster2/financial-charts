# Howard Lutnick

41st US Secretary of Commerce under [[Donald Trump]] (confirmed February 18, 2025). Former Chairman and CEO of [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] for ~30 years. Co-chaired Trump's 2024 transition team, then became the administration's chief tariff architect, creating a conflict-of-interest scandal when Cantor (now run by his son [[Brandon Lutnick]]) began trading [[Tariff refund claims market|tariff refund claims]] — betting against the very policy he helped design. Separately entangled in [[Jeffrey Epstein]] file disclosures (March 2026) that contradicted his public claims of having cut ties in 2005.

---

## Synopsis

Howard Lutnick's career is defined by two reinventions and a third that may undo the first two. He joined [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] out of college in 1983, rose to CEO at 29, and then rebuilt the firm from near-extinction after losing 658 employees on 9/11 — including his brother Gary. That rebuilding, funded by a $180M relief fund and 25% of firm profits redirected to victims' families for five years, made him a national figure and the Financial Times' Person of the Year in 2001. By the 2020s, he'd expanded Cantor's empire into two publicly traded spinoffs — [[BGC Group]] (interdealer brokerage, ~$10B market cap) and [[Newmark]] (commercial real estate, ~$4B market cap) — while quietly becoming the custodian for [[Tether]]'s ~$140B in US Treasury reserves and acquiring a 5% equity stake (~$600M) in the world's largest stablecoin issuer.

The second reinvention was political. After donating to [[Hillary Clinton]] in 2016, Lutnick pivoted to [[Donald Trump]], raising $75M+ for the 2024 campaign and serving as transition team co-chair alongside [[Linda McMahon]]. Trump rewarded him with the Commerce Secretary nomination in November 2024. The Senate confirmed him 51-45 on strict party lines in February 2025, after a hearing where he dismissed inflation concerns about tariffs as "nonsense" and pledged to use CHIPS Act funding as leverage to extract larger commitments from semiconductor firms. He divested his Cantor, BGC, and Newmark holdings, handing day-to-day control to sons Brandon and Kyle.

The third act — still unfolding — threatens the other two. Cantor under Brandon's leadership explored brokering [[Tariff refund claims market|tariff refund claims]] in July 2025, creating the appearance that the Lutnick family was profiting from the legal vulnerability of tariffs Howard himself designed. Separately, the 2026 release of [[Jeffrey Epstein]] files revealed that Lutnick maintained business dealings with Epstein through at least 2014 — including a joint investment in Adfin Solutions, a 2012 visit to Epstein's island, and a $50K Epstein donation to a dinner honoring Lutnick in 2017 — contradicting his repeated public claim that he cut off all contact in 2005. Congressional investigations from [[Ron Wyden]], [[Elizabeth Warren]], and [[Jamie Raskin]] are probing both the tariff conflict and the Epstein ties. [[Public Citizen]] and the Democracy Defenders Fund have called for his resignation.

## Evolution

The story of Howard Lutnick is the story of a man who rebuilt a firm from catastrophe, built a financial empire from the wreckage, then bet it all on political power — and may lose the bet over the same instinct for deal-making that made him.

1961-1983: Lutnick was born in Jericho, Long Island, to Solomon Lutnick (a history professor at Queens College) and Jane Lutnick (a sculptor and art teacher at Long Island University). His mother died of lymphoma when he was 16, during his senior year of high school. His father died the following year from an accidental chemotherapy drug overdose, during Lutnick's freshman year of college. Orphaned as a teenager, he later described extended family as having abandoned him and his siblings — brother Gary and sister Edie — cementing a worldview of radical self-reliance. [[Haverford College]] gave him a full scholarship, and he graduated with a BA in economics in 1983.

1983-1996: Lutnick joined [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] straight out of college, recruited by Rod Fisher (a partner and nephew of founder B. Gerald Cantor) at Cantor's encouragement. The fit was immediate — within 18 months, he was running a division managing the personal investments of Cantor and his associates, expanding it into one of the firm's most profitable units. By 1991, at age 29, he was president and CEO. His early bet was on technology: he invested $250M developing eSpeed, an electronic trading platform for US Treasuries that launched in 1998 and went public in 1999. When B. Gerald Cantor died in 1996, Lutnick wrestled management control from the Cantor family in a settlement that made him chairman. He was running one of Wall Street's largest bond brokerages before turning 35.

September 11, 2001: [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] occupied floors 101-105 of the North Tower of the [[World Trade Center]]. Lutnick survived because he was taking his son Kyle to his first day of kindergarten. His brother Gary was in the office. Of Cantor's 960 New York employees, 658 were killed — roughly 24% of the total 2,753 people who died in Manhattan that day. The firm went from $1M in daily profit to daily losses overnight. In the days that followed, Lutnick made a decision that would define perceptions of him for decades: he stopped paychecks for deceased employees, reasoning the firm would collapse if he didn't. The backlash was immediate and brutal — grieving families felt betrayed. But within two months, he authorized $45M in bonuses to victims' families and pledged 25% of Cantor's profits for the next five years plus 10 years of continued health benefits. The Cantor Fitzgerald Relief Fund ultimately distributed $180M. The Financial Times named him Person of the Year. He attended roughly 20 funerals a day for 35 days. Twenty years later, the rebuilt firm employed 12,000 people worldwide, 60 of them children of 9/11 victims.

2004-2018: Post-9/11, Lutnick methodically spun Cantor's divisions into independent entities. In August 2004, he separated the voice brokerage business into BGC Partners L.P. (named for B. Gerald Cantor). In 2008, BGC merged with eSpeed to form [[BGC Group|BGC Partners, Inc.]], effectively taking BGC public through eSpeed's existing listing. In 2011, BGC acquired [[Newmark]] Knight Frank and Grubb & Ellis assets, creating Newmark Grubb Knight Frank — a major commercial real estate services platform. Newmark was spun off as an independent public company in November 2018. By the mid-2020s, Cantor controlled two public companies: [[BGC Group]] (interdealer brokerage, ~$10B market cap as of late 2025) and [[Newmark]] (commercial real estate, ~$4B market cap). Lutnick retained a 60% ownership stake in private Cantor and controlling interests in both public entities, giving him a net worth estimated at $1.5-2B.

2021-2024 — The Tether play: In late 2021, [[Cantor Fitzgerald]] became the primary custodian for [[Tether]]'s US Treasury holdings — the assets backing the world's largest stablecoin, USDT. By 2024, Cantor held custody of approximately $133-140B in Treasuries, generating tens of millions in annual fees. In January 2024, Lutnick publicly vouched for Tether's reserves ("they have the money"), a statement that carried unusual weight given Tether's history of CFTC and NYAG settlements over reserve misrepresentation. In April 2024, Cantor acquired a 5% equity stake in Tether through a convertible debt investment — valued at roughly $600M at the ~$12B implied valuation. At a July 2024 [[Bitcoin]] conference in Nashville alongside Trump, Lutnick announced a $2B Bitcoin lending facility using BTC as collateral. The Tether relationship created a massive revenue stream and political connection: Cantor was simultaneously the custodian of the largest stablecoin's reserves, an equity owner of the issuer, and headed by a man positioning himself as the next Commerce Secretary.

2024 — The political pivot: Lutnick's political journey reversed conventional direction. He donated to [[Hillary Clinton]] in 2016 and to [[Chuck Schumer]] and [[Kamala Harris]] earlier in his career. But by 2020, he was fundraising for Trump. In 2024, the commitment became total: he co-hosted a Trump fundraiser in London in June, then raised $15M at his Bridgehampton home in August. Across the 2024 cycle, he contributed over $14M to conservative groups — nearly $9M to MAGA Inc. (making him the PAC's 9th-largest donor), $1.7M to the Congressional Leadership Fund, and $2M to Turnout for America. In August 2024, Trump named him co-chair of the presidential transition alongside [[Linda McMahon]]. Lutnick became the public face of the transition, appearing frequently on cable news and demanding "loyalty" from prospective appointees. The transition role positioned him for Treasury Secretary, but Trump ultimately chose [[Scott Bessent]] for Treasury and tapped Lutnick for Commerce on November 19, 2024.

2025 — Commerce Secretary: The Senate confirmed Lutnick 51-45 on February 18, 2025, with every Democrat and both independents voting against. At his January 29 confirmation hearing, he called tariffs on [[China]] the administration's "highest priority," dismissed inflation concerns as "nonsense," and pledged to deploy tariffs "country by country" as leverage. Once in office, he became the administration's chief tariff implementer, focusing on reciprocal tariffs against [[Canada]], [[Mexico]], China, and the [[EU]], plus blanket taxes on steel and aluminum imports. On semiconductors, he signaled willingness to withhold CHIPS Act grants to extract larger investment commitments — pressuring firms to match [[TSMC]]'s $165B total US pledge. He voided $7.4B in CHIPS funding earmarked for the nonprofit Natcast (National Center for the Advancement of Semiconductor Technology). The Commerce Department under Lutnick raised $76.4B in tariff revenue in his first year and announced $9.94T in investment commitment deals. To comply with ethics requirements, Lutnick divested his Cantor, BGC, and Newmark holdings, effective May 16, 2025. Sons [[Brandon Lutnick]] (chairman) and Kyle Lutnick (executive vice chairman) assumed control of [[Cantor Fitzgerald]].

2025-2026 — The scandals converge: Two crises now threaten Lutnick's position. First, the tariff conflict: in July 2025, WIRED reported that Cantor salespeople had explored brokering [[Tariff refund claims market|tariff refund claims]], with internal emails showing a $10M trade completed and capacity for "several hundred million" more. When the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs in [[Learning Resources v Trump]] (February 20, 2026), claims bought at ~20 cents on the dollar became worth face value — a potential 5x return. Senators [[Ron Wyden]] and [[Elizabeth Warren]] sent a letter to [[Brandon Lutnick]] demanding answers about potential insider dealing (August 2025). Rep. [[Jamie Raskin]] opened a House investigation in February 2026, questioning whether the Lutnick family used nonpublic information. Cantor formally denied executing any tariff refund trades, claiming salespeople explored the idea but never completed transactions. Second, the Epstein files: documents released in early 2026 revealed that Lutnick's contacts with [[Jeffrey Epstein]] extended far beyond his public claim of cutting ties in 2005. In 1996, Epstein sold a property at 11 East 71st Street to an entity called Comet Trust, which in 1998 sold it to Lutnick — making him Epstein's next-door neighbor. In December 2012, Lutnick visited Epstein's island Little St. James with his wife and children. Four days later (December 28, 2012), both signed contracts to invest in Adfin Solutions — Lutnick through CVAFH I, Epstein through Southern Trust Company. Correspondence continued through 2014 (Cantor Ventures fundraising discussions) and 2017 (Epstein's $50K donation to a dinner honoring Lutnick) and 2018 (emails about the Frick Collection expansion). In March 2026, Lutnick agreed to testify voluntarily before the House Oversight Committee. [[Public Citizen]] and the Democracy Defenders Fund called for his resignation on March 10, 2026.
