**Pratt & Whitney** is a major aircraft engine manufacturer and subsidiary of [[RTX]] (formerly Raytheon Technologies), producing commercial and military jet engines. Its GTF (geared turbofan) family — the PW1000G series — powers the [[Airbus]] A220, A320neo, and the [[Embraer]] E-Jet E2, representing the most significant commercial engine architecture shift in decades.

## Synopsis

The GTF engine is Pratt & Whitney's strategic bet and its operational headache simultaneously. The geared turbofan design introduces a reduction gearbox between the fan and the low-pressure turbine, allowing each to spin at optimal speed — delivering 15-20% fuel savings over conventional turbofans. This advantage won P&W the engine for the Airbus A320neo (competing with [[CFM International]]'s LEAP), the A220, and the Embraer E2 family. The installed base is growing rapidly, with thousands of GTF engines now in service globally.

The problem is durability. A contaminated metal powder defect in the high-pressure turbine disc, discovered in 2023, forced a massive inspection and recall campaign affecting ~3,000 engines. Airlines grounded aircraft for months awaiting engine shop visits, costing [[RTX]] an estimated $3-7B in charges and severely disrupting carrier operations (particularly at [[IndiGo]], [[Spirit Airlines]], and European operators). The recall exposed the risk of sole-source engine programs — airlines flying GTF-powered narrowbodies had no alternative powerplant and no spare engines. P&W's military portfolio (F135 engine for the [[F-35]], F119 for the [[F-22]]) provides stable defense revenue, but the GTF crisis dominated investor attention through 2024-2025 and will continue to weigh on RTX's aftermarket revenue timing as the inspection program works through the fleet.
