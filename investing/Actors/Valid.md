Brazilian digital security and identification solutions company, a key player in [[Brazil]]'s ICP-Brasil digital certificate ecosystem. Founded 1957, provides smart cards, digital certificates, government IDs, and IoT connectivity solutions across [[Brazil]], Argentina, and Spain.

## Synopsis

Valid operates in Brazil's regulated digital identity infrastructure market with R$2B+ in annual revenue and strong recurring cash flows from certificate renewals. The company benefits from mandatory digital certificate requirements across Brazilian tax filing, banking integration, and government services — a structural growth driver as PIX adoption and gov.br digitization accelerate. With institutional ownership at 68% (Alaska Investimentos leading at 27%), the stock trades at 14.78% dividend yield despite revenue CAGR of only 1.58% over five years, suggesting either deep value or structural headwinds.

The investment case centers on competitive dynamics: [[Experian]]'s Serasa subsidiary, previously the largest Autoridade Certificadora in Brazil, announced exit from digital certification in February 2024, potentially creating market share upside for remaining players including Valid, Certisign, and government-owned Serpro. Valid also benefits from international diversification through its top-5 global SIM manufacturing business (900M connected devices), reducing dependence on Brazil's political cycles while providing exposure to global IoT growth.

However, profit growth of 34.57% CAGR over five years against flat revenue suggests either margin expansion or cost cutting rather than organic growth — a pattern that may not be sustainable. The high dividend yield and institutional concentration indicate mature business characteristics in a regulated market with limited expansion opportunities beyond digital transformation tailwinds.

## Evolution

The story of Valid is the story of Brazil's security printing evolution from analog to digital, shaped by three foreign ownership transitions and the country's gradual embrace of digital identity infrastructure.

• 1957-1993: Thomas de La Rue foundation — Founded as Brazilian subsidiary of British security printer Thomas de La Rue, establishing operations in São Paulo to serve Latin America's growing demand for banknotes, government documents, and secure printing. The subsidiary built relationships with Brazilian banks and government agencies that would prove crucial decades later. De La Rue's global expertise in security printing gave the operation technical credibility and access to international standards, positioning it as Brazil's premier private security printer during the military government era (1964-1985).

• 1993-1999: American Banknote acquisition — American Banknote Corporation acquired Thomas de La Rue's Brazilian operations for $45M, part of ABC's broader international expansion strategy. The acquisition coincided with Brazil's Real Plan (1994) and economic stabilization, creating demand for modern financial infrastructure. Under American ownership, the company expanded beyond traditional printing into emerging technologies like magnetic stripe cards and early smart card solutions, anticipating the digital payments transition. The timing proved prescient as Brazilian banks began modernizing payment systems in response to hyperinflation's end.

• 1999-2006: ABNote transition and digital pivot — Renamed to American Banknote Brasil (ABNote), the company developed Brazil's first ICP-Brasil digital certificate in 2001, establishing itself as pioneer in the government-mandated digital identity framework. This technological leap from analog printing to cryptographic solutions required substantial R&D investment but created regulatory moats that persist today. The digital certificate business generated recurring revenue streams as Brazilian law began requiring digital signatures for corporate tax filings and banking transactions.

• 2006: IPO and public company birth — Listed on B3 (then Bovespa) as foreign parent American Banknote Corporation sought liquidity and geographic diversification. The IPO raised R$480M (selling 57% equity), providing capital for expansion into adjacent markets like SIM card manufacturing and international operations. Going public in Brazil rather than the US signaled management's confidence in domestic growth opportunities and reduced currency exposure for a Brazil-focused business.

• 2010: Rebranding to Valid — Name change reflected strategic positioning as integrated "valid" solution provider rather than subsidiary of foreign printer. The rebranding coincided with Brazil's digital infrastructure buildout and growing smartphone adoption, creating demand for SIM cards and mobile identity solutions. Management emphasized recurring revenue from digital certificates over cyclical security printing, targeting 60%+ revenue from technology solutions versus traditional printing.

• 2010s: Diversification and international expansion — Expanded SIM manufacturing operations globally, becoming top-5 worldwide supplier with 900M connected devices. International diversification reduced Brazil political risk while capturing IoT growth in developed markets. Acquisitions in Argentina and Spain provided geographic expansion within Portuguese/Spanish-speaking regions. The SIM business complemented digital certificates by serving similar customers (telecom operators, device manufacturers) with related security technologies.

• 2018: Final name evolution — Changed to Valid Soluções S.A., emphasizing solutions capability over hardware manufacturing. The rebrand reflected evolution toward software and services as physical printing declined and digital identity became mainstream. Revenue mix shifted toward recurring digital services (certificates, cloud-based identity management) and away from one-time printing contracts.

• 2024-present: Competitive landscape shift — Serasa [[Experian]]'s exit from digital certification (February 2024) removed largest competitor from Autoridades Certificadoras market, potentially creating market share opportunity for remaining players. However, Serpro's government backing and Certisign's established position limit Valid's ability to capture entire Serasa market share. The consolidation reflects broader industry maturation as digital certificate adoption reaches steady-state levels in corporate Brazil.

## Business Segments

### ID & Digital Government
Digital identity, interoperability, and digital services. Authorized under ICP-Brasil to issue digital certificates (e-CPF, e-CNPJ, NF-e). Valid is one of the main Autoridades Certificadoras alongside Certisign, Soluti, and government-owned Serpro following Serasa [[Experian]]'s February 2024 exit. Certificates require in-person validation and expire annually (A1) or every 1-3 years (A3), driving recurring revenue.

Government partnerships include state governments for digital ID initiatives (Paraná CNH issuance, Piauí digital identity program, Espírito Santo police digitization, Amazonas digital transformation strategy).

### Banking & Payments  
EMV payment cards and digital banking services. Valid ranks as a top-10 global credit and debit card manufacturer, serving major Brazilian banks including Itaú Unibanco, Banco do Brasil, Bradesco, Santander Brasil, and Caixa Economica Federal.

### Trusted Connectivity
Solutions for connectivity providers, device makers, and IoT service providers. Valid is the top-5 largest SIM manufacturer worldwide with 900M connected devices using Valid technology. This segment provides international diversification beyond Brazil's regulated markets.

### Security Printing
Traditional security printing services to financial institutions, telecom companies, state governments, and public agencies. Includes driver's licenses, identity cards, and other secure documents. In 2010, produced 465.1M cards, 15.4M driver's licenses/identity cards, and 12.3K tons of security paper.

## Growth Drivers

### Government Digitization
- PIX adoption — Brazil's instant payment system requires digital certificates for business integration
- gov.br platform — unified government services portal driving digital certificate demand
- eSocial compliance — mandatory digital labor reporting system
- NF-e electronic invoicing — required for business-to-business transactions
- Tax filing digitization — Receita Federal requirements

### International Expansion
- Diaspora opportunity — ~4M Brazilians abroad need certificates for Brazilian bureaucracy, premium pricing ($275 vs ~R$150-200 domestic)
- SIM manufacturing — global IoT and connectivity growth
- LATAM expansion — operations in Argentina and Spain

### Market Consolidation
- Serasa [[Experian]] exit from certificate renewals
- Potential acquisition targets among private ACs
- Government push for digital identity standardization

## Risks

### Government Dependency
- Heavy reliance on government contracts and regulated markets
- Exposure to Brazil political cycles and policy changes
- Potential changes to ICP-Brasil framework

### Competition
- Larger players with more capital (though [[Experian]] withdrawn from renewals)
- Government-owned Serpro with direct federal ties
- New entrants in e-signature and digital identity

### Concentration Risk
- Geographic concentration in Brazil (though mitigated by global SIM business)
- Sector concentration in regulated digital identity markets
- Customer concentration among major banks and telecoms

### Technology Risk
- Need for continuous investment in security and compliance
- Potential disruption from blockchain-based identity solutions
- Changes in international smart card/SIM standards

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Pure-play exposure to Brazil's digital transformation, one of few public options
- Regulatory moats with ICP-Brasil authorization and compliance expertise
- Recurring revenue from annual certificate renewals and government contracts
- International diversification through global SIM manufacturing
- Market share gains possible from Serasa [[Experian]]'s withdrawal from renewals
- High dividend yield (14.78%) suggests undervaluation relative to cash generation

### Bear Case
- Government dependency creates political and regulatory risks
- Competitive pressure from larger players and government-owned alternatives
- Technology disruption risk from blockchain and alternative identity solutions
- Economic sensitivity to Brazil's macroeconomic cycles
- Limited organic growth (1.58% revenue CAGR) suggests mature market
- High dividend yield may indicate lack of reinvestment opportunities
