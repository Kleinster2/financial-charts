## Philosophy: scale vs. craft

Marks has framed the tension between asset growth and investment returns as the central conflict for publicly traded asset managers — the conflict between shareholders (who want AUM growth → fee revenue) and fund investors (who want returns, which often require saying no to capital).

Marks (late 2025): *"To us, the main negative [of being publicly traded] would be pressure from the shareholders to grow for growth's sake. We were concerned that the clients would think that we had divided loyalties."*

*"I think of us as artisans, you know, kind of like craftspeople. It's not an assembly line. It's not scope and scale."*

The flagship distressed debt vehicle, the Opportunities fund, has depended on economic dislocations. During the 2010s US expansion, opportunities were scarce. Marks's managers sometimes asked to raise smaller subsequent pools when they believed fewer good deals were available — the opposite of the industry norm where each fundraise is expected to be larger.

*"There's nothing wrong with private credit. The only question will be, in my opinion, when the tide goes out, you'll find out who made prudent credit decisions, individual lending decisions, and who didn't."*

The 2026 [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026|private credit crisis]] is testing this proposition in real time.

*Source: FT (Sujeet Indap), Apr 1, 2026*

## Public listing and Brookfield acquisition

| Date | Event | Valuation |
|------|-------|-----------|
| 2012 | IPO | $6.5B |
| 2019 | Brookfield acquires ~62% stake | $7.7B implied ($4.7B paid) |
| Sep 2025 | Brookfield acquires remainder | $11.5B implied |

IPO'd in 2012 at $6.5B. Paid healthy dividends to shareholders over seven years. Sold a near two-thirds stake to [[Brookfield]] in 2019 at an implied valuation of $7.7B — a modest premium to IPO price despite years of strong performance. In Sep 2025, Brookfield purchased the remaining stake at an implied $11.5B, making Oaktree wholly owned. Oaktree continues to operate independently within [[Brookfield]]'s $1T+ platform.

The valuation trajectory ($6.5B → $7.7B → $11.5B over 13 years) contrasts sharply with peers who stayed public: [[Ares Management]] peaked at $60B market cap, [[Blue Owl]] at $40B, [[Apollo]] at well over $100B. The gap reflects Oaktree's deliberate choice not to chase AUM growth for its own sake.

## Data centre infrastructure

[[Oaktree Capital]] backs [[Pure Data Centres]] Group, a UK-based DC operator that launched Europe's first off-grid data centre microgrid in Dublin (Mar 2026). The 110MW facility runs on biomethane with HVO backup, bypassing [[Ireland]]'s strained grid entirely. Pure's exec chair Gary Wojtaszek: "Europe has been energy constrained for a long time. I see this as something that's really going to explode everywhere." Five more microgrids are under construction in Dublin. See [[Global DC grid strain]].

*Source: FT, Mar 17 2026*

## Lloyd's of London reinsurance — Allianz deal (Dec 2025)

[[Oaktree Capital]] set up a new vehicle at [[Lloyd's of London]] to reinsure risks covered by [[Allianz]]. Under the structure, Oaktree funds a portion of payouts on claims underwritten by Allianz — deal value in the hundreds of millions of dollars. The structure benefits from Lloyd's AA credit rating, which reduces the collateral Oaktree must post.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Counterparty | [[Allianz]] |
| Venue | [[Lloyd's of London]] |
| Scale | Hundreds of millions of dollars |
| Structure | Oaktree funds portion of Allianz claims payouts |
| Rationale (Allianz) | Diversify counterparty risk away from traditional reinsurers |
| Similar deals | [[Blackstone]]-[[AIG]] (Syndicate 2479), [[Blackstone]]-[[The Fidelis Partnership]] ($170M) |

This marks Oaktree's entry into commercial reinsurance — a new asset class for the firm beyond its core distressed debt and credit strategies. The deal is part of a broader wave of alternative capital entering insurance via [[Lloyd's of London|Lloyd's]], seeking [[Insurance float|float]].

[[Allianz]] head of reinsurance Thorsten Fromhold (Dec 2025): *"We're buying ever more reinsurance... Let's partner with alternative capital"* — citing counterparty credit risk concentration with [[Munich Re]], [[Swiss Re]], and [[Hannover Re]].

*Source: FT, Dec 8 2025*
