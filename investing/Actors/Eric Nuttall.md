# Eric Nuttall

Partner and Senior Portfolio Manager at [[Ninepoint Partners]], a Toronto-based independent investment management firm. One of Canada's most prominent energy fund managers and a frequent media commentator on oil markets.

## Career

Began at Sprott Asset Management in 2003 as a Research Associate, progressed through Analyst and Associate PM roles to Portfolio Manager in 2010. Moved to Ninepoint in 2017. His views on energy are frequently sought by Canadian and international financial media.

## 2026 Hormuz crisis commentary (March 30)

In a television interview ([source](https://youtu.be/sFAd6Szmf28)), Nuttall called the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure "the worst energy crisis of our lifetimes" and said the level of market complacency is "astounding." Key arguments:

### Scale comparison — worse than COVID

Nuttall provided the most direct COVID comparison of any analyst during the crisis:

| Metric | Iran conflict (2026) | COVID (2020) |
|--------|---------------------|--------------|
| Production lost / demand destroyed | ~900M bbl (forecast, ME production this year) | 870–880M bbl (demand) |
| Daily disruption | ~11M bbl/day production down | ~20M bbl/day demand down (peak) |
| Nature of shock | Supply — physical barrels unavailable | Demand — barrels available, nobody buying |

The comparison inverts the usual framing. COVID destroyed more barrels of *demand* per day at peak, but Nuttall argues the cumulative barrel loss from the Iran conflict will *exceed* COVID's total — making it the larger disruption in volume terms. The supply-side nature is worse because you can't stimulate your way out of missing barrels.

### $177/bbl demand destruction threshold

Using the historical threshold of 5.5% of global GDP spent on oil as the point where demand destruction kicks in, Nuttall calculated a price target of $177/bbl if the strait doesn't reopen. This is the highest specific price target from any named analyst during the crisis.

### Safety buffers — being consumed

Nuttall catalogued the temporary measures absorbing the shock:
- Unsanctioning of Russian barrels + floating storage
- Unsanctioning of Iranian barrels
- Modest global SPR releases

All "rounding errors" relative to the disruption scale.

### "The day after" — structural thesis

Rather than buying energy stocks on the geopolitical spike, Nuttall focuses on what the market looks like *after* the strait reopens:

1. Glut thesis is dead. Coming into 2026, consensus expected the biggest oil surplus in history. That surplus has been consumed — inventories approaching multi-year lows by end of March.

2. Permanent political risk premium. At least $10-20/bbl enduring premium because [[Iran]] has weaponized the world's most critical energy chokepoint. Future impairment can be triggered by a "$30,000 drone."

3. Production recovery will be slow. [[Kuwait]] said 3-4 months to fully ramp post-war. [[Iraq]] in full force majeure with over 1M bbl/day at risk of below-surface reservoir damage — potential permanent or semi-permanent productive capacity loss.

4. Long-dated reserves in politically secure areas (i.e., [[Canada]], US) have become "wildly more valuable."

### Home team advantage — Canada thesis

Nuttall acknowledged the self-interest ("I'll be accused of home team advantage being in Canada") but argued the crisis permanently reprices countries with:
- Long-dated reserves
- Politically secure geography
- Adequate takeaway capacity

This is bullish for Canadian oil sands producers ([[Canadian Natural Resources]], [[Suncor]]) and US shale.
