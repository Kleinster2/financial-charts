**Ireland** is a Western European republic, founding member of the [[IEA]] (1974), and a major corporate tax hub for global tech and pharma firms. It is also the global canary in the coal mine for the DC power crunch — more than a fifth of Ireland's electricity is consumed by data centres, the highest share of any country.

## Synopsis

Ireland's investment story is inseparable from its data centre problem. The country parlayed a 12.5% corporate tax rate into a tech beachhead that now accounts for 13% of GDP and a disproportionate share of booming corporation tax revenues — but the physical infrastructure hasn't kept pace. Data centres consume more than a fifth of Ireland's electricity (heading to a third by 2034), and in 2022 they were already eating 53% of the country's renewable energy output. Wholesale power prices run a third above the European average. The grid is full.

The regulatory response has been a four-year experiment the rest of the world is watching. [[CRU]] imposed an effective moratorium on new DC connections in 2021 — one of only three major markets globally to do so (alongside [[Amsterdam]] in 2019 and [[Singapore]]). In December 2025, CRU lifted the freeze with a condition: new projects must source 80% of annual power from additional renewable generation built in Ireland within six years of startup. That's a demanding bar given that 42% of Irish power still comes from natural gas, against an 80%-renewables-by-2030 target the country is nowhere near meeting.

The gap between what the rules require and what the grid can deliver is spawning creative workarounds. [[Oaktree Capital]]-backed [[Pure Data Centres]] launched Europe's first off-grid DC microgrid in Dublin (110MW, biomethane/HVO, March 2026), with five more under construction. [[Schroders]] Greencoat Renewables is investing in collocated renewables + DC sites north of Dublin. But biomethane costs more than natural gas, and operators still want grid connections for demand swings — the off-grid model works as a regulatory bypass, not yet as an economic one. The key question is whether these workarounds can scale to the ~5.8GW of additional DC capacity CRU says Ireland needs, or whether the 80% rule becomes a de facto cap on growth that pushes hyperscalers to less constrained geographies.

## Data centre energy crisis

Ireland was a European DC pioneer but became the first country where the power strain forced regulatory action. DCs consumed 53% of Ireland's renewable energy supply in 2022. Wholesale power prices are 1/3 higher than the rest of [[Europe]].

**Moratorium and reversal:**
- 2021: [[CRU]] (Commission for Regulation of Utilities) imposed an effective moratorium on new DC connections
- Dec 2025: CRU lifted the moratorium — new projects allowed, but must source 80% of annual power from additional renewable generation in Ireland within 6 years of startup
- CRU flagged ~5.8GW of additional demand capacity needed for the DC sector medium-term
- [[EirGrid]] (grid operator) and [[ESB]] (network operator) expected to detail implementation rules by end of March 2026

**Dublin microgrid wave (Mar 2026):**
- [[Pure Data Centres]] (UK, backed by [[Oaktree Capital]]) launched Europe's first off-grid DC microgrid in Dublin — 110MW, runs on biomethane/HVO
- Five other microgrids under construction in Dublin (AVK-SEG building three)
- Cost issue: biomethane more expensive than natural gas — DCs still want grid connections for gas at scale
- [[Schroders]] Greencoat Renewables investing in first combined renewables + DC site (Drogheda, County Louth)

**Global precedent:** [[Amsterdam]] imposed a DC moratorium in 2019, [[Frankfurt]] added regulations in 2022. [[McKinsey]] estimates up to 1/3 of global DC demand is unmet.
