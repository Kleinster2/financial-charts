Martin Shkreli is a former hedge fund manager and pharmaceutical executive who became the face of drug pricing predation after raising [[Daraprim]]'s price 5,456% in September 2015, was convicted of securities fraud in August 2017, served five years in federal prison, and now carries a lifetime [[FTC]] ban from the pharmaceutical industry plus $64.6M in antitrust disgorgement.

---

## Synopsis

Shkreli's career arc is a study in regulatory arbitrage gone wrong — specifically, the gap between what's legal in pharmaceutical pricing (almost anything) and what's legal in securities markets (much less). The Daraprim price hike from $13.50 to $750 per pill was never the crime he went to prison for; it was the conduct at his hedge funds [[MSMB Capital]] and [[Retrophin]] — lying to investors about fund performance, misappropriating company funds through sham consulting agreements, and running a Ponzi-like structure using new money to cover old losses. The distinction matters: the pricing was a PR catastrophe but arguably legal under the US system, while the securities fraud was straightforward embezzlement.

The numbers tell the story of escalating risk tolerance. Elea Capital imploded with a $2.3M Lehman judgment (2007). MSMB Capital lost $7M+ on a single naked short of [[Orexigen Therapeutics]] (2011). Retrophin hemorrhaged $11M+ through sham agreements to pay off defrauded MSMB investors. Turing paid $55M for Daraprim and hiked it to generate enough revenue to fund operations. Each venture was larger, more leveraged, and more dependent on the next one working. The criminal conviction (3 of 8 counts, 7-year sentence) addressed the hedge fund fraud; the FTC antitrust case ($64.6M, lifetime pharma ban, upheld by the Second Circuit January 2024, cert denied October 2024) addressed the Daraprim monopoly scheme — specifically, the distribution restrictions designed to block generic entry.

Post-prison (released May 2022), Shkreli has pivoted to crypto promotion, biotech short-selling from his personal account, and a photonic computing venture (Q/C Technologies, NASDAQ: QCLS). The lifetime pharma ban theoretically constrains him, but he continues public commentary on drug pricing and pharma stocks. His pattern — find an under-exploited niche, lever up, talk aggressively, push until something breaks — appears unchanged.

## Evolution

The story of Martin Shkreli is the story of a working-class Brooklyn kid who discovered that financial markets reward aggression until they don't — and that the pharmaceutical pricing system has almost no ceiling until Congress decides to look.

2000-2006 — The prodigy narrative. Shkreli interned at Cramer, Berkowitz and Company at age 17, reportedly recommending a biotech short that paid off and drawing an [[SEC]] investigation (cleared). Stints at [[UBS]] and Intrepid Capital Management followed. He graduated from [[Baruch College]] in 2004 with a BBA. By 2006 he founded Elea Capital Management, his first hedge fund, at 23. The prodigy label stuck — he made Forbes 30 Under 30 in December 2012 — but the early career already showed the pattern: high-conviction biotech bets, aggressive public positioning, and a willingness to trash stocks he was shorting in online forums.

2006-2008 — Elea Capital: first blowup. Elea lasted roughly two years. In October 2007, [[Lehman Brothers]] won a $2.3M default judgment against Shkreli and Elea for failing to cover a put option transaction — he'd bet wrong on a broad market decline. Lehman collapsed in September 2008 before collecting, but the judgment's existence became legally significant later: Shkreli failed to disclose the total loss to subsequent investors at MSMB Capital, which became part of his fraud charges.

2009-2014 — MSMB Capital: the Ponzi mechanics. Shkreli co-founded MSMB Capital Management in September 2009 with childhood friend Marek Biestek (MSMB = their initials). The fund focused on biotech shorts, but the signature trade was a catastrophe: on February 1, 2011, MSMB naked-shorted 32 million shares of [[Orexigen Therapeutics]] (OREX) at ~$2.50 after an FDA rejection tanked the stock from $9.09. The fund failed to locate shares to borrow, failed to settle a short position of over 11 million shares, and [[Merrill Lynch]] ultimately closed the position at a loss exceeding $7M. Shkreli papered over the losses by lying to investors about fund performance and using money from new investors to pay off earlier ones — textbook Ponzi structure. He also filed FDA citizen petitions against [[Navidea Biopharmaceuticals]] and [[MannKind Corporation]] while shorting their stocks, and made an unsolicited $378M hostile bid for [[AMAG Pharmaceuticals]] that went nowhere.

2011-2014 — Retrophin: the piggy bank. Shkreli founded [[Retrophin]] in February 2011 under the MSMB umbrella, ostensibly as a rare disease biotech. In practice, it became the vehicle for covering MSMB's losses. Between September 2013 and March 2014, Shkreli and attorney Evan Greebel created four sham consulting agreements that funneled over $7.6M in cash and Retrophin stock to defrauded MSMB investors, plus $3.4M in settlement payments to seven more — total damage to Retrophin exceeding $11M. He also used Retrophin funds for personal expenses, hired private investigators, and traded company stock during blackout periods. The board fired him in September 2014. Retrophin later sued for $65M in damages (settled June 2019 from prison; Retrophin actually paid Shkreli an undisclosed amount). Under Shkreli, Retrophin had also acquired Thiola (tiopronin, for cystinuria) and raised its price from $1.50 to $30 per pill — a 2,000% increase that was a dry run for Daraprim. Retrophin rebranded as [[Travere Therapeutics]] in November 2020.

2015 — Turing and Daraprim: the price heard round the world. Shkreli founded Turing Pharmaceuticals in February 2015, weeks after leaving Retrophin. On August 10, 2015, Turing acquired [[Daraprim]] (pyrimethamine, an anti-parasitic critical for HIV/AIDS patients with toxoplasmosis, FDA-approved since 1953, off-patent but with no generic available) from [[Impax Laboratories]] for $55M — Impax had valued it at $19M NPV with ~$4M annual net sales. In September 2015, Turing raised the price from $13.50 to $750 per pill. The 5,456% increase made global headlines. The Infectious Diseases Society of America, the HIV Medicine Association, and even [[PhRMA]] (the industry's own lobby) condemned it. [[Hillary Clinton]], [[Bernie Sanders]], and [[Donald Trump]] all attacked the pricing during the 2016 campaign. All 50 state attorneys general plus DC and Puerto Rico called on Turing to reverse the hike. [[Imprimis Pharmaceuticals]] CEO Mark Baum announced a compounded alternative for $0.99/pill in October 2015. Shkreli promised to lower the price "in the weeks ahead" — on November 23, 2015, Turing announced it would not reduce the list price, offering only 50% hospital volume discounts and smaller pill bottles. The deal's anticompetitive structure — distribution restrictions specifically designed to prevent generic manufacturers from obtaining samples for bioequivalence testing — became the basis of the later FTC antitrust case.

November-December 2015 — KaloBios: the final spin. On November 18, 2015, an investor group led by Shkreli announced acquisition of >50% of [[KaloBios Pharmaceuticals]], a failing biotech. Shkreli became CEO and Chairman the next day. The stock surged from ~$2 to over $14 in five trading days as shorts were squeezed. On December 17, 2015, the FBI arrested Shkreli at his Manhattan apartment — on the MSMB/Retrophin fraud charges, not the drug pricing. KaloBios fired him December 21 and filed Chapter 11 by December 29.

February 2016 — Congressional theater. Subpoenaed by the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform, Shkreli appeared on February 4, 2016 alongside Turing and [[Valeant Pharmaceuticals]] executives. Attorney [[Benjamin Brafman]] advised him to invoke the Fifth Amendment on every question except two (confirming his name and that he was following counsel's advice). Rep. Trey Gowdy questioned him; Rep. Elijah Cummings rebuked him for grinning. Chairman Jason Chaffetz dismissed him. Shkreli tweeted afterward calling members of Congress "imbeciles."

2017 — Trial and conviction. The trial lasted over a month in Brooklyn federal court before Judge Kiyo A. Matsumoto. On August 4, 2017, at approximately 2:37 PM on the fifth day of deliberations, the jury found Shkreli guilty on 3 of 8 counts: two counts of securities fraud (MSMB Capital and MSMB Healthcare) and one count of conspiracy to commit securities fraud (Retrophin). He was acquitted on five counts including wire fraud. On September 13, 2017, bail was revoked after he posted on Facebook offering $5,000 per strand of Hillary Clinton's hair during her book tour — the judge ruled it constituted "solicitation of an assault," triggering a Secret Service investigation. He was sent to the Metropolitan Detention Center in Brooklyn.

March 2018 — Sentencing and forfeiture. On March 9, 2018, Judge Matsumoto sentenced Shkreli to 7 years. She called him "genuinely remorseful" but faulted him for "repeatedly minimized" misconduct, citing an "egregious multitude of lies." Four days earlier, the court ordered forfeiture of $7.36M in assets: $5M cash from an E-Trade account (his bail collateral), the Wu-Tang Clan album "Once Upon a Time in Shaolin" (purchased for $2M in November 2015 — the only copy of an album recorded in secret over six years), an unreleased [[Lil Wayne]] album "The Carter V," a [[Picasso]] painting, and his stake in Vyera Pharmaceuticals. Separately, New York State had already seized an Enigma machine and letters by [[Isaac Newton]], [[Charles Darwin]], and [[Ada Lovelace]] against a $1.26M tax warrant in December 2016. In April 2018, the SEC secured a consent order banning Shkreli from the securities industry.

2018-2022 — Prison and civil penalties. Shkreli served time at multiple facilities: Metropolitan Detention Center Brooklyn, USP Canaan, FCI Fort Dix (NJ), and finally Federal Correctional Complex Allenwood (low security). Federal inmate number 87850-053. From Fort Dix in 2019, the Wall Street Journal reported he was using a contraband cellphone to effectively direct Vyera Pharmaceuticals operations. His appeal failed: the Second Circuit unanimously affirmed the conviction. In April 2020, he requested compassionate release citing COVID-19; denied. Meanwhile, the FTC brought the antitrust hammer: in January 2020, the FTC and seven states (CA, IL, NY, NC, OH, PA, VA) sued Shkreli, Vyera, and Phoenixus AG (Turing's latest name) for an anticompetitive scheme to monopolize Daraprim. Co-defendant Kevin Mulleady and Vyera settled in December 2021 (Vyera agreed to $40M). On January 14, 2022, Judge Denise Cote (SDNY) ruled against Shkreli after a bench trial: lifetime ban from the pharmaceutical industry and $64.6M in disgorgement. She found his conduct "egregious, deliberate, repetitive, long-running, and ultimately dangerous." The Second Circuit unanimously upheld in January 2024. The [[Supreme Court]] denied cert on October 7, 2024 — final. The Wu-Tang album was sold by the government in July 2021 to WTC Endeavours Limited (Hong Kong) for ~$2.23M, then resold to PleasrDAO (a crypto/NFT collective) for $4M.

2022-present — Post-prison. Shkreli was released from Allenwood on May 18, 2022 to a halfway house in Queens after ~5 years (good behavior credits). Supervised release completed September 14, 2022 — terms prohibited self-employment involving access to client money. Within days of release, he hosted a Twitter Spaces session talking up crypto (DeFi, [[Uniswap]], [[Algorand]], [[Solana]]). He announced "Druglike," a drug discovery software platform. He launched "Martin Shkreli Inu" cryptocurrency. In 2024, he claimed on a 7-hour X Spaces event to have created the DJT Solana memecoin with Barron Trump (unverified; the token subsequently crashed ~95%). He has been actively shorting biotech stocks from his personal account: [[Cassava Sciences]] (November 2024, stock fell 24%), INmune Bio (June 2024, stock fell 56%). In December 2024, he invested in TNF Pharmaceuticals, which pivoted to photonic computing as Q/C Technologies (NASDAQ: QCLS). In June 2024, he livestreamed the Wu-Tang album to ~4,900 listeners on X, claiming he'd "burned the album and sent it to like, 50 different chicks." PleasrDAO sued for misappropriation of trade secrets; a judge ordered Shkreli to surrender all copies (August 2024) and refused to dismiss the lawsuit (September 2025). He runs a Substack newsletter and continues pharma stock commentary despite the lifetime ban.
