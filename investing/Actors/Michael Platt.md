**Michael Platt** — Founder of [[BlueCrest|BlueCrest Capital Management]] (2000). Built the firm into a major hedge fund managing >$30bn at peak, then in 2015-2016 returned all external client capital (~$8bn) and converted BlueCrest into a family office managing only his personal wealth. Now operates from Jersey, [[Geneva]], London, New York, and [[Dubai]]. Estimated net worth ~$15bn+. Cited in the [[Financial Times|FT]]'s September 2025 [[Pod shop compensation]] explainer as one of the alternative bidders for hedge-fund trading talent.

## Synopsis

Platt's career bisects neatly around the 2015-2016 decision to return all external client capital and run [[BlueCrest]] as a family office. The pre-conversion arc is a conventional hedge-fund-founder trajectory — [[JPMorgan Chase|JPMorgan]] proprietary trading desk → BlueCrest founding in 2000 → scaling to >$30bn AUM. The post-conversion arc is structurally unusual: Platt chose to shrink the LP footprint to zero and operate on proprietary capital alone, citing fee compression, regulatory burden, and the operational distraction of LP relations. The bet has paid: post-2016 returns include +95% (2021), +153% (2022), and +73% (2025) on a ~$50-60bn capital base, compounding personal net worth that's now estimated at ~$15bn+. The 2025 Dubai DIFC registration extends the firm's already-extensive offshore footprint. In industry framing — including the [[Financial Times|FT]]'s September 2025 [[Pod shop compensation]] explainer — Platt's BlueCrest stands as the proof-of-concept that a hedge-fund founder can deliver scaled trading returns without LP scaffolding, and as a structural alternative bidder for top trading talent against the multi-manager pod shops.

## Career

### Pre-BlueCrest

Worked at [[JPMorgan Chase|JPMorgan]] proprietary trading desk in London before co-founding [[BlueCrest]] in 2000 with William Reeves. Background in fixed-income arbitrage and macro.

### BlueCrest founding (2000)

Built BlueCrest as a multi-strategy hedge fund focused on macro, rates, and convertible arbitrage. The firm grew rapidly through the 2000s and 2010s, peaking at >$30bn in AUM at one point.

### The 2015-2016 family-office conversion

The decision Platt is most associated with: returning all external capital to LPs and converting BlueCrest into a proprietary trading firm investing only his personal wealth. He cited fee compression, regulatory burden, and the operational distraction of LP relations as reasons.

The move was the most prominent of its kind in hedge-fund history. Most hedge fund founders take the opposite path — scaling AUM as long as performance justifies it. Platt instead chose to shrink the LP base to zero and operate on proprietary capital. Post-2016 returns have been extraordinary (multiple years of +50% to +150% returns), validating the structural decision — the family-office vehicle has no fee drag and no drawdown-trigger pressure from external investors.

### Post-2016 strategy

BlueCrest's proprietary trading has emphasised:
- Macro and rates positioning (Platt's original specialty)
- Currency trading (FX shorts have been a recurring theme)
- Quantitative strategies through dedicated systematic books
- Talent retention via partnership-style economics rather than LP-passthrough comp

## Relevance to the talent war

The [[Financial Times|FT]]'s September 2025 [[Pod shop compensation]] explainer named Platt's BlueCrest specifically as one of the firms that may absorb hedge-fund trading talent if the multi-manager pay arms-race cools. The structural argument: a family office investing one person's wealth can offer compensation structures (partnership equity, no public disclosure, family-office tax efficiency) that LP-funded pod shops cannot match.

BlueCrest does not publish its hiring strategy, but the scale of capital (~$50-60bn) and the returns track record mean the firm is structurally capable of competing with [[Millennium]], [[Citadel]], and [[Point72]] for top trader profiles. The firm's Dubai expansion in 2025 added a geographic talent pool to the existing London / New York / Geneva / Jersey footprint.

## Investment relevance

Why track Platt:

1. Family-office hedge-fund precedent. Platt's 2015-2016 conversion is the canonical example of a top hedge fund founder choosing proprietary capital over scaling LP-funded AUM. Subsequent conversions (smaller funds following his lead) suggest the model resonates.

2. Returns benchmark. Post-2016 BlueCrest performance is one of the highest-returning trading franchises in finance. The numbers are not directly comparable to LP-funded competitors, but they validate that without fee/drawdown constraints, top trading talent can deliver multiples of conventional hedge fund returns.

3. Talent war positioning. As one of the cited alternative bidders for hedge-fund PMs, BlueCrest is a structural lid on how aggressive [[Pod shop compensation|multi-manager pay packages]] can scale before BlueCrest-style family offices take the talent.
