# Wachtell Lipton

Wachtell Lipton Rosen & Katz — the most profitable law firm in the world on a per-lawyer basis, specializing in M&A, corporate governance, and takeover defense. Invented the poison pill (shareholder rights plan) in 1982, fundamentally reshaping the landscape of hostile takeovers and corporate control.

## Synopsis

Founded in 1965 by Martin Lipton, Herbert Wachtell, Leonard Rosen, and George Katz, the firm operates on a deliberately small-scale model: roughly 270 lawyers compared to thousands at peers like [[Skadden]] or Sullivan & Cromwell. This generates revenue per lawyer north of $7M — no other firm comes close. The economics work because WLRK focuses exclusively on bet-the-company transactions (mega-mergers, proxy fights, activism defense) where clients pay premium fees without blinking. The firm does not do volume work, lateral hiring, or international expansion; it is a New York-centric advisory practice that relies on reputation and repeat relationships.

The firm's influence extends beyond deal execution into corporate law doctrine. Martin Lipton's 1982 invention of the poison pill became the single most important defensive mechanism in M&A history, effectively giving boards the power to block hostile bids. WLRK remains the go-to defense counsel in contested M&A: when [[Elon Musk]] made his hostile bid for [[Twitter]] in 2022, the board's advisors included WLRK. For Harvey, the AI legal platform, the connection runs through former corporate partner Gordon Moodie (now CPO at [[Harvey]]). The firm's relevance to the vault is as a recurring actor in any significant M&A event — if a deal is large enough, WLRK is probably on one side.
