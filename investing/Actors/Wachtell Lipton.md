# Wachtell Lipton

Wachtell Lipton Rosen & Katz — the most profitable law firm in the world on a per-lawyer basis, specialising in bet-the-company M&A, corporate governance, and takeover defence. Invented the [[Poison pill|poison pill]] (shareholder rights plan) in 1982. Lockstep partnership and one-NY-office model is the archetypal alternative to [[Big Law transformation|"Kirklandisation"]]; the firm is now under sustained partner-level poaching pressure for the first time in its history. With [[Debevoise]]'s June 2025 addition of a non-equity partner tier, Wachtell is now the *only* major US law firm operating a pure all-equity-partner lockstep partnership.

## Synopsis

Founded in 1965 by [[Marty Lipton|Martin Lipton]], Herbert Wachtell, Leonard Rosen, and George Katz — four Jewish [[NYU Law School]] graduates who felt unwelcome at established "white shoe" New York firms. The original deal was a handshake; the firm still has no formal partnership agreement. The four built a niche in M&A defence work that older firms found unseemly, then rode the 1970s conglomerate-building era and the 1980s era of hostile raiders ([[Carl Icahn]], [[T. Boone Pickens]]) to industry-shaping influence — pre-emptive [[Poison pill|poison pills]] in the boardroom, defence in court.

Sixty years on, the firm remains <300 lawyers in one Midtown Manhattan office, ~80 partners, ~$1.4bn in 2025 revenue, and the highest profits per equity partner of any major US firm (~$12M in 2025, per Am Law 100). It advised on nine of the twenty largest US M&A transactions of 2025 ([[M&A tracker]]) and on six of the top ten US deals last year by value, including the pending [[Paramount Skydance]]–[[Warner Bros Discovery]] $109.9bn transaction, the [[Union Pacific]]–[[Norfolk Southern]] $87.7bn rail merger, the [[Saudi PIF|PIF]]-led [[Electronic Arts]] $48.1bn take-private, the [[Charter Communications]]–[[Cox Communications]] $35.2bn cable consolidation, the [[SoftBank]]-led $30bn [[OpenAI]] investment, and [[Palo Alto Networks]]'s $24.7bn acquisition of [[CyberArk]].

The firm's distinctive economics rest on three structural choices: lockstep compensation (pay tied to seniority, no internal competition for fees), one-partner-one-vote governance (despite no partnership agreement), and project-based fees with a large discretionary "success" component. The 2022 [[Twitter]] / [[Elon Musk]] deal closed with a $90M Wachtell fee against time sheets that suggested under $30M — that gap is the model. [[Andrew Nussbaum]] and [[William Savitt]] have co-chaired the executive committee since 2023; Savitt represents [[OpenAI]] in its dispute with [[Elon Musk]], cross-examined the billionaire in early May 2026, and then saw an advisory jury reject Musk's damages claim on May 18. Real authority within the firm still concentrates around [[Marty Lipton]] (94, in office almost daily) and especially [[Edward Herlihy]] (79, his protégé and the firm's largest individual revenue generator across decades of bank-sector dealmaking).

## 2025-2026 partner exodus

For most of the firm's history, lateral departures were essentially nil. Six years ago — with the 2019/2020 departure of M&A partner Ed Lee to [[Kirkland & Ellis]] — that ended; Lee has since gone on to advise on some of Kirkland's largest deals. Since the start of 2025, eight partners have left.

| Departure | Practice | Destination | Reported package |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Josh Feltman]] | Corporate restructuring (head) | [[Kirkland & Ellis]] | $80M / 3 years |
| Four partners (group) | M&A / deal | [[Latham & Watkins]] | Not disclosed |
| Three other partners | Mixed | Various | Not disclosed |
| Ed Lee (2019/20, predecessor) | M&A | [[Kirkland & Ellis]] | Not disclosed |

The defection pattern fits the [[Big Law transformation]] economics: a high-revenue mid-career Wachtell partner is structurally underpaid against peer market rates because lockstep transfers compensation upward with tenure and laterally to non-deal practices (litigation, antitrust, tax) that cannot match a top originator's revenue. A guaranteed $80M / 3-year package at Kirkland converts that lockstep tax into immediate cash and removes career-long retention risk.

## Lockstep modifications (2025-26)

The firm has explicitly broken with several of [[Marty Lipton]]'s 1990 "35 principles" in response to the defections, while preserving the core architecture:

- *Compensation transparency reduced.* Partners are no longer fully informed about each other's compensation, ending the historical visibility that made lockstep self-policing.
- *Points system tilted toward deal lawyers.* Equity points have been reallocated away from litigators and toward partners who originate deal-fee revenue.
- *First-year partner equity halved.* New partners receive roughly half the equity allocation they would have received under the prior system, conserving capacity for senior-tier discretion.
- *Younger leadership promotions.* [[Jacob Kling]] (40, [[Edward Herlihy]] protégé) and David Lam (50) named co-heads of the M&A practice; Kling joined the executive committee alongside [[Kevin Schwartz]] (Lipton's son-in-law).

Direction of travel is to compress, not abandon, lockstep — keeping firm-wide median pay above industry benchmarks while increasing the senior committee's ability to top up retention packages for partners under outside threat. Insiders quoted in the FT split on whether the changes go far enough; some associates were unsettled by the cultural shift, others noted that the litigation specialists losing points are the next defection candidates.

## Why the model still pays

Despite the defection wave, the 2025 financial scoreboard is the strongest in the firm's history. In late February 2026, [[Andrew Nussbaum]] emailed the firm to note Wachtell had advised on transactions worth $230bn announced in the preceding five days *and* won a landmark corporate-governance case at Delaware's Supreme Court — ostensibly the textbook expression of the lockstep model's cross-practice integration. That same week, two partners left.

The economics that hold the model together:

1. *Cross-practice integration.* Bet-the-company assignments require simultaneous senior attention from M&A, litigation, antitrust, tax, and executive comp. Lockstep removes internal billing fights that would block this in a points-by-revenue firm.
2. *Project-based "success" fees.* The Twitter/Musk $90M-on-$30M-of-time-sheets fee is the model. Wachtell's revenue is fee-event-driven, not hour-rate-driven, which compounds with deal size — the bigger the deal, the bigger the discretionary fee.
3. *Selective hiring + small partnership.* Roughly 80 partners, no acqui-hires, deeply vetted. Per-partner revenue stays high because the denominator stays small.
4. *Multi-decade rainmaker continuity.* [[Edward Herlihy]] has advised on virtually every major bank-sector transaction over three decades; [[Marty Lipton]] continues to drive corporate-governance doctrine at 94. A firm that retires partners by mid-60s (the Kirkland model) gives up that compounding.
