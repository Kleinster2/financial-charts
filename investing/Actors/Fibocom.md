**Fibocom** — Shenzhen-based wireless-module supplier and the second major Chinese player in the global cellular IoT module market.

Fibocom matters because it makes the Chinese module story structural rather than single-name. The company generated roughly RMB 8.19 billion of revenue in 2024, held about 10% of global cellular IoT module revenue in Q1 2024, and extends the same embedded-connectivity logic that makes [[Quectel]] strategically sensitive. Commercially, it is a diversified wireless-module supplier spanning [[5G]], LTE, smart, Wi-Fi, automotive, robotics, and AIoT deployments. Politically, it gets swept into the same U.S. debate over whether Chinese module vendors should remain inside critical connectivity paths across industrial systems, logistics assets, and defense-adjacent infrastructure.

## Why it matters

Fibocom is smaller than [[Quectel]], but that is exactly why it matters analytically. If Washington only had to worry about one dominant Chinese module vendor, the substitution path would be easier to imagine. Fibocom's presence means the issue is a clustered supplier base with enough scale to shape the embedded-connectivity market as a whole.

## U.S. policy pressure

- September 2023, [[Reuters]] reported that U.S. officials were considering whether equipment from Fibocom and [[Quectel]] should be added to the [[Covered List]].
- April 2026, the broader module-risk debate argued that Fibocom's embedded position inside connected systems creates surveillance and operational-disruption risk if Chinese state leverage becomes part of the threat model.
- Fibocom is therefore part of the same policy basket as [[Quectel]], even if public attention remains more concentrated on the larger peer.

## Evolution

- 1999, Fibocom was founded in Shenzhen as China's electronics ecosystem was moving from assembly into higher-value wireless components. That timing matters because the company grew alongside the country's deepening hardware supply-chain base.
- The 4G and early IoT years turned Fibocom into a meaningful module supplier, but the later push into automotive, robotics, and AIoT is what broadened the strategic relevance of the company beyond commodity communications modules.
- 2023 to 2024, Fibocom posted strong scale and still-improving profitability, which reinforced the idea that Chinese suppliers were entrenching themselves across the module layer rather than competing only on price.
- By 2026, the issue was no longer simply whether Fibocom could keep growing. It was whether Western infrastructure buyers and regulators would keep tolerating Chinese suppliers in the embedded-connectivity path.
