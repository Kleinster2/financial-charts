**WeWork** — Coworking and flexible workspace company, the emblematic startup-era cautionary tale. Founded 2010 by [[Adam Neumann]] and [[Miguel McKelvey]]. Valued at $47B by [[SoftBank]] in January 2019, filed Chapter 11 in November 2023, emerged June 2024 with [[Yardi Systems]] as 60% owner. Now EBITDA-positive, ~600 locations, $3.98B revenue.

---

## Synopsis

WeWork is the most expensive lesson in venture-era corporate governance. [[SoftBank]]'s Vision Fund poured ~$16B into a commercial real estate subletting operation dressed as a tech company, peaking at a $47B private valuation in January 2019. The August 2019 S-1 filing revealed [[Adam Neumann]]'s self-dealing — $5.9M trademark payments to himself, buildings he owned leased back to the company, 20:1 supervoting shares, family on the payroll — and the IPO imploded within six weeks. Neumann exited with ~$1.7B. The company burned through $3-4B/year in losses for four straight years before filing Chapter 11 on November 6, 2023, listing $18.65B in liabilities against $15.06B in assets.

The post-bankruptcy story is unexpectedly functional. WeWork emerged on June 11, 2024 after seven months in Chapter 11, shedding $4B in debt and ~$12B in future lease obligations, exiting ~160 unprofitable locations, and renegotiating ~170 leases. [[Yardi Systems]] (through affiliate Cupar Grimmond) invested $337M for a 60% stake. Under CEO John Santora (a 47-year Cushman & Wakefield veteran), the company hit six consecutive months of EBITDA profitability by early 2025, posted $3.98B revenue (+8.5% YoY), and is forecast to reach $101M net profit in 2025. The business model has shifted from long-term master leases to asset-light management and revenue-share agreements, including a ~1M sq ft enterprise contract with [[Amazon]]. The question is whether this is a real business at a $750M post-emergence valuation, or a zombie brand coasting on sunk-cost leases.

## Evolution

The story of WeWork is the story of what happens when venture capital's growth-at-all-costs logic meets a fundamentally low-margin real estate business — and nobody asks the obvious questions until it's too late.

- 2008–2010 (GreenDesk): [[Adam Neumann]] and [[Miguel McKelvey]] met at a rooftop party in Tribeca around 2005. In May 2008 they launched GreenDesk, an eco-friendly coworking space in Brooklyn, partnering with a local landlord. By 2009 it had 100+ offices renting at $350–$2,400/month. They sold their stakes to the landlord in 2010 for ~$500K each — enough to start over. The insight was real: freelancers and small companies wanted flexible, furnished office space without long-term leases. The business model was straightforward arbitrage — sign long leases, subdivide, mark up.

- 2010–2013 (SoHo launch, first unicorn): First WeWork location opened April 2011 in SoHo, Manhattan. Brooklyn real estate developer Joel Schreiber bought 33% for $15M, valuing the company at ~$45M before it had a single paying member. By end of 2011: four NYC locations, a waitlist, near break-even. [[Benchmark]] led the Series A in 2012 at ~$97M. The Series C in February 2014 hit $1.5B — unicorn status in under four years, with [[JPMorgan]] leading. At this stage the financials actually worked: small scale, high occupancy, NYC rents supporting the markup. The problem was what came next.

- 2014–2016 (hypergrowth): Neumann pivoted from "profitable coworking company" to "world's first physical social network" — language designed to justify tech-company multiples on a real estate business. [[Goldman Sachs]] and T. Rowe Price led the Series D at $5B. Locations multiplied across the U.S. and internationally. Series F brought Chinese investors (Hony Capital, Legend Holdings) at $16.9B. Revenue was growing — $436M in 2016 — but so were losses ($0.4B). The unit economics of signing long-term leases and subletting short-term only work at high occupancy in strong markets. WeWork was scaling into weak markets to justify valuations, not profitability.

- 2017 (SoftBank arrives): The relationship that defined WeWork's trajectory began on December 6, 2016. [[Masayoshi Son]] toured WeWork HQ — a planned 2-hour visit cut to 12 minutes because Son was late to meet president-elect Trump at Trump Tower. He invited Neumann into his SUV. Son asked: "In a fight, who wins — the smart guy or the crazy guy?" Neumann answered correctly: the crazy guy. Son told him to think bigger. He sketched out a $4.4B investment on his iPad in the car. Within 30 minutes, Son emailed a photo of the digital napkin contract. The money arrived in August 2017 via the Vision Fund, valuing WeWork at $20B. This single meeting — 12 minutes of tour, 30 minutes in an SUV — produced the largest venture investment in a real estate company in history. [[SoftBank]] saw WeWork as the [[Alibaba]] of physical space. What they actually bought was a lease arbitrage business run by a charismatic founder with no financial discipline.

- 2018 (peak expansion, peak excess): Revenue hit $1.8B but losses reached $1.9B — the company lost more than it earned. Locations expanded to 287 across 23 countries. Membership approached 500K. WeWork went on an acquisition spree: Meetup ($156M), Flatiron School, Conductor (SEO), Teem ($100M), Spacious. The company bought a $60M Gulfstream G650ER jet. On a flight to Israel, the jet was recalled by its owner after marijuana was found on board hidden in a cereal box. Rebekah Neumann launched WeGrow, a private school in Chelsea designed by Bjarke Ingels, tuition $22K–$42K/year. In January 2019, the company rebranded to "The We Company" to reflect these broader ambitions. [[SoftBank]] added another $3B in November 2018 (pushing valuation toward $42B) and $2B in January 2019 (peak $47B). At this point SoftBank had invested over $10B. Neumann had cashed out ~$700M in personal share sales.

- 2019 (the fall): The S-1 filed August 14, 2019 was the moment the market read the fine print. Key revelations: Neumann held 20:1 supervoting shares. He charged WeWork $5.9M for the "We" trademark, which he personally owned. He leased four buildings he owned back to WeWork with $236.6M in future minimum rent owed to him. Rebekah Neumann had a contractual role in CEO succession. The filing disclosed $1.9B in losses on $1.8B revenue in 2018, and another $900M loss in H1 2019 alone. The company introduced "Community Adjusted EBITDA" — a metric that excluded nearly all costs — which became a punchline. By September, the IPO target was slashed from $47B to $20–30B. [[SoftBank]] itself urged WeWork to pull the offering. On September 24, Neumann resigned as CEO. On September 30, the S-1 was formally withdrawn. In October, SoftBank organized a $9.5–10B rescue package at ~$8B valuation. Neumann's exit terms: ~$970M for remaining shares, $185M consulting fee, $500M credit line — roughly $1.7B total. The man who nearly bankrupted the company walked away a billionaire. WeWork laid off 2,400 employees in November.

- 2020–2021 (triage, then SPAC): Sandeep Mathrani, a veteran real estate executive from [[Brookfield]]/GGP, took over as CEO in February 2020 — just weeks before COVID emptied offices worldwide. He closed WeLive and WeGrow, divested non-core acquisitions (Meetup, Flatiron School, Conductor), renegotiated leases, and cut headcount from ~15,000 to ~5,000. Despite COVID, the model stabilized. In March 2021, WeWork announced a SPAC merger with BowX Acquisition Corp (Vivek Ranadive's vehicle) at a $9B valuation with an $800M PIPE. Trading began October 20, 2021 under ticker "WE" on NYSE. Opening price ~$11.78. Revenue was $2.57B in 2021 with a $4.6B loss — the worst single-year loss in the company's history, driven by restructuring charges.

- 2022–2023 (final decline): Revenue recovered to $3.24B in 2022 with losses narrowing to $2.3B, but occupancy never recovered to pre-COVID levels in many markets. Mathrani stepped down in May 2023; David Tolley became interim CEO. The stock, already in single digits, required a 1-for-40 reverse split in August 2023 to stay listed. By late 2023 the situation was terminal: $18.65B in liabilities, leases that couldn't be supported by occupancy, and no path to profitability at scale. On November 6, 2023, WeWork filed Chapter 11 in New Jersey. [[SoftBank]]'s total loss: estimated at $11.5B. [[Masayoshi Son]] had called the investment "foolish" in 2020 and said he was "embarrassed and ashamed" about Vision Fund management in 2022.

- 2024 (emergence): WeWork exited ~160 unprofitable locations, renegotiated ~170 leases, eliminated $4B in debt and ~$12B in future lease obligations. [[Yardi Systems]] (through affiliate Cupar Grimmond) invested $337M for 60% ownership. [[SoftBank]] retained a 20% stake (down from ~70% pre-bankruptcy). The plan was approved May 30, 2024. WeWork emerged on June 11, 2024, debt-free, under new CEO John Santora. Neumann had submitted a $500–650M bid to reacquire WeWork during bankruptcy — rejected by the court. He abandoned it in May 2024.

- 2025–present (the quiet rebuild): Under Santora, the company hit six consecutive months of EBITDA profitability by early 2025. Revenue reached $3.98B (+8.5% YoY). Net profit forecast: $101M in 2025, $343M by 2028. The business model shifted toward asset-light management agreements and enterprise contracts — including ~1M sq ft managed for [[Amazon]] across NYC, Silicon Valley, Dallas, and Nashville. WeWork now operates ~600 locations across 37 countries. It's no longer pretending to be a tech company. The question is whether the brand — after becoming synonymous with startup excess — can survive as a real estate services firm. Early signs in NYC suggest demand is there. The $80–100M in planned property upgrades signal a company that believes it has a future.

## S-1 revelations (August 14, 2019)

The self-dealing disclosures that killed the IPO:

- $5.9M payment to Neumann's personal LLC for the "We" trademark (returned after backlash)
- Four buildings owned by Neumann leased back to WeWork ($236.6M in future minimum lease payments)
- $362M loan from WeWork to Neumann to exercise stock options
- Neumann held 20:1 supervoting shares
- Spousal succession clause giving Rebekah Neumann influence over CEO replacement
- "Community Adjusted EBITDA" — excluded nearly all costs
- Family members and friends on payroll throughout the organization
