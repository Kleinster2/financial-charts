**Chinese teapot refineries** — Independent (non-state) Chinese refineries, concentrated in Shandong province. Primary buyers of sanctioned Iranian and Russian oil. Iran's "economic lifeline."

---

## What are teapots

Private Chinese refineries operating outside the state-owned system ([[Sinopec]], [[CNPC]], [[CNOOC]]). Called "teapots" because they were originally small-scale — now some are massive. Officially known as "local refineries" in [[China]].

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Share of China refining capacity | ~25% |
| Location | Mostly Shandong province |
| Number of refineries | ~40 with import quotas |
| Key product | Refined fuels for domestic market |

### Origins

Teapots originated in northeastern China to process crude from the Shengli oil field, historically one of China's backbone production areas. For most of their existence, teapots had no right to import or process foreign crude. This changed in 2015 when Beijing granted import licenses to teapots meeting certain requirements: decommissioning highly polluting crude distillation units, building natural gas storage capacity, and other environmental standards. Qualifying refineries received import quotas from the Ministry of Commerce. When they first gained import access, teapots had a diverse slate of suppliers — but as sanctions tightened on [[Iran]], [[Russia]], and [[Venezuela]], and discounts on sanctioned crude grew, teapots stepped into the void vacated by risk-averse buyers.

[[Erica Downs]] ([[Columbia Center on Global Energy Policy]], Mar 4 2026): *"The teapots are much smaller, less sophisticated, and they rely on the discounts they can receive on sanctioned crudes to boost their bottom lines. And in some cases, they probably depend on these crudes for their survival."*

## Why teapots matter

**They're the demand side of sanctions evasion.**

| Sanctioned supplier | Teapot role |
|---------------------|-------------|
| [[Iran]] | Primary buyer — "economic lifeline" |
| [[Russia]] | Major buyer of discounted Urals crude |
| [[Venezuela]] | Some purchases |

**Iranian oil = ~12% of China's crude oil imports** (~1.4M bpd out of 11.6M bpd total, 2025) — almost entirely through teapots. [[Reuters]] estimated China saved $10B on crude oil imports through sanctioned crude discounts (2023 calculation).

### Why national oil companies don't buy Iranian crude

[[Sinopec]], [[CNPC]], and [[CNOOC]] were major Iranian crude buyers 10-15 years ago but exited entirely due to US sanctions risk. These are global companies with global operations — they have a vested interest in maintaining access to the US dollar financial system. Teapots, by contrast, have little or no need for dollar system access. Being sanctioned wouldn't be catastrophic for a Shandong refinery the way it would be for Sinopec. This risk tolerance, combined with the pursuit of discounted barrels, made teapots Iran's primary customer.

Downs (Mar 4 2026): *"The national oil companies have a vested interest in maintaining access to the US dollar financial system... if you look at the teapots who are still buying Iranian crude today, I suspect that they have little or no interest in maintaining access to the US dollar financial system."*

## How the system works

```
Iran/Russia exports oil
    → Shadow fleet transports
        → Ship-to-ship transfer near Malaysia
            → Relabeled as "Malaysian" or "Indonesian"
                → Teapot refinery in Shandong buys
                    → Refined into domestic fuel
```

**Why teapots do it:**
- Sanctioned oil sells at $10-20/barrel discount
- Higher margins than buying Brent-priced crude
- Government tacitly allows it via quota system

## Quota system

Beijing controls teapot imports through quota allocations:

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Allocator | Ministry of Commerce |
| Frequency | Tranches throughout year |
| 2025 allocation | ~7-8M tons to ~20 teapots per tranche |
| 2026 total | 257M metric tons (non-state firms), unchanged from 2025 |
| Exhaustion risk | Teapots stop buying when quota runs out |

**2026 quota structure:** First two batches expected to cover ~70% of yearly allocation. In 2025, the first two batches accounted for each refiner's full-year allocation.

**Fall 2025 bottleneck:** Many teapots exhausted quotas by Oct-Nov, stopped buying Iranian crude → 52M barrels stuck in floating storage → Iran's USD inflows collapsed → rial accelerated decline.

## US sanctions on teapots (2025)

**Historic escalation:** US began directly sanctioning Chinese refineries for first time.

| Date | Refinery | Violation |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Mar 2025 | **Shandong Shouguang Luqing Petrochemical** | ~$500M Iranian oil purchases |
| Apr 2025 | **Shandong Shengxing Chemical** | $1B+ Iranian crude, including from IRGC-QF front company |
| May 2025 | **Hebei Xinhai Chemical Group** | Iranian oil purchases |

**Impact:**
- Five Shandong refineries stopped buying Iranian crude in April 2025
- Others continued but with more caution
- Non-sanctioned tankers joined trade to reduce exposure

> "Teapot refineries are the largest purchasers of Iranian oil, providing the Islamic [[Republic]] its primary economic lifeline." — Treasury Secretary Bessent

## Operating rates

| Period | Capacity utilization |
|--------|---------------------|
| Feb 2025 | <45% (lowest in 2+ years) |
| Mar 2025 | 46% (slight recovery) |
| Late 2025 | Subdued, weak margins |

Of 49 independent refineries in Shandong, only 27 were operational as of early 2025 — the rest idled without access to domestic crude production.

**Drivers of low utilization:**
- US sanctions disrupting supply
- Weak domestic fuel demand (China slowdown)
- Quota constraints
- Margin compression
- Since Jan 2025, Beijing allows only 40-80% consumption tax offset on fuel oil imports (vs. 100% prior), adding ~$2/bbl to import costs
- EV adoption and LNG-powered trucks eating into transportation fuel demand

## Comeback and consolidation (mid-2025)

Despite the headwinds, bankrupt teapots are being acquired and restarted under new ownership:

| Refinery | Buyer | Status |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Shandong Changyi Petrochemical (ex-[[Sinochem]]) | Weifang Hongrun Petrochemical | **Resumed operations** on domestic crude |
| Zhenghe Group | In talks with buyers | Bankrupt, pending acquisition |
| Shandong Huaxing Petrochemical Group | In talks with buyers | Bankrupt, pending acquisition |

All three are applying for government import quotas — combined ask of up to ~300K bpd.

**Central vs. local government tension:** Beijing wants to consolidate — shuttering small plants to clear the way for a large-scale private refining and petrochemical complex on Yulong Island. But Shandong provincial government is actively supporting teapot survival as an economic driver, even increasing consumption tax rebates for six local refiners. Advanced liquids-to-chemicals complexes in neighboring provinces are eroding Shandong's competitiveness, adding urgency to the provincial pushback.

## Supply diversification

[[Rystad Energy]] suggests teapots may pivot from [[Iran]]ian crude toward [[Russia]], Brazil, or Middle Eastern suppliers if sanctions make Iranian oil costlier. Alternatively, they reduce runs further. China's crude imports hit a record 11.6M bpd in 2025 as low prices and geopolitical tensions spurred stockpile building.

### Double supply shock (Feb-Mar 2026)

Teapots faced two supply disruptions in rapid succession:

1. **[[Venezuela]] disruption (Feb 2026):** Trump's [[Maduro capture 2026|removal of Maduro]] and US takeover of Venezuelan crude marketing raised questions about continued flow. [[Venezuela]] supplied ~400K bpd to China in 2025 (3-4% of total crude imports), virtually all to teapots. Even if Venezuelan oil remained available, pricing likely worsened.

2. **Iran/Hormuz disruption (Mar 2026):** In anticipation of Venezuelan shortfalls, teapots had pivoted to buying more Iranian heavy crude (decent substitute, available at discount). Then the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran conflict]] and [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure disrupted that supply too.

### Buffers

Despite the double squeeze, several buffers exist:
- Iranian and Russian crude in **floating storage** off the coast of China and Malaysia — had been building up before the strikes
- Iranian oil in **bonded storage** at Chinese ports, available to draw on
- China's [[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]] provides ~120 days of net crude oil import cover at 2025 levels (strategic + commercial stockpiles combined)
- Russian crude remains available as a substitute

Downs (Mar 4 2026): *"If you look at the Iranian crude and perhaps other flows that might be disrupted by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, they're sitting in a pretty good position."*
