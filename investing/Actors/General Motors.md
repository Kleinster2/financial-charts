## EV pullback (2025-2026)

GM rolled out a $7 billion EV expansion plan in 2022, with electric trucks as the centerpiece. Targeted 600,000 EVs/year in North America by 2025. Actual December 2025 run rate: ~8,000/month (~96K annualized).

| Metric | Planned | Actual |
|--------|---------|--------|
| NA EV target (2025) | 600,000/year | ~96K run rate (Dec 2025) |
| Silverado EV sales (2025) | — | ~11,000 |
| Supplier compensation | — | ~$400M paid out |
| Total EV/supplier charges | — | $50B+ across industry |

Barra continues to make the case against hybrids while Ford pivots to extended-range EVs. GM idled the Detroit factory building electric trucks (late March 2026) due to weak demand.

CFO Paul Jacobson on tariff uncertainty: *"It was like driving 24 Hours of Daytona in a fog. We had no idea where we were going."* On EV reset: *"The landscape shifted so dramatically that we had to reset, otherwise we were going to be climbing this mountain for more than a decade."* Suppliers demanded compensation: *"Everybody was saying, 'You need to true me up.'"*

On gas prices from Iran war: 4-6 months of higher prices needed before most Americans would reconsider fuel-efficient vehicles. *"We certainly don't see it today."*

Key supplier impact: [[Magna]] built a $575M factory in St. Clair, Michigan specifically for GM battery enclosures; now mostly empty. GM lifted exclusivity restrictions on the plant.

*Source: WSJ (Sharon Terlep), March 31, 2026*

## Defense (GM Defense + Pentagon talks Apr 2026)

GM operates a dedicated defense subsidiary, GM Defense, working with the US military on capabilities including autonomous vehicles capable of transporting wounded personnel. The company is also part of the consortium developing a new lunar rover for [[NASA]]'s [[Artemis]] program; the rover's battery and off-road capabilities are of direct interest to military planners — a dual-use read-through that is exactly the [[Defense procurement reform]] thesis architected by [[Dan Driscoll]] and [[Pete Hegseth]].

In April 2026 the [[Wall Street Journal|WSJ]] (originally) and [[Financial Times|FT]] (Davies, Mackinnon — Apr 15, 2026) reported that the [[Pentagon|Department of War]] held wide-ranging talks with GM's CEO and senior executives about contributing components or parts to existing weapons systems, including through advanced manufacturing techniques such as 3D printing. The discussions go beyond GM Defense's existing contracts and focus on how Detroit auto manufacturing can break supply-chain bottlenecks at the [[Defense Primes]]. Talks pre-date the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] and are explicitly preliminary — focused on components, not whole weapons systems.

GM statement: "For more than 100 years, GM has supported America's security, safety and those who protect our nation. While that commitment continues, we do not comment on speculation."

The strategic signal: [[Pete Hegseth]]'s [[Pentagon|Department of War]] is operationalizing the [[Defense procurement reform|90/10 procurement inversion]] — moving from purpose-built specialist contracts toward commercial off-the-shelf and dual-use capacity. GM is the more naturally positioned of the two Detroit automakers in the talks; [[Ford]] does not run an equivalent defense subsidiary.

For investors the structural read is that GM has optionality on a defense-revenue stream the market does not yet price. The EV pullback above documents a margin and capacity-utilization problem that defense work could help offset, particularly at idled facilities like the Detroit electric-truck plant. Whether senior management is willing to lean into a defense pivot — and whether [[Mary Barra]] sees it as margin-accretive use of underutilized capacity vs a political distraction — will determine how seriously this scales beyond preliminary talks.

*Sources: FT (Davies, Mackinnon — Apr 15, 2026), originally [[Wall Street Journal|WSJ]].*

## Q1 2026 results (Apr 28, 2026) — Supreme Court IEEPA ruling drives guidance raise

GM reported Q1 2026 earnings before market open and the headline was the FY guidance raise tied directly to the [[2026 Supreme Court IEEPA ruling]] reducing GM's expected tariff costs by ~$500M. Adj EPS $3.70 vs $2.62 consensus — a 41% beat on the EPS line — though revenue at $43.62B was down 1% YoY.

| Metric | Q1 2026 | Q1 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $43.62B | $44.02B (-1%) |
| Adj EPS | $3.70 | beat consensus $2.62 by 41% |
| Adj earnings (basis) | $4.3B | +21.9% YoY |
| GAAP net income | lower | EV restructuring + supplier comp charges |

### Guidance raise — Supreme Court tariff windfall

| FY 2026 guidance | Old | New |
|---|---|---|
| Adj EBIT | $13.0-15.0B | $13.5-15.5B (+$500M) |
| Adj EPS | $11.00-$13.00 | $11.50-$13.50 (+$0.50) |

The raise reflects GM's expected receipt of tariff rebates after the [[Supreme Court]] ruled against the [[Trump administration]]'s use of [[IEEPA|International Emergency Economic Powers Act]] to impose certain tariffs. GM had been booking incremental commodity and freight costs against the prior IEEPA-era rates. The legal reversal flows back as a near-term margin tailwind.

CFO [[Paul Jacobson]] characterization (Q1 call): the company expects "incremental commodity and freight costs versus original guidance" but the tariff rebate offsets and exceeds those headwinds. The raise is structural — reflecting policy reversal — not operational.

### Supreme Court ruling — broader read

The IEEPA ruling is a cross-actor catalyst because the [[Trump administration]]'s use of IEEPA to impose tariffs was the dominant tariff vehicle for 2025-2026. Other actors with disclosed IEEPA-tariff exposure who could see similar rebate windfalls:

| Actor | IEEPA exposure | Likely rebate path |
|---|---|---|
| [[Ford]] | Steel, aluminum, components | Direct, through similar guidance update |
| [[Stellantis]] | Mexican / Canadian production | Direct |
| [[Toyota]] / [[Honda]] / [[Nissan]] | Japanese-origin imports | Indirect, via dealer-network economics |
| [[Caterpillar]] | Steel, foreign components | Direct |
| [[Boeing]] | Aluminum, titanium | Already partially disclosed |
| [[Apple]] | Chinese-origin assembly | Indirect, via supply-chain margins |

This is the cleanest broad-market positive in the Q1 earnings cycle so far — the IEEPA ruling reverses a real cost line for the dollar-importer cohort, and FY guides should bake in that reversal across multiple sectors over the next 30-60 days. Watch for similar guidance updates from the auto OEM peer group at their respective Q1 prints.
