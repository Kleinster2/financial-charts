**Zenefits** was a cloud-based HR platform for SMBs, co-founded by [[Parker Conrad]] and Laks Srini in 2013. Reached ~$4.5B valuation before a 2016 compliance scandal — unlicensed insurance brokers operating in multiple states — led to Conrad's resignation, regulatory fines, and a dramatic fall that became a Silicon Valley cautionary tale about growth-at-all-costs culture.

## Synopsis

Zenefits' original pitch was elegant: give away HR software for free and monetize through health insurance brokerage commissions — a loss-leader model that drove explosive SMB adoption. The company signed up thousands of small businesses and raised over $580M from [[Andreessen Horowitz]], [[TPG]], [[Fidelity]], and others at a $4.5B peak valuation. The problem was that the growth machine outran compliance infrastructure. Zenefits employees were selling health insurance in states where they lacked brokerage licenses, a violation that CEO Parker Conrad allegedly knew about and tried to work around using internal software tools that let employees cheat on licensing exams. When the scheme was exposed in late 2015, Conrad resigned and the company brought in David Sacks (co-founder of [[Yammer]], early [[PayPal]] executive) as CEO to stabilize operations.

The aftermath was a textbook down-round restructuring. The $4.5B valuation was cut to $2B, headcount was slashed from ~1,600 to ~450, and Zenefits paid $3.4M in regulatory fines to settle with California, Washington, and other state insurance departments. Sacks refocused the business on paid software subscriptions rather than insurance commissions, but the reputational damage was done. [[TriNet]] acquired Zenefits in 2022 for an undisclosed amount, likely pennies on the peak valuation dollar. The lasting significance is as a launching pad: Parker Conrad took the lessons from Zenefits' failure and built [[Rippling]], which reached a $13.5B valuation by 2024 by solving the same HR/payroll/benefits problem with better compliance architecture and a compound software strategy.
