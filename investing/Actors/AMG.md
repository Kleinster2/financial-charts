**AMG Critical Materials N.V.** (Euronext Amsterdam: AMG) — Dutch critical-metals group operating integrated mining, processing and engineering businesses. The lithium story sits on a **Brazil-to-Germany route**: spodumene concentrate from the Mibra mine (Minas Gerais, [[Brazil]]), converted in [[China]] from ~6% to ~100% lithium content, then processed into lithium hydroxide at AMG's **Bitterfeld-Wolfen plant in [[Germany]]**. AMG is attempting to break that China-intermediation model by building domestic European LiOH capacity — but the timeline has slipped.

| Key processing asset | Bitterfeld-Wolfen LiOH plant, [[Germany]] |

AMG matters for three structural reasons:

1. **Only vertically integrated Brazil-to-Europe lithium route.** Most Brazilian producers ([[Sigma Lithium]], [[Atlas Lithium]]) export concentrate to Chinese converters. AMG's Bitterfeld plant is the European Union's bet on a non-China LiOH supply chain.
2. **Long-life resource base.** Mibra has been operating since 1945 (originally for tantalum), with 20.3 million tonnes of measured + indicated resources including lithium, tantalum, niobium and tin. It is one of the older continuously operated critical-metals mines in the Americas.
3. **Europe's critical-minerals strategy test case.** The [[Critical minerals]] concept note tracks how the EU's Critical Raw Materials Act translates into actual capacity. AMG's Bitterfeld ramp is a live experiment in whether private European capital can compete with Chinese integrated processing.

## Mibra mine — operations

Located 225 km northwest of Rio de Janeiro / 130 km southwest of Belo Horizonte, near the municipality of Nazareno in Minas Gerais.

| Product | Status |
|---------|--------|
| Tantalum concentrate | Primary historical product |
| Niobium concentrate | Co-product |
| Tin concentrate | Co-product |
| Lithium concentrate | Expanding — spodumene from tailings + dedicated plant |

**Lithium strategy at Mibra:**

- Initial lithium output came from reprocessing historical tantalum-mining tailings.
- A new dedicated lithium concentrate plant opened 2026 alongside the existing Mibra operation.
- Targeting integrated lithium-hydroxide downstream: "technical-grade" LiOH in 2026, "battery-grade" LiOH production scaling through 2030.

**Resource base:** 20.3 Mt measured and indicated, multiple minerals co-extracted — economics benefit from polymetallic recovery rather than lithium-only cost basis.

## Bitterfeld-Wolfen — the European LiOH bet

AMG is building lithium-hydroxide processing capacity in Bitterfeld-Wolfen, [[Germany]]:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Location | Bitterfeld-Wolfen, Saxony-Anhalt, [[Germany]] |
| Target capacity | **20,000 tpa LiOH** |
| Original timeline | Full capacity by end-2024 |
| Revised timeline | **End-2026** (two years late) |
| 2030 ambition (original) | 100,000 tpa LiOH |

The Bitterfeld project is the only European LiOH conversion plant of scale under construction. The two-year slip — now at 20 ktpa end-2026 — reflects both construction/commissioning challenges and the reality that Chinese LiOH supply crashed pricing throughout 2024-2025, undermining the near-term economics of a European challenger. The 2030 100 ktpa target has been effectively shelved; current guidance focuses on getting 20 ktpa operational.

**Why it still matters:** Even at 20 ktpa, Bitterfeld represents meaningful non-[[China]] LiOH capacity. If the EU implements meaningful critical-minerals carve-outs (domestic-content rules, tariffs on Chinese LiOH, IRA-style subsidies), the Bitterfeld ramp becomes the fulcrum. European battery manufacturing ([[Northvolt]] successors, VW Salzgitter, Stellantis-ACC, CATL Erfurt) needs LiOH, and shipping it from China now runs against the bloc's supply-chain security framework.

## European auto OEM target

AMG has stated its target is **14% market share of European EV LiOH demand by 2030** — with the Mibra-Bitterfeld integrated chain servicing ~2.5 million vehicles per year of equivalent battery throughput.

The math is the stress test:
- European EV sales 2025: ~3M vehicles, scaling toward 8-10M by 2030.
- LiOH requirement per EV: ~10-15 kg.
- 2030 European LiOH demand at 2.5M-vehicle capture: ~30,000 tpa.
- AMG Bitterfeld at full 20 ktpa + potential future expansion = in-range of 14% share assumption.

The assumption is plausible *if* the European EV ramp hits forecast, *if* EU/national governments steer OEMs toward non-China LiOH through regulation or incentives, and *if* AMG can execute the delayed Bitterfeld ramp. Three conditional ifs.

## Investment lens

Unlike [[Sigma Lithium]] and [[Atlas Lithium]] (Brazilian concentrate plays), AMG is a **processing-margin bet**. Upside is driven not by the $23,000/t spot lithium-carbonate rally but by the **LiOH conversion spread** between Chinese and European processors, and by whether EU regulation widens that spread.

### Structural read

- Concentrate prices track spot lithium (upside in Apr 2026 rally).
- LiOH prices are 2-3x concentrate on a $/t-Li₂O-equivalent basis (processing premium).
- AMG captures both: mining margin at Mibra, processing margin at Bitterfeld.
- But the capex overhang is front-loaded, and the 2024-2025 China LiOH price crash forced the ramp delay.

### Diversified critical-metals business

AMG is not a pure-play lithium. Roughly half of EBITDA historically comes from tantalum, vanadium, silicon metal, and the company's vacuum-heat-treatment-systems segment (AMG Engineering). The lithium story is leveraged but not existential.
