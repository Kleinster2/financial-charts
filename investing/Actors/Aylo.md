#actor #private #media #canada

Aylo — Canadian pornographic conglomerate, owner of [[Pornhub]], [[Brazzers]], [[RedTube]], [[YouPorn]], and ~15 other major adult brands. Headquartered in Montreal, Quebec. Owned by [[Ethical Capital Partners]] (ECP) since March 2023, acquired for $400M (seller-financed) from previous owners [[Feras Antoon]], [[David Tassillo]], and [[Bernd Bergmair]]. Revenue ~$460M (2021). Blocked in 23 US states, France, and the UK as of early 2026 over age verification laws. Under FTC consent order with 10+ years of federal oversight.

## Synopsis

The key question for Aylo is whether the adult entertainment industry's dominant platform operator can survive a simultaneous regulatory, legal, and payment-rail assault — or whether the compounding pressure makes the business structurally uninvestable regardless of its monopoly position on traffic.

Aylo operates 3 of the top 10 most-visited porn sites globally. [[Pornhub]] alone drew ~4 billion global visits in October 2025 (Semrush). That traffic dominance is Aylo's moat and its curse: it makes the company the single biggest target for every regulator, prosecutor, and payment processor in the content moderation space. The company has been hit from every angle — [[Visa]] and [[Mastercard]] suspended Pornhub in December 2020 after a [[New York Times]] exposé by Nicholas Kristof, gutting subscription revenue. The FTC and Utah imposed a $15M penalty (Sep 2025, $5M paid) plus mandatory CSAM/NCM prevention programs and independent monitoring for at least a decade. A December 2023 deferred prosecution agreement already carried $1.8M in fines over [[GirlsDoPorn]] sex trafficking content. And the company has voluntarily blocked itself in 23 US states (plus France and the UK) rather than implement age verification, hemorrhaging traffic to competitors who don't comply either.

The second question is whether [[Ethical Capital Partners]] — a firm founded in 2022 by criminal defense lawyers and a cannabis entrepreneur, with no visible track record in media, tech, or content moderation — can actually execute a compliance-first turnaround. ECP bought a distressed asset: MindGeek was valued at ~$1.5B pre-scandal but sold for $400M, and the purchase was structured as a promise of payment to the former owners, not a cash deal. Managing partner [[Fady Mansour]] (Lebanese-Canadian lawyer) controls 75%+ of voting shares. The firm's name is aspirational — the real question is whether "ethical" operation of the world's largest porn platform is economically viable when payment rails are hostile, regulators are expanding oversight, and the political mood in both parties favors restriction.

The third question is structural: does voluntary geo-blocking kill the business or save it? Aylo blocks states rather than build age verification infrastructure, arguing (correctly) that competitors simply ignore the laws and absorb the traffic. Indiana's AG has already argued that going dark doesn't constitute compliance. If courts agree, Aylo faces compliance costs with no competitive advantage. If the age verification wave reaches federal level — or if the EU's Digital Services Act framework expands enforcement — the exit options narrow to fire sale or shutdown.

## Evolution

The story of Aylo is the story of how a handful of entrepreneurs built the internet's largest pornography empire through aggressive acquisition, then watched it buckle under the weight of content moderation failures that made it a political target for both parties.

- 2004: [[Stephane Manos]] and [[Ouissam Youssef]], Concordia University graduates, founded Mansef (a portmanteau of their surnames) in Montreal as a holding company for thumbnail gallery post websites and affiliate networks. They launched [[Brazzers]] and various pay sites. Separately, German entrepreneur [[Fabian Thylmann]] had created NATS (Next-Generation Affiliate Tracking Software) in the late 1990s, a tool for marketing pornography across websites.

- 2007-2010: Matt Keezer started [[Pornhub]] under Interhub in 2007, with Mansef as a partner. Thylmann used proceeds from selling NATS to acquire Privat Amateure, MyDirtyHobby, Webcams, and [[Xtube]] between 2006-2010. In March 2010, Thylmann bought the assets of Mansef and Interhub for a combined price that loaded $130M in debt, merging everything into a new entity called Manwin. The consolidation gave one company control over both the largest tube sites (free, ad-supported) and the largest paid studios — a vertical integration that would define the industry.

- 2011-2012: Manwin went on an acquisition spree that would have made any roll-up PE firm proud. [[YouPorn]] (May 2011), [[Twistys]] (June 2011), a Playboy TV partnership (Nov 2011), [[Digital Playground]] (Jan 2012), [[Reality Kings]] (Sep 2012). The 2010 purchases had already generated $130M in debt; in 2011 the company raised $362M in financing from 125 secret investors including [[Fortress Investment Group]], [[JPMorgan Chase]], and — remarkably — [[Cornell University]]. At its peak, a company spokesperson claimed Manwin properties were among the top five users of internet bandwidth globally.

- 2013: Thylmann, facing tax evasion charges after extradition from Belgium to Germany, sold his stake to senior managers [[Feras Antoon]] (CEO) and [[David Tassillo]] (COO) for €73M (~$100M). The company rebranded from Manwin to MindGeek in October 2013. The true ownership structure remained opaque — [[Bernd Bergmair]], an Austrian entrepreneur who had sold [[RedTube]] to Manwin in 2013, was later revealed by the [[Financial Times]] (December 2020) to be the company's majority owner, a fact concealed for seven years through layered offshore entities in Luxembourg and Curaçao.

- 2020: The inflection point. Nicholas Kristof published "The Children of Pornhub" in the [[New York Times]] (December 4, 2020), documenting cases of child sexual abuse material and non-consensual content on Pornhub. Within days, [[Visa]] and [[Mastercard]] suspended payment processing for Pornhub. The company purged ~10 million unverified videos (80% of its library) and restricted uploads to verified users only. But the damage was done — subscription revenue, which had been the core business model, collapsed overnight. The payment rail severing proved more devastating than any lawsuit or fine.

- 2022-2023: Antoon and Tassillo resigned in June 2022 under pressure from management, the board, and Bergmair. The company was effectively in distressed-sale mode. In March 2023, [[Ethical Capital Partners]] — a firm founded just one year earlier in Montreal by cannabis entrepreneur Rocco Meliambro and criminal defense lawyer [[Fady Mansour]] — acquired MindGeek for $400M. Court documents later revealed this was considerably less than the $525M asking price, and the purchase was structured as a promise of payment to the former owners rather than cash on the barrel. The [[Financial Times]] noted ECP's team included "lawyers and former cannabis investors." MindGeek rebranded as Aylo in August 2023.

- 2023-2025: The regulatory noose tightened further under ECP's watch. December 2023: $1.8M fine plus deferred prosecution agreement over knowingly hosting [[GirlsDoPorn]] sex trafficking content. The Canadian Privacy Commissioner found PIPEDA violations. State age verification laws proliferated — starting with Louisiana and Texas, then spreading to Utah, Virginia, and beyond. Aylo's response was to block access entirely in states with verification requirements, arguing that implementing ID checks created privacy risks and that competitors simply ignored the laws. By early 2026, Pornhub was dark in 23 states, France, and the UK. September 2025: the FTC and Utah secured a landmark consent order — $15M penalty ($5M paid), mandatory CSAM and non-consensual material prevention programs, independent third-party monitoring, and requirements that all uploaded content verify performers are consenting adults. The FTC complaint named 14 Aylo entities across Cyprus, Ireland, Delaware, and Quebec.

- 2026: The company faces an existential strategic question. Each blocked state represents lost traffic that flows to smaller competitors, VPNs, or social media platforms with far less content moderation. France was historically a top-3 traffic country. Indiana's attorney general has argued that merely blocking access doesn't constitute compliance with state law. If that theory prevails, Aylo faces the worst of both worlds: compliance costs without competitive advantage, in an industry where the dominant player's only sustainable moat is scale.

## Payment rails

The Visa/Mastercard suspension remains the single most important structural constraint on Aylo's business. When card networks pulled from Pornhub in December 2020, it severed the primary subscription payment mechanism. Aylo has partially worked around this through alternative payment processors and crypto, but the card networks' absence limits monetization of its massive free-tier traffic. The subscription model that generated $460M in 2021 revenue operated under fundamentally different payment conditions than what exists today.

The broader lesson: payment processors have become de facto content regulators. When Visa and Mastercard make compliance decisions, they have more immediate economic impact than any government fine. The $1.8M DPA and $15M FTC penalty are rounding errors compared to the revenue lost from payment rail access.
