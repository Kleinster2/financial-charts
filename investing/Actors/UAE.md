# UAE

The [[UAE]] is the Gulf's most important hybrid of oil exporter, sovereign-capital pool, and regional financial hub. Abu Dhabi supplies the hydrocarbon base and the sovereign balance sheet, while [[Dubai]] provides logistics, finance, and commercial openness. The April 2026 Hormuz shock showed the real vulnerability inside that model: even a wealthy dollar-pegged exporter with roughly $270 billion of reserves can become dependent on wartime access to official dollar liquidity if tanker flows, investor confidence, and regional payment plumbing all come under strain at once.

## April 2026 Hormuz shock - dollar backstop test

The [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] turned the [[UAE]] from a Gulf safe-haven story into a live test of whether a dollar-pegged energy exporter can still lose access to dollar liquidity when physical oil exports are disrupted. The WSJ reported that central bank governor [[Khaled Mohamed Balama]] raised with [[Scott Bessent]], Treasury officials, and the [[Federal Reserve]] the possibility of a precautionary dollar swap line or other financial backstop after the war damaged Emirati oil-and-gas infrastructure, interrupted tanker traffic through the [[Strait of Hormuz]], and threatened Dubai's standing as a regional financial hub.

The important point is not that the dirham peg broke, it did not. The point is that a country with roughly $270 billion of reserves and one of the world's strongest sovereign balance sheets still worried that prolonged export disruption, capital-flight risk, and investor doubt could make official dollar access strategically important. The same report said Emirati officials warned that tighter dollar liquidity could push some oil sales into [[Chinese yuan]] or other currencies, which is less a clean de-dollarization pivot than an admission that the dollar system becomes most visible when access to it is in question.

### Regional self-help before Fed help

Secondary reproductions of the WSJ piece carried two additional details that matter for the Gulf financial map:

- [[Bahrain]] set up a roughly $5 billion swap line with the [[UAE]] earlier in April to support financial stability.
- Abu Dhabi raised around $4 billion in private placements arranged by banks including [[Goldman Sachs]], paying up for speed rather than waiting for cheaper financing.

Together, those steps show the UAE was already building layered liquidity buffers, first through regional and market channels, before any formal US rescue architecture existed.
