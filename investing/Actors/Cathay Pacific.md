## Freight — wafer transport dominance

Cathay Pacific's freight division handles approximately 30% of global semiconductor wafer transport (FT, Parikh, Mar 22). This concentration parallels the chip production concentration in East Asia — wafers manufactured at [[TSMC]] (Taiwan), [[Samsung Electronics|Samsung]], and [[SK Hynix]] (South Korea) rely on a Hong Kong-based logistics hub.

## Iran conflict impact (March-April 2026)

### March: schedule-share opportunity

Mounting extra services as existing flights sold out — benefiting from Gulf carrier cancellations. Doubled fuel surcharges on tickets starting Mar 18. ~30% fuel hedged, better positioned than unhedged Asian carriers. See [[Iran conflict airline disruption]].

Freight division has limited access to its regional hub in [[Dubai]], creating wafer delivery delays — a secondary bottleneck beyond the fab-level chemical input shortages. See [[Iran conflict semiconductor energy risk]].

### April 27: cut wave (direction reversal)

Per [[Financial Times|FT]], Cathay Pacific joined [[Deutsche Lufthansa|Lufthansa]], [[Delta Air Lines|Delta]], and [[Qantas]] in cutting flights as [[Jet fuel|jet fuel]] doubled to ~$200/bbl ([[Argus Media|Argus]] NW Europe — Asian benchmarks moved similarly). The ~30% hedge cushioned the early-March spike but does not insulate against a sustained doubling, especially since the hedge benchmark is crude rather than jet kerosene (basis-risk gap as the [[Crack spread|crack spread]] blew out).

The cuts are first-mover demand destruction in the [[Refining slate rigidity]] framework — they reduce jet demand without rebalancing crude, because refiners produce roughly fixed product slates and the rest of the slate has to ration too.

*Source: FT, "Why flying less won't do much to ease the oil crisis" (Apr 27, 2026).*
