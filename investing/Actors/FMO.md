# FMO

Dutch entrepreneurial development bank and one of the larger bilateral development finance institutions in the world. Invests in private sector enterprises across emerging markets with a portfolio of ~EUR 12B in committed investments spanning 85+ countries.

## Synopsis

FMO (Nederlandse Financierings-Maatschappij voor Ontwikkelingslanden) was established in 1970 as the Dutch government's primary vehicle for private-sector development investment. The Dutch state holds 51%, with the remainder held by Dutch commercial banks, trade unions, and other institutional investors. Unlike aid agencies, FMO operates on commercial terms — it expects market-rate returns on its equity and debt investments, which is what makes it relevant to the investing vault. The bank deploys EUR 2-3B annually across financial institutions, energy, agribusiness, and infrastructure in Africa, Asia, and Latin America.

FMO's significance lies in its role as a signaling mechanism for frontier-market investment. When FMO commits capital to a fund or company, it validates the opportunity for commercial co-investors who lack the local knowledge or risk appetite to go first. The bank was an LP in [[Delta40]]'s $20M fund (Feb 2026), focused on African logistics and supply-chain technology. FMO's portfolio has taken reputational hits — the 2018 Agua Zarca dam controversy in Honduras (linked to the murder of activist Berta Caceres) led to an internal overhaul of environmental and social risk processes. Total assets run ~EUR 14B with a consistent AA+ credit rating from Fitch.
