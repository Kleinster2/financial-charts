**Costa Rica** is a Central American republic of 5.2M people that has built an outsized economic profile on renewable energy, ecotourism, and nearshore services — generating nearly 100% of electricity from renewables and hosting a growing tech/BPO sector serving [[United States]] clients.

## Synopsis

Costa Rica's investment relevance sits at the intersection of nearshoring and green credibility. The country runs almost entirely on hydropower, geothermal, and wind — 98-99% renewable electricity for over a decade — making it one of the few nations with a genuine claim to green manufacturing. This matters as [[United States]] companies look to reshore supply chains with ESG-compliant sourcing: Costa Rica's free trade zones already host [[Intel]] (chip packaging and testing, though the fabrication plant closed in 2014), medical device assembly (the country is a top-10 global exporter of medical devices), and a fast-growing IT services sector. GDP per capita (~$13,500) is the highest in Central America, and the country maintains a stable democratic system without a military (abolished in 1948).

The risks are structural: the fiscal deficit runs ~5-6% of GDP, public debt-to-GDP is ~70%, and the colon has been under persistent depreciation pressure. An [[IMF]] Extended Fund Facility ($1.78B, 2021) imposed fiscal consolidation that brought the primary surplus back but at the cost of social spending. The [[IEA]] accession process (ongoing as of 2026) would formalize the country's energy credentials but has limited direct market impact. For investors, Costa Rica is relevant primarily as a nearshoring node — not a standalone equity market (the Bolsa Nacional de Valores is tiny and illiquid) but as a destination for FDI from US medtech, semiconductor packaging, and services firms.
