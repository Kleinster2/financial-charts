**Hello Robot** — Small open-source mobile manipulator company building Stretch, a $25K assistive robot for home care and research. Founded by ex-[[Google]] robotics director Aaron Edsinger and Georgia Tech professor Charlie Kemp. Grant-funded, no VC rounds — closer to a research platform than a commercial robotics startup.

---

## Synopsis

Hello Robot sits at the intersection of assistive robotics and open-source AI research. The company's bet is that a lightweight, affordable mobile manipulator (~$25K, 54 lbs) can serve the home care market that humanoid robots are too expensive and complex to reach. Stretch is not trying to compete with [[Agility Robotics]] or [[Figure AI]] in warehouse logistics — it targets older adults, ALS patients, and people with disabilities who need help with daily tasks like eating, drinking, and household chores.

The business model is unusual for robotics: almost entirely grant-funded ($2.8M total, from NIH and ALS Association), open-source software (ROS 2 + Python), and a hardware sale at $24,950. No VC, no unicorn valuation, no factory buildout. The company has ~21 employees across 3 continents. This is a research-first play that happens to sell hardware — more like Willow Garage's lineage than the humanoid arms race. The risk is obvious: grant dependency and tiny scale. The upside is that if home assistive robotics takes off as aging demographics demand, Stretch has a head start on the use cases that matter most.

## Founders

Aaron Edsinger — Director of Robotics at [[Google]] before founding Hello Robot. Previously founded Meka Robotics and Redwood Robotics, both acquired by [[Google]] in 2013. MIT PhD. Designed robots at Google that became the foundation for several internal programs.

## Competitive positioning

Hello Robot occupies a niche that the big humanoid players aren't targeting:

| Dimension | Hello Robot | Humanoid players |
|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| Target market | Home care, research | Warehouse, factory |
| Price | $25K | $50K-$150K+ |
| Form factor | Wheeled mobile arm | Bipedal humanoid |
| Software | Open-source | Proprietary |
| Funding | ~$3M grants | $100M-$1B+ VC |
| Scale | ~21 people | 100-1000+ |

Not competing with [[Agility Robotics]], [[Figure AI]], [[Boston Dynamics]], or [[Tesla]] Optimus. Closer to research platforms like Clearpath or the old Willow Garage PR2. The assistive robotics niche is small (~$1.5B 2025, growing ~17% CAGR) but underserved — aging demographics in developed countries create structural demand.

## Investment case

Bull:
- Assistive/home robotics is an underserved niche with strong demographic tailwinds
- Open-source strategy builds ecosystem and research community (100+ institutions)
- Founders have deep pedigree (Edsinger's companies acquired by [[Google]], Kemp's decade of assistive robotics research)
- $25K price point is accessible vs. humanoid alternatives
- Grant funding = no dilution, no investor pressure to pivot

Bear:
- Tiny company (~21 people) with minimal funding (~$3M)
- Grant-dependent — no commercial revenue engine
- No VC backing limits growth, hiring, manufacturing scale
- Consumer/home robotics has a graveyard of failed companies ([[iRobot]] near-collapse, Jibo, Kuri)
- Could be a feature, not a company — if [[Google]] or a humanoid player builds a home care mode, Stretch becomes a research curiosity
