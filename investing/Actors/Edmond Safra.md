**Edmond Safra** (August 6, 1932 - December 3, 1999) was a Lebanese-Brazilian banker who built two major financial institutions and became one of the wealthiest bankers in the world. Born in Beirut to a Sephardic Jewish banking dynasty — the Safra family had been in banking since Ottoman-era Syria — he founded [[Republic New York Corporation]] in 1966 and [[Trade Development Bank]] in Geneva, establishing himself as the preeminent private banker for wealthy Middle Eastern, Latin American, and European clients.

Safra sold Trade Development Bank to [[American Express]] in 1983 for ~$550M but the relationship soured badly — Amex launched a smear campaign against him (later proven and resulting in a public apology and $8M charitable payment). He then rebuilt his Geneva operations as Safra Republic Holdings. In 1999, Safra agreed to sell Republic New York and Safra Republic to [[HSBC]] for $10.3B, one of the largest banking acquisitions of the era.

Safra died on December 3, 1999 — just weeks before the HSBC deal was to close — from smoke inhalation in a fire at his fortified Monaco penthouse at La Belle Epoque. The fire was set by his nurse Ted Maher, who was attempting a staged rescue to ingratiate himself with his employer. Maher was convicted of arson in 2002.

Safra's brother [[Joseph Safra]] went on to build [[Banco Safra]] into Brazil's largest privately held bank. The Edmond J. Safra Foundation continues his philanthropic legacy with over $1B in grants.
