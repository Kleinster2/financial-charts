**Elsevier** is the world's largest scientific publisher, operating ~2,800 journals (including The Lancet and Cell) and critical research infrastructure (ScienceDirect, Scopus) as the STM division of [[RELX]].

## Synopsis

Elsevier is the archetype of a legal monopoly built on publicly funded research. The business model is elegantly extractive: academics produce research funded by taxpayers, peer-review it for free, and then universities pay Elsevier subscription fees to access it — with gross margins estimated at 35-40% on the STM segment. This model has survived decades of criticism, open-access mandates, and boycotts (the 2012 "Cost of Knowledge" petition gathered 20,000+ signatures) because Elsevier controls two things that are genuinely hard to replicate: the prestige hierarchy (publishing in The Lancet or Cell still makes careers) and the citation/discovery infrastructure (Scopus indexes 90M+ records and is embedded in university evaluation systems worldwide). [[RELX]]'s STM segment generates ~GBP 3B revenue, making Elsevier the single largest revenue contributor.

The threat vector is AI. Large language models can summarize and synthesize research faster than any journal database, potentially disintermediating the discovery function that gives Elsevier its lock-in. [[OpenAI]], [[Google]], and [[Meta]] have all trained models on scientific literature, and tools like Semantic Scholar and Elicit already offer AI-powered research synthesis. Elsevier's response has been to invest in its own AI tools (embedding them in ScienceDirect and Scopus) and to aggressively enforce copyright against AI training data use. The strategic question is whether Elsevier becomes the AI layer on top of scientific knowledge (using its proprietary corpus) or gets disintermediated by general-purpose AI tools that make the underlying journal format irrelevant. For [[RELX]] investors, Elsevier is both the highest-margin division and the one with the most existential AI exposure.
