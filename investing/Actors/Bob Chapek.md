Bob Chapek — former CEO of [[Disney]] (Feb 2020–Nov 2022). Previously ran Disney Parks (Experiences division). Lasted less than three years before the board replaced him with returning [[Bob Iger]] in a November 2022 coup. Tenure marked by COVID, Florida political disputes, and shareholder discontent. Iger now habitually refers to him as "my former successor" rather than by name.

## The unstable dual structure

Iger's 2020 plan was to stay on as executive chairman with creative oversight while Chapek ran operations — a construct Iger modeled on [[Mark Parker]]'s move at [[Nike]]. Iger kept the corner office (and its private shower) while Chapek moved down the hall. Iger defended the arrangement: "He was keeping the trains running on time. Why should I leave the office?" The structure "quickly went bad," Iger conceded; COVID "made that a lot harder, in fairness to him and everyone." Iger left the company in December 2021, by which point the two Bobs were barely talking.

---

## Tenure under pressure

Chapek took the job just as COVID hit, forcing park closures and studio disruption. He reorganized Disney's media business around streaming and changed parks pricing structures — moves Iger said had "no urgent need," arguing Chapek "brought in bureaucracy and... layers of management." Controversies compounded the pressure: Disney's handling of Florida's "Don't Say Gay" bill (employees wanted leadership to oppose it), and the [[Scarlett Johansson]] lawsuit over Black Widow's simultaneous Disney+ and cinema release.

People close to Chapek argue Iger thought he could "control" a previously "loyal soldier" and took to criticizing him once the relationship broke. As one put it to the FT: "Did [Chapek] make a ton of mistakes? Yes. But was the deck stacked against him? Also yes." Iger denies fomenting or undermining from outside.

---

## Ouster

Chapek was fired in November 2022 — "10 months and three weeks" after Iger's December 2021 exit — when chair [[Susan Arnold]] called Iger back. Unlike Iger's planned, muted departures, Chapek's was abrupt; the FT notes he left "without even time to send a goodbye email." He did not respond to the FT's request for comment.
