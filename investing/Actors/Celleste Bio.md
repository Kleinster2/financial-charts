#actor #company #foodtech #biotech #israel #private

Celleste Bio — Israeli foodtech start-up producing cell-cultured cocoa butter as a drop-in replacement for conventional cocoa butter. Backed by [[Mondelez]]. In April 2026, announced production of the world's first lab-grown chocolate bars (a dozen), made at the [[Cadbury]] Bournville factory in Birmingham from its cell-cultured cocoa butter.

## Synopsis

Celleste is betting that cell-cultured cocoa butter can enter the conventional chocolate supply chain without reformulation, recipe change, or equipment change — a drop-in commodity rather than a novel ingredient. If that property holds at scale, the company sits at an unusual intersection: a biotech-style cost curve solving a procurement-team problem for a consumer packaged goods industry that is structurally short of West [[Africa]] cocoa. The $5.6M raised so far is small relative to the scale ambition (50,000 tonnes/year), so the commercial milestone at Bournville matters mainly as a capital-markets event — it is the kind of technical de-risking that precedes a larger round or a direct offtake-plus-capex commitment from [[Mondelez]]. The key uncertainty is regulatory geography: US/Israel approval by end-2027 is plausible; EU timelines are slower, which could fragment the supply chain between regions.

## Technology

Celleste takes a tiny cell sample from cocoa beans and grows those cells in large, controlled tanks, feeding them sugars, water, and vitamins. The process produces the same fats and flavour compounds found in conventional cocoa butter. Per CEO [[Michal Beressi Golomb]] (FT, April 2026):

> "Unlike other alternatives, it performs identically to conventional cocoa butter. It's a drop-in replacement ... so it has a lot of meaning in terms of production processes for the chocolate manufacturers."

> "The question whether we could make real chocolate, real milk chocolate, with cell-cultured cocoa butter is proven: we did it."

The production analogy she uses: the bioreactor is "the new cocoa tree for the cocoa cells," supplying what a cocoa pod on a tree would — water, sugar, and vitamins.

This drop-in property is the company's core pitch to chocolate manufacturers. Unlike cocoa-free alternatives from [[Voyage Foods]] (grape seeds, sunflower protein) or [[Win-Win]] (fermented cereals and legumes), Celleste's output does not require reformulated recipes or new processing equipment. The buyer is the procurement team, not the consumer.

## Commercial strategy

- Facilities intended to be co-located with chocolate manufacturers — Beressi Golomb invoked a "Willy Wonka and Charlie and the Chocolate Factory" image of pipes carrying cocoa butter directly into the client's factory, cutting shipping-related CO₂
- Targeting renewable energy and water recycling as part of the sustainability pitch
- Claims ~10,000 m² of land saved per unit of production vs. conventional cocoa farming

Target economics: 50,000 tonnes/year at $12,000-$15,000/tonne implies peak run-rate revenue of $600M-$750M if fully built out — still small vs. the ~2M tonne/year global cocoa butter market, but material as a supply-diversification hedge for a single chocolate manufacturer.

## Economics context — why now

Cocoa bean prices ran from under $3,000/tonne in early 2023 to a 2024 peak near $12,000/tonne, and cocoa butter spot spiked above €40,000/tonne in late 2024 before easing to ~€5,000 as West [[Africa]] yields improved and European/US demand softened. That price arc, driven by [[Climate change]]-linked weather damage and chronic under-investment in [[Ivory Coast]] and [[Ghana]] replanting, is what triggered major chocolate manufacturers to fund alternatives. See [[Cocoa]] for the full price history.

Celleste's target price is above the current conventional cocoa butter price but within the range it traded at for most of 2024-early 2025. The pitch to [[Mondelez]] is not cost leadership today — it is insulation from the next supply shock.

## Analysis

Celleste is the first cell-cultured cocoa player to produce actual finished chocolate bars at a major manufacturer's industrial facility, rather than at a lab bench. Bournville as the venue matters: Cadbury is [[Mondelez]]'s flagship chocolate brand, and putting the pilot run through Bournville signals that [[Mondelez]] is willing to associate the technology with its premium British chocolate identity, not just hide it in an unmarketed ingredient stream.

Two read-throughs for the category:

The drop-in claim resets the bar for competitors. If validated at scale, it removes the reformulation friction that slowed plant-based dairy adoption by commercial bakers and beverage companies — a key reason plant-based dairy struggled to break out of retail-facing product categories into B2B ingredient streams. [[Kokomodo]] and [[Food Brewer]] will need to demonstrate similar equivalence, not just approximation.

The $5.6M raise is small relative to the scale target. A 50,000 tonne/year facility is a nine-figure capex project. Either [[Mondelez]] underwrites the first plant directly (extending its role from investor to offtake partner + capital provider), or Celleste raises a meaningful Series B before 2027 regulatory approval. The Bournville production milestone is the kind of de-risking event that typically precedes such a round.

Felenbok's "moonshot" critique (via Win-Win; FT, April 2026) remains live: lab-grown technologies face higher energy use, capital cost, and regulatory hurdles than ingredient-substitution alternatives. Celleste's rebuttal is that drop-in equivalence compresses the regulatory pathway to a food-safety question, not a novel-food category question — but that will be tested jurisdiction by jurisdiction.
