# Raizen

World's largest sugarcane [[ethanol]] producer — JV between [[Shell]] (44%) and [[Cosan]] (44%), with ~12% public float (IPO'd 2021). Operates 24 mills across sugar, ethanol, power generation, and [[Shell]]-branded fuel distribution in Brazil and Argentina. Pioneer in second-generation cellulosic ethanol. Ticker: RAIZ4 on [[B3]].

---

## Synopsis

Raízen is Brazil's largest corporate debt crisis since [[Americanas]]. A world-class sugar/ethanol/fuel distribution franchise — R$255B revenue, 7,000+ Shell-branded stations, 1.3 GW biomass — buried under R$73B gross debt that was R$52B just 12 months ago. Leverage went from 1.3x to 5.3x in 18 months through a toxic combination: reckless expansion into too many verticals simultaneously, a disastrous 9-mill [[Louis Dreyfus Company|Biosev]] acquisition that never integrated, an [[E2G]] cellulosic ethanol bet that underdelivered, and a [[Selic]] rate shock that turned manageable floating-rate debt into a self-reinforcing spiral (R$7.6B interest in 9 months, faster than operations generate cash).

All three rating agencies stripped investment grade in one week (Feb 9-10, 2026) — Fitch dropped 5 notches in 7 hours. The forced-selling cascade from investment-grade-mandated institutional holders cratered bonds from par to 30-55 cents. Stock collapsed from R$7.60 IPO (2021) to R$0.55, below [[B3]]'s R$1 minimum.

The rescue attempt has narrowed to a R$4B Shell-led injection (R$3.5B Shell + R$500M [[Rubens Ometto]]'s family office [[Aguassanta]]) after joint talks with [[Cosan]] and [[BTG Pactual]] collapsed on Mar 4. This covers ~16% of the R$25B creditors demanded and the R$18-25B banks estimate is needed. The gap will be filled by creditor haircuts — debt-to-equity conversion at ~35% face value is the market expectation. Raízen is formally pursuing extrajudicial recovery (out-of-court restructuring) and has issued a going-concern warning. Shell is heading toward de facto control while Cosan, which ring-fenced its Sep 2025 R$10B raise from Raízen, is sidelined. If extrajudicial fails and this tips into recuperação judicial, it becomes Brazil's largest corporate restructuring ever — gross debt exceeding Americanas' R$43B.

## Q3 FY25/26 results (Feb 13, 2026)

R$15.65 billion net loss — the quarter that formalized the crisis in the accounts. CEO Nelson Gomes on the Q3 earnings call (Feb 13): *"In terms of capital structure, we got to an inflection point. All of our operational transformation plan has not been enough to mitigate the imbalance we have in the company's capital structure."*

[[Citigroup]] analyst Gabriel Barra (Feb 13): *"The main negative highlight was the fact that the high interest payments and the disbalanced capital structure increase the risk of operational continuity of the company."*

Cesar Fernandez, partner at [[Alpha Credit Advisors]] (Feb 13): *"Today's earnings conference call appears to have helped alleviate some of those tail-risk concerns, particularly around liquidity and near-term execution. For the move to be sustained, however, the market will need clearer visibility on deleveraging plans and balance-sheet resilience."*

| Ethanol sales | 778K m³ | 895K m³ | -13% |
| Sugar sales | 1.33Mt | 1.17Mt | +14% (accelerated to generate cash) |
| Distribution EBITDA | R$1.6B | R$1.07B | +50% (police crackdown on illegal fuel) |

The R$11.1B impairment: non-cash write-down required after credit downgrades raised cost-of-capital assumptions, reducing present value of future cash flows. The company acknowledged that a significant portion of invested capital — across ethanol plants, cane fields, and expansion projects — is no longer expected to generate returns justifying its carrying value. Management (Feb 13): *"This provision has no cash impact and may be reversed in the future."*

Operational contraction: cane crushing collapsed 23% due to drought-damaged yields in São Paulo and the shrinking mill footprint from asset sales. Ethanol volumes fell 13%. Sugar sales rose 14% as Raízen accelerated shipments to generate cash.

The debt spiral: interest costs consumed R$7.6B in 9 months — faster than operations generate cash. At 5.3x leverage (was 1.3x just 18 months ago), the debt is self-reinforcing: interest accrues → more debt → more interest.

CFO Lorival Nogueira Luz Jr. said the company targets leverage of 2.0-2.5x EBITDA — implying R$25-30B in capital is needed to bridge the gap from 5.3x.

## Evolution — the "foretold crisis"

The crisis unfolded over two years through five compounding failures:

1. Reckless expansion (2016-2024):
- Heavy capex across fuel distribution, convenience stores (Oxxo JV with [[Femsa]]), [[E2G]], sugar/ethanol capacity
- Created "a more complex portfolio than ideal and high leverage at an unfavorable moment in the interest rate cycle" (internal source)
- Organizational bloat — new structures and management layers "distracted leadership's attention"

2. Disastrous Biosev acquisition (2021):
- Acquired 9 mills from [[Louis Dreyfus Company]] — some in regions where Raízen had never operated, far from existing clusters
- Couldn't capture synergies or efficiency gains
- Several of these mills now being sold back at losses

3. E2G underdelivered:
- [[E2G|Second-generation ethanol]] bet fell short of expectations
- Bonfim plant (world's largest E2G facility) not delivering promised returns

4. Selic rate shock:
- [[Selic]] hikes in 2025 crushed floating-rate borrowers
- Reverse factoring (90-day) was converted to longer-term bonds at much higher rates
- R$10.9B in new financial instruments replaced R$6-8B/year of reverse factoring
- Gross debt surged R$21B in 12 months (R$52B → R$73B)

5. Asset sales too slow:
- R$5B divested in 12 months but couldn't keep pace with debt costs
- CEO Nelson Gomes replaced Ricardo Mussa (Oct-Nov 2024) to lead restructuring
- Cost-cutting and simplification plan launched but overtaken by the debt spiral

One bright spot: intensified enforcement against fuel fraud — shutdown of Refit alone opened ~10% of the distribution market in São Paulo and Rio, absorbed by compliant operators like Raízen.

## The rescue battle

### Phase 1: Competing bids (Feb 16-19)

Two competing proposals were on the table:

Shell plan (Feb 18): ~R$5B injection (Shell >R$3.5B, Cosan remainder), keep Raízen whole, negotiate with creditors after. Shell had previously demanded equal contributions but shifted after failing to find a third investor. Creditor banks favored this simpler/faster approach.

BTG/Cosan plan: R$8.3B+ total (Cosan + BTG R$3B, BTG PE arm R$5.5B into distribution business), split into two companies. Some creditor banks opposed — argued splitting weakens the company and complicates debt negotiations.

CEO Nelson Gomes (Feb 16): shareholders "ready to inject capital." Fitch (Feb 19): pledges were "too little, too late" for the 8-notch downgrade.

### Phase 2: Lula intervenes, BTG deal advances, then collapses (mid-Feb – Mar 4)

Before the Carnival holiday (pre-Feb 18), President [[Lula]] personally convened a meeting in Brasília with executives from [[Cosan]], [[Shell]], [[BTG Pactual]], [[Petrobras]] CEO [[Magda Chambriard]], Finance Minister [[Fernando Haddad]], and [[BNDES]] head [[Aloizio Mercadante]]. The meeting discussed selling strategic Raízen assets to [[Petrobras]] — the partnership didn't move forward. Petrobras denied Chambriard attended and denied studying any Raízen asset acquisition. Lula's involvement underscored mounting government concern at a sensitive political moment — he is seeking reelection, and Raízen is central to Brazil's energy transition agenda.

After the Brasília meeting, negotiations intensified. BTG and Shell presented proposals, with further talks in London and São Paulo. Separately, [[Cosan]] approached [[BNDES]] for financial support for Raízen — facing resistance, with officials wary of increasing exposure as Raízen's credit deteriorates. BNDES officials said the company must present a concrete capitalization plan first. BNDES had already invested R$409M in Cosan's Sep 2025 share offering.

Raízen paid $33.5M in interest on its 2037 dollar bonds that week — another hit to its balance sheet, but avoiding a default trigger.

Feb 24-25: Shell, Cosan, BTG appeared to advance on a joint deal. Restructuring details leaked: corporate split (Raízen Energia vs. fuel distribution), debt-to-equity conversion at ~35% of face value, [[BTG Pactual]] PE funds to acquire distribution stake for R$5.5B (~$1.05B), share offerings for creditor exits. Creditors sent letters demanding up to R$12B from shareholders specifically, arguing Shell and Cosan are profitable enough to contribute more. Some creditors argued R$25B total capital is needed.

Feb 2026: Raízen issued going-concern warning — "significant uncertainty" over ability to continue operating.

Mar 3: [[Shell]] Brazil CEO Cristiano Pinto da Costa confirmed Shell would inject R$3.5B (~$664M), expected Cosan to match — but Cosan concluded it couldn't match Shell's scale.

Mar 4: talks collapsed. Cosan abandoned its previously proposed R$1B injection. [[BTG Pactual]] PE funds gave up acquiring the distribution stake for R$5.5B. BTG and Shell couldn't agree on: (1) length and size of Shell brand royalties contract, (2) size of debt-to-equity conversion from creditors, (3) target leverage reduction, (4) treatment of fiscal liabilities held by Raízen. Shell also opposed BTG's demand for an immediate corporate split — Shell and major creditors wanted recapitalization first.

Shell Brazil CEO Pinto da Costa (Mar 4): *"This is the view of Shell and the main creditors. If we do this with the company still lacking stability, there could be a very high risk in implementing this strategy."*

If Shell's plan is rejected, bankruptcy filing is the likely course — which would also hurt [[Cosan]], whose debt level remains challenging after its Sep 2025 BTG-led capital raise. Cosan's exposure to Raízen could plunge the company back into crisis.

### Phase 3: Shell-only rescue + extrajudicial path (Mar 5)

Mar 5: Raízen officially disclosed a slimmed-down proposal and opened the door to out-of-court restructuring:

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total injection | R$4B (~$765M) |
| Shell contribution | R$3.5B |
| Ometto family ([[Aguassanta]]) | R$500M (may require a loan to fund) |
| Cosan corporate | Nothing — dropped R$1B previously proposed |
| Debt-to-equity conversion | ~R$25B of debt converted to equity |
| Shell ownership | Would become majority owner |
| Balance sheet | Shell would consolidate Raízen debt onto its own books |
| Structure | Keep whole first, consider split later |
| Restructuring path | Extrajudicial recovery (out-of-court) if needed |
| Fallback | Bankruptcy filing if plan rejected |

Shares fell 13% on the day. The R$4B offer is a fraction of what creditors demanded (R$25B) and what banks estimate is needed (R$18-25B). Shell is heading toward de facto control — the injection dilutes Cosan, whose corporate entity contributed nothing.

## Why shareholders won't inject more

- [[Shell]] (44%) — initially resisted going above 50% due to [[IFRS]] consolidation of R$73B gross debt. As of Mar 5, Shell has accepted becoming majority owner and consolidating the debt — a major reversal.
- [[Cosan]] (44%) — raised R$10B in Sep 2025 ([[BTG Pactual]] R$4.5B, [[Perfin]] R$2B, Ometto R$750M) but explicitly ring-fenced from [[Raizen]]; [[Cosan]] shares fell 21% on announcement. Cosan concluded it cannot match Shell's scale of support. Its own debt remains challenging — exposure to Raízen could plunge Cosan back into crisis.
- [[Rubens Ometto]] — retains 50.01% voting control of Cosan via [[Aguassanta]], but BTG partners are now largest economic shareholders at ~25%. Ometto's R$500M Raízen commitment may require a loan to fund.
