**John Bogle** — founder of [[Vanguard]] (1975) and creator of the first retail index mutual fund. Pioneered low-cost passive investing, fundamentally reshaping the asset management industry. Died January 16, 2019, at 89.

## Synopsis

Bogle's contribution to finance is singular: he proved that most active managers underperform their benchmarks after fees, then built an institution — [[Vanguard]] — structured as a mutual company owned by its fund shareholders, ensuring that cost savings flow to investors rather than to management company profits. The first index fund he launched in 1976 (now [[VOO]]'s predecessor) was derided as "Bogle's Folly" and raised only $11M against a $150M target. By the time of his death, Vanguard managed over $5.6T and the passive investing revolution he started had captured more than 50% of US equity fund assets.

The deeper legacy is structural. Bogle's fee compression forced the entire asset management industry to cut costs — [[BlackRock]]'s [[iShares]] ETF platform, [[Schwab]]'s zero-commission brokerage, and [[Fidelity]]'s zero-fee index funds are all downstream consequences of the competitive pressure Vanguard created. His public advocacy against speculation, financial engineering, and excessive intermediation made him a rare figure: a finance industry titan who argued his own industry extracted too much from the economy. The irony is that Bogle himself opposed ETFs (preferring traditional mutual funds) and resisted international expansion, positions his successors at Vanguard reversed after his departure.
