# Amogy

Brooklyn-based climate tech startup developing proprietary ammonia-cracking catalysts and modular ammonia-to-power systems for decarbonizing maritime shipping, power generation, and heavy industry. Founded in 2020 by four [[MIT]] PhD alumni, Amogy has raised ~$302M across 9 rounds, reaching a $700M valuation as of July 2025. The company's catalyst splits NH3 → N2 + H2 up to 70% more efficiently than state-of-the-art, paired with fuel cells or hydrogen engines to generate zero-carbon electricity without combustion (no NOx). First commercial deployments targeting 2026 in South Korea with [[GS Engineering & Construction]], [[HD Hyundai Infracore]], and manufacturing partner [[Samsung Heavy Industries]].

## Synopsis

Amogy is building the bridge between ammonia infrastructure and clean power. The thesis is simple: ammonia (NH3) is already manufactured and transported at global scale for fertilizer, it's liquid at moderate pressure, and it carries more hydrogen per unit volume than liquid hydrogen itself. The problem has always been cracking it efficiently — traditional approaches require massive high-temperature reactors. Amogy's proprietary catalysts work at lower temperatures, in compact modular systems, achieving 70% better efficiency than incumbents. The company doesn't burn ammonia (avoiding the NOx problem plaguing direct-combustion approaches in Japan and Korea), instead cracking it into hydrogen and feeding that to fuel cells. Valuation at $700M on ~$302M raised signals investor confidence, but revenue is pre-commercial — the bull case rests entirely on execution of the 2026-2027 deployment pipeline (1 MW Pohang pilot, JGC catalyst deployment, Kinetics floating power). The bear case: ammonia cracking competes with direct ammonia combustion approaches favored by Japan's power utilities, and the IMO carbon tax (2027) could arrive before Amogy can scale.

## Evolution

The story of Amogy is the story of ammonia's unfulfilled promise as a hydrogen carrier — and four MIT engineers who bet they could solve the cracking problem at scale.

- 2020 — Founding. Seonghoon Woo, then working in semiconductors, reached out to fellow MIT PhD alumni Hyunho Kim (catalysts), Jongwon Choi (materials), and Young Suk Jo (energy systems, specifically ammonia/hydrogen engines). Jo had been running experiments on ammonia cracking during his PhD. Kim was developing new catalysts to create ammonia fuel. The founding insight: existing ammonia cracking required massive high-temperature reactors because the catalysts were inefficient. If you could redesign the catalyst from scratch — new material recipes, lower activation temperatures — you could miniaturize the whole system into something that fits on a vehicle or ship. They incorporated Amogy Inc. in Brooklyn.

- 2021 — Proof of concept. Built and flew the world's first ammonia-powered drone, demonstrating 3x flight time versus battery-powered equivalents. The drone was small but the signal was large: the catalyst worked at compact scale. Closed a $3.1M seed round (Mar 2021) followed by a ~$20M Series A (Dec 2021) led by [[AP Ventures]] with participation from [[Amazon]]'s [[Climate Pledge Fund]] and [[DCVC]]. Amazon's involvement validated the thesis for climate-focused institutional capital.

- 2022 — Scaling demonstrations. Successfully demonstrated an ammonia-powered farming tractor, then an 18-wheel semi-truck — the world's first ammonia-powered zero-emission truck. Each demonstration increased the power output of the system, proving the catalyst could scale. Raised a $46M bridge round (Jun 2022) led by [[SK Innovation]] with [[Amazon]] (Climate Pledge Fund), [[AP Ventures]], [[Aramco Ventures]], and Newlab.

- 2023 — Maritime breakthrough and Series B. Retrofitted and sailed the NH3 Kraken, the world's first ammonia-powered tugboat, in upstate New York. This was the critical demonstration — maritime is where ammonia-to-power has the biggest addressable market, given the [[International Maritime Organization]]'s push toward decarbonization. Closed a $139M Series B-1 (Mar 2023) led by [[SK Innovation]] with [[Temasek]], Korea Zinc, [[Aramco Ventures]], [[AP Ventures]], MOL PLUS, Yanmar Ventures, Zeon Ventures, and DCVC. Extended to $150M in a Series B-2 (Jun 2023) adding [[Mitsubishi Corporation]], Mitsubishi Heavy Industries America, Marunouchi Climate Tech Growth Fund, and Synergy Marine. The investor list read like a who's-who of Asian heavy industry — Korea, Japan, Singapore, Saudi Arabia all represented. Opened offices in Norway and Singapore.

- 2024-2025 — Commercialization push. Completed ATHENA, a state-of-the-art ammonia testing and manufacturing facility in Houston, Texas — a former oil & gas facility repurposed for clean energy (symbolic and practical). Announced first pilot deployment of ammonia cracking catalyst with [[JGC Holdings]] (Apr 2025), a major global engineering firm. Signed manufacturing deal with [[Samsung Heavy Industries]] (Nov 2025) to produce Amogy's ammonia-to-power systems at a dedicated Korean facility. Secured strategic partnership with Kinetics ([[Karpowership]] initiative) for floating power plants / floating AI data centers (Dec 2025). Raised $56M Series C (Jan 2025) with BHP Ventures joining, then expanded to $80M (Jul 2025) co-led by [[Korea Development Bank]] and KDB Silicon Valley LLC, with BonAngels Venture Partners, Pathway Investment, and JB Investment. Valuation reached $700M. Opened Seoul office. A*STAR (Singapore) R&D partnership for catalyst development and Jurong Island demonstration.

- 2026 — Deployment year. 1 MW pilot with Pohang, South Korea (with [[GS Engineering & Construction]], [[HD Hyundai Infracore]]) underway, targeting commercial scale-up to 40 MW by 2028-29. Dozens of projects with multinational corporations reportedly in pipeline.

## Technology

Amogy's core IP is a portfolio of proprietary ammonia cracking catalysts using base metals and precious metals that:
- Crack NH3 into N2 + H2 at lower temperatures than conventional systems
- Achieve up to 70% higher efficiency than state-of-the-art
- Enable compact, modular reformer design (vehicle/ship-scale, not industrial plant-scale)

The full system integrates: liquid ammonia storage → catalytic cracker/reformer → hydrogen fuel cell or hydrogen engine → electric power output. Zero carbon emissions at point of use. No combustion = no NOx, no CO2, no particulates.

This is a critical distinction from the competing approach in Japan and Korea, where utilities plan to co-fire ammonia in existing coal plants. Co-firing still involves combustion, still produces NOx, and still requires some fossil fuel alongside the ammonia. Amogy's approach is fully carbon-free.

Product lines:
- Catalysts — standalone catalyst material sold to industrial partners (JGC deployment)
- Ammonia-to-power systems — modular units (500 kW to 1+ MW) integrating catalyst + fuel cell/engine, scalable in parallel
- Amogy Powerpack — integrated ammonia power modules for vehicles and vessels

## Market context

Ammonia as a fuel has two competing adoption vectors:

1. Direct combustion / co-firing — Japan and Korea plan to blend ammonia into existing coal plants. Simpler integration but produces NOx, still requires fossil co-fuel, and can't achieve zero emissions. Favored by [[JERA]], [[IHI Corporation]], and major Japanese utilities.

2. Cracking to hydrogen — Amogy's approach. Zero combustion, zero NOx, zero carbon at point of use. Requires new catalyst technology and system integration. Higher efficiency per unit ammonia consumed.

Key market drivers:
- [[International Maritime Organization]] carbon tax starting 2027 — creates direct economic incentive for shipping decarbonization
- Japan/Korea ammonia infrastructure buildout — both countries importing ammonia from US and Middle East as "the next LNG"
- AI data center power demand — Kinetics partnership targets floating data centers as emerging market
- 60% of global emissions come from power generation, maritime, and heavy-duty transportation — Amogy's three target sectors

## Insights

- The investor base tells the story: SK Innovation (Korea), Aramco Ventures (Saudi), Temasek (Singapore), Mitsubishi (Japan), BHP (Australia mining), Amazon (logistics). These are strategic bets by companies that either produce ammonia, ship goods, or mine — all needing decarbonization pathways for hard-to-abate sectors.
- Houston facility choice is deliberate — repurposing oil & gas infrastructure for clean energy, with access to Gulf Coast ammonia supply chains and workforce.
- The IMO 2027 carbon tax is the single biggest near-term catalyst. Shipping companies face real costs for carbon emissions, and ammonia is the highest-density non-carbon fuel that can be stored and bunkered with existing infrastructure.
- $700M valuation on zero revenue is a bet on the cracking efficiency advantage holding up at commercial scale. The 70% efficiency claim is from MIT News (Nov 2025) — independent validation matters.
