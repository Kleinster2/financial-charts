**Jared Kaplan** is a co-founder of [[Anthropic]] and the theoretical physicist whose scaling laws research at [[OpenAI]] provided the empirical foundation for the modern AI arms race — proving that model performance improves predictably with compute, data, and parameters.

## Synopsis

Kaplan's career arc — from string theory and cosmology at Johns Hopkins to co-authoring the most consequential empirical paper in AI — illustrates how the AI revolution drew from unexpected disciplines. His 2020 paper "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" (with [[Sam McCandlish]] and others) established that language model loss decreases as a power law with scale across three dimensions: compute, dataset size, and parameter count. The implications were staggering: if you could predict performance improvements from spending more money, then AI development became an engineering and capital allocation problem rather than a pure research problem. Every lab's subsequent decision to spend billions on larger models — [[OpenAI]]'s GPT-4, [[Google]]'s Gemini, [[Anthropic]]'s Claude — traces back to the confidence Kaplan's paper provided that scale would deliver returns.

Kaplan left [[OpenAI]] in early 2021 with six colleagues including [[Dario Amodei]], [[Daniela Amodei]], [[Sam McCandlish]], and [[Tom Brown]] to co-found [[Anthropic]], motivated by safety concerns about OpenAI's commercial trajectory. At Anthropic, Kaplan has continued research on scaling and alignment while the company has grown to a $60B+ valuation. His physics background informs a distinctive approach to AI safety: treating model behavior as an empirical phenomenon to be measured and predicted rather than a philosophical problem to be debated. The investment relevance is indirect but foundational — Kaplan's scaling laws are the intellectual bedrock for every hyperscaler's AI capex budget ([[Microsoft]], [[Google]], [[Amazon]], [[Meta]]), and any evidence that scaling laws are hitting diminishing returns would be among the most bearish possible signals for AI infrastructure spending.
