#actor #aerospace #brazil #public

**Embraer** — Brazilian aerospace company and the world's third-largest aircraft manufacturer. Dominates the 70-150 seat commercial jet segment, operates a growing defense export platform around the [[C-390 Millennium]], and runs a high-margin aftermarket services business. ADR trades as EMBJ on NYSE (changed from ERJ in Nov 2025); primary listing EMBR3 on [[B3]].

---

## Synopsis

Embraer is in the middle of a structural re-rating. From a 2020 low of $3.96 (post-Boeing collapse, post-COVID), the ADR has climbed to ~$65 — a 16x move driven by backlog growth, margin expansion, and defense diversification. The $31.6B firm backlog ($50B including options) provides multi-year revenue visibility. FY2025 revenue hit $7.58B (+18% YoY), with 2026 guidance of $8.2-8.5B.

The investment case rests on three pillars. First, the E-Jet E2 program is hitting inflection — 157 orders in 2025 (best year ever), including [[Avelo Airlines]] as the first US E2 customer. The 120-146 seat E195-E2 fills a gap that [[Airbus]] and [[Boeing]] have largely abandoned. Second, the [[C-390 Millennium]] military transport is becoming a NATO standard — 11 countries, 42+ orders, with a [[Northrop Grumman]] partnership targeting the US Air Force via an autonomous aerial refueling tanker variant. Third, Services & Support (25% of revenue, 15.5% EBIT margin) is a recurring-revenue engine that grows with the installed fleet.

The key risks are the E175-E2 scope clause freeze (blocking the large US regional market until at least 2031), [[Eve Air Mobility]] cash burn ($200-250M/year on an unproven eVTOL program), US tariff exposure ($54M cost in 2025), and single-engine-supplier dependency on [[Pratt & Whitney]]'s GTF program. The balance sheet is clean — $109M net cash, 0.1x EBITDA leverage — but 2026 FCF guidance ($200M+) implies a significant step-down from 2025's $491M. Analyst consensus is Buy with $80 average target (12 analysts, 0 Sell).

## E-Jet E2 program

The E2 family (E190-E2, E195-E2) is Embraer's commercial growth engine. Powered by [[Pratt & Whitney]] PW1000G GTF engines, new wings, fly-by-wire — 15-25% fuel improvement over E1.

| Model | Seats | Range | MTOW | Status |
|-------|-------|-------|------|--------|
| E175-E2 | 80-90 | TBD | 44,600 kg | Development frozen (scope clause) |
| E190-E2 | 97-114 | 2,850 nm | 56,400 kg | In service, 7 operators |
| E195-E2 | 120-146 | 3,000 nm | 62,500 kg | In service, 12 operators |

2025 was the best E2 sales year ever: 157 firm orders + 140 options. Total E2 backlog: ~489 firm orders.

Key orders:
- [[Avelo Airlines]]: 50 firm E195-E2 + 50 purchase rights ($4.4B list value) — first US E2 customer. Deliveries delayed from mid-2027 to 2028.
- [[LATAM Airlines]]: 24 firm E195-E2 + 50 options
- [[SAS]]: 45 E195-E2
- TrueNoord (lessor): 20 E195-E2
- [[ANA]]: 15 E190-E2
- [[Helvetic Airways]]: up to 8 E195-E2

E175-E2 scope clause problem: US major airline pilot unions cap regional carrier aircraft at 76 seats and 86,000 lb (39,000 kg) MTOW. The E175-E2 at 44,600 kg is well above. Development frozen since 2022; restart 2029 at earliest, service entry 2031-2032. Meanwhile the original E175 (E1) keeps selling — [[SkyWest]] ordered 60 + 50 options ($3.6B) in 2025.

GTF engine recall: Peak 35 E2s grounded in 2025 due to PW metal powder defect recall. Down to 5 grounded by early 2026 — "practically resolved" per Embraer. But single-supplier risk remains.

## Evolution

The story of Embraer is the story of a state-created national champion that nearly died, was privatized into competitiveness, almost got absorbed by [[Boeing]], and emerged from that collapse as a stronger, more diversified company.

- 1969: Founded by military government decree as Empresa Brasileira de Aeronautica in Sao Jose dos Campos, [[Brazil]]. Original mission: build the EMB 110 Bandeirante turboprop for the Brazilian Air Force. First president Ozires Silva. The state provided captive demand (80-unit FAB order) and subsidized development — classic industrial policy in the Embraer mold that [[Brazil]] would later replicate with [[Petrobras]] in deep-water oil.

- 1972-1980s: Bandeirante enters commercial service (Transbrasil, Apr 1973). Embraer expands into military trainers (the Tucano) and small regional aircraft. Modest export success but fundamentally a government-dependent operation — revenues tied to Brazilian defense procurement and state-airline orders.

- Early 1990s — near-death: Financial crisis hits. Workforce slashed from 12,600 (1990) to 3,200. The company approaches bankruptcy. Ozires Silva returns to lead a 1,152-day privatization campaign. This is the inflection that everything else flows from — without privatization, Embraer likely follows the path of other state aerospace experiments (Argentina's FMA, Indonesia's IPTN) into irrelevance.

- Dec 7, 1994 — privatization: Sold to private investors. Brazilian government retains a golden share with veto power over control changes, military programs, technology transfers, name/logo changes, and spare parts supply. Government also appoints one board member. The golden share is still active today — it's the mechanism that killed the [[Boeing]] deal's most aggressive terms and ensures Embraer remains Brazilian-controlled.

- 1996-2005 — ERJ-145 and E-Jet launch: The ERJ-145 family (37-50 seats) puts Embraer on the global map. Eventually 1,222 aircraft delivered — the regional jet standard for a decade. In 2002-2004, Embraer launches the E-Jet program (E170/E175/E190/E195) to move up into the 70-130 seat segment. This is the product line that defines the company today.

- 2017: E-Jet E2 program launches. Next-gen variants with [[Pratt & Whitney]] GTF engines, new wings, fly-by-wire. The E195-E2 at 120-146 seats pushes Embraer into territory where it directly competes with the [[Airbus]] A220.

- Feb 2019 — the Boeing deal: [[Boeing]] agrees to acquire 80% of Embraer's commercial aviation division for $4.2B (implying $5.25B total). Embraer retains executive jets and defense. A second JV is planned for the [[C-390 Millennium]]. President Bolsonaro approves. The market reads it as Boeing absorbing Embraer's commercial business — ERJ trades as a takeover arb.

- Apr 25, 2020 — Boeing walks away: Boeing terminates the deal, citing Embraer's failure to meet closing conditions. Embraer calls this "manufactured" — the real driver was Boeing's 737 MAX crisis and COVID cash crunch making a $4.2B outflow untenable. ERJ crashes to $3.96 (Oct 2020). In hindsight, the collapse was the best thing that happened to Embraer — it kept the commercial division intact, forced management to execute independently, and set up the re-rating that followed. Arbitration concludes Sep 2024: Boeing pays $150M (the contractual termination fee). Embraer had sought $300-400M.

- 2019 — new CEO: [[Francisco Gomes Neto]] takes over (May 2019). Previously at MANN+HUMMEL. Inherits a company in crisis — deal uncertainty, then deal collapse, then COVID. His tenure coincides with the turnaround: revenue from ~$4.2B (2021) to $7.6B (2025), backlog from ~$15B to $31.6B, and the stock from single digits to $65+.

- 2021-2023 — the rebuild: Revenue CAGR ~16%. [[C-390 Millennium]] gains European traction (Portugal, Hungary, Netherlands). Executive jets recover post-COVID. E2 production ramps despite [[Pratt & Whitney]] GTF recall headwinds. The stock rises 73% in 2023, then 106% in 2024.

- 2024-2026 — inflection: The E2 program hits critical mass (157 orders in 2025, best year ever). [[Avelo Airlines]] becomes the first US E2 customer. [[Northrop Grumman]] partners on a KC-390 tanker variant targeting the US Air Force. [[Eve Air Mobility]] completes first hover flight. Backlog crosses $30B. The company is no longer a niche regional jet maker — it's a diversified aerospace platform with defense, services, and (optionally) eVTOL optionality.

## Competition

### E195-E2 vs Airbus A220-300

| Feature | E195-E2 | [[Airbus]] A220-300 |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Seats | 120-146 | 130-160 |
| Range | 3,000 nm | 3,450 nm |
| Cabin | 2-2 (no middle seat) | 2-3 |
| Firm orders | ~489 (E2 family) | 950+ |
| Deliveries | ~168 (E2 family) | 800+ |
| Target market | Regional carriers | Mainline carriers on thin routes |

The A220 wins on range and capacity; the E195-E2 wins on per-trip fuel cost and passenger comfort (2-2 seating). [[Bombardier]] exited commercial aviation entirely — sold the C Series to [[Airbus]] (which became A220). [[Boeing]] has no offering in the 100-150 seat segment. Small narrowbody aircraft are now just 8.5% of total narrowbody orders (vs 20.5% historically) — Embraer fills a niche the duopoly has deprioritized.

## Investment case

Bull:
- $31.6B backlog ($50B incl. options) — multi-year revenue visibility
- E2 orders at inflection: 157 in 2025, first US customer ([[Avelo Airlines]])
- [[C-390 Millennium]] becoming NATO standard — 11 countries, [[Northrop Grumman]] partnership for US market
- Services & Support: 25% of revenue, 15.5% margin, growing with installed fleet
- Clean balance sheet: $109M net cash, 0.1x leverage, 9.1-year debt maturity
- [[NetJets]] $5B+ Praetor deal anchors executive jet franchise
- Revenue CAGR ~16% (2021-2025) with double-digit growth continuing
- BRL weakness as structural margin tailwind
- Tariff relief (Feb 2026) unwinding $80M in costs
- [[Eve Air Mobility]] optionality (2,700 commitments, 2027 certification target)

Bear:
- US tariff exposure (10% if restored/maintained) — $54M FY2025 cost
- 2026 FCF guidance ($200M+) implies 58% decline from 2025's $491M
- E175-E2 scope clause blocks largest potential market until 2031+
- [[Eve Air Mobility]] burns $200-250M/year on unproven eVTOL — Embraer owns 71.9%
- [[Pratt & Whitney]] GTF single-supplier risk — recall grounded 35 aircraft at peak
- [[Airbus]] A220 has 2x+ the E2's orders and growing mainline carrier traction
- USAF explicitly backed KC-46 over KC-390 — US defense market may not open
- [[Avelo Airlines]] deliveries already delayed one year (to 2028)
- Turboprop cancellation (Nov 2025) removed a future growth avenue
- BRL appreciation would compress margins (unhedged exposure)
