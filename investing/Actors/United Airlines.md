## Apr 17, 2026 — merger idea hits reality

[[Reuters]] reported that [[American Airlines]] publicly rejected any merger discussions with [[United Airlines]] and said it was not interested in such a combination. That response followed earlier [[Reuters]] reporting that United CEO [[Scott Kirby]] had pitched the idea of combining with American to [[Trump]] in late February.

The readthrough is that the industrial logic for consolidation may still tempt management teams when fuel costs spike and capacity discipline tightens, but the political path is vanishingly narrow. A United-American combination would almost certainly trigger aggressive scrutiny on fares, competition, labor, and hub overlap. In practice, the rumor says more about stress inside the airline system than about an actionable M&A path.

## Q1 2026 earnings — guide slashed $7-11 from $12-14 on Iran-war fuel (Apr 22, 2026)

United delivered a genuinely strong Q1 on operational metrics but simultaneously cut the full-year 2026 adjusted EPS range to $7-$11 from the start-of-year $12-$14 and announced a 5-point capacity cut for the rest of 2026. The guide revision is the second-largest full-year cut by a US airline in recent memory and is explicitly attributed to the [[Operation Epic Fury|Iran war]] jet-fuel shock. The stock fell 5.6% on Apr 22.

Guidance reset:

| Line | New (Apr 22) | Prior (Jan) | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| FY2026 adjusted EPS | $7.00-$11.00 | $12.00-$14.00 | -$4.00 to -$3.00 at midpoints |
| Q2 2026 adjusted EPS | $1.00-$2.00 | Consensus ~$3.50 | Well below |
| Q3/Q4 capacity YoY | Flat to +2% | Prior plan +5% | -5 points |

Fuel cost math. Rising jet fuel prices from Iran-war geopolitics inflicted approximately $340M in additional costs during Q1 alone, and forward curves have continued upward. The 5-point capacity cut is the classic "shrink into the shock" airline playbook — pulling back to protect unit economics rather than chase load factor with fuel at current levels. Capacity compares directly to the [[Iran conflict airline disruption]] framework: airlines are now retracting guide ahead of confirmed demand destruction.

The structural signal. UAL's guide move is the cleanest corporate-level confirmation to date that the [[Iran conflict oil price timeline|oil price timeline]] is now a real cost-income channel for consumer-facing names, not just a macro overlay. Jet fuel is the most elastic of petroleum products to Brent/WTI moves, so airlines are the early-warning sector for the same fuel shock that UAL management has quantified at $340M for one quarter. Read-across to [[American Airlines]] (reporting Apr 23 BMO), [[Delta Air Lines]], and European legacy carriers; also a negative datapoint for [[Boeing]] order timing if capacity discipline sticks into 2027.

Demand side not yet cracking. Management explicitly framed the cut as supply-side — fuel and capacity rationalization — not demand destruction. Premium cabins and international held up; leisure demand softened but did not collapse. The bull case for re-rating depends on the jet-fuel curve normalizing (see [[Iran conflict oil price timeline]] for the scenarios) before the capacity cuts have to be extended into 2027.
