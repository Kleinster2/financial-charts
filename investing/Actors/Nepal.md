#actor #country #south-asia #emerging-market

**Nepal** — Landlocked South Asian country between [[India]] and [[China]]. Population ~30 million. The most remittance-dependent major economy globally on a GDP-share basis (~10%), with the bulk of foreign worker stock concentrated in the [[GCC]] (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE) and Malaysia. Currency (NPR) is pegged to the [[Indian rupee]] (INR) at a fixed band, so Nepal's FX dynamics inherit Indian rupee volatility plus its own remittance-flow signal.

The investing-vault relevance is concentrated in two channels: (1) Nepal as the canonical sensitivity point for any Gulf-remittance shock (the [[Iran conflict remittance flows|Iran war]] channel pushes Nepal first and hardest), and (2) Nepal as a stress signal for the broader South Asian sovereign-credit cohort — if Nepal moves, it's a leading indicator for [[Bangladesh]] / [[Sri Lanka]] / [[Pakistan]].

## Iran war exposure — remittance and energy

Nepal is the most exposed major economy to two simultaneous Iran-war transmission channels:

1. Remittance compression — ~10% of GDP comes from remittances, the highest share globally. The bulk of the worker stock is in the [[GCC]]. Any Gulf-side employment shock — direct (worker risk, repatriations) or indirect (employer earnings compression) — translates into outsized GDP impact in Nepal vs other South Asian remittance-recipient countries. The [[Times of Oman]] scenario set (3+ months conflict → 30% remittance reduction) implies a ~3% GDP hit for Nepal alone — larger than any single fiscal stimulus the country can mount. See [[Iran conflict remittance flows#Structural setup — GCC migrant workforce]].
2. Energy passthrough via [[India]] — Nepal imports ~100% of petroleum products via Indian refiners ([[Indian Oil Corporation]] is the largest supplier). The Iran war's impact on Indian fuel prices (see [[India]] [[Iran conflict country responses#Pattern 1 — OECD stockpile expansion|consumer-side FX defense]]) passes through to Nepal with a small lag. Nepal lacks any strategic petroleum reserve and has no diversified import path.

The currency peg to the [[Indian rupee]] adds a third dimension — Nepal cannot independently devalue to absorb the shock; whatever the [[Reserve Bank of India|RBI]] does to the INR, the NPR follows. The combination (peg, no SPR, no IMF program, high remittance dependence) makes Nepal arguably the single most fragile major South Asian economy to a sustained Iran-war scenario, even before sovereign-rating coverage exists to flag the stress.

## Why no sovereign rating

Nepal has not been formally covered by [[S&P Global]], [[Moody's]], or [[Fitch Ratings]] historically because:

- Sovereign debt is dominantly multilateral (World Bank, ADB) rather than market-issued
- No active foreign-currency Eurobond program
- Domestic-currency debt is held by [[Nepal Rastra Bank]] (central bank) and domestic banks under captive-buyer dynamics

The absence of a rating means Nepal does not appear on the named cohort in [[Iran conflict sovereign credit risk]] (which is anchored to S&P's March 12 framework). But the underlying credit risk is materially worse than the named cohort — Nepal would be the first sovereign in the South Asian cohort to face genuine debt-service capacity questions if a sustained shock materialized.
