# Index Ventures

European-born venture capital firm that became arguably the most successful VC to cross the Atlantic. Founded in Geneva in 1996 out of a Swiss bond-trading family office, now headquartered in London with offices in San Francisco, New York, and Geneva. Total capital raised: $15B+ across 30 years. Equal partnership structure with eight investing partners.

---

## Synopsis

**Index Ventures** is a top-tier global VC that occupies a unique position: the only European firm to have genuinely won in the US market. The numbers back it up — in 2025 alone, Index netted ~$9B in realized gains and unsold shares from six exits, headlined by a $7.2B stake in [[Figma]] (from a $2M seed check), $3.8B from [[Wiz]] (via [[Alphabet|Google]]'s $32B acquisition), and $1.8B from [[Scale AI]] (Meta's 49% purchase). Its 2012 vintage fund returned a DPI of 11x vs. the industry average of 1.5x. A 2015 growth fund returned 5.1x DPI. The firm has $15B in total capital raised, structured across seed (Origin), venture, and growth funds. Current portfolio gems include [[Revolut]] ($75B, preparing IPO — potentially larger than the Wiz exit), [[Discord]] (confidentially pursuing IPO), and a recent position in [[Anthropic]] ($183B round, Sep 2025). The partnership is navigating a generational transition: Danny Rimer (55) has flagged Shardul Shah, Nina Achadjian, Martin Mignot, and Jan Hammer as the next generation, following Mike Volpi's retirement in 2023. The firm has been disciplined about what it avoids — no crypto, no defense, no wealth management — though it's acknowledged playing catch-up on AI after initially backing only [[Cohere]] and [[Mistral]] among frontier labs.

## Evolution

The story of Index Ventures is the story of how a Swiss family's bond-trading desk became one of the world's most successful venture capital firms by being early to the thesis that Europe could produce globally dominant tech companies — then proving it by actually winning in Silicon Valley.

- **1976:** Gerald Rimer founds Index Securities in Geneva — a Swiss franc bond-trading firm. No connection to tech.
- **1992:** Gerald recruits his son Neil Rimer to join the firm. Neil starts a technology investment arm, recognizing that the same analytical rigor applied to bonds could work for startup equity. This is the proto-Index.
- **1996:** Neil Rimer, his brother David Rimer, and Giuseppe Zocco officially found Index Ventures in Geneva. First pilot fund: $17M — tiny even by 1990s European VC standards. Followed by a $180M second fund. The pitch: bring Silicon Valley-style venture capital to Europe.
- **2002:** Danny Rimer (Neil's younger brother, then in his early 30s) joins after a stint as an investment banking analyst in California. Opens the London office, which becomes the firm's base. The family firm starts professionalizing.
- **2003-2005:** Index begins investing in Israel (which becomes a core market). Raises €300M for Fund III. The European tech scene is still nascent — few startups, fewer exits.
- **2005:** First blockbuster exit — eBay acquires [[Skype]] for $2.6B. Index was an early backer. This puts them on the map and validates the thesis that European startups can produce massive outcomes.
- **2007:** Raises €350M for Fund IV, with 15-20% allocated to Israeli companies. The Israel connection proves prescient — it eventually leads to [[Wiz]].
- **2011-2012:** Danny Rimer moves back to California and opens the San Francisco office. Index Ventures V raises $700M. This is the pivotal moment: the firm stops being "a European VC that sometimes invests in the US" and becomes a genuinely transatlantic operation. Rimer backs early-stage creative companies like Patreon, Glossier, and — crucially — writes a $2M seed check into [[Figma]].
- **2012:** Fund VI raises €350M. This vintage ultimately returns a DPI of **11x** (vs. 1.5x industry average). The Figma seed is the cornerstone.
- **2014:** Announces a $550M early-stage fund for Europe, US, and Israel. Promotes Shardul Shah to partner.
- **2015:** Raises a $780M growth fund that returns **5.1x DPI**. Performance starts attracting the best founders transatlantically.
- **2016:** Spins off life sciences business as Medicxi Ventures — a clean separation that keeps Index focused on tech.
- **2017:** Fund VII raises €400M. Portfolio includes early investments in what become major public companies: [[Roblox]], [[Robinhood]], [[Datadog]].
- **2021:** Raises $3.1B across three funds — $900M venture (Fund XI), $2B growth (Fund VI), $200M seed (Origin I). Peak of the ZIRP bubble, but Index's fund construction stays disciplined.
- **2022:** Raises $300M for Origin II (seed fund). Market downturn — while competitors scramble, Index leans into the thesis that the best startups emerge in downturns.
- **2023:** Mike Volpi, longtime SF partner who led [[Scale AI]] and other key investments, retires at 56. This is the first major test of Index's succession plan — partners step down in their mid-50s to make room. Volpi retains board seats and starts his own firm.
- **2024 (Jul):** Raises $2.3B — $800M venture (Fund XII) and $1.5B growth (Fund VII). Brings total capital raised to $15B. Opens NYC office.
- **2025:** Banner exit year. [[Figma]] IPOs (Jul) — Index's $2M seed becomes a $2.2B stake ($7.2B including total gains). [[Wiz]] sale to [[Alphabet|Google]] for $32B — Index holds ~12%, worth $3.8B (from $240M+ invested by Shardul Shah). [[Scale AI]] 49% stake sold to [[Meta]] — $1.8B to Index. Plus exits in Nexthink, Dream Games, and Wealthfront. Total: ~$9B.
- **2025 (Sep):** Joins [[Anthropic]]'s $13B round at $183B valuation — first major AI lab bet beyond [[Cohere]] and [[Mistral]]. Achadjian, Mignot, and Shah all lead early AI startup investments.
- **2026 (Jan):** Bloomberg profiles the firm's succession planning. Danny Rimer (55) names next-gen leaders: Shah, Achadjian, Mignot, Hammer. The partnership model — equal ownership, shared carry — is designed to prevent the Kleiner Perkins problem.

## Investment philosophy

**What they do:**
- Seed to IPO — full lifecycle via Origin (seed), Venture, and Growth funds
- Transatlantic: European founders building global companies, US startups with international DNA
- "Marrying Silicon Valley and Europe" — companies like [[Datadog]] (French founders, US company) are the archetype
- Relationship-driven — Shah stopped making investment decisions over Zoom; in-person only
- Equal partnership — no formal leader, shared carry and decision-making

**What they don't do:**
- No crypto (resisted the 2021 temptation)
- No defense
- No wealth management
- Relatively cautious on AI until 2025 — only [[Cohere]] and [[Mistral]] before the Anthropic round
- No mega-funds — largest single fund is $2B growth (2021)

**Brand:** "If you know, you know" (Achadjian). Low profile, no social media chest-thumping, 40+ languages spoken at the firm. The anti-a16z.

## Succession

Index has a deliberate succession framework: partners step down in their mid-50s. This is rare in VC (most work into their 60s-70s) but explicitly designed to avoid the Kleiner Perkins cautionary tale, where failed succession destroyed the firm's position.

**Next generation** (named by Rimer, Jan 2026):

| Name | Age | Base | Signature |
|------|-----|------|-----------|
| Shardul Shah | 42 | NYC | Wiz ($4.1B), cybersecurity |
| Nina Achadjian | — | SF | AI startups, California profile |
| Martin Mignot | — | NYC | Revolut, European fintech |
| Jan Hammer | — | London | Robinhood, fintech |

**Risk:** The old guard (Rimer, Neil Rimer, Volpi) drove most of the marquee exits. The next generation needs their own Figma-scale wins. The Anthropic bet, at $183B, is unlikely to return 11x. Revolut IPO is the near-term test case for Mignot.

## Insights

- The 11x DPI on the 2012 fund is extraordinary — it means LPs got $11 back for every $1 committed, roughly 7x the industry average for that vintage. This is why Index can raise whatever it wants
- The firm's European DNA is an underappreciated advantage: it gives them deal flow in Israel (Wiz), UK (Revolut), France (Datadog, Mistral), and other markets that pure US VCs miss
- The equal partnership model is unusual — most VC firms have a managing partner who takes outsized carry. Index's structure prevents internal power struggles but makes it harder to identify a "face" of the firm
- Not following the AI hype early (pre-2025) may paradoxically be a strength — they avoided overpaying in the 2023-2024 AI FOMO window and entered Anthropic at what might be a more rational price
- The succession plan is the firm's most interesting strategic bet: if it works, Index proves that VC firms can outlast their founders (most can't). If it stumbles — if Shah/Achadjian/Mignot/Hammer can't generate their own Figma-scale outcomes — the firm declines the way Kleiner Perkins did
