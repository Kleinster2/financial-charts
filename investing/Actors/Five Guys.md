# Five Guys

**Five Guys** — privately held, family-controlled US premium-burger chain (founded 1986 in Arlington, Virginia by the Murrell family), with roughly 1,900–2,000 locations across about 30 countries and about $3.3–3.6 billion in system-wide sales (2024), positioned as the "fresh, never frozen" quality-and-portion leader against [[Shake Shack]], [[In-N-Out Burger]], and [[Whataburger]] in the [[Restaurants]] sector.

One-line read — Five Guys is the founder-controlled anchor of the US premium-burger segment, compounding hard internationally (international AUV ~$2.4M, ~68% above domestic) even as US average unit volume erodes under a ~$21 average check; it is not directly investable, so the only public read-through to its model is peer [[Shake Shack]].

Five Guys Enterprises LLC is one of the largest privately held restaurant companies in the US and the most international of the American "better burger" cohort. The Murrell family built it from a single Arlington storefront (1986) into a roughly 2,000-unit, ~30-country system without taking it public, without a conventional venture or private-equity round, and on a deliberately narrow menu — burgers, fries, hot dogs, shakes — made from fresh (never frozen) 80/20 ground chuck, hand-formed in-store, with free toppings and free peanuts. The model trades speed for perceived quality and generous portions, which carries a price premium: the average Five Guys meal ran about $20.84 in 2024, roughly 50% above quick-service peers. That premium is now the central tension. US average unit volume has fallen ~10.6% from its 2022 peak ($1.718M) to ~$1.536M (2024) as value-seeking consumers trade down — the same premium-versus-value bifurcation visible across the [[Restaurants]] sector ([[McDonald's]] value channels resilient, premium softening). The offsetting story is international: ~450 units across ~29 countries run AUV near $2.444M, ~68% above the US, and the family has leaned into Europe and the Gulf, securing a £185m refinancing (July 2025) to fund more European drive-thrus.

## Ownership and family control

The company is controlled by the Murrell family. The brand name refers to the five Murrell sons — Jim, Matt, Chad, Ben, and Tyler; originally the "five guys" were founder Jerry Murrell and his first four sons, redefined once Tyler was born two years after the first store opened. The sons hold operating roles: Matt and Jim travel the system visiting stores, Chad runs training, Ben selects franchisees, and Tyler runs the bakery. Jerry and Janie Murrell remain the founding owners.

Sourcing on the cap table conflicts. Most accounts describe Five Guys as wholly family-owned with no outside equity — Jerry Murrell has long emphasized refusing investor and franchise money in the early decades. Some aggregator sources claim the family controls ~75% with a minority held by outside investors (one names Miller Investment Management), but this is not confirmed by primary filings or company statements; as a private company Five Guys discloses no cap table. Treat the chain as founder-controlled, with any outside minority unverified.

## Scale and unit economics

| Metric | US | International |

The unit-economics split is the story. US AUV peaked at ~$1.718M in 2022 and has slid ~10.6% since, to ~$1.536M, as a ~$20.84 average check — roughly 50% above quick-service peers — runs into a trade-down consumer. International AUV, by contrast, sits ~68% higher than the US at ~$2.444M, because the same premium positioning reads as aspirational abroad and the footprint skews toward high-traffic urban and mall sites. The company-owned mix is high for a chain that franchised aggressively (~39% of US units corporate in 2024), and the 2024 buyback pushed it higher still.

## Funding history

Five Guys took no venture or private-equity equity rounds; expansion was funded by franchising and reinvested cash. The family retains control and discloses no cap table. Capital events of record:

| Date | Event | Amount | Type |
|------|-------|--------|------|
| 1986 | Founding capital (Murrell family) | under $70,000 | Family equity |
| 2003 | Franchising begins (300+ permits in ~18 months) | — | Franchise fees / royalties |
| 2024 | Buyback of 106 franchised units from largest US franchisee | ~$200M | Internal / debt |
| Jul 2025 | Five Guys Europe refinancing (maturing 2030) | £185M | Debt facility |

Two recent moves stand out:

- 2024 franchise buyback — repurchased 106 franchised restaurants for ~$200M from its largest US franchisee, a re-corporatization that lifts the company-owned mix and concentrates more unit-level economics in-house.
- 2025 European refinancing — Five Guys Europe secured a £185m debt facility (announced 23 July 2025, maturing 2030) to fund UK and European growth, with a stated focus on more drive-thru sites. The [[United Kingdom]] alone had 170+ restaurants at the time.

Because there is no public equity to tap, incremental growth capital comes from debt (the European facility) or internal cash flow — a structural governor on how fast a founder-controlled operator can press its international advantage.

## Competitive positioning

Five Guys anchors the US premium / "better burger" segment alongside three reference points:

| Peer | Status | Note |
|------|--------|------|
| [[Shake Shack]] | Public | "fine casual"; higher reported AUV; the tradeable proxy for the segment |
| [[In-N-Out Burger]] | Private (Snyder family) | West Coast; very high AUV; narrowest geography; does not franchise |
| [[Whataburger]] | PE-controlled | Texas / US South; majority sold to [[BDT & MSD Partners]] (2019) |

Both Shake Shack and In-N-Out are reported to run higher AUV and margins than Five Guys; Five Guys' distinguishing edge is geographic breadth (the most international of the cohort) and the fresh-never-frozen, large-portion positioning. Smaller premium players (Smashburger, owned by Jollibee) round out the field. The broader frame is the [[Restaurants]] sector — value QSR ([[McDonald's]], [[Yum! Brands]]) sits below on price, fast-casual ([[Chipotle]]) is adjacent on check, and delivery flows through [[DoorDash]].

## Why it matters / investability

Five Guys is not directly investable — no ticker, no public equity, no disclosed cap table. Its relevance to the vault is threefold:

1. Read-through to public peers — the cleanest listed proxy for the premium-burger model is [[Shake Shack]]; Five Guys' domestic AUV trend and pricing power are a private-market data point on the same consumer.
2. Trade-down signal — a ~$21 average check makes Five Guys a sensitive gauge of premium-QSR demand; its US AUV decline (−10.6% from 2022) corroborates the value-over-premium bifurcation seen across [[Restaurants]] ([[McDonald's]] value resilience versus premium softness).
3. International franchising playbook — the most globally extended US burger brand, and a case study in scaling a quality-positioned concept through master franchising and a ring-fenced European JV (the £185m facility).

## What to watch

- US average unit volume — the core domestic-health gauge; further declines from ~$1.536M signal the premium check is losing the trade-down fight, while stabilization or recovery signals pricing power holding.
- Average check versus QSR peers — currently ~50% above; if the gap widens into a slowing-consumer backdrop, traffic risk rises; menu or value-format moves would signal a defensive response.
- International unit growth and AUV — Europe and the Gulf are the growth engine (AUV ~$2.4M); the pace of drive-thru openings funded by the £185m facility is the read on the next leg.
- Company-owned mix — the 2024 buyback pushed corporate ownership toward ~40% of US units; further buybacks concentrate economics and capex in-house, changing the cash-flow profile.
- Ownership and succession — fully founder-controlled with no disclosed succession plan; any move toward outside capital, a minority sale, or (unlikely) an IPO would reprice the whole premium-burger comp set, [[Shake Shack]] included.

## Analysis

Two forces pull Five Guys in opposite directions. Domestically, the model's defining feature — premium ingredients and large portions at a premium price — has become a liability in a trade-down cycle: a ~$21 average check sits awkwardly above value QSR just as consumers rotate toward it, and the ~10.6% AUV decline since 2022 is the symptom. Internationally, the same premium positioning reads as aspirational, AUV runs ~68% higher, and the family's capital (the £185m European facility, the drive-thru push) is following that signal.

The 2024 franchise buyback is the structural tell. Repurchasing 106 units for ~$200M and lifting the company-owned mix toward ~40% means the family is choosing to own more of the domestic economics outright rather than collect 6% royalties — a vote of confidence in unit-level cash flow, but also a shift that puts more capital and operating risk on the balance sheet of a company with no public equity to tap. Funding then has to come from debt or internal cash, which constrains how fast a privately held operator can press its international edge.

The open question is governance and time horizon. Five Guys is one of the last large US restaurant brands that is genuinely founder-controlled, with no disclosed succession plan and no outside equity. That independence is the source of its consistency, but it also means the eventual liquidity event — generational transfer, a minority sale, or a sale to a strategic or PE buyer (the path [[Whataburger]] took with [[BDT & MSD Partners]]) — is unscheduled, and would be a material event for the premium-burger comp set and for the only tradeable proxy, [[Shake Shack]].
