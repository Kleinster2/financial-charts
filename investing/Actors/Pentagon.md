**Pentagon** — headquarters of the [[United States]] Department of Defense, commonly used as a metonym for the US military establishment and its civilian leadership. The world's largest defense buyer, with an annual budget exceeding $800B. Since [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s September 2025 executive order, the agency is also formally authorized to use the secondary title "Department of War" in non-statutory communications, and the Pentagon official quoted in the April 2026 [[General Motors|GM]]/[[Ford]] reporting used that phrasing on the record.

## "Department of War" rebrand (EO 14347, Sep 5, 2025)

[[Donald Trump|Trump]] signed Executive Order 14347, "Restoring the United States Department of War," on September 5, 2025. The order authorized "Department of War" as a secondary title and allowed [[Pete Hegseth]] to use the title "Secretary of War" in official correspondence and public communications. The legal name of the agency remains "Department of Defense" — only an act of Congress can formally change it — but the Pentagon now refers to itself as the "Department of War" on its website and in public messaging.

The rebrand was suggested to [[Donald Trump|Trump]] before inauguration by [[Anduril]]'s [[Palmer Luckey]], per the [[Anduril]] note. Hegseth was given 60 days to recommend legislative actions to formalize the change. Critics frame the rebrand as political theatre; [[Donald Trump|Trump]] said publicly he was "not sure" Congress needed to codify the change.

For investors the practical signal is in the rhetoric: Pentagon communications under [[Pete Hegseth]] now lean explicitly into a war-footing posture, which is consistent with the broader [[Defense procurement reform]] campaign and the [[Trump defense budget|$1.5T FY27 budget]] push.

## Procurement reform under Hegseth and Driscoll

The Pentagon under [[Pete Hegseth]] (cabinet) and [[Dan Driscoll]] (Army Secretary) is running the most aggressive challenge to the [[Defense Primes]] business model since the post-Cold War "Last Supper" of 1993. The doctrine, codified at cabinet level in November 2025 and enforced by [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s January 8, 2026 executive order, aims to invert the Army's purchasing mix from roughly 90% purpose-built military solutions to 90% commercial off-the-shelf.

The April 15, 2026 reporting that the Pentagon held wide-ranging talks with [[General Motors]] and [[Ford]] CEOs about supplying weapons-system components is the operational expression of this doctrine. A senior Pentagon official told the FT: "The Department of War is committed to rapidly expanding the defence industrial base by leveraging all available commercial solutions and technologies to ensure our warfighters maintain a decisive advantage." Talks pre-date the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] and are explicitly preliminary.

See [[Defense procurement reform]] for the full timeline and structural framing.

*Source on GM/Ford talks: FT (Davies, Mackinnon — Apr 15, 2026), originally reported by [[Wall Street Journal|WSJ]].*
