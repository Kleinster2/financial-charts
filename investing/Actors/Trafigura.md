# Trafigura

Swiss commodity trading house, one of the world's largest. Major trader of crude oil, refined products, and metals (zinc, copper). Operates TFG Marine, a bunkering JV with [[Frontline]] and [[Golden Ocean Group]], ranked #9 globally (9.7M MT). Private, founded 1993, HQ [[Geneva]], [[Switzerland]].

## Funding history

Private; no external funding rounds. Employee-owned partnership structure — roughly 1,100 employee-shareholders hold equity. Capitalization is internal equity plus revolving credit facilities (syndicated across Gulf, European, Asian, and North American banks) — the industry-standard working-capital structure for [[Commodity trading houses]].

## Evolution

- 1993–1999: Formation. Founded by ex-Marc Rich traders after that firm's internal split (the rump of which became [[Glencore]]). Small oil book; geographic focus Latin America + [[Africa]].
- 2000–2009: Scale phase. Oil-products growth plus entry into non-ferrous metals (zinc, copper, lead) via the Nyrstar stake and a series of mine-offtake deals. [[Rotterdam]] tankage and Latin American concessions build out.
- 2010–2014: Diversification. Infrastructure push: terminals, pipelines, storage globally; [[Puma Energy]] spinoff builds midstream/downstream fuels network. [[Claude Dauphin]] still CEO.
- 2015–2019: Post-Dauphin transition. Jeremy Weir assumes CEO (2014); metals business scales. Legal settlements with US DoJ for [[Brazil]]-related bribery cases (Odebrecht era) hit [[Puma Energy|Puma]] and [[Petrobras]] chains.
- 2020–2022: Profit step-change. [[Oil]] volatility + [[Russia]]-[[Ukraine]] dislocation drive aggregate equity above $16B. Record trading profit across crude, products, and metals.
- 2023–2024: Normalization + reputational stress. Net profit $7.4B (2023) → $2.8B (2024). [[Nickel]] fraud scandal in 2023 (allegations of loss-hiding in Chinese subsidiary) forces governance review.
- 2025: Leadership transition. Richard Holtum becomes CEO; Weir moves to Executive Chairman.
- 2026 Mar: Iran war response. $3B revolving credit facility added to buffer margin-call demand as Hormuz closure spikes Brent.

## 2026 [[Iran]] war impact

Trafigura was among the commodity traders that absorbed early losses in the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]]. Per [[Oliver Wyman]]'s 2026 commodity trading report (released April 2026), the firm's position going into the war reflected industry consensus that oil prices would fall; the war spiked them. Losses came from short futures positions hit by the [[Brent crude]] surge from ~$70 to $100+ within three weeks, plus margin calls on physical-cargo hedges and the cost of replacing pre-sold cargoes trapped inside the closed [[Strait of Hormuz]]. The FT reported that losses at Trafigura and [[Mercuria Energy Group|Mercuria]] both ran into the billions early in the war, though some positions had reversed by April.

### $3B additional credit facility (March 2026)

Trafigura announced in early March 2026 an additional $3B credit facility as "liquidity buffer, if required during a period of heightened commodity price volatility." [[Vitol]] secured a parallel $3B facility and [[Gunvor]] $1.5B. The facility sizes reflected the combined scale of margin calls and inventory-funding needs when futures prices move $20–30/bbl in days while physical cargo ownership remains unchanged. See [[Commodity trading houses]] for the working-capital mechanics.
