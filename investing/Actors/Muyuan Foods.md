# Muyuan Foods

World's largest pig farming enterprise by sales volume, dual-listed in Shenzhen (002714.SZ) and Hong Kong (02714.HK). Founded in 1992 by [[Qin Yinglin]] and [[Qian Ying]] with 22 pigs in rural Henan province; now sells ~78 million hogs annually. Raised $1.4B in its [[Hong Kong]] secondary listing in February 2026 — the first Chinese pig farmer with A+H dual listing. Pioneered multistorey hog farming (up to six storeys) and is now exporting the model to [[Vietnam]] via a $454M joint venture with [[BAF Vietnam]].

---

## Synopsis

Muyuan is the scale winner of [[China]]'s post-African-swine-fever consolidation. The 2018 ASF outbreak killed roughly half the national herd and destroyed smallholder economics, triggering a government push toward commercially managed farms with tighter biosecurity. Muyuan rode this wave: production capacity reached 81 million pigs in 2024, nearly a tenth housed in multistorey buildings with proprietary environmental controls, automated feeding, and data-driven genetic selection. The company turned a CNY 4.3B net loss into a CNY 17.9B net profit in a single year (2024), driven by hog price recovery and cost discipline — a reminder of how violently cyclical this business is.

The Hong Kong IPO (Feb 6, 2026, HK$10.7B gross proceeds, priced at top of range) funds the international expansion bet. The BAF Vietnam project — a VND 12 trillion ($454M) complex in Tay Ninh province housing 64,000 sows, targeting 1.6 million market pigs annually from 2027 — is the first export of China's multistorey hog technology. If it works, Muyuan becomes the template for vertically integrated, technology-dense livestock farming across [[Southeast Asia]]. Beijing is simultaneously capping domestic herd size to support prices, which pushes producers offshore.

The core risk is pork cycle exposure. Muyuan swings from billions in profit to billions in losses depending on hog prices — FY2023 net loss was CNY 4.3B, FY2024 net income was CNY 17.9B. The stock trades at the mercy of a commodity cycle the company cannot control. Debt ratio sits at 58.7%, high for an agricultural company, though operating cash flow surged to CNY 37.5B in 2024. The R&D team (6,300 staff) and automation push aim to create a cost floor below competitors, but that advantage erodes when everyone copies the same vertical farming playbook.

## Evolution

The story of Muyuan is the story of how China industrialized its pork supply chain in a single generation — from backyard household farming to skyscraper-scale vertical integration — and how one couple from rural Henan rode that transformation to build the world's largest pig enterprise.

1992-2000 — Backyard origins. [[Qin Yinglin]], born 1965 in Neixiang County, Henan, studied animal husbandry at Henan Agricultural University and graduated in 1989 into a China where 300 million rural households still raised a few pigs each. He spent three years at Nanyang Food Company, where he met [[Qian Ying]], a veterinary science graduate. In 1992 they quit, returned to Qin's hometown, and started with 22 pigs. By 1994 they had 2,000; by 1997, 10,000. The Henan location was no accident — China's most populous province, deep in the agricultural heartland, with cheap land and labor. In 2000, Qin formally incorporated Muyuan Farming.

2014 — Shenzhen IPO. The listing on Jan 28, 2014 gave Muyuan access to capital markets just as Chinese pork demand was accelerating with urbanization. The company began professionalizing operations: standardized genetics, centralized feed production, biosecurity protocols. While competitors still relied on dispersed contract farming, Muyuan went all-in on vertically integrated, company-owned farms.

2018-2019 — African swine fever and the great consolidation. ASF arrived in China in August 2018 and killed roughly half the national herd by 2019 — the largest animal disease event in modern history. Hog prices doubled. Smallholders who lost herds couldn't afford to restock. The government responded with a 2019 notice formally permitting multistorey livestock buildings and encouraging commercial-scale operations with superior biosecurity. This was the structural break that made Muyuan's model the national standard. The company opened the world's largest pig farm complex — 21 six-storey buildings combining feed production, farming, and slaughtering — and began scaling production from tens of millions toward 80 million head.

2020-2023 — The pork cycle giveth and taketh. The post-ASF price spike made Muyuan wildly profitable, but herd rebuilding across the industry created oversupply by 2022-2023. Hog prices collapsed. FY2023 net loss: CNY 4.3B. This is the fundamental tension: Muyuan has the best cost structure in China (targeting CNY 12/kg average farming cost), but when the entire industry overproduces, even the low-cost producer loses money. The company used the downturn to push automation — automated inspection and alarm systems for hog health, automated feeding to reduce labor costs — betting that the next upcycle would arrive with a leaner cost base.

2024 — Turnaround. Hog prices recovered as Beijing moved to cap the national herd (~400M pigs) to support prices. Muyuan swung to CNY 17.9B net income on CNY 137.9B revenue, selling 71.6 million hogs. Operating cash flow hit CNY 37.5B (+280% YoY), debt ratio fell to 58.7%. The pork cycle delivered exactly as the company had positioned for.

Feb 2026 — Hong Kong IPO and internationalization. Listed on [[HKEX]] Feb 6, raising HK$10.7B ($1.4B), priced at the top of the range (HK$39/share). First pig farmer with A+H dual listing. Day 1 close: HK$40.52 (+3.9%). The prospectus revealed the international strategy: a $454M joint venture with [[BAF Vietnam]] to build [[Vietnam]]'s first multistorey pig farms in Tay Ninh province (64,000 sows, 1.6M market pigs/year from 2027, plus 600,000-tonne feed mill). The broader ambition: help BAF reach 100 farms, 450,000 sows, and 10 million pigs by 2030. Ian Lahiffe, an agriculture consultant in Beijing, described the Vietnam project as a "copy and paste" of Muyuan's domestic model and predicted other countries would buy Chinese technology rather than reinvent it. [[Guangxi Yangxiang]], another Chinese producer, had attempted a similar expansion into South Korea but was blocked by animal rights activists.

## Multistorey farming technology

Muyuan operates farms up to six storeys, with floors assigned by lifecycle stage: fifth and sixth floors for pregnancy and lactation, fourth floors as nurseries. The buildings incorporate sterilisation and deodorisation systems, proprietary environmental control, and automated inspection/alarm/feeding systems. The model reduces farmland needs by 4,000+ acres across the company's portfolio. About 10% of Muyuan's 81M annual capacity is in multistorey buildings.

The approach is now industry-standard among major Chinese producers. [[Zhongxin Kaiwei Modern Farming]] operates the most extreme example: two 26-storey "pig skyscrapers" in Ezhou, Hubei province, housing up to 1.2M pigs annually — the tallest livestock buildings in the world. [[Guangxi Yangxiang]] runs 11-storey farms (one reportedly 17 storeys). [[Qinglian Food]] operates three four-storey farms in Zhejiang. Wang Tenghao of Qinglian estimates 20-30 multistorey farms now operate in Zhejiang alone, up from one a decade ago.

Outside China, multistorey pig farming remains rare. Germany's "Schweinehochhaus" (pig high-rise), an East German-era six-storey facility, closed in 2023 after repeated animal cruelty accusations. Animal welfare groups criticize the Chinese model over what they describe as barren, overcrowded artificial environments and argue concentrated populations increase disease risk despite biosecurity claims.

The 2019 Chinese government notice permitting multistorey livestock buildings and a regulation requiring pig farms to be 500+ metres from residences were the policy catalysts. Land scarcity, labor shortages from rapid urbanization, and post-ASF biosecurity concerns all push in the same direction.
