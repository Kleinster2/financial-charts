# Emposat

[[China|Beijing]]-based commercial provider of satellite command, telemetry, control, and imagery-downlink services. Supplied the ground-segment infrastructure under the [[Earth Eye Co]] / [[TEE-01B]] "in-orbit delivery" contract with [[Iran]]'s [[IRGC]] Aerospace Force (see [[IRGC Chinese satellite targeting March 2026]]). Under the agreement, Emposat enables the IRGC to send commands, receive telemetry and imagery, and direct satellite operations from anywhere in the world via Emposat's global ground-station network spanning Asia, Latin America, and other regions. This geographic dispersion is the operational differentiator: when [[Israel]] hit Iranian domestic satellite ground stations in 2025-2026, the Emposat network — physically located outside Iran — remained intact. Emposat was founded by [[Richard Zhao]], previously 15 years at the [[China Academy of Space Technology]]; a report from the US House China Select Committee flagged Emposat as closely tied to the [[People's Liberation Army]] Aerospace Force. [[Aidan Powers-Riggs]] ([[CSIS]]): "Emposat is a rising star in China's commercial space sector, but it's still a product of the state and military establishment. It was founded by veterans of China's state-run space programme and bankrolled by investment from national military-civil fusion funds." Emposat did not respond to FT requests for comment.

## Ground-segment architecture

Emposat operates commercial satellite ground stations across multiple continents. For a client like the IRGC Aerospace Force, this gives three operational properties that a domestic-only network cannot:

1. Survivability — stations located outside [[Iran]] are not on Israeli or US target lists; they are inside third-country commercial space and political jurisdictions, complicating physical action.
2. Global tasking — commands and imagery can be routed from anywhere the Chinese commercial space footprint reaches, without forcing traffic through Iranian infrastructure that was being degraded during the war.
3. Data continuity — even if Iranian mission control is struck, the satellite keeps operating against Emposat stations, and Iranian analysts can draw imagery remotely.

The implication, per [[Jim Lamson]] ([[James Martin Center for Nonproliferation Studies]]): "Iran's satellite ground stations, which were hit in 2025 and 2026, can be hit very easily by missiles from a thousand miles away. You can't just hit a Chinese ground station located in another country."

## Institutional provenance

Emposat is technically a commercial company, but three features place it inside the [[Military-civil fusion|military-civil fusion]] perimeter:

- Founder [[Richard Zhao]] spent 15 years at [[China Academy of Space Technology]] — China's primary state-run satellite-design institution.
- Personnel have been linked by the House China Select Committee to Chinese satellite-launch command centres (the institutional home of PLA space operations).
- Funding has flowed from national military-civil fusion investment vehicles, which are explicitly designed to blur the commercial-state boundary in strategic sectors.

A former senior Western intelligence official quoted by the FT: "There is no way that any Chinese company could do something like launch a satellite without somebody in the administration giving it the go-ahead."

## Policy exposure

As of April 2026, Emposat is named in the House China Select Committee report but not publicly sanctioned by OFAC or by EU equivalents. Natural policy responses following the FT investigation:

- Designation under Executive Order 13382 or 13694 — limiting any US nexus.
- Pressure on third-country governments hosting Emposat ground stations to withhold licensing renewals.
- Targeting of Chinese banks that process IRGC payments to Emposat under [[Secondary sanctions]] — see [[Scott Bessent]]'s April 14 announcement of outreach to Chinese banks ([[Operation Economic Fury]]).

## Why this model scales

Emposat's cost-to-serve a new foreign customer is mostly software and uplink/downlink capacity on existing stations. The marginal cost of adding another customer (including IRGC-like sanctioned states) is small relative to the Rmb250mn-scale contracts Earth Eye signs. This is a unit-economics argument for why the architecture will expand if not actively disrupted — each customer pays for the next one, and the ground-station footprint is a shared asset whose incremental use does not trigger new Chinese state action visible to US intelligence.
