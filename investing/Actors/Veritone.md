# Veritone

AI platform company (NASDAQ: VERI) that develops aiWARE, an operating system for processing unstructured data using machine learning. Founded in 2014 by brothers Chad and Ryan Steelberg, IPO'd on NASDAQ. Revenue $92.6M in FY2024 (down 7.4% YoY), deeply unprofitable (–$88M operating loss), micro-cap (~$440M market cap as of Jan 2026). Two segments: aiWARE SaaS Solutions (government, enterprise) and Content Licensing & Media Services. The company's most controversial product is [[TRAC]], a non-biometric AI surveillance tool used by 400+ law enforcement agencies that tracks people by body attributes (clothing, hair, body size) to circumvent facial recognition bans. Public sector is only 6% of revenue but the fastest-growing segment. Recent catalysts: Veritone Data Marketplace launch (Mar 2026), Washington Post content licensing deal (Mar 2026), FedRAMP and CJIS compliance for government contracts.

## Synopsis

Veritone is an AI infrastructure company that straddles two very different businesses. The media side — content licensing, ad insertion, digital asset management — generates most of the revenue (~94%) but is declining. The government/public sector side is tiny (6%) but fast-growing, driven by [[TRAC]], the surveillance tool that MIT Technology Review broke in May 2025 as the first non-biometric tracking system used at scale by US law enforcement. TRAC lets police search video footage by body attributes (clothing, hair, body size, accessories) rather than faces, sidestepping the 15-state patchwork of facial recognition bans. 400+ agencies use it, including the DOJ (since Aug 2024), DHS, and DOD (via a $249M BPA). The ACLU called it "a categorically new scale and nature of privacy invasion." The bull case: Veritone is building the data infrastructure layer for government AI — FedRAMP/CJIS certified, Data Marketplace (Mar 2026), Washington Post licensing deal — and TRAC is the wedge product. The bear case: the company has never been profitable, burns $30M+ annually, did a $10M equity offering at $1.41/share (Jun 2025), revenue is declining, and TRAC faces inevitable regulatory/legal challenges.

## Evolution

The story of Veritone is the story of an AI platform company searching for a viable market. Chad and Ryan Steelberg founded the company in 2014 in Irvine, CA, building aiWARE as a general-purpose AI operating system for processing unstructured data — initially targeting media companies for ad insertion, content management, and digital rights. The platform integrates hundreds of AI models (speech-to-text, NLP, object recognition) into a unified engine.

The company IPO'd on NASDAQ and rode the media AI wave to $149.7M revenue in 2022, but the content licensing market contracted sharply in 2023 (–33% revenue), exposing the fragility of the media business. Operating losses ballooned to –$99.6M. Management pivoted hard toward government and public sector, where aiWARE's ability to process video, audio, and image data mapped naturally onto law enforcement needs.

The pivot's most visible — and controversial — product is TRAC, launched as a way to help police identify suspects without using facial recognition. By scanning body attributes rather than biometrics, TRAC slips through the legal cracks of facial recognition bans in 15 states. The DOJ began using it in August 2024, and by May 2025 when MIT Technology Review broke the story, 400 agencies were customers. Internally, Veritone calls it "the Jason Bourne app." The ACLU immediately raised alarms, calling it the first non-biometric tracking system at scale and warning it enables surveillance capabilities "literally not possible any time before in human history."

Despite the controversy — or because of it — public sector became Veritone's fastest-growing market. In 2026, the company launched Veritone Data Marketplace (VDM, Mar 10), a governed exchange for rights-cleared AI training datasets, and signed a multi-year content licensing deal with The Washington Post (Mar 5). Both leverage FedRAMP and CJIS compliance. But the financial picture remains dire: $10M equity raise at $1.41/share (Jun 2025), continued operating losses, and a micro-cap valuation that prices in significant doubt about survival.

## Investment case

**Bull:**
- TRAC is a wedge into a massive government surveillance market with no direct competitor at scale
- FedRAMP + CJIS certification creates barriers for competitors
- Data Marketplace could become the compliant data exchange for government AI
- Public sector is fastest-growing segment; DOJ/DHS/DOD are anchor customers
- Revenue re-accelerating in 2H 2025 per management guidance
- Micro-cap + controversial product = potential for narrative-driven re-rating

**Bear:**
- Never profitable; cumulative losses >$250M; $10M equity raise at $1.41 signals distress
- Media business is declining and may not stabilize
- TRAC faces inevitable regulatory and legal challenges — one bad court case could end it
- Civil liberties backlash could trigger new legislation specifically covering non-biometric tracking
- 38M diluted shares with continued dilution expected
- Tiny public sector revenue (6% of total) — the narrative is far ahead of the numbers
