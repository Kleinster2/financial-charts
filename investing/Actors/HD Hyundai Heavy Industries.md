# HD Hyundai Heavy Industries

World's largest shipbuilder by order backlog and vessel weight, headquartered in Ulsan, [[South Korea]]. Part of the [[HD Hyundai]] group (separate from [[Hyundai]] Motor Group). Merged with HD Hyundai Mipo in Dec 2025, consolidating large and mid-sized vessel production. Listed on KRX (329180.KS) at 516,000 KRW, market cap ~$36B, forward PE ~16.6x.

## Synopsis

Korean shipbuilders are in a structural supercycle, and HD Hyundai Heavy is the biggest beneficiary. The thesis: Korea's Big Three ([[HD Hyundai Heavy Industries]], [[Samsung Heavy Industries]], [[Hanwha Ocean]]) are the TSMC of the ocean — an oligopoly in high-complexity vessels (LNG carriers, LPG/ammonia carriers) that Chinese yards can't match on quality, safety certification, or technology. Korea holds 83.8% of the global LNG carrier market. HD KSOE (the parent holding) alone holds a $74.2B order backlog — three to four years of guaranteed production. Revenue grew from $7.0B (2022) to $10.5B (2024), with TTM at $11.6B. The profitability inflection is the real story: from -$960M pretax (2021) to $560M (2024) to $1.19B TTM, with the Big Three projected to earn combined 10T+ KRW operating profit in 2026 for the first time ever, margins reaching 15-20%. The company is also becoming a US defense partner — signed an MoU with [[Huntington Ingalls Industries]] for US Navy logistics vessels (Oct 2025), the first Korean-American shipbuilding collaboration. At 516,000 KRW (21% off the Jan 2026 all-time high of 652,000 KRW), the pullback creates a potential entry for a multi-year earnings ramp.

## The Korean shipbuilding supercycle

Korea's shipbuilding industry exported a record $31.8B in vessels in 2025 (+22% YoY). The Big Three hold a combined order backlog of $137B (~192T KRW), approaching 2008-cycle levels. 2026 order target: $46.4B combined (+27% from 2025). Combined operating profit expected to exceed 10T KRW for the first time.

Why Korea wins:
- 83.8% share of global LNG carrier market — these are the most complex commercial vessels ($260M+ each, ~3 years to build)
- Gas carriers (LNG, LPG, ammonia) make up 60%+ of order books — highest margin segment
- Chinese yards dominate bulk carriers and tankers but can't match Korean quality/certification for gas carriers
- Fleet replacement cycle: aging global fleet + IMO environmental regulations forcing newbuilds
- Steel costs stable, labor costs stabilized by foreign worker influx

The industry structure:

| Company | 2025 Orders | 2026 Order Backlog | Strength |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|----------|
| HD KSOE (HD HHI + Mipo + Samho) | $18.2B (129 vessels) | $74.2B | Scale, LNG, naval, AI navigation |
| [[Samsung Heavy Industries]] | ~$10B | $31.6B | LNG, offshore, Amogy partnership |
| [[Hanwha Ocean]] | ~$8B | $31.4B | Defense, US entry (Philly Shipyard), nuclear subs |

## HD Hyundai Mipo merger (Dec 2025)

HD HHI merged with HD Hyundai Mipo to create a unified entity. HHI is the surviving company. The merger consolidates:
- HHI: large vessels (LNG carriers, VLCCs, large container ships)
- Mipo: mid-sized vessels (product tankers, chemical tankers, smaller container ships)

The combined entity has flexibility across the full vessel size spectrum and can allocate dock capacity to the highest-margin orders.

## Defense and US strategic partnership

The MASGA ("Make American Shipbuilding Great Again") framework — a $150B US-Korea shipbuilding cooperation — has turned Korean yards into America's strategic industrial partners.

Key developments:
- MoU with [[Huntington Ingalls Industries]] (Oct 2025) to jointly design US Navy logistics support vessels — first Korean-American shipbuilding collaboration
- HD Hyundai participating in WDS 2026 (Saudi Arabia's defense exhibition)
- [[Hanwha Ocean]] acquired Philly Shipyard ($100M) with $5B expansion plan to build 20 ships/year including nuclear submarines
- Korean naval expertise increasingly exportable as global rearmament accelerates

This dovetails with [[Long global rearmament]] and [[Long defense AI]] theses.
