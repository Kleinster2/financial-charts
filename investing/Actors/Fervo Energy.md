#actor #private #energy #geothermal #usa

**Fervo Energy** — Enhanced geothermal startup using horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing techniques borrowed from shale oil/gas to tap subsurface heat in locations beyond traditional volcanic zones. Backed by [[Breakthrough Energy Ventures]] ([[Bill Gates]]) and a company formerly run by Energy Secretary [[Chris Wright]]. Targeting a $2B-$3B IPO in 2026.

---

## Synopsis

Fervo is a bet that the shale revolution playbook works underground for heat, not just hydrocarbons. This is the geothermal equivalent of where shale was around 2008-2010 — technology proven in concept, costs falling fast, skeptics focused on whether it scales, and a massive demand pull that didn't exist five years earlier. The difference is the demand driver is AI instead of peak oil anxiety, and the political tailwind is bipartisan instead of contested.

The demand side is solved. Hyperscalers need 24/7 [[firm power]] now. [[Google]] already signed a 115 MW PPA (2026 delivery) off Fervo's Nevada pilot. All of Cape Station's output is pre-sold before the plant is finished. Geothermal PPAs have repriced sharply higher — [[Ormat Technologies]] CEO Doron Blachar says he can sell everything he builds. Political cover is locked: geothermal is the only renewable that survived the Trump administration's hostility (incentives intact while wind/solar got cut), and Energy Secretary [[Chris Wright]]'s former company invested in Fervo. The national energy emergency declaration explicitly named geothermal alongside fossil fuels and nuclear.

The technology transfer is real but unproven at scale. Co-founders [[Tim Latimer]] (drilling engineer from Houston) and Jack Norbeck (shale researcher) literally imported shale techniques — horizontal drilling, hydraulic fracturing, land-leasing strategy. The cost curve is moving (well costs $13M to ~$3M, drill bit life 10x improvement from 200 ft to 2,000 ft). Fervo has snapped up 600,000+ acres, and BLM lease prices surged 282% in 2025 as competitors followed. But all the data comes from a small Nevada pilot with one year of production history.

The key unknown is thermal decline — how fast the rocks cool around fractures, which determines how often you re-drill, which determines the economics. Nobody knows yet. [[BCG]] partner Mike Matson: none of them have operated long enough. Cape Station in Utah is the real test. The $2B-$3B IPO target is aggressive for a company whose first commercial plant isn't finished, but nuclear stocks have soared, [[Ormat Technologies|Ormat]] is surging, and the market is paying up for anything that delivers firm clean power to data centers. The $421M debt package (Mar 19, 2026) finances Phase 1 — the IPO funds the buildout beyond that.

## Technology — enhanced geothermal systems

Traditional geothermal (0.3% of global electricity in 2024) requires heat + fluid + permeability — a combination largely limited to volcanic/rift zones like Iceland and [[Japan]]. Developers spend millions drilling only to find no steam. [[Baseload Capital]] CEO Alexander Helling: *"The risk profile is too high, and the return is too moderate."*

Fervo's EGS approach:

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Drill two horizontal wells deep into hot rock |
| 2 | Connect them underground via hydraulic fracturing |
| 3 | Pump cold water at high pressure down one well |
| 4 | Water absorbs heat from surrounding rock |
| 5 | Hot water rushes up second well |
| 6 | Closed-loop system runs water through turbines (no steam) |

Helling: *"Suddenly, I don't need to find water, just heat."* And heat is everywhere if you drill deep enough.

[[Department of Energy]] projects: enhanced geothermal in the US could provide up to 120 GW — enough to power ~100M homes.

## Thermal decline risk

The critical unknown. As rocks around fractures cool, power output drops and new wells are needed. One year of Nevada pilot data shows a small amount of thermal decline — but the project is much smaller than Utah.

[[BCG]] partner Mike Matson: *"None of them have operated for long enough to know how long the wells will stay productive and when they will need to re-drill and how often."*

Drilling wells is the costliest part of geothermal. How often Fervo re-drills determines the economics.

## Community and environmental risks

Water loss: studies show up to 20% of water in an EGS can be lost to porous rock. Latimer stresses Fervo uses only "minimal" brackish water unusable for crops or cities. Expects higher water recapture as well designs improve.

Induced seismicity: worst case is triggering a nearby fault line (also documented in fracking areas). Most enhanced geothermal tremors are imperceptible, but [[South Korea]] 2017 incident caused widespread damage and 135 injuries. Fervo follows DOE best practices. Latimer: *"You don't want to be the next nuclear and have NIMBYism."*

## Market context

Geothermal PPAs averaged $60-70/MWh ~4 years ago. Today much higher — AI-driven electricity demand surge (75% increase by midcentury per [[BloombergNEF]]). [[Ormat Technologies]] CEO Doron Blachar: *"Whatever we can build, we can sell."*

Trump highlighted geothermal's role in his national energy emergency declaration (Day 1 back in office). Administration has kept geothermal incentives intact while scrapping wind and solar.

[[Breakthrough Energy Ventures]] partner Christina Karapataki: *"Reliable domestic energy that fits the national security needs is obviously a priority, and that has benefited geothermal."* She previously worked at [[SLB]] and was initially skeptical EGS could work.

## Investment case

Bull:
- Only renewables sector with Trump admin support (incentives intact)
- 24/7 [[firm power]] at ~90% capacity factor — same as nuclear, faster to deploy
- Hyperscaler demand insatiable ([[Google]] PPA, all Utah output pre-sold)
- First-mover in EGS with 600K+ acres and cost curve improving
- Oil/gas majors validating the space ([[SLB]], [[Occidental Petroleum]])
- IPO timing in a market rewarding power/AI infrastructure plays

Bear:
- Unproven at scale — Cape Station is the first real commercial test
- Thermal decline data is sparse (1 year, small pilot only)
- May not compete with cheap natural gas on cost
- High upfront drilling costs
- Induced seismicity risk ([[South Korea]] precedent)
- Water use optics in arid western US
- IPO at $2B-$3B is expensive for a pre-revenue-at-scale company
