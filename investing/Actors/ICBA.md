ICBA is the Independent Community Bankers of America, the primary trade association representing approximately 5,000 community banks across the [[United States]]. Its most active policy campaign targets the [[Farm Credit System]]'s tax advantages, which ICBA argues create an unfair competitive playing field in agricultural lending.

## Synopsis

Community banks remain the backbone of rural American lending — they hold a disproportionate share of agricultural loans, small business credit, and residential mortgages in markets too small or too relationship-dependent for national banks. ICBA's membership collectively holds over $5T in assets, but the average member bank is a fraction of that, operating with thin margins in rate-sensitive, geographically concentrated portfolios. The association's political influence comes from geographic distribution rather than dollar weight: community banks exist in nearly every congressional district, giving ICBA unusual grassroots leverage on Capitol Hill.

The "Reform and Refocus the Farm Credit System" campaign is ICBA's signature policy fight. The [[Farm Credit System]], a GSE created in 1916, enjoys a federal tax exemption and implicit government backing that lets it offer agricultural loans at rates community banks cannot match. ICBA argues FCS has drifted far from its original mission of serving underserved rural borrowers — now competing directly with private banks for creditworthy farm operations and even non-agricultural lending. The lobbying push aligns with broader concerns about GSE mission creep, but has gained limited legislative traction given the Farm Bureau's counter-lobbying. For investors, the ICBA-FCS tension is a proxy for the structural pressure on community bank margins.
