# Panama Canal

**Panama Canal** — critical global shipping chokepoint in [[Panama]], with Chinese port operations raising Monroe Doctrine concerns and creating a potential US pressure point.

The investing significance is route optionality and control. Drought can restrict transits; geopolitics can change who operates the ports at either end; both affect shipping costs, toll revenue, and US-China supply-chain leverage.

## Chinese presence

[[Hutchison Ports]] / [[CK Hutchison]]:
- Operates ports on both canal ends
- Balboa (Pacific) and Cristobal (Atlantic)
- 25-year concession (renewed)
- [[Hong Kong]]-based, [[China]]-linked

US concerns:
- Strategic chokepoint controlled by [[China]]-linked firm
- Intelligence gathering potential
- Leverage over US trade flows
- Monroe Doctrine violation (foreign control in hemisphere)

Statements:

Possible actions:

## Investment implications

| Scenario | Winners | Losers |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Status quo | Hutchison, shipping lines | — |
| US pressure | US port operators, Panama alternatives | Hutchison, [[China]] shipping |
| Expansion | Construction companies | — |

US port operators could benefit if Hutchison is pushed out.

[[Shipping]] reroutes: if the canal is disrupted, alternative routes such as [[Suez Canal]] or US rail can benefit at the margin.

## Water crisis (2023-2024)

Drought impact:
- Reduced transits due to low water
- Ships rerouted around South America
- Highlighted infrastructure vulnerability
- Expansion projects under discussion

---

## May 2026 chokepoint-power read

The FT's May 24 visual story ties the [[Panama Canal]] to two different chokepoint risks. The first is climate: the 2023 drought and low-water restrictions are the non-war analogue to Suez's Ever Given blockage. The second is control: [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s threats to take control of the canal and [[Panama]]'s cancellation of Chinese-linked port contracts have made the canal part of a US-China strategic contest.

[[Lars Jensen]] of [[Vespucci Maritime]] argues the pressure could revive [[China]]'s interest in a rival [[Nicaragua]] canal, but the investing significance is option value rather than immediate construction probability. [[Beijing]]'s stronger near-term tools are inspections, flag pressure, port investment, and retaliatory treatment of Panamanian-linked shipping.
