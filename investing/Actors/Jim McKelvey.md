Jim McKelvey is the co-founder of [[Block]] (formerly Square) alongside [[Jack Dorsey]]. A glass artist and serial entrepreneur, his inability to accept a credit card payment for his glasswork in 2009 became the founding story of one of fintech's most consequential companies.

## Synopsis

McKelvey's origin story is one of the most cited in fintech lore: a buyer wanted to pay $2,000 for a glass faucet handle at an art fair, but McKelvey couldn't process the card. That friction point led him and [[Jack Dorsey]] to build Square's first card reader — a white plastic dongle that plugged into a phone's headphone jack. The simplicity of the product (and the low barrier to merchant adoption) democratized card acceptance for micro-merchants who'd been locked out of traditional payment processing. Square's IPO in November 2015 valued the company at ~$2.9B, though the stock struggled initially before the Cash App pivot drove a rerating.

Unlike Dorsey, McKelvey stepped back from day-to-day operations at [[Block]] relatively early, turning his attention to teaching (Washington University in St. Louis), investing, and writing. His 2021 book "The Innovation Stack" distilled the thesis that successful startups don't just build products — they build interlocking systems of innovations that are collectively inimitable. He also co-founded LaunchCode, a nonprofit providing free tech education and job placement, and Invisibly, a data marketplace that pays users for their attention data. McKelvey remains on [[Block]]'s board and holds a significant equity position, but his influence on the company's direction is secondary to Dorsey's.
