**Lionsgate** (NYSE: LION) is a sub-scale, library-rich pure-play content company — the standalone studio that emerged in May 2025 when [[Lions Gate Entertainment]] separated its film/TV studio from its premium-cable arm [[Starz]]. It makes and distributes 30–40 films and 100+ TV shows a year, owns a 20,000+ title library, and controls franchises including [[John Wick]], [[The Hunger Games]], Saw, Twilight, and The Expendables.

## One-line read

Lionsgate is a leveraged, library-rich pure-play studio post-Starz separation, where the bet is that a 20,000+ title catalog throwing off ~$1B/yr plus a reloaded franchise slate either re-rates the equity on improving studio OIBDA, or makes the company a clean takeout for a larger platform — against the drag of ~$1.6B net debt and a still-negative full-year FCF.

## Corporate structure: a two-step separation

Lionsgate's listing is the product of a deliberate two-stage unwind of the old [[Lions Gate Entertainment]] conglomerate, designed to surface the value of the studio separately from the declining linear/streaming Starz business.

### Step 1 — the de-SPAC (May 2024)

Rather than a direct spinoff, the studio first went public by merging into [[Screaming Eagle Acquisition Corp]] (Nasdaq: SCRM), a blank-check vehicle sponsored by Eagle Equity Partners V (Harry Sloan, Jeff Sagansky, Eli Baker — serial media-SPAC operators behind DraftKings and Skillz).

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Announced | December 2023 |
| Studio enterprise value | $4.6B |
| Closed / first trade | May 13–14, 2024 (Nasdaq: LION) |
| Gross proceeds to parent | ~$350M ($274.3M PIPE + $75.7M trust after redemptions) |
| Post-deal ownership | Lions Gate Entertainment 87.2%; SPAC/PIPE holders ~12.8% |
| Use of proceeds | Debt paydown |

The SPAC's role was purely as the go-public vehicle and a capital top-up — it created a separately traded studio entity with a public market price while Starz was still attached, setting the table for the full split.

### Step 2 — the Starz separation (May 2025)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Shareholder approval | April 23, 2025 (>99% of both share classes) |
| Completed | May 7, 2025 |
| Studio | Lionsgate Studios Corp → NYSE: LION |
| Premium cable | [[Starz]] Entertainment Corp → Nasdaq: STRZ |
| Share structure | Dual class (LGF.A voting / LGF.B non-voting) collapsed to single class |
| Rationale | Two pure-play companies; cleaner investor bases; studio positioned as M&A target |

The two halves have both beaten the market since the split — see [[#The two halves since separation]].

## FY2026 financials (year ended March 31, 2026)

Reported May 21, 2026. The full year was a net loss, dragged by a weak first half (the [[John Wick]] spinoff *Ballerina* underperformed in June 2025), but the fourth quarter was the strongest studio quarter in over a decade on the back of *The Housemaid* and *Now You See Me: Now You Don't*.

The Q4-vs-full-year split is the story: studio profitability is real but lumpy, swinging on a handful of theatrical outcomes. Adjusted OIBDA of $165.4M in a single quarter against $261.3M for the full year shows how concentrated the value creation is.

Multi-year revenue comparisons are not shown because pre-FY2026 figures were consolidated with Starz; FY2026 is the first clean pure-play studio year. ## Library

The library is the ballast under a volatile theatrical business — a recurring, high-margin annuity that streamers and networks license regardless of any single film's box office.

| Metric | Value |

Library revenue running consistently above $1B is the single most important argument for a valuation floor and for Lionsgate as an acquisition target: a buyer acquires a deep, monetizable IP catalog without building one.

## eOne acquisition

In December 2023 Lionsgate bought Entertainment One ([[eOne]]) from [[Hasbro]] for ~$375M cash plus assumption of production financing loans (~$500M total). The deal added a 6,500-title film/TV library and a scripted/unscripted TV business. Hasbro had paid $4B for eOne in 2019 and exited to refocus on branded toy IP — Lionsgate acquired the content assets at a steep discount to fold into its library-monetization engine.

## Runway AI partnership

Lionsgate is the first major studio to strike a formal generative-AI training-and-production deal, partnering with [[Runway]].

| Stage | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Sept 2024 | First-of-its-kind deal: Runway trains a custom AI model on Lionsgate's film/TV library; used for pre-visualization, storyboarding, post-production |
| June 2026 | Lionsgate takes an equity stake in Runway; joint development program for AI+content co-productions, starting with a short-form episodic series drawn from existing IP |

Vice chairman [[Michael Burns]] has said AI could save the studio "tens of millions" annually. The program has not been frictionless — TheWrap reported the custom model ran into problems, with the library reportedly not large or diverse enough to train a robust general-purpose model. The financial payoff is therefore a watch item, not a booked result. See [[AI Video Generation]] for the broader tooling landscape.

## What to watch

- Theatrical slate outcomes — *The Hunger Games: Sunrise on the Reaping* (Nov 20, 2026) and *Michael* are the next big swings. A hit lifts Motion Picture segment profit and the multiple; a miss (as with *Ballerina*) pressures FCF and the deleveraging path.
- Net debt and FCF — net debt is $1,598.7M and full-year adjusted FCF was negative. Sustained $1B+ library revenue plus positive FCF supports a re-rating; rising leverage signals constraint.
- Library revenue run-rate — TTM $1.0B for three straight quarters. A break back below $1B would signal catalog monetization weakening and remove the valuation floor.
- M&A signals — a formal strategic review, or a bid from [[Legendary Entertainment]] or a streamer, points to a takeout. The Feltheimer extension through 2031 points the other way for now.
- Runway AI program — whether the equity-stake JV delivers real cost savings and shippable AI content, or stalls on the reported model/IP problems.
- Adjusted OIBDA trajectory — Q4 FY2026 hit a 12-year quarterly high ($165.4M) against $261.3M for the full year. Whether that strength carries into FY2027 determines if the Q4 turn was a slate artifact or a step-change.

## Analysis

Lionsgate is two assets bundled into one leveraged small-cap: a stable, high-margin library annuity (~$1B/yr, three quarters running) and a volatile theatrical/TV production business whose results swing on a few releases a year. FY2026 shows both faces — a $181.8M full-year net loss alongside the best studio quarter in twelve years. The investment question is which face the market should weight.

The structural tension is leverage against lumpiness. With $1.6B net debt and negative full-year FCF, Lionsgate cannot afford a string of *Ballerina*-style misses; but the library plus a single hit (Q4's $190.4M FCF) can flip the cash picture quickly. That asymmetry is precisely what makes the company a perennial takeout candidate: a larger platform ([[Netflix]], [[Amazon]], a streamer needing a content engine, or a financial buyer like [[Legendary Entertainment]]) acquires a 20,000+ title catalog and live franchises without the build cost, and absorbs the leverage against a bigger balance sheet. The entire two-step separation — de-SPAC then Starz split — was engineered to produce exactly this: a clean, separately valued, acquirable pure-play studio. The Feltheimer extension through 2031 is the counterweight, signaling the board will run it independently unless the right bid arrives.

The Runway AI deal is the wild card — genuinely first-mover (equity stake, custom model, AI co-productions), but with reported execution problems and an unproven payoff. If "tens of millions" in annual savings materializes against a sub-$300M OIBDA base, it is margin-relevant; if it stalls, it is a press-release option.
