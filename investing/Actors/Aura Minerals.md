#actor #mining #gold #brazil #public

**Aura Minerals** — Mid-tier gold and copper producer across the Americas, dual-listed on [[Nasdaq]] (AUGO, since July 2025) and [[B3]] (AURA33). Notable for a sophisticated treasury strategy using [[Cross-currency swaps]] to convert BRL debt into synthetic USD, matching liability currency to gold revenues.

Aura runs six producing mines across [[Brazil]], [[Mexico]], and Honduras with a development pipeline in [[Brazil]] and Guatemala, having compounded output from 236koz (2023) to a record 280,414 gold-equivalent ounces (GEO) in 2025 through an acquisition string (Borborema, MSG, Bluestone, Era Dorada — see [[#Evolution]]). The [[#Financials|financial signature]] is the tell: a high-margin operator whose IFRS bottom line is dominated by non-cash items. FY2025 revenue rose 55% to $921.7M with $547M adjusted EBITDA and a $458.9M operating profit, yet Aura reported a $79.3M net loss because ~$416M of finance costs — largely mark-to-market on the [[Cross-currency swaps|BRL swaps]] — plus a >100% effective tax rate swamped the operating line, leaving adjusted net income of $205.7M as the cleaner read. The equity re-rated from a $24.25 [[#Evolution|US IPO]] (July 2025) to $60.89 (Jun 12, 2026) as gold ran, and [[S&P Global]] upgraded the credit to BB- in March 2026. Instrument detail, share structure, and the peer price record live in [[Aura Minerals securities note]].

One-line read: Aura is a high-growth, high-yield mid-tier gold producer compounding output toward 600koz by 2028, where the bet is that operational execution and a firm gold tape outrun both the IFRS earnings noise its BRL-swap treasury creates and the multi-country execution risk its acquisition spree carries.

## Leadership

Aura is a controlled company: founder and Chairman Paulo Carlos de Brito holds the controlling economic stake through Northwestern Enterprises Ltd., so public AUGO and AURA33 holders take economic exposure under a founder-controlled structure. Day-to-day leadership has been stable since the late-2010s turnaround.

- Paulo Carlos de Brito — founder and Chairman of the Board since 2016; controlling shareholder.
- Rodrigo Barbosa — President, CEO and director since 2017; mechanical engineer (Mackenzie), MBA from USC. Architect of the deleveraging, dividend, and production-growth strategy.
- João Kleber Cardoso — CFO since 2020; owns the [[Cross-currency swaps|cross-currency-swap]] treasury program.

## Evolution

Aura's arc is a deleveraging-and-growth story executed under Rodrigo Barbosa, capped by a US listing that re-rated the equity into the 2025–26 gold bull.

| Phase | What happened |
|---|---|
| Pre-2017 | Over-levered, underperforming multi-asset miner; legacy TSX line (ORA) |
| 2017–2020 | Barbosa-led turnaround: balance-sheet repair, dividend initiation, [[B3]] BDR listing (AURA33, sponsored Level III — a primary fundraising listing) |
| 2020–2024 | Production base ~236–267koz; treasury program matures (Almas BRL 1.0B debenture + swap, Oct 2024); Bluestone (Cerro Blanco) acquisition announced |
| 2025 | Era Dorada + Bluestone closed (Jan); $196M [[Nasdaq]] IPO at $24.25 (Jul 16, AUGO); Borborema commercial production (Sep); MSG/Serra Grande closed (Dec); record 280,414 GEO |
| 2026 | Record Q1 production (82,137 GEO); [[S&P Global]] upgrade to BB- (Mar); guidance 340–390koz with a 600koz-by-2028 target |

The 2025 [[Nasdaq]] IPO is the pivot: it added US and global institutional capital and index eligibility to a name whose primary trading line had been the Brazilian BDR, funded the MSG upfront payment, and gave the acquisition engine a public-equity currency.

## Financials

The defining feature of Aura's financials is the gap between a genuinely high-margin, fast-growing operating business and an IFRS net line dragged into losses by non-cash items.

| Metric (US$M) | FY2023 | FY2024 | FY2025 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Revenue | 416.9 | 594.2 | 921.7 |
| Cost of goods sold | 290.9 | 342.9 | 386.9 |
| Gross profit | 126.0 | 251.3 | 534.9 |
| Gross margin | 30.2% | 42.3% | 58.0% |
| Operating income | 87.7 | 204.1 | 458.9 |
| Adjusted EBITDA | — | — | 547 |
| Finance costs (incl. non-cash swap/FX) | 54.0 | 157.8 | 416.1 |
| Income tax | 6.4 | 82.7 | 131.2 |
| Net income (IFRS) | 31.9 | (30.3) | (79.3) |
| Adjusted net income | — | — | 205.7 |

*Source: company FY2025 results (revenue $921.7M, adjusted EBITDA $547M, net loss $79.3M, adjusted net income $205.7M verified against the Q4/FY2025 release) and audited income-statement detail; figures in US$M.*

The operating engine compounded hard: revenue more than doubled across three years as production grew and gold ran, gross margin widened from 30% to 58%, and operating income rose 5x to $458.9M. Yet IFRS net income swung from a $31.9M profit (2023) to a $79.3M loss (2025). The driver is the finance line, which ballooned from $54M to $416M — far larger than Aura's cash interest — because it carries the non-cash mark-to-market on the [[Cross-currency swaps|BRL-to-USD swaps]] (the notional grew with the Oct-2024 Almas swap) plus FX, compounded by a >100% effective tax rate on non-deductible items. Adjusted net income of $205.7M (+152%) is the operating read; the GAAP loss is an artifact of the same treasury structure the company uses to hedge its currency mismatch.

## Almas debenture + swap (October 2024)

Textbook example of EM corporate treasury strategy: access cheap local BRL funding, eliminate currency mismatch via swap.

### Debenture terms

| Term | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Issuer | Aura Almas Mineração S.A. |
| Amount | BRL 1.0B (~$175M) |
| Type | Simple, non-convertible, secured with surety guarantee |
| Maturity | 6 years |
| Rate | 100% DI + 1.60% p.a. (252 business days) |

Deal evolution: originally sized at BRL 500M at CDI+1.75%. An oversubscribed order book allowed upsizing to BRL 1.0B at a tighter spread (CDI+1.60%).

### Cross-currency swap with [[Itaú BBA]]

| Term | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Notional | BRL 1.0B |
| Aura receives | 100% DI + 1.60% (offsets debenture coupon) |
| Aura pays | USD/BRL exchange rate variation |
| Net effect | Synthetic USD debt at ~6.975% equivalent |
| Improvement | Final 6.975% vs initially anticipated 7.13% |

Why this works: gold revenues are USD-denominated, so converting BRL debt to synthetic USD creates a natural hedge — liability currency matches revenue currency. The cost is accounting volatility: the swap is marked to market each period, and BRL/rate moves flow through the [[#Financials|finance line]] as non-cash gains or losses, which is the main reason IFRS net income diverges from adjusted net income.

## Financial strategy

### Debt philosophy

- Target leverage: 50-70% debt/total capex for project financing
- Focus on improving banking relationships and reducing cost of debt
- Balance-sheet strengthening under CEO Barbosa, reflected in the Mar-2026 [[S&P Global]] upgrade to BB-

### FX/commodity approach

| Strategy | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Cross-currency swaps | Convert local debt to USD |
| Natural hedges | USD revenues vs synthetic USD debt |
| Gold exposure | Unhedged (benefits from spot price) |
| FX sensitivity | Costs in BRL/MXN; 2023 AISC would have been 7% lower at constant FX |

### Dividend policy

Quarterly dividend payments — one of the highest dividend yields in the mining industry since 2020 (a high-single-digit yield in 2025 that compressed toward ~3–4% as the stock re-rated). Maintains payouts while funding greenfield development, which makes dividend sustainability a function of whether record EBITDA can cover both the payout and the capex pipeline.

## Analysis

The central tension is that Aura is, operationally, a clean high-margin growth story — 58% gross margin, a $459M operating profit, +55% revenue, record production — whose headline IFRS result is a net loss. Both facts are true and they share one cause: the BRL-debt-to-synthetic-USD swap program. The same structure the note credits as sophisticated treasury (matching USD liabilities to USD gold revenue) forces large non-cash mark-to-market swings through the finance line, which grew from $54M to $416M as the notional scaled and BRL/rates moved. Adjusted net income of $205.7M is the operating truth; the $79.3M GAAP loss is an accounting shadow of the hedge. A reader who anchors on the IFRS bottom line misreads the business, and a reader who ignores it misses the earnings volatility a controlled, EM-funded miner carries.

The re-rating has done much of its work. AUGO compounded ~+150% from its $24.25 IPO, outrunning both the gold-miners ETF (GDX) and a direct Americas peer ([[Equinox Gold]]), on the combination of a record gold tape, acquisition-led production growth, and the scarcity value of a high-yield Nasdaq-listed mid-tier. At $60.89 against a ~$67.60 average target, the second leg depends on hitting the 600koz path and gold staying bid rather than on further multiple expansion.

The growth is acquisition-built — Borborema, MSG/Serra Grande, Bluestone/Cerro Blanco, Era Dorada — which is simultaneously the engine and the risk: integrating mines across [[Brazil]], [[Mexico]], Honduras, and Guatemala while ramping greenfield projects concurrently. The treasury program is the financial counterpart to that operating ambition — it lets a Brazil-run, BVI-domiciled miner fund cheaply in BRL while reporting in USD — and the [[Aura Minerals securities note|AURA33 BDR]] / AUGO dual listing is the capital-markets counterpart, giving the name both a Brazilian retail base and a US institutional one.
