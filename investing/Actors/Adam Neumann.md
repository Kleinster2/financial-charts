**Adam Neumann** — Israeli-American entrepreneur, co-founder and former CEO of [[WeWork]]. Extracted over $2.1B from a company that went bankrupt. Now building [[Flow]], a residential real estate startup valued at $2.5B (April 2025) backed by [[a16z]]. Net worth ~$2.3B — one of the few founders to get richer as their company went to zero.

---

## Synopsis

Neumann is the most financially successful failed founder in startup history. He co-founded [[WeWork]] in 2010, rode [[SoftBank]]'s $16B into a $47B private valuation, then watched the August 2019 S-1 filing expose a pattern of self-dealing — trademark payments, building leasebacks, supervoting shares, family on the payroll — that killed the IPO within six weeks. He was forced out on September 24, 2019, but negotiated exit terms worth ~$1.7B ($970M for shares, $185M consulting fee, $500M credit line). He had already cashed out ~$700M in pre-IPO share sales. When [[WeWork]] filed Chapter 11 in November 2023, Neumann bid $500–650M to reacquire the company — the bankruptcy court rejected it. He walked away with more money than every WeWork employee and most WeWork investors combined.

The second act is [[Flow]], a residential real estate startup. [[a16z]] invested $350M in August 2022 at $1B+ — their largest single check ever, for a company with no operations. [[Marc Andreessen]]'s blog post framed it as solving America's housing crisis through "community and genuine ownership." Flow raised an additional $100M+ Series B in April 2025 at $2.5B valuation. It operates six properties in Florida and Saudi Arabia, with a 466-unit Miami tower under construction ($155M financing). The thesis is that Neumann's WeWork playbook — brand, community, technology layer on physical space — works better in residential because tenants stay longer and churn less. Flow projects positive cash flow in 2025. Whether Neumann deserves a second chance is a question about markets, not morality — and [[a16z]] has answered it with capital.

## Evolution

The story of Adam Neumann is the story of charisma as a financial instrument — a founder who understood that in the venture era, the ability to sell a vision was worth more than the ability to execute one.

- 1979–2001 (Israel): Born in Beersheba to two Ben-Gurion University medical school graduates. Parents divorced when he was 7. His mother moved the family to Indianapolis for medical training, then back to Israel. By age 22, Neumann had lived in 13 different homes. He spent formative years on Kibbutz Nir Am in southern Israel — later cited as the philosophical underpinning of WeWork's communal model. Enlisted in the Israeli Navy at 17, graduated from the Naval Academy in 1996, and served as an officer for five years. His younger sister Adi Neumann (born 1982) became Miss Teen Israel and an international model. In 2001, Neumann moved to New York City at 22, enrolling at Baruch College's Zicklin School of Business as an international student. He dropped out before finishing (returned and completed his BA in 2017).

- 2002–2007 (Krawlers, early hustle): First venture was Krawlers, a baby clothing company making infant apparel with built-in knee pads. Partnered with fashion designer Susan Lazar, sold through trade shows. The business was marginal but Neumann's salesmanship stood out — he could sell anything. Around 2006, Krawlers merged with Baby Egg, another baby apparel firm. Neumann set up HQ in Brooklyn's Dumbo neighborhood. Through this he met architect [[Miguel McKelvey]] at a rooftop party in Tribeca around 2005 — McKelvey tipped him off about affordable commercial space in Dumbo. In 2007, Neumann met Rebekah Paltrow through mutual friends. Rebekah is the first cousin of actress Gwyneth Paltrow (their fathers were brothers). They married in 2008.

- 2008–2010 (GreenDesk to WeWork): In May 2008, Neumann and McKelvey launched GreenDesk, an eco-friendly coworking space in Brooklyn, partnering with a local landlord. By 2009: 100+ offices, $350–$2,400/month pricing. They sold their stakes to the landlord in 2010 for ~$500K each. The key insight — freelancers and small companies wanted flexible, furnished space without long-term leases — was real. Neumann used the proceeds plus a $15M investment from Brooklyn developer Joel Schreiber (33% stake) to launch WeWork. First location opened April 2011, SoHo, Manhattan. From the start, Neumann framed it as a tech company, not a real estate firm. The mission statement he'd eventually put in the S-1: "to elevate the world's consciousness."

- 2011–2016 (ascent): [[WeWork]] grew from 4 NYC locations in 2011 to a $16.9B valuation by 2016. [[Benchmark]] led the Series A at ~$97M. [[JPMorgan]] led the Series C at $1.5B — unicorn in under four years. [[Goldman Sachs]] came in at Series D ($5B). Neumann's pitch evolved: WeWork wasn't coworking, it was "the world's first physical social network." The language was designed to justify tech multiples on a real estate business. It worked. Revenue hit $436M in 2016 with $0.4B in losses — already showing the fundamental tension between growth and unit economics. Neumann cultivated a messianic persona: tequila at job interviews, barefoot meetings, a "creator" identity that resonated with the WeWork membership. Rebekah became chief brand and impact officer and co-founded WeGrow, a private school.

- 2017–2018 (SoftBank era, peak excess): The pivotal meeting happened December 6, 2016. [[Masayoshi Son]] was visiting WeWork HQ but had only 12 minutes before leaving to meet president-elect Trump. He invited Neumann into his SUV. Son asked: "In a fight, who wins — the smart guy or the crazy guy?" Neumann answered correctly. Son told him to think bigger. He sketched a $4.4B investment on his iPad in the car and emailed a photo of the digital napkin 30 minutes later. The money arrived in August 2017 via the Vision Fund at a $20B valuation. With [[SoftBank]]'s backing, Neumann accelerated everything: acquisitions (Meetup for $156M, Flatiron School, Teem for $100M), the $60M Gulfstream G650ER jet, the "We Company" rebrand in January 2019. Neumann cashed out ~$700M in personal share sales through this period, including ~$300M in a 2017 SoftBank tender offer. At the $47B peak valuation (January 2019), his paper net worth exceeded $4B.

- 2019 (the fall): The S-1 filed August 14, 2019 was a document that destroyed $40B in perceived value. Revelations: $5.9M payment to Neumann's LLC for the "We" trademark (returned after backlash). Four buildings he owned leased back to WeWork with $236.6M in future minimum rent owed to him personally. A $362M loan from WeWork to Neumann to exercise stock options. 20:1 supervoting shares. Rebekah's contractual role in CEO succession. "Community Adjusted EBITDA." $1.9B in losses on $1.8B revenue in 2018. The IPO target dropped from $47B to $20–30B. [[SoftBank]] itself urged withdrawal. On September 24, Neumann resigned. On September 30, the S-1 was pulled. SoftBank organized a rescue at ~$8B valuation. Neumann's exit terms: ~$970M for shares, $185M consulting fee, $500M credit line — roughly $1.7B. In November, 2,400 WeWork employees were laid off. The contrast — billionaire founder exits, thousands of workers don't — became one of the defining images of startup-era inequality.

- 2020–2021 (SoftBank lawsuit, settlement): In May 2020, Neumann sued [[SoftBank]] for backing out of a $3B tender offer (part of the 2019 rescue package). SoftBank had withdrawn the offer citing regulatory concerns and COVID. Settled February 2021: Neumann received ~$480M in stock, $50M for legal fees, $50M non-compete fee, gave up his board seat. Total SoftBank payout in settlement: ~$1.6B. Forbes estimated Neumann's net worth at $2.3B after WeWork's October 2021 SPAC listing. The man who nearly destroyed the company was richer than most of its investors.

- 2022–present (Flow): In August 2022, [[a16z]] invested $350M in [[Flow]], Neumann's residential real estate startup — their largest single check ever, at a $1B+ valuation, for a company with zero operating history. [[Marc Andreessen]]'s blog post pitched the housing crisis angle. Between exiting WeWork and launching Flow, Neumann had quietly acquired stakes in 4,000+ apartment units across Atlanta, Nashville, Miami, and Fort Lauderdale. Flow operates six properties in Florida and Saudi Arabia. A 466-unit Miami tower (Flow House) is under construction with $155M in financing. In Saudi Arabia, Flow partnered with Sico Capital and Safa on a 920-unit development funded by a ~$293M (1.1B SAR) real estate fund. Series B in April 2025 raised $100M+ at $2.5B valuation. Flow projects positive cash flow in 2025. Neumann also launched Workflow (coworking within Flow properties — a return to WeWork's model) and Flow Trip (a media company with 13,000 paid subscribers, reportedly profitable). He has discussed an eventual Flow IPO.
