## Synopsis

**Sable Offshore** (NYSE: SOC) is a single-asset offshore oil company trying to restart the Santa Ynez Unit — three platforms (Harmony, Heritage, Hondo) in federal waters off Santa Barbara, California — shut in since the 2015 [[Plains All American Pipeline]] spill. Sable acquired the assets from [[ExxonMobil]] in February 2024 for $643M (seller-financed) via a SPAC merger with Flame Acquisition Corp. The asset has ~50,000 bbl/day production capacity and 102 undrilled opportunities across 76,000 acres, but Sable has generated essentially zero revenue — FY2025 net loss was $410M, FCF was -$769M, and the company ended the year with $98M cash against $943M debt. The entire investment thesis is a binary regulatory bet: can Sable get the Las Flores onshore pipeline restarted against fierce opposition from California regulators, environmental groups, and Santa Barbara County? A March 3, 2026 DOJ Office of Legal Counsel opinion that the Defense Production Act could preempt California restrictions sent the stock up 74% in a week, but no executive order has actually been issued. The company is also under SEC investigation for alleged insider information leaks by CEO Jim Flores to select investors including golfer Phil Mickelson. Short interest stands at 22.75% of shares out (44.9% of float). At ~$17.60 and a $2.6B market cap, the market is pricing in meaningful probability of production restart — but the path there runs through federal courts, California politics, and a balance sheet that can't survive indefinitely without it.

## The asset: Santa Ynez Unit

The Santa Ynez Unit (SYU) is what this entire company is. Everything else — the SPAC shell, the management team, the legal strategy — exists to serve this one asset.

**Physical infrastructure:**
- **Three offshore platforms:** Harmony, Heritage, Hondo — in federal waters off Santa Barbara County
- **16 federal leases** across ~76,000 acres
- **112 existing wells** plus 102 identified undrilled opportunities
- **Las Flores Canyon (LFC) onshore facility:** crude oil treatment plant, natural gas processing plant, co-generation power plant, 540,000 bbl crude storage
- **Pipeline 324:** 10.8-mile crude line from LFC to Gaviota Pump Station
- **Pipeline 325:** 113-mile crude line from Gaviota to Pentland Station (Kern County)

**Production history:**
- First discoveries: 1968. Production began: 1981
- Expansion (Heritage + Harmony platforms): 1988
- 2014 (last full year): ~29,000 bbl/day oil + 27 MMcf/day gas
- **Shut in since May 2015** after Pipeline 324 ruptured (operated by [[Plains All American Pipeline]])
- Decline rate: ~8%/year (low for offshore)
- Post-restart target: 50,000+ bbl/day with ESPs and new wells

**Why it's valuable (on paper):**
- Low-cost, low-decline offshore asset in a region where California natural gas prices (SoCal City Gate) trade well above WAHA (~$3+/MMBtu vs often negative in West Texas)
- ExxonMobil valued the asset at $2.5B+ before writing it down and selling for $643M
- If Sable hits 50,000 bbl/day, bulls project ~$500M/year FCF by 2027 (Berman Capital estimate)
- No new offshore drilling permits in California — asset is effectively irreplaceable

## Evolution

The story of Sable Offshore is the story of one man's bet that he can do what ExxonMobil couldn't: restart a politically toxic oil asset in the most hostile regulatory environment in America.

- **1968–2014:** The Santa Ynez Unit operated for decades as a prolific offshore oil field. ExxonMobil (via its Mobil subsidiary) ran three platforms producing ~29,000 bbl/day. The infrastructure was mature, the reserves were proven, and the asset was a quiet cash machine — until May 19, 2015.

- **May 2015: The Refugio Beach spill.** Pipeline 324, operated by [[Plains All American Pipeline]], ruptured near Refugio State Beach, spilling ~120,000+ gallons of crude oil across 3,700 acres of coastline and into the Pacific. It was the largest California coastal oil spill since 1969. Federal investigators found Plains failed to prevent and detect corrosion. A jury convicted Plains on nine criminal counts. Plains paid $3.3M in criminal fines (the statutory maximum), $61M in civil penalties, and $230M in lawsuit settlements. The spill shut in the entire SYU — all three platforms went dark.

- **2015–2022: ExxonMobil's failed restart.** Exxon spent seven years trying to bring SYU back online. The regulatory gauntlet proved impenetrable: the California Fire Marshal (OFSM), Santa Barbara County, the California Coastal Commission, and environmental groups all had veto power at different points in the process. Santa Barbara County denied key permits. Cal Fire changed requirements. Environmental groups sued. In early 2024, Exxon gave up, wrote down $2.1B, and sold for $643M — calling it "continuing challenges in the state regulatory environment."

- **2021: Jim Flores forms Flame Acquisition Corp.** Flores, a Houston oil veteran who'd led five E&P companies, created a SPAC (NYSE: FLME) and zeroed in on the Santa Ynez assets. His pitch: a small, aggressive operator could navigate California politics where a multinational couldn't.

- **November 2022: Flame agrees to buy SYU from ExxonMobil for ~$625-643M.** ExxonMobil provided a 5-year, $643M term loan at 10% interest to finance the purchase. The deal included a reversion clause: if production didn't restart by January 1, 2026, the assets would revert to Exxon without compensation. (This deadline was later extended.)

- **February 14, 2024: SPAC merger closes.** Flame and Sable Offshore completed their reverse merger. SOC began trading on the NYSE. The Santa Ynez complex became Sable's sole asset.

- **April 2024: Hunterbrook Media publishes short report.** Hunterbrook (whose investment affiliate Hunterbrook Capital was short SOC) published a detailed report arguing that Sable's restart was a "pipe dream." Key claims: Flores' prior company Sable Permian Resources went bankrupt in 2020; Flores had ties to Plains (the company responsible for the 2015 spill); the regulatory hurdles were insurmountable; and auditors had flagged going concern risk.

- **May 15, 2025: First production restarts.** Sable began producing oil from six wells on Platform Harmony and flowing crude to Las Flores Canyon. The company completed anomaly repairs and hydrotesting on Pipelines 324 and 325 per the 2020 federal consent decree. But the real bottleneck — commercial sales via the onshore pipeline — remained blocked.

- **May 2025: $295M equity raise at $29.50/share.** Sable raised capital to fund operations and infrastructure spending while locked out of commercial sales.

- **October 2025: The insider trading scandal.** Hunterbrook published a second report alleging that CEO Flores held a private conference call with select investors (including golfer Phil Mickelson) where he shared material non-public information, including the need to raise up to $200M by year-end 2025. Sable formed a special committee to investigate. The SEC opened a formal investigation.

- **November 2025: Sable responds to Hunterbrook.** Disputed allegations about accounts payable and outlined plans for comprehensive debt refinancing in Q1 2026.

- **December 2025: Emergency pipeline permit granted.** Federal regulators approved an emergency special permit for parts of the Las Flores pipeline system to restart. Environmental groups immediately challenged it in court.

- **Late February 2026: Two blows in a week.** On Feb 27, Sable reported FY2025 results: $410M net loss, $769M negative FCF, $98M cash vs $943M debt. The same week, Santa Barbara Superior Court Judge Donna Geck upheld an injunction blocking pipeline restart — ruling the California Fire Marshal still had the last word on restart authorization, despite Sable's argument that federal PHMSA preemption had stripped state authority. Stock fell -15%.

- **March 3, 2026: The DOJ opinion.** DOJ Office of Legal Counsel's T. Elliot Gaiser issued a 22-page opinion stating that presidential orders under the Defense Production Act could preempt California laws blocking Sable's pipeline. The opinion was written in response to a Department of Energy query. Gaiser invoked the Supremacy Clause and argued federal energy directives could override state regulation — and that the president didn't even need to personally activate the DPA; the Secretary of Energy could do so. Stock surged +37% on March 5, then +74% over the following week.

- **March 2026 (present):** No DPA executive order has been issued. California Governor Newsom's office dismissed the opinion as "as lawless as his emergency tariffs that the Supreme Court just struck down." Sable is also pursuing an alternative offtake strategy — acquiring an offshore storage and treating vessel (OS&T) that could enable oil sales without the onshore pipeline, targeting Q4 2026 production of 50,000+ bbl/day via ship loading within the federal lease area. The legal battle is scheduled to go before a federal judge panel this summer.

## The regulatory gauntlet

The pipeline restart requires approvals from multiple overlapping authorities, each with its own politics:

| Authority | Role | Status |
|-----------|------|--------|
| **PHMSA** (federal) | Pipeline safety — consent decree compliance | ✅ Completed hydrotesting, anomaly repairs |
| **Cal Fire / OSFM** | Fire Marshal pipeline restart authorization | ❌ Reviewing DOJ opinion; has not approved restart |
| **Santa Barbara County** | Land use permits, ownership transfer | ❌ Denied Exxon's permits; injunction upheld Feb 2026 |
| **California Coastal Commission** | Coastal zone permits for valve installation | ❌ Disputes Sable's jurisdiction claims |
| **State of California** (AG) | Legal opposition to federal preemption | ❌ Contesting PHMSA preemption in court |
| **Environmental groups** (EDC et al.) | Litigation | ❌ Injunction blocking restart; appealing federal permits |
| **DOJ / White House** | DPA invocation to preempt state laws | ⏳ OLC opinion issued Mar 3, 2026 — no executive order yet |

**Key legal dynamics:**
- Judge Donna Geck (Santa Barbara Superior Court) upheld injunction in late Feb 2026 — Fire Marshal retains authority until federal judges rule on preemption this summer
- DOJ opinion by T. Elliot Gaiser (OLC) argues DPA can preempt all state laws, grant criminal immunity to anyone acting under a DPA order, and doesn't require an actual emergency
- California's response: "as lawless as his emergency tariffs that the Supreme Court just struck down" — will fight in federal court
- Gaiser acknowledged he did not independently verify Sable's factual claims — accepted them at face value

## Alternative offtake: OS&T vessel

Sable's Plan B if the onshore pipeline stays blocked:
- Acquire and upgrade an **offshore storage and treating vessel** (OS&T)
- Would enable tanker loading of crude directly from the SYU federal lease area — bypassing the onshore pipeline entirely
- Target: oil sales beginning Q4 2026 at 50,000+ bbl/day
- Still requires some regulatory approvals
- If successful, makes the onshore pipeline fight less existential — but vessel infrastructure is expensive and operationally more complex

## Investment case

**Bull:**
- DPA invocation would be a game-changer — federal preemption of all California restrictions
- 50,000 bbl/day production from proven, low-cost, low-decline asset
- $500M/year FCF potential at full production (bull case)
- Asset acquired for $643M from Exxon's $2.5B+ book value — massive implied discount
- OS&T vessel as pipeline bypass contingency
- Trump administration clearly sympathetic — DOJ opinion, national energy emergency declaration
- 102 undrilled opportunities for production growth

**Bear:**
- $98M cash, $943M debt, -$769M FCF — balance sheet can't survive indefinitely
- 46.5% share dilution in one year with more likely needed
- CEO's prior company went bankrupt; insider trading investigation ongoing
- California will fight federal preemption in court — could take years
- Even the DOJ opinion isn't an executive order — no concrete action yet
- 22.75% short interest (44.9% of float) — significant skepticism
- OS&T vessel is expensive and unproven as primary offtake
- If oil prices fall significantly, the economics weaken even with production
- Piotroski F-Score of 1 — worst possible financial health signal
- "Going concern" risk flagged by auditors

## Macro context: Iran conflict energy shock

The entire Sable thesis changes complexion under the [[Iran conflict economic disruption|2026 Iran-Hormuz energy shock]]. As of March 11:

- WTI above $100/bbl, Brent ~$93. Jet fuel in Singapore hit $230/bbl
- ~18M bbl/day of global supply disrupted (net) — [[Strait of Hormuz]] traffic down 86%
- [[Jeff Currie]] ([[Carlyle]]): "no policy response can stop this" — calls it a structural regime change, not a trade
- California's argument that SYU production is unnecessary ("contributes ~0.05% to crude oil production — a drop in the bucket") becomes politically untenable with oil above $100
- The Trump administration's national energy emergency declaration, combined with DOJ DPA opinion, gains urgency when there's an actual energy emergency
- Domestic offshore production in federal waters becomes a national security argument, not just an economic one
- If Currie's "revenge of the old economy" thesis plays out (sustained commodity supercycle like 2001-2014), SYU's 50,000 bbl/day capacity at $80-100+ oil is worth dramatically more than at $65-70

**The bear counterpoint:** Even in an energy emergency, California courts and regulators may not yield. Federal preemption via DPA has never been tested at this scale. And Sable's balance sheet may not survive long enough to benefit — $98M cash against $943M debt with -$769M annual FCF means time is the enemy.
