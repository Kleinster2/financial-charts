## SemiAnalysis as live case study (Apr 2026)

[[Dylan Patel]] on [[Invest Like the Best]] (Apr 23, 2026) used the firm itself as a worked example of [[Idea-execution inversion|the idea-execution inversion]] now reshaping information-services economics. The firm is an unusually clean test case because token spend is visible in real time and headcount is small enough to track per-person leverage.

### Token spend trajectory

| Timeframe | Cloud Code spend (annualized) |
|---|---|
| 2025 (full year) | "Tens of thousands" of dollars |
| Late Dec 2025 inflection | [[Claude Opus|Opus 4.6]] launch + non-technical-staff adoption push by [[Doug O'Laughlin]] |
| Early Apr 2026 (last podcast) | $5M run rate |
| Apr 23, 2026 (Patel statement) | $7M run rate |
| Salary base | ~$25M |
| Token spend / salary | ~28% (>25%) |
| Trajectory | If extrapolated, exceeds 100% of salary by year-end 2026 |

Patel's framing: "Thankfully, I don't have to decide between people and AI because our company's growing so fast." The choice posed at other firms — token spend vs. headcount — does not bind here yet. The question is whether the productivity multiplier compounds faster than [[Anthropic]] enterprise pricing.

### Three productivity case studies

The Apr 23 interview surfaced three concrete instances of what the spend produced:

| Project | Pre-AI baseline | Post-AI outcome | Token cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chip reverse-engineering app | "Entire team's job" at [[Intel]] (per ex-Intel hire who built it) | One person: GPU-accelerated SEM-image-to-material-overlay app on [[CoreWeave]] server. Identifies copper/tantalum/germanium/cobalt at every transistor gate; full finite-element analysis of the chip stackup. Outputs feed the firm's data-services product | "A couple thousand dollars" |
| BLS task evaluation framework + [[Phantom GDP]] coinage | "Team of 200 economists, a year" | Malcolm (former bank economist): scraped [[FRED]] + multiple paid-API economic datasets, ran regressions, graded all ~2,000 [[Bureau of Labor Statistics|BLS]] tasks against AI capability. Created cross-model benchmark across the same 2,000 evals. About 3% of BLS tasks doable with AI today | Not disclosed; "completely cracked out on Claude" |
| US grid mapping + energy supply/demand | Incumbent: 100 people, 10 years, ~$900M data-services market | Jeremy: scraped every US power plant, every transmission line above a voltage threshold, mapped supply/demand at micro-region resolution. Customers — energy traders who buy [[SemiAnalysis]] data center data — said it compares favorably to incumbent | ~$6,000/day × 3 weeks ≈ $126,000 |

The strategic Patel takeaway is recursive: "I'm going to commoditize these energy services data companies. Who's going to come commoditize me if I don't move faster?" The firm's competitive position rests on being further down the inversion's adoption curve than its peers — which is itself a moat that erodes if rivals catch up.

### Why information-services firms come first

[[Investment fund]] customers (including hypothetically internal teams at [[Citadel]]/[[Jane Street]]) keep buying [[SemiAnalysis]] data despite having internal research capacity, per Patel: "It's cheaper for them to buy the data from us and then to build on top of it than it is to build it themselves." The structural argument is that an information-services firm whose comparative advantage is *speed of idea-selection-and-shipment* (not headcount) survives even as customer firms also adopt AI. The vulnerability is to a younger or smaller competitor that picks better ideas faster.

*Source: [[Dylan Patel]] on [[Invest Like the Best]] (Apr 23, 2026)*
