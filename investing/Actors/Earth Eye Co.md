# Earth Eye Co

[[China|Chinese]] commercial satellite builder and operator that developed and transferred [[TEE-01B]] — the ~0.5-metre-resolution imaging satellite operated by [[Iran]]'s [[IRGC]] Aerospace Force since late 2024 (see [[IRGC Chinese satellite targeting March 2026]]). Earth Eye describes its export model as "in-orbit delivery": it launches and commissions satellites from Chinese territory and transfers operational control to foreign customers only after the spacecraft is verified on orbit. The company's website claims one prior in-orbit transfer to an unnamed [[Belt and Road]] participant. Executive and engineering staff are disproportionately linked to the [[Seven sons of national defence]] Chinese universities known for [[PLA]] collaboration. Earth Eye did not respond to Financial Times requests for comment on the IRGC contract.

## Export model — in-orbit delivery

The structure is commercially distinctive:

1. [[Earth Eye Co]] contracts with a foreign customer — in the [[IRGC]] case, a Rmb250mn (~$36.6mn) agreement signed by an IRGC Aerospace Force brigadier general in September 2024.
2. Earth Eye launches the satellite from Chinese territory ([[Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center]], June 2024 for TEE-01B).
3. The company commissions and verifies the satellite on orbit.
4. Operational control transfers to the customer, with ground infrastructure supplied by [[Emposat]] under a linked services agreement.

The hardware never leaves Chinese territory before handover — this avoids the export-licensing pathway that US and allied regulators have traditionally monitored. The customer takes control of a Chinese-built, Chinese-launched, Chinese-ground-station-operated asset. Payment in renminbi bypasses dollar-clearing and [[Secondary sanctions]] chokepoints for the underlying transaction.

## TEE-01B — the known case

[[TEE-01B]] was launched from [[Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center]] in June 2024 and transferred to [[IRGC]] control in late 2024. The satellite imaged [[Prince Sultan Air Base]] ([[Saudi Arabia]]), [[Muwaffaq Salti Air Base]] ([[Jordan]]), the US Fifth Fleet area at Manama ([[Bahrain]]), [[Erbil International Airport]] ([[Iraq]]), [[Camp Buehring]] and [[Ali Al Salem Air Base]] ([[Kuwait]]), [[Camp Lemonnier]] ([[Djibouti]]), and [[Duqm International Airport]] ([[Oman]]) during the March 2026 war. Civilian Gulf infrastructure also imaged: [[Khor Fakkan]] port, [[Qidfa]] desalination plant ([[UAE]]), and [[Alba]] aluminium smelter ([[Bahrain]]).

## People and provenance

Per [[Aidan Powers-Riggs]] ([[CSIS]]), Earth Eye's executive and engineering teams include multiple alumni of the [[Seven sons of national defence]] — the seven Chinese universities designated as national-defence institutions and deeply embedded in [[PLA]] research relationships. This is less a formal state link than a pipeline: these universities are primary sources of Chinese aerospace engineering talent, and graduates rotate into [[China Academy of Space Technology]] (CAST) and the commercial space sector.

A former senior Western intelligence official quoted by the FT: "There is no way that any Chinese company could do something like launch a satellite without somebody in the administration giving it the go-ahead."

## Investment and policy lens

Earth Eye is private and not publicly sanctioned as of April 2026, though the FT investigation provides grounds for designation. Natural policy responses:

- Sanctions on Earth Eye under Executive Order 13382 ([[WMD]]) or equivalent — cutting off any US financial interface.
- Secondary sanctions on the [[Emposat]] relationship or [[Belt and Road]] counterparties that contract with Earth Eye.
- Pressure on the commercial-launch supply chain (cryogenic fuels, satellite bus components, optical payloads).

A broader strategic question: the company is a model, not a single actor. The in-orbit delivery architecture can be replicated by other Chinese commercial-space firms — see [[Chang Guang Satellite Technology]] for the Houthi-imagery precedent. Sanctions policy that focuses on the individual firm without disrupting the architecture will displace rather than suppress.
