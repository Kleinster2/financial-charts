**Planet Labs** PBC (NYSE: PL) is the largest commercial Earth observation company by constellation size, operating 200+ imaging satellites that capture the entire planet's landmass daily. Founded by three ex-[[NASA]] scientists in a garage, Planet pioneered the model of manufacturing hundreds of cheap, disposable CubeSats using consumer-grade smartphone components — an approach later copied by [[SpaceX]]'s [[Starlink]] and others. The company went public via SPAC in December 2021 and trades at ~$8.3B market cap as of March 2026, roughly 3x its $2.8B SPAC valuation.

---

## Synopsis

Planet sells subscriptions to the world's most frequently updated satellite imagery dataset. Its PlanetScope constellation of SuperDove satellites images all of Earth's land surface daily at 3m resolution, while its SkySat fleet provides 50cm on-demand tasking and the new Pelican constellation pushes into sub-meter territory with onboard NVIDIA Jetson AI processing. Customers span US government intelligence agencies, agricultural companies, insurers, humanitarian organizations, and financial firms using the data as alternative signals. Revenue hit $244M in FY2025 (Jan '25) and is tracking to ~$282M TTM, with gross margins expanding from 23% in FY2021 to 58% as the satellite fleet scales.

The investment case hinges on three inflection points converging: the business turned free-cash-flow positive for the first time (TTM FCF $40.7M), massive government contracts are flowing in ($230M [[SKY Perfect JSAT]] deal, €240M German security contract), and the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran conflict]] is creating unprecedented demand for exactly what Planet sells — near-real-time imaging of conflict zones. Planet's March 2026 decision to delay commercial imagery of the Middle East by up to 14 days, while maintaining instant access for government/military users, underscores both the strategic value of the data and the uncomfortable reality that the company is now a participant in information warfare, not just a neutral data provider.

## Evolution

The story of Planet Labs is the story of NASA engineers who looked at the $500M-per-satellite paradigm and asked: what if we built them like smartphones instead?

- 2010 — Founding. [[Will Marshall]], [[Chris Boshuizen]], and [[Robbie Schingler]] — all scientists at [[NASA]] Ames Research Center in Mountain View, California — founded the company as Cosmogia, Inc. Marshall had been working on [[PhoneSat]], an experiment testing whether a commercial smartphone could serve as a satellite's brain. The answer was yes, and the insight was radical: if you could build a satellite from $500 worth of phone parts, you could build hundreds and image the whole Earth daily instead of waiting months for one expensive bird to revisit a location. They built the first prototype in a garage.

- 2013 — First launches. Dove 1 and Dove 2 demonstration CubeSats launched in April 2013, proving the concept. Dove 3 and 4 followed in November. In June, Planet announced Flock-1, an unprecedented constellation of 28 Earth-imaging CubeSats.

- 2014 — ISS deployment. The Flock-1 constellation was delivered to the International Space Station in January and deployed via the NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer — the first large-scale commercial CubeSat deployment. The company began building toward 131 satellites by mid-2015.

- 2015 — BlackBridge acquisition, $95M raise. Planet raised $95M in January (total VC funding to $183M by May) and in July acquired BlackBridge, the German operator of the RapidEye constellation of five medium-resolution satellites. RapidEye's "red edge" spectral band was valuable for agricultural applications. Combined fleet: 87 Doves + 5 RapidEyes.

- 2017 — Terra Bella and SkySat from Google. In April, [[Google]] sold its Terra Bella subsidiary and the SkySat constellation of high-resolution (50cm) imaging satellites to Planet. In exchange, Google took an equity stake and signed a multi-year data purchasing agreement. This gave Planet its first sub-meter resolution capability and a blue-chip strategic partner. That year Planet also launched 88 additional Doves, pushing toward daily global coverage.

- 2018 — Growing pains. Planet launched its 298th satellite but laid off ~10% of the workforce in July. In December, the company announced the acquisition of Boundless Spatial, a geospatial software firm — but the deal soured when Planet's lawyers discovered Boundless executives had failed to disclose material details about NGA contracts. The purchase price was renegotiated from $40M to $16M.

- 2020-2021 — Constellation maturity, SPAC. By 2020, Planet had 120+ active satellites imaging all global landmass daily. In January 2021, a launch of 48 SuperDoves pushed the fleet past 200 active satellites. Revenue was $113M (FY2021) with 600+ customers, 90% on annual subscriptions. In July 2021, Planet announced plans to go public via merger with dMY Technology Group Inc IV (SPAC) at a $2.8B enterprise valuation. The deal raised $434M total, including a $200M PIPE led by [[BlackRock]] and joined by Google, Koch Strategic Platforms, and Marc Benioff's TIME Ventures. The company registered as a Public Benefit Corporation and began trading on NYSE December 8, 2021. Post-merger cash: over $500M, with management guiding to adjusted EBITDA profitability by early 2025 and ~$700M revenue by early 2026.

- 2022-2023 — Revenue acceleration, more M&A, layoffs. Revenue surged 46% to $191M in FY2023 (Jan '23) as government contracts scaled. Planet acquired VanderSat (microwave satellite data, 2021), Salo Sciences (environmental/carbon monitoring, 2022), and Sinergise (the platform behind Sentinel Hub, August 2023). But the original SPAC-era guidance was proving optimistic — revenue reached $221M in FY2024 (Jan '24), well short of the implied trajectory to $700M. In August 2023, Planet laid off 10% of staff (~120 people), with CEO Marshall taking responsibility publicly.

- 2024 — Restructuring, next-gen constellation. Another 17% workforce cut (~180 people) in June 2024 as the company "aligned resources with market opportunities." Revenue growth slowed to 11% for FY2025 ($244M). But the technology pipeline accelerated: in August, Planet launched Tanager-1, its first hyperspectral satellite for the Carbon Mapper Coalition to detect methane and CO2 emissions. Pelican-1, the first of the next-generation high-resolution constellation with NVIDIA Jetson edge computing, followed in November.

- 2025 — Contract bonanza, FCF inflection. The year the business started proving out. In January, Planet signed a $230M deal with [[SKY Perfect JSAT]] to build and operate 10 dedicated Pelican satellites for the Japanese operator (~7-year revenue recognition). In July, a €240M German government contract for dedicated satellite capacity for European security. Pelican-2 launched in January, Pelican-3 and -4 in August. Planet announced a Berlin manufacturing facility to double Pelican production capacity. In November, the [[Google]] Project Suncatcher partnership was announced — space-based AI computing, with two prototype satellites targeted for early 2027. Most importantly: TTM free cash flow turned positive at $40.7M, the first time in the company's history. Q3 FY2026 (Oct '25) revenue hit $81.3M (+33% YoY), beating consensus by 10%.

- 2026 — War premium. The [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran conflict]] lit up demand for exactly what Planet sells. The stock surged 9.2% on March 4 as conflict-driven demand expectations hit. Then came the imagery restrictions — four-day delay on Gulf/Iraq/Kuwait imagery starting March 6, expanded to 14 days including Iran by March 10 — raising fundamental questions about whether Planet is a neutral data provider or a de facto participant in the US-allied information architecture. Stock trades at $24.79 (March 13, 2026), near its all-time high of $28.78 (January 2026).

## Business model

Planet runs a SaaS-style subscription model on top of a vertically integrated satellite manufacturing and operations stack.

### Revenue segments

| Segment | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| EO data subscriptions | Annual access to PlanetScope daily global imagery + SkySat tasking. ~90% of customers are annual subs. |
| Government contracts | US (FAS, NOAA, Sandia, NASA, NIWC-PAC/[[SeaVision]]) and international (German BAAINBw, SKY Perfect JSAT) |
| Dedicated capacity | Build-operate-transfer model for Pelican constellations (JSAT deal, German deal) |
| Analytics/AI | Planetary Variables, change detection, forest monitoring (NICFI/Norway partnership) |

### Constellation architecture

| Constellation | Count | Resolution | Revisit | Role |
|---------------|-------|------------|---------|------|
| PlanetScope (SuperDove) | 200+ | 3m | Daily global | Baseline monitoring |
| SkySat | 21 | 50cm | On-demand | High-res tasking |
| Pelican | 4 (growing) | Sub-meter | Rapid revisit | Next-gen, NVIDIA Jetson edge AI |
| Tanager | 1 | Hyperspectral | Targeted | Methane/CO2 detection (Carbon Mapper) |

The disposable satellite model is the core innovation: Doves are designed to operate ~2 years, burn up in orbit, and get replaced by the next generation using newer consumer electronics. This means the constellation continuously improves without requiring expensive long-lived hardware.

## March 2026: wartime imagery restrictions

The most consequential moment in Planet's history as a business, because it forced the company to choose sides.

### Timeline

- March 4: PL surges +9.2% to $26.36 as the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran conflict]] drives demand expectations for satellite imagery — military intelligence, damage assessment, [[Strait of Hormuz]] ship tracking, OSINT applications.
- March 6: Planet introduces a 96-hour (4-day) delay on newly captured imagery of Gulf states, Iraq, Kuwait, and adjacent conflict zones. Imagery of Iran itself remained immediately available. Authorized government and military users (US/NATO) retained real-time access.
- March 9-10: The delay is extended to 14 days and the restriction zone is broadened to include Iran, nearby Allied military bases, and the wider Gulf region. Commercial and media users are now operating with a two-week imagery gap.

### Context

Planet had satellite imagery showing damage from Iranian drone and missile strikes on US/Allied installations — the Fifth Fleet HQ in Bahrain, early-warning radar in Qatar, military facilities in Kuwait. The images documented the Minab school strike that Iranian authorities say killed 168 people. This data was being used by open-source intelligence analysts, journalists, and humanitarian organizations for independent verification.

### Planet's position

The company calls it "responsible data practices" — temporary restrictions to prevent adversarial actors from using near-real-time imagery for battle damage assessment or targeting. Planet explicitly states it is "not a result of a requirement from any government." Imagery continues to be collected and will eventually be released.

### Precedent

Planet imposed a 30-day delay on Gaza imagery during the 2023-2024 hostilities to restrict [[Hamas]] access. The Iran restriction is broader geographically but shorter in delay.

### The tension

Critics argue the delays limit independent verification of battlefield damage, shape public narratives, and create an information vacuum that AI-generated misinformation can fill. Supporters argue real-time imagery in the hands of adversaries could endanger military personnel. Both are right. The deeper issue: Planet's daily global imaging capability has made it a strategic asset that cannot remain fully neutral during active US military operations, regardless of the company's PBC charter.

## Investment case

![[pl-vs-spy-price-chart.png]]
*PL vs SPY normalized since January 2022. Planet massively underperformed through the post-SPAC hangover, then surged on government contract wins and the Iran conflict demand premium.*

### Bull

- FCF inflection achieved — business is now self-funding
- Government contract pipeline ($230M JSAT + €240M Germany) provides multi-year revenue visibility
- Iran conflict is a structural demand driver: intelligence agencies, defense ministries, and allied governments all need more satellite imagery, not less
- Pelican constellation with on-orbit AI processing is a generational upgrade — positions Planet for tasking-as-a-service at higher margins
- Google Suncatcher validates the satellite operations platform for adjacencies beyond Earth imaging
- Gross margins expanding steadily (23% → 58%) as fixed-cost constellation scales

### Bear

- Still unprofitable on a GAAP basis — net losses of $123M+ annually
- Valuation at ~$8.3B on $282M TTM revenue implies ~30x P/S — pricing in growth that hasn't happened yet
- The original SPAC guidance ($700M revenue by early 2026) was wildly optimistic — actual: $244M. Credibility gap.
- SPAC-era share dilution: from 80M to 303M shares outstanding
- Competition from Maxar ([[MDA Space]]), [[Airbus]] Defence & Space, [[BlackSky Technology|BlackSky]], and Umbra (SAR)
- Wartime imagery restrictions create reputational risk: if governments can effectively dictate Planet's data release policies, the "neutral data provider" positioning erodes
- Two rounds of layoffs in 2023-2024 (10% + 17%) suggest the cost structure hasn't fully stabilized

### Analyst consensus

Strong Buy. Median PT $26.00, range $16.40–$33.00 (19 analysts). Stock at $24.79 is near the median.
