#actor #shipping #germany #public

**Hapag-Lloyd AG** — German container shipping company, **5th-largest globally** by TEU capacity. Formed 1970 from merger of Hamburg-American Line (HAPAG, 1847) and Norddeutscher Lloyd (1857). Listed on Frankfurt Stock Exchange (FWB: HLAG). Acquiring [[ZIM Integrated Shipping]] for $4.2B.

## ZIM acquisition ($4.2B, Feb 2026)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Target | [[ZIM Integrated Shipping]] (100% of shares) |
| Price | **$35/share cash** (58% premium to Feb 13 close) |
| Total value | **$4.2B** |
| Structure | Hapag-Lloyd acquires international operations; Israeli PE fund [[FIMI Opportunity Funds]] takes Israeli operations as "New Zim" (16 vessels) |
| Golden share | Israeli government's strategic "golden share" transfers to FIMI subsidiary |
| Status | Signed MOU; requires Israeli government approval + regulatory clearance |
| Worker reaction | ZIM workers' union launched strike at Haifa HQ |

### Deal structure — two-part split

This isn't a clean full acquisition. The deal splits ZIM into:

1. **Hapag-Lloyd gets:** ZIM's international routes (East Asia–Americas, intra-Asia) + ~130 vessels. Hapag-Lloyd becomes a stronger #5 global carrier with expanded network.
2. **"New Zim" (FIMI):** Retains 16 vessels, Israel trade routes (EU, US, Mediterranean, Black Sea), and the strategic national shipping mandate. This preserves Israel's supply chain security — ZIM has historically been the government's maritime lifeline in wartime.

The golden share transfer is the political flashpoint — the Israeli government must approve losing direct control over the shipping fleet it considers a strategic asset. This is the most likely source of deal friction beyond standard antitrust.

### Strategic logic for Hapag-Lloyd

- **Scale:** adds ~145 ships and ZIM's trade lanes, filling gaps in Hapag-Lloyd's network
- **Route complementarity:** ZIM strong in Asia–Americas; Hapag-Lloyd strong in transatlantic + Asia–Europe
- **Asset-light arbitrage:** ZIM charters most of its fleet, so Hapag-Lloyd is buying routes/customers/market share without heavy vessel ownership obligations
- **Pricing power:** consolidation in a fragmented industry (top 5 carriers control ~65% of global capacity)

### Risks to closing

- Israeli government may delay/block golden share transfer during active war
- EU competition review for shipping consolidation
- Union opposition (strike already launched)
- MAC clause risk if ZIM operations are materially impaired by Iran conflict
- See [[ZIM Integrated Shipping#Merger arb spread]] for spread analysis
