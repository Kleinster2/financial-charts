**Steve Soboroff** is a Los Angeles real estate developer, civic operator, and political fixer who has spent five decades oscillating between private development and unpaid (or controversially paid) public service roles. Managing partner of [[Soboroff Partners]], the developer behind [[Playa Vista]] and [[The Park at Cross Creek]], and a serial appointee to LA's most visible commissions.

---

## Synopsis

Soboroff's career is a case study in the LA model of public-private entanglement. He built his commercial real estate practice at [[Soboroff Partners]] starting in 1978, specializing in retail leasing and shopping center development across Southern California — clients included Target, Circuit City (95 transactions), Office Depot, and Orchard Supply Hardware (24 LA-area stores). But his real leverage comes from decades of civic roles: senior adviser to Mayor [[Richard Riordan]], president of the LA Board of Recreation and Parks (built 123 parks in 24 months), and the dealmaker behind the [[Staples Center]] (now Crypto.com Arena), which brought the Lakers, Kings, and Clippers downtown with nearly $1B in private capital from [[Philip Anschutz]] and zero public funding.

His highest-profile development was [[Playa Vista]], the ~1,000-acre former Howard Hughes Aircraft site in West LA. Backed by a [[Morgan Stanley]]/[[Goldman Sachs]]/[[Oaktree Capital]] venture, Soboroff took over a project that was $700M underwater and delivered 3,100+ residential units, 2M sq. ft. of office space (leased in 15 months vs. projected 15 years to Silicon Beach tech tenants), 20 parks, and a school. Phase 2 (2,600 units) was approved April 2010. Brookfield acquired the project in 2012.

Most recently, Soboroff served 90 turbulent days as Chief Recovery Officer for the City of Los Angeles after the January 2025 Palisades Fire — a role that collapsed amid salary controversy ($500K for 90 days, later walked back), accusations of being sidelined by Mayor [[Karen Bass]], and a leaked Zoom call where he told Hollywood talent executives to hold onto Palisades properties because city investments would "triple" lowball offers. He departed April 11, 2025, saying "They haven't asked me to do anything in a month and a half."

## Evolution

The story of Steve Soboroff is the story of a man who discovered early that proximity to power in Los Angeles is itself a form of real estate — and then spent fifty years developing both.

- 1948-1971: Born in Chicago to Irving and Evelyn Soboroff, a middle-class secular Jewish family running a retail lingerie store. Enrolled at the University of Arizona in 1966, initially rejected from the MS Finance program with an 11th-percentile test score — he appealed directly to the dean and was admitted. Studied under Roy Drachman, founder of the Urban Land Institute. During college summers, worked as chauffeur and assistant to actor Kirk Douglas, a connection made through Douglas's wife Anna, who was a customer of his parents' store. This early pattern — leveraging social connections across domains — would define his career.

- 1969-1978: Joined J.K. Eichenbaum's shopping center development firm at $750/month and learned the retail leasing trade working on Lakewood Shopping Center projects alongside Ben Weingart. During this period he also started what became his 58-year (and counting) match with Big Brothers Big Sisters, paired with 9-year-old Terry Alan Williams in 1968. By the mid-1970s he was running an annual real estate seminar at UCLA that drew up to 1,500 retailers — he ran it for 25 years, through 2000.

- 1978-1993: Founded [[Soboroff Partners]] as managing partner. First year: closed 400 store lease deals at double the standard rate with three employees. Built the firm into a significant Southern California retail leasing and development shop — clients ranged from big-box chains (Target, Circuit City, Office Depot, Pep Boys) to specialty retail. Acquired Malibu Creek Plaza (now Malibu Village) in 1988. The private development work was profitable but not what made him a player.

- 1993-2001 — The Riordan era: This was the inflection. Soboroff became unpaid Senior Adviser to Republican Mayor [[Richard Riordan]] and took on a sequence of civic roles that remade his reputation. President of the Board of Recreation and Parks (appointed August 1994), where he built 123 parks in 24 months with no new hires. Chaired the Proposition BB Oversight Committee for $2.4B in LAUSD school repairs (passed April 1997 with 71% voter approval, covering 700+ schools). Advanced the $2B [[Alameda Corridor]] rail project — stalled for 23 years until Soboroff and [[Philip Anschutz]] completed it in six months. Most consequentially, conceived and brokered the [[Staples Center]]: the plan to bring the Lakers, Kings, and Clippers downtown near the Convention Center (which was losing $50M/year). Anschutz committed nearly $1B in private capital. No public funding. Soboroff convinced [[Donald Sterling]] (Clippers) to participate despite initial resistance. Opened October 17, 1999.

- 2001 — Mayoral campaign: Ran for Mayor of LA as the only prominent Republican. Endorsed by Riordan and John McCain. Campaign slogan: "A problem solver. Not a politician." Spent $687K of his own money. Finished third with 106,189 votes (21.25%), within 3% of eventual winner James Hahn (125,139 votes, 25.05%). Won the Valley and Westside but didn't make the runoff. The campaign ended his run of civic appointments but opened the door to his biggest development deal.

- 2001-2010 — Playa Vista: Became president, then chairman and CEO of Playa Capital Co. LLC, the developer of [[Playa Vista]], a ~1,000-acre master-planned community on the former Howard Hughes Aircraft Company land in West LA near Marina del Rey. The project was a venture of [[Morgan Stanley]], [[Goldman Sachs]], and [[Oaktree Capital]] — previous owners had been $700M upside-down. Soboroff requested triple the salary competitors offered and got it. Delivered Phase 1: 3,100+ residential units (6,000+ residents), 2M sq. ft. of office space leased in 15 months to Silicon Beach tech tenants (including [[Electronic Arts]]), a fire station, library, elementary school, 20 parks, and the LA Clippers Training Center. Preserved 70% as open space and relinquished 415 acres of development rights for Ballona Wetlands restoration. Phase 2 (2,600 units, "The Village") approved April 2010. He stepped down end of April 2010 but retained part ownership. Brookfield acquired the project in 2012. Environmental opposition (STOMP LA, CEQA lawsuits, methane seep discoveries in 2000) was intense throughout.

- 2011 — Dodgers interlude: Appointed Vice Chairman of the [[Los Angeles Dodgers]] by [[Frank McCourt]] on April 19, 2011. One day later, MLB seized control of the franchise. Soboroff defended McCourt publicly, calling MLB's action "irresponsible" and citing a potential 20-year, $3B TV deal with Fox. Resigned June 25, 2011. Total tenure: 67 days.

- 2013-2023 — Police Commission: Appointed to the LA Board of Police Commissioners by Mayor [[Eric Garcetti]] (August 2013), elected president September 10, 2013. Served two terms through June 2023. Oversaw the LAPD body-worn camera rollout (late 2015), secured a $250K [[Los Angeles Dodgers|Dodgers]] donation for camera funding. The Rihanna iPhone incident (May 2014) — she dropped and cracked his phone at a Clippers playoff game, signed it, donated $25K to LAPD; Soboroff auctioned it on eBay for $66,500; an anonymous donor added $25K; total raised $116,500 for LAPD cadet programs. Simultaneously served as independent director of [[Macerich]] (NYSE: MAC), a major retail REIT, from 2014-2023, holding ~19,596 shares (~$371K as of 2025).

- 2019 — The Park at Cross Creek: Opened the first new retail center in Malibu in 35 years after a 15-year development and entitlement process. 39,350 sq. ft. on ~6 acres at 23401 Civic Center Way. Anchored by [[Whole Foods]] (24,529 sq. ft.), with [[Tesla]], Blue Bottle Coffee, Howdy's Sonrisa Cafe, and Barefoot Dreams. Soboroff held ~20% stake; partners Cindy McAfee and Michael Heslov held comparable stakes; the Gerschel family held the majority. Sold in July 2024 for $80M to DSB Properties (David Blatt, Van Nuys) at 100% occupancy, 4.5% cap rate. Soboroff called it "my best work."

- 2025 — Fire recovery and fallout: His Pacific Palisades home was destroyed in the Palisades Fire on January 7, 2025. Mayor [[Karen Bass]] — whom Soboroff had endorsed in 2022 — appointed him Chief Recovery Officer in mid-January. Mandate: coordinate rebuilding of ~5,000 damaged structures, target 1,000 residential rebuilds in year one. Initial proposed salary of $500K for 90 days (to be paid from charitable donations, not taxpayer funds) drew immediate backlash; LA City Councilmember Monica Rodriguez called it "obscene." Soboroff agreed to work for free February 8-10, then claimed Bass had privately assured alternative compensation, saying "I was lied to" before retracting. A leaked Zoom call with WME, CAA, and UTA executives — where he urged them to hold Palisades properties, promising city investments would "triple" lowball offers — compounded the controversy. He was progressively marginalized; Bass reopened Palisades to the public without informing him. A Palantir recovery app he championed sat "three-quarters built" with the city "sitting on the paperwork." He departed April 11, 2025: "They haven't asked me to do anything in a month and a half, nothing, zero."

## Personal
