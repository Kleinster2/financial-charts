# Nasdaq Private Market

Nasdaq Private Market (NPM) is a US-based secondary trading platform for shares of private, late-stage venture-backed companies, originally founded by [[Nasdaq]] in 2013 and spun out as a standalone entity in 2021 with investments from [[Goldman Sachs]], [[Morgan Stanley]], [[Citigroup]], and SVB Financial. NPM facilitates company-sponsored tender offers, structured liquidity programs, and bilateral block trades for pre-IPO names, providing a regulated venue for early employees and investors to monetize stakes before a public listing.

NPM is one of the principal venues where [[SpaceX]] secondary trading occurs. Per Greg Martin (co-founder, [[Rainmaker Securities]], April 2026), SpaceX is *"consistently one of the most actively traded names on our platform because there's nothing else like it in the private markets today. Demand has also almost always outpaced supply, and that's been true even during periods where broader secondary market activity has been more muted."* As of early April 2026, the SpaceX/[[xAI]] combined entity was marked at $1.54T on NPM \u2014 a ~13.6% discount to the $1.75T target valuation in the upcoming [[SpaceX IPO 2026|SpaceX IPO]].

Prior to 2021, NPM operated as an internal division of [[Nasdaq]]. The 2021 spin-out into an independent company was backed by a consortium of major US investment banks plus SVB Financial. Post-spin-out ownership has not been publicly broken down; the banks collectively hold a minority stake alongside employee and management equity.

## Why secondary platforms matter for SpaceX

For SpaceX specifically, the existence of NPM and competing platforms (Forge, EquityZen, [[Rainmaker Securities]]) creates a continuous mark on the company's value between funding rounds. The ~$1.54T NPM mark is the most current institutional reference point for the IPO underwriters \u2014 if the public IPO prices materially below the secondary mark, secondary holders take losses on day one. This creates an implicit floor on the IPO price, but also a ceiling on how aggressively the syndicate can stretch the valuation without breaking the secondary anchor.
