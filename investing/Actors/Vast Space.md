**Vast Space** — Commercial space station builder. [[Haven-1]] (NET May 2026 launch on [[Falcon 9]]) is targeted as the first standalone commercial LEO platform. Building [[Haven-2]] for [[Commercial LEO Destinations]] Phase 2 award (NASA selection mid-2026). CEO: [[Max Haot]]. Founder/financier: Jed McCaleb (~$1B+ committed). Distinct from [[Vast.ai]] (GPU cloud) and [[VAST Data]] (vector DB) — same name, different companies.

---

## Synopsis

Vast Space is the operationally furthest-along of the [[Commercial LEO Destinations]] candidates — [[Haven-1]] is in final integration ahead of NET May 2026 launch on [[SpaceX]] [[Falcon 9]], making it the first free-flying commercial space station regardless of CLD outcome. Founded 2021 by Jed McCaleb (founder of [[Stellar]] cryptocurrency platform and former [[Ripple]] CTO) and capitalized primarily by McCaleb personally. Acquired Launcher (small-lift rocket startup) in 2023 and absorbed [[Max Haot]] as president, then CEO. Vast occupies the demonstrate-then-scale strategic slot in CLD: Haven-1 proves operational competence over a 3-year demonstration period; [[Haven-2]] (multi-module successor) is the Phase 2 bid. The CEO has emerged as the public-facing voice of industry-side defense of the original 2018 commercialization framework against [[Jared Isaacman]]'s Q1 2026 alternative-NASA-owned-core-module proposal.

## Evolution

- 2021 — Vast incorporated by Jed McCaleb in Long Beach, California. Initial focus on artificial-gravity station architecture (rotating habitat).
- Feb 2023 — Acquired [[Launcher]] (small-lift rocket startup founded by Max Haot). Haot joins Vast as president; Launcher's engine technology integrates into Vast's station program.
- Late 2023 — Pivoted from artificial-gravity emphasis to free-flying single-module Haven-1 architecture; targeted launch on Falcon 9 to compress development timeline.
- 2024 — Awarded NASA contract under Commercial LEO Destinations framework for crewed access to Haven-1.
- Jul 2025 — Haven-1 primary structure completed (ahead of original schedule).
- 2025-2026 — Haven-1 final integration; mission planning for NET May 2026 launch.
- Q1 2026 — Filed RFI response opposing Isaacman's alternative CLD framework. Public industry-side voice for original commercialization framework.

## Haven-2 (CLD Phase 2 candidate)

Haven-2 is Vast's proposed permanent successor to ISS — a multi-module station designed for indefinite presence, with NASA as the anchor customer. Selection targeted mid-2026 under [[Commercial LEO Destinations]] Phase 2.

## Isaacman CLD restructuring proposal (Q1 2026)

Per CEO [[Max Haot]] (Apr 2026 [[Space Capital]] interview): NASA administrator [[Jared Isaacman]] proposed an alternative CLD framework where NASA designs and owns a "core module" temporarily attached to ISS, with industry building it under a contracting model closer to SLS than commercial cargo. This is a partial reversal of the 2018 Trump-era commercialization framework.

Vast filed an RFI response opposing the proposal. Key arguments:
- ISS has leak issues and a Russia partnership that's geopolitically constrained — pushing the timeline matters
- Existing CLD budget can support two commercial winners on the original framework, with crew utilization shared
- NASA pivoting away from commercialization risks the ~$4B private capital deployed across CLD entrants and damages NASA's standing as a reliable anchor customer for future commercial space programs
- Once NASA is a customer, Japan, Europe, UAE, Saudi Arabia all follow as additional customers — only commercial framework unlocks that growth

Haot's commercial business model: NASA + sovereign space agencies as the verifiable existing market. Vast does not attribute material revenue to the speculative new markets (in-space pharma, media, private missions) — those are upside.

See [[Commercial LEO Destinations]] for the program-level structure and competitor map.
