**JD.com** — [[China]]'s second-largest e-commerce platform by revenue and the country's largest direct-sales retailer (NASDAQ: JD, HKEX: 9618). Known for its first-party retail model and self-built logistics network. Launched Joybuy European e-commerce in six countries (Mar 15, 2026); partnering with [[BYD]] on fast-charging station infrastructure.

---

## Synopsis

JD.com is the anti-[[Alibaba]]. Where Alibaba built a marketplace connecting buyers and sellers (asset-light, high-margin), JD.com built an [[Amazon]]-style operation that owns inventory, runs its own warehouses, and delivers packages with its own fleet. That bet on vertical integration looked crazy for years — it burned cash, delayed profitability, and drew skepticism from investors who preferred Alibaba's platform model. But it gave JD something no competitor could replicate overnight: a logistics network covering 99% of Chinese counties with same-day or next-day delivery. [[JD Logistics]], spun out via a 2021 Hong Kong IPO (raising HK$24.6B), now operates over 1,600 warehouses and generates RMB 217B in annual revenue, with 18.8% growth in FY2025 and rising third-party volumes as it opens the network to external merchants.

The company sits at RMB 1,309B ($187B) in FY2025 revenue, up 13% YoY, with over 700 million annual active customers. But the headline growth masks a strategic pivot that is squeezing margins. JD launched a low-price offensive in 2023-2024 to claw back share from [[PDD Holdings]]' [[Pinduoduo]], lowered barriers for third-party sellers, and in February 2025 entered food delivery — directly challenging [[Meituan]] with zero-commission merchant onboarding and RMB 10B in subsidies. The New Businesses segment (food delivery, JD Property, international logistics) more than doubled revenue to RMB 49.3B in FY2025 but lost RMB 46.6B in the process. Q4 2025 showed the strain: revenue grew just 1.5% (missing estimates), marketing expenses surged 50%+, and the company posted a RMB 2.7B net loss. Full-year net income held at RMB 19.6B, but operating margin compressed to 0.2% from 3.3%.

Founder [[Richard Liu]] (Liu Qiangdong) built the company from a single market stall in 1998, pivoted online during [[SARS]] in 2003-2004, and took it public on [[NASDAQ]] in 2014 at a $27.3B valuation backed by [[Tencent]]. He stepped back from the CEO role in April 2022 after a 2018 sexual assault allegation in [[Minneapolis]] (arrested, not charged, settled the civil suit in October 2022) wiped $10B off JD's market cap. Current CEO Sandy Xu Ran, the former CFO, is navigating the low-price war. Liu remains chairman and retains control through super-voting shares — and in 2025 made a splashy return to operational headlines, announcing plans for 3 million logistics robots, 1 million autonomous vehicles, and 100,000 delivery drones over five years. The [[BYD]] Flash Charging partnership (Mar 2026), which combines JD's logistics sites with BYD's 1,500 kW chargers, signals ambitions well beyond e-commerce.

## Evolution

The story of JD.com is the story of a contrarian bet on owning the hard parts of e-commerce — inventory, warehouses, trucks, couriers — in a market where everyone else went asset-light, and then spending two decades proving it was right while nearly going broke several times.

1998-2003: [[Richard Liu]] started Jingdong with RMB 12,000 (~$1,700) borrowed from relatives, selling magneto-optical storage products from a stall in [[Beijing]]'s [[Zhongguancun]] electronics district. The business was tiny but legitimate in a market rife with counterfeit goods — Liu's reputation for authentic products would become the founding principle of JD's brand. By 2003 he had expanded to a dozen physical retail stores across northern [[China]].

2003-2004: [[SARS]] nearly killed the business. The epidemic forced Liu to close all physical stores, and Jingdong hemorrhaged over RMB 8M. Liu moved the entire operation online in 2004, initially posting products on bulletin board forums and taking orders via QQ chat. It was a survival decision, not a strategy memo — but it put Jingdong online right as Chinese internet adoption was accelerating. The first-party model (JD buys from suppliers, stocks inventory, sells to consumers) was set from day one, inherited from the physical retail DNA.

2007-2013: The logistics bet. In 2007, Liu made the decision that defined JD: building a proprietary logistics network from scratch. Every investor and board member thought it was reckless. [[Alibaba]] was printing money with [[Taobao]]'s zero-inventory marketplace, and here was JD burning cash on warehouses and delivery trucks. Liu's logic was simple — Chinese logistics was terrible (5-7 day delivery was standard, damage rates were high), and whoever solved last-mile delivery would own the customer. JD expanded from 3C electronics into books (2008), general merchandise (2010), fresh food, and apparel. The domain switched from 360buy.com to jd.com in 2013. Revenue grew from roughly $1B in 2009 to $18B by 2013, but the company was still losing money every year, plowing everything back into fulfillment infrastructure.

2014: The [[NASDAQ]] IPO and [[Tencent]] alliance. JD priced at $19/share, above range, raising $1.78B at a $27.3B fully diluted valuation — the largest U.S. IPO by a Chinese internet company at that point. The real story was the concurrent [[Tencent]] deal: Tencent took a 20% stake (including a $1.3B private placement at IPO price) and handed JD exclusive access to [[WeChat]] and Mobile QQ shopping channels, gaining a weapon against [[Alibaba]] in the process. The Tencent partnership gave JD access to 1.29B WeChat users as a traffic funnel — something no amount of marketing spend could replicate.

2016-2017: The [[Walmart]] era. [[Walmart]] entered by selling its struggling Chinese e-commerce operation Yihaodian to JD in exchange for a 5.9% equity stake (~$1.5B). Walmart quickly increased to 12.1% by year-end 2016, and the two companies jointly invested $500M in Dada-JD Daojia (on-demand delivery platform). The partnership gave JD grocery credibility and gave Walmart an e-commerce channel in China. Revenue cleared $55B in 2017. The logistics network now covered most of China, with warehouse count exceeding 500.

2018: The Minneapolis crisis. In August 2018, [[Richard Liu]] was arrested in [[Minneapolis]] on suspicion of sexual assault during a University of Minnesota executive MBA program dinner. He was released without charges the next day and returned to China. Prosecutors cited "profound evidentiary problems." But the damage was swift — JD's stock dropped 32%, wiping roughly $10B off the market cap. The accuser, Liu Jingyao, filed a civil lawsuit. The overhang would persist for four years.

2019-2020: Recovery and the Hong Kong listing. Revenue crossed $82B in 2019 and $114B in 2020 as COVID-19 drove a surge in online shopping — JD's logistics network proved its value when competitors' third-party couriers were overwhelmed. In June 2020, JD completed a secondary listing in [[Hong Kong]] (HKEX: 9618), raising HK$30B ($3.9B). The listing was partly a hedge against U.S.-China tensions and rising delisting risks. JD's stock hit all-time highs above $100/ADS in early 2021.

2021: The spinoff year. JD Logistics IPO'd on HKEX in May 2021, raising HK$24.6B ($3.2B) — Hong Kong's second-largest IPO that year. JD Health had already listed in December 2020, raising $3.5B (shares jumped 56% on day one). JD Industrials was carved out as a separate entity. The spinoffs unlocked value but also added complexity. Revenue hit $149B. Then [[China]]'s tech crackdown arrived — but JD navigated it better than most. [[Alibaba]] got hit with an $18.2B antitrust fine; [[Didi]] was pulled from app stores; JD's treatment of delivery workers and direct employment model (versus gig labor) actually aligned with Beijing's "common prosperity" messaging.

2022: The leadership transition and Tencent exit. [[Richard Liu]] had decamped to London during [[China]]'s zero-Covid curbs, leaving behind a lavish residence built in ancient Chinese style atop JD's [[Beijing]] headquarters — the time abroad gave him space to think about European opportunities that would shape JD's strategy years later. In April 2022, he stepped down as CEO, naming Xu Lei as his successor. In October 2022, Liu settled the Minneapolis civil lawsuit on undisclosed terms, just days before trial — removing a four-year cloud. But the bigger shock came in December: [[Tencent]] distributed its 17% JD stake ($16.4B) as a special dividend to shareholders, dropping to just 2.3%. The move reflected regulatory pressure on tech cross-holdings, but it also meant JD lost its largest strategic shareholder. JD renewed [[WeChat]] access for three years in exchange for $220M in stock. Revenue hit RMB 1.06T (~$152B) — crossing the trillion-RMB mark — but growth had slowed to 10%.

2023: CEO churn and the PDD wake-up call. Xu Lei lasted barely a year as CEO before "retiring for personal reasons" in May 2023. Sandy Xu Ran, the CFO since 2020, took over. The real issue was [[PDD Holdings]]: [[Pinduoduo]]'s hyper-aggressive pricing and [[Temu]]'s international expansion were stealing mindshare and market share among price-sensitive consumers. Revenue growth decelerated to 4% in 2023. JD launched its own low-price offensive, lowering third-party seller barriers and adding RMB 10B subsidy programs. In August 2024, [[Walmart]] sold its entire remaining 9.4% stake for $3.6B — ending the eight-year partnership. Walmart cited JD's stagnant stock (up just 3% over eight years) and chose to reinvest in Sam's Club China directly.

2024-2025: The multi-front war. JD went on offense across every front simultaneously. In February 2025, JD launched food delivery — directly attacking [[Meituan]]'s core business with zero-commission merchant onboarding, social insurance for 100,000+ full-time riders (a first in China), and massive consumer subsidies. The combined subsidy burn across JD, Meituan, and [[Alibaba]]'s Ele.me reached RMB 80B in 2025. JD also leaned into [[China]]'s government trade-in subsidy program (RMB 300B national fund for electronics and appliances), which played directly to its strengths in 3C and home appliances. Revenue rebounded to RMB 1,309B (+13%), and annual active customers crossed 700M. But the costs were enormous: the New Businesses segment lost RMB 46.6B, free cash flow collapsed from RMB 43.7B to RMB 6.5B, and Q4 2025 posted a net loss. Q2 2025 showed the profit squeeze clearly — net income fell to RMB 6.2B from RMB 12.6B a year earlier. [[Alibaba]] entered the food delivery race in late April, intensifying the subsidy war. JD scaled back food delivery subsidies in its August quarterly call, citing the need to "refine the technology," though analysts said it remained committed to the sector.

2025-2026: The international push. The UK became JD's first international target market — and its first credibility test. In March 2024, JD considered a bid for British electronics retailer Currys but abandoned it. In 2025, JD embarked on a fresh plan — codenamed "project arc" — to acquire Argos, a UK retailer with an extensive logistics network selling everything from toys to camping equipment. Months of discussions with Argos's owner [[J Sainsbury]] collapsed in acrimony in September 2025: [[J Sainsbury|Sainsbury's]] abruptly terminated negotiations on a Sunday, a day after news of the talks leaked, saying JD was only willing to negotiate on a "materially revised set of terms and commitments." A person close to the talks said JD tried to rip up "almost every part" of the deal after conducting due diligence on what the company described as a "complex carve out." The two failed UK approaches dented credibility — Clive Black, head of consumer research at Shore Capital: *"There is a health warning for anyone JD.com knocks on the door of now"* (Sep 19, 2025). In July 2025, JD made a €2.2B offer for [[Ceconomy]] (German electronics retailer, owner of MediaMarkt and Saturn), approved by Ceconomy's management and board — one of the largest Chinese acquisitions in Europe in recent years. [[Bernstein]] analysts framed the strategic logic: JD's objective was "to serve as a retail partner for Chinese brands as they increasingly expand overseas" while "low growth and rampant competition in domestic ecommerce creates a clear need to look for new growth opportunities" (Sep 2025). JD.com then made its biggest overseas move on March 15, 2026: launching the Joybuy branded e-commerce service across six European countries — the [[UK]], [[Germany]], [[France]], Belgium, Luxembourg, and the [[Netherlands]]. The operation emulates JD's [[China]] model wholesale: buy inventory from global consumer brands ([[Apple]], [[Samsung]]), stock it in company-owned depots, deliver with company-controlled logistics. JD secured 60+ warehouses covering 300,000 sq m and 49,000+ lockers across the continent, importing automated picking technology from China (hundreds of robots per facility). Joybuy promises same-day delivery to 40M consumers for orders placed before 11am above a minimum threshold — more than 100,000 products from electronics to groceries. This is a direct challenge to [[Amazon]]'s European positions, and it compounds the pressure from [[PDD Holdings|Temu]] and [[Shein]], which already undercut Amazon by shipping direct from Chinese factories. JD previously tried Europe via Ochama (2022), a smaller venture in the [[Netherlands]] that Ed Sander, a Dutch digital retail analyst, described as having ended up as "an online shop for the Asian diaspora." CEO Sandy Xu Ran framed the expansion as two-directional: helping Chinese brands go global while bringing European brands into [[China]]. Joybuy's opening salvo is pure loss-leader: [[Apple]] AirPods priced 20% below recommended retail in the UK with free delivery (FT Lex, Mar 17). The competitive context is shifting — [[Alibaba]] has replicated its asset-heavy model for [[AliExpress]], and [[PDD Holdings]] is doing likewise for [[Temu]], meaning all three Chinese e-commerce giants are now building owned logistics overseas rather than relying on cross-border shipping. Even so, the timeline for returns is sobering: [[Alibaba]] launched AliExpress 16 years ago but by 2025 still reaped only one-fifth of its retail e-commerce sales from international markets.

2026 (domestic): [[Richard Liu]] returned to the headlines with ambitious automation plans: 3 million logistics robots, 1 million autonomous vehicles, and 100,000 delivery drones over five years. Liu's thesis is that AI and robotics will cut China's social logistics costs from 14% of GDP to under 10%. On March 13, JD and [[BYD]] unveiled their first jointly developed Flash Charging station in [[Shenzhen]] — combining BYD's 1,500 kW chargers with JD retail (convenience stores, coffee shops). JD's logistics sites will host future charger deployments, extending the partnership into a nationwide network. The question is whether JD can sustain the multi-front burn (food delivery, autonomous logistics, European expansion, charging infrastructure) while its core retail margins compress.

## Core thesis

JD's investment case hinges on the logistics moat. The first-party model and owned delivery network create structural advantages in trust (authentic goods guarantee), speed (same-day delivery), and supply chain control that no marketplace competitor can replicate cheaply. The $32B cash pile provides a long runway. The risk is margin destruction: the low-price war with [[PDD Holdings]], the food delivery cash burn against [[Meituan]], and the autonomous logistics buildout could consume years of profits. Operating margin went from 3.3% to 0.2% in a single year. If the food delivery and low-price bets work, JD becomes the dominant full-stack commerce platform in [[China]]. If they don't, the company has burned through its margin of safety for nothing. At ~$37B market cap on $187B revenue, the market is pricing in significant skepticism — under 10x this year's earnings, half of [[Alibaba]]'s multiple, roughly a third of [[Amazon]]'s (FT Lex, Mar 17). Shares are down ~33% over the past year.

## Strengths

- Logistics infrastructure covering 99% of Chinese counties — 1,600+ warehouses, same-day/next-day delivery
- First-party model ensures authenticity and quality control (critical in a market with counterfeiting problems)
- $32.2B in total liquidity — can sustain prolonged subsidy wars
- 700M+ annual active customers with high-trust brand reputation
- [[JD Logistics]] generating revenue from external clients (18.8% growth), not just captive
- Government trade-in subsidy programs play to JD's electronics/appliance strength — though subsidies are being rolled back as of early 2026 (FT, Mar 17)

## Risks

- Operating margin collapsed to 0.2% (from 3.3%) — food delivery and low-price subsidies eating profits
- Free cash flow down 85% YoY (RMB 43.7B to RMB 6.5B) — not sustainable long-term
- [[PDD Holdings]] and [[Pinduoduo]]'s cost structure is fundamentally leaner — JD may not win on price
- Food delivery is a proven cash incinerator (RMB 46.6B New Businesses loss in FY2025)
- [[Richard Liu]]'s super-voting control and executive turnover (three CEOs in four years) raise governance questions
- Government trade-in subsidies on electronics/appliances being rolled back (FT, Mar 17) — removes a tailwind that directly benefited JD's core 3C category
- China macro softness — consumer confidence weak, deflationary pressures
- U.S.-China tensions and delisting risks for NASDAQ-listed ADRs (mitigated by HK listing)

JD rallied 8.0% on Apr 14 to $31.25 (from $28.94 Apr 13 close), a +3.4σ move on a stack of near-simultaneous positive datapoints. Verified in DB (latest close 2026-04-14 $31.25).

### Reading the upgrade cycle

Macquarie's jump from Neutral → Outperform is the more interesting move: the upgrade price target ($35) came off a base where JD had been trading ~$28, so the implied ~25% upside from Macquarie plus Barclays' tighter ~30% upside to $41 reset sell-side into a more constructive camp after the Q4 2025 margin disappointment had weighed on the stock. "Improving earnings visibility" is code for: the low-price war and food-delivery burn that crushed operating margin from 3.3% → 0.2% in FY2025 is losing intensity, with JD having already scaled back food delivery subsidies on its August call and the New Businesses operating loss ceiling coming into view.

The 20-F filing formalized disclosures already in the Mar 5 earnings (net revenues RMB 352.3B for Q4, RMB 1,309.1B full year, $1.00 per ADS dividend, $3.0B in 2025 buybacks) plus the CNY 10B senior notes offering (2031 / 2036 tranches). No fresh material shock — the filing itself was a non-event, but it cleared a calendar overhang and coincided with the [[China]] Q1 2026 GDP print of 4.8% (vs 4.6% consensus), a mild macro tailwind for Chinese ADRs even as export growth softened.

### Context within [[KWEB]]

KWEB had lagged through early April on Iran-oil-shock risk-off; the Apr 14 session saw a broad China tech rally with JD among the stronger names. JD still trades at ~10x FY2025 earnings — roughly half of [[Alibaba]]'s multiple and about a third of [[Amazon]]'s (FT Lex, Mar 17) — so the analyst ratchet is revaluing a depressed multiple off low-price-war scar tissue rather than pricing accelerating growth.
