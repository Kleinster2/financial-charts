# Kpler

Commodity data and analytics platform. Tracks global shipping flows for oil, LNG, dry bulk, and refined products. Key source during the [[Strait of Hormuz]] crisis for real-time traffic analysis and China/Middle East flow data.

---

## Synopsis

Kpler is one of the core commodity-intelligence firms that matters because it sits closest to the physical flows. Its edge is not macro commentary but granular tracking — tankers, cargoes, pipeline bypasses, storage, and destination shifts. In crises like the 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] disruption, Kpler becomes more important than bank strategy notes because it can tell you what is actually moving, what is stuck, and where the missing barrels are supposed to arrive.

The firm's value is highest when market structure breaks. During the Iran war, Kpler data underpinned multiple key observations: Iranian cargoes were still moving while Gulf neighbors were choked off; China remained massively exposed to Middle East crude even with large onshore stocks; and the market was underestimating the lag between barrels already on the water and the coming physical shortage. That is what made Matt Smith's CNBC appearance useful — not because he predicted prices, but because he translated logistics into oil math.

## Strait of Hormuz crisis (March 2026)

Kpler's Matt Smith framed the market's core mistake as a timing problem: traders were seeing disruption headlines but not yet feeling the physical shortage because pre-conflict cargoes were still on the water.

Key Kpler calls:
- Iran was acting as gatekeeper, not full blocker — its own barrels kept moving while rivals' exports stalled
- China remained deeply exposed despite large storage buffers
- Bypass infrastructure (Saudi east-west, Fujairah, shadow fleet, SPR releases) did not fully close the supply gap
- The real shortage would hit when cargoes expected to arrive in Asia simply failed to show up — the "oh dear" moment

This made Kpler one of the most important sources for understanding whether the oil spike was panic, logistics, or true physical scarcity. The answer was: increasingly true scarcity.
