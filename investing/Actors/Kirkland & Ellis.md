# Kirkland & Ellis

World's largest law firm by revenue and the central engine of the [[Big Law transformation|Kirklandisation]] reshaping elite US corporate law. First firm to clear $10B in annual revenue (2025). Dominates [[Private equity]] dealmaking and [[Liability management exercises|restructuring]] — the go-to firm for [[Apollo]], [[Ares Management]], [[BlackRock]], [[KKR]], and most major PE shops, which creates conflicts when borrowers and lenders clash.

## The Kirkland scale-plus-pay model

Kirkland's strategic pivot to private equity in the early 2000s proved prescient. The firm now represents virtually every major PE firm, fund, and portfolio company in buyouts, restructurings, and exits. The compensation structure is the inverse of [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s lockstep system: top originators command annual pay above $30M, mid-tier underperformers are routinely de-equitised, and senior partners are pushed toward retirement by their mid-60s to make room for younger talent. The economic and cultural framework is closer to a [[Goldman Sachs|Wall Street investment bank]] than a traditional partnership — and is the model the FT and academic commentators (John Morley, [[Yale Law School]]) call "Kirklandisation."

H1 2025: 225 transactions worth $190B advised, including five mega-deals over $10B.

Core practices:
- Private equity M&A
- Leveraged finance
- Restructuring/bankruptcy
- Fund formation
- High-stakes litigation

## Lateral recruiting from Wachtell

Six years ago, M&A partner Ed Lee left [[Wachtell Lipton]] for Kirkland — the first Wachtell M&A defection in the firm's history. Lee has since gone on to advise on some of Kirkland's largest deals.

In 2025, [[Josh Feltman]], previously head of [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s corporate restructuring practice, signed a reported $80M / 3-year package to join Kirkland. Per the FT, this was the largest such move from Wachtell to date and is the central data point in the [[Big Law transformation]] frame: a guaranteed 3-year cash package converts the structural lockstep underpayment of a top mid-career originator into immediate pay.

## The Altice conflict

**Sep 2025**: [[Altice]] (Optimum) hired Kirkland + Evercore to advise on $26B debt restructuring.

**Nov 2025**: Altice filed antitrust lawsuit alleging "illegal cartel" among creditors — [[Apollo]], [[Ares Management]], [[BlackRock]].

**Post-filing**: Kirkland withdrew. All three defendants are major Kirkland clients. Kellogg Hansen (antitrust boutique) had architected the lawsuit.

Textbook illustration of why PE-side dominance creates structural conflicts in distressed situations.
