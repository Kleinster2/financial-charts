# Dan Driscoll

**Dan Driscoll** is US Secretary of the Army under [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s second term and the named architect of the [[Defense procurement reform]] campaign — the most aggressive challenge to the [[Defense Primes]] business model since the post-Cold War "Last Supper" of 1993. His public goal: shift Army procurement from 90% purpose-built military solutions to 90% commercial off-the-shelf, and have at least one prime out of business within two years.

For markets, Driscoll matters because he turns defense-procurement reform from a generic Washington complaint into a named, time-bound campaign with winners, losers, and observable contract-award signals.

## Synopsis

Driscoll is the named operational architect of the most public challenge to the [[Defense Primes]] in a generation. His thesis is simple and on the record: the primes have used decades of consolidation to convince the Pentagon it needs purpose-built specialty systems where commercially available products are equal to or better. His structural goal is a 90/10 inversion of the Army's purchasing mix — 90% commercial off-the-shelf, 10% purpose-built — and his political goal, stated to industry audiences in May 2025, is for at least one prime to be out of business within two years. The doctrine he runs at Army level is mirrored at cabinet level by [[Pete Hegseth]] (November 2025 procurement strategy) and enforced at White House level by [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s January 8, 2026 [[Trump defense budget|executive order]] capping prime executive pay and restricting capital returns until production capacity is built. The April 2026 [[Pentagon]] talks with [[General Motors]] and [[Ford]] CEOs are the operational extension of Driscoll's framework into commercial manufacturing — exactly the kind of dual-use industrial-base reconstitution the [[Defense supply chain]] note has been documenting through other voices. For investors, every Driscoll public statement is a candidate prime-stock catalyst, and any contract award outside the prime ecosystem is a data point along the inversion trajectory.

## Procurement reform campaign

Driscoll has run a serial public campaign through 2025-2026 to restructure how the Army buys weapons systems. Key public moves:

| Date | Forum | Move |
|------|-------|------|
| May 2025 | [[Defense]] industry remarks | "I would measure it as success if in the next two years, one of the primes is no longer in business, and the rest of them have all gotten stronger." |
| Oct 2025 | AUSA conference | Lays out Silicon Valley acquisition vision; Army to do less business with traditional primes |
| Nov 2025 | Breaking Defense | Accuses [[Defense]] industrial base and primes of "conning the American people and the Pentagon and the Army into thinking that it needed military specific solutions, when in reality, a lot of these commercial solutions are equal to or better." |
| Dec 2025 | 2025 review | Frames the year as "less business with primes" and a "better-funded Army network" |
| Feb 2026 | [[Bloomberg]] | Warns major US [[Defense]] contractors to "adapt and change or die," explicitly raising commercial giants that have never done DoD business as substitutes |
| Apr 2026 | [[Financial Times|FT]]/[[Wall Street Journal|WSJ]] — [[General Motors|GM]]/[[Ford]] talks | [[Pentagon]] discussions with [[General Motors]] and [[Ford]] surface as the operational extension of the doctrine |

The 90/10 inversion is the structural claim: the Army today buys roughly 90% purpose-built military solutions and 10% commercial; Driscoll's stated target is 10% purpose-built and 90% commercial.

*Sources: Breaking Defense (May 2025, Oct 2025, Nov 2025, Dec 2025), [[Bloomberg]] (Feb 12, 2026), Responsible Statecraft, Washington Times.*

## Relationship to [[Pete Hegseth]] and [[Donald Trump|Trump]]

Driscoll is the Army-level operational architect; [[Pete Hegseth]] is the cabinet-level promoter; [[Donald Trump|Trump]] supplies the executive-order enforcement. The three layers reinforce: Driscoll says primes have "conned" the country; Hegseth rolls out the off-the-shelf procurement strategy in November 2025; Trump signs the Jan 8, 2026 executive order capping prime executive pay and restricting buybacks until production capacity is built.

[[Anduril]] sits inside this triangle by design. [[Palmer Luckey]] personally suggested the [[Pentagon|Department of War]] rebrand to Trump; Hegseth visited Anduril HQ in December 2025 and praised the "Arsenal of Freedom"; Driscoll publicly endorsed Luckey's pattern of innovation. The political coalition behind the procurement reform is also the customer base for the insurgent vendors.
