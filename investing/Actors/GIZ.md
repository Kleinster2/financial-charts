# GIZ

German federal enterprise implementing international cooperation projects on behalf of the German government, primarily the Federal Ministry for Economic Cooperation and Development (BMZ). Operates in 120+ countries with ~25,000 employees, making it one of the world's largest development agencies.

## Synopsis

GIZ (Deutsche Gesellschaft fur Internationale Zusammenarbeit) was formed in 2011 by merging three predecessor organizations (GTZ, DED, InWEnt) into a single implementation agency. Unlike development banks such as [[FMO]] or [[KfW]] that deploy capital for returns, GIZ is primarily a technical cooperation agency — it provides expertise, training, and institution-building rather than equity or debt. Annual turnover runs ~EUR 4B, almost entirely funded by the German federal government and EU commissions. GIZ does not invest for financial returns, which makes its role in the investing vault indirect: it shapes markets through capacity-building, regulatory reform, and infrastructure planning in emerging economies.

GIZ's relevance to investment analysis comes through its role as a market-maker for private capital. When GIZ builds regulatory frameworks for renewable energy in sub-Saharan Africa or designs vocational training programs for semiconductor manufacturing in Southeast Asia, it creates the enabling conditions that make commercial investment viable. The agency backed [[Delta40]]'s $20M fund (Feb 2026) focused on African logistics, reflecting Germany's strategic interest in diversifying supply chains away from [[China]] dependence. GIZ increasingly operates at the intersection of development and industrial policy — climate adaptation, digital infrastructure, and supply-chain resilience are where German development spending and German commercial interests converge.
