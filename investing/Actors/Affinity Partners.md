**Affinity Partners** — Miami-based investment firm founded by [[Jared Kushner]] six months after leaving the White House (July 2021). Anchor-funded by [[Saudi PIF|PIF]] with $2B despite unanimous opposition from PIF's own screening committee. Operating entity: A Fin Management LLC.

---

## Synopsis

Affinity Partners is less an investment firm than a case study in how political access converts to capital. Kushner launched it six months after leaving the Trump White House; eleven months later, [[Mohammed bin Salman|MBS]]'s [[Saudi PIF|PIF]] committed $2B as anchor investor — overruling the fund's own due diligence committee, which voted unanimously against on grounds of management inexperience, excessive fees, and reputational risk. By mid-2024, Affinity had collected $157M in management fees ($87M from Saudi government alone) while generating zero returns for investors. The fee-to-return ratio is the tell: Gulf sovereigns aren't paying for alpha, they're paying for access to a former White House senior adviser whose policy positions consistently favored their interests.

The firm's deal history is thin for $4.8B in commitments — an Amazon aggregator, solar, a German fitness startup, an Israeli insurer, and the headline-grabbing $52.5B [[Electronic Arts|EA]] LBO alongside [[Silver Lake]] and PIF. Sen. [[Ron Wyden]] described Affinity as "likely part of a compensation scheme involving U.S. political figures designed to circumvent FARA." In March 2025, despite pledging not to fundraise during Trump's second term, Kushner began seeking $5B+ from foreign governments while simultaneously serving as Special Envoy for Peace.

## The PIF deal

The anchor investment is the defining story. In December 2021, PIF committed $2B — making it Affinity's foundational LP. What makes this unusual:

PIF's screening committee — composed of current or former heads of [[Saudi Aramco]], [[Dow Chemical]], the [[Saudi Central Bank]], and the Saudi Industrial Development Fund — voted unanimously against investing. Their objections, per NYT and House Oversight documents:

- "Inexperience of the Affinity Fund management"
- The kingdom would bear "the bulk of the investment and risk"
- Operations were "unsatisfactory in all aspects"
- "Excessive" management fee
- "Public relations risks" from Kushner's prior government role

The full PIF board, chaired by [[Mohammed bin Salman|MBS]], overruled the screening committee. The commitment came ~11 months after Kushner left government — a speed that investigators noted was unusual for a first-time fund with no track record.

## Congressional investigations

### House Oversight (2022)

Chairwoman Carolyn Maloney launched formal probe (June 2022) of the $2B PIF investment. Committee stated the deal creates "the appearance of a quid pro quo for your foreign policy work during the Trump Administration." After eight weeks of non-compliance, Affinity produced documents consisting "mainly of publicly-available materials" and zero communications regarding the PIF investment decision.

### Senate Finance — Wyden (2020–2024)

The most sustained investigation. Key findings across multiple releases:

- Dec 2020: Wyden and Rep. Castro launch investigation into Kushner conflicts of interest
- Oct 2022: Investigation continues into influence on U.S. foreign policy
- June 2024: Probes Gulf state payments and potential FARA loophole — Kushner receives millions annually from Saudi, UAE, and Qatari government-owned entities without FARA disclosures
- Sep 2024: New findings — $157M in fees, $87M from Saudi, zero returns, "unsatisfactory" capital deployment. Describes Affinity as "likely part of a compensation scheme involving U.S. political figures designed to circumvent FARA"
- Oct 2024: Wyden and Rep. Raskin request appointment of special counsel to investigate Kushner for FARA violations

### Comer (R, Aug 2023)

Even the Republican Oversight chairman acknowledged it: "I have been vocal that I think that what Kushner did crossed the line of ethics." Noted it "happened after he left office" but was "still no excuse." Did not issue subpoena.

## Second-term fundraising (2025–2026)

Dec 2024: Kushner claimed on a podcast that Affinity would "not have to raise capital for the next four years" in order "to avoid any conflicts."

Feb 19, 2025: Trump appoints Kushner Special Envoy for Peace (triggering 30-day financial disclosure requirement).

Nov 2025: Kushner visited Riyadh ahead of [[Mohammed bin Salman|MBS]]'s Washington visit — diplomatic cover for what was also a fundraising trip.

Mar 2026 (NYT, 5 sources): Kushner is actively seeking $5B+ from foreign governments for Affinity while simultaneously serving as envoy. Affinity representatives have already met with [[Saudi PIF|PIF]]; Kushner will approach [[UAE]] and [[Qatar]] sovereign wealth funds next. Fundraising effort expected to last most of 2026. Affinity has negotiated "first look" rights with PIF — meaning Saudi Arabia's $930B sovereign fund gives Kushner's firm priority access to deals before other managers see them.

Mar 11, 2026: CREW sends letter to the White House demanding Kushner's financial disclosure and resolution of conflicts of interest. Law professor Kathleen Clark: investors seek "freedom from legal constraints and impunity that only the president can deliver."

The Gaza reconstruction initiative — branded "Board of Peace" — adds another layer: Affinity is positioning for post-conflict infrastructure contracts in a region where Kushner holds the diplomatic portfolio.

### Academic assessments

- Andreas Krieg ([[King's College London]]): characterizes the arrangement as "geoeconomic statecraft" using "business entanglements and commercial interdependence" as power tools
- Kristian Coates Ulrichsen ([[Rice University]] Baker Institute): describes Trump's "transactional approach" as prefiguring current administration tactics
- Hamzeh Al Gaaod (independent economist): Gulf investments are "quite sizeable and significant" but "very clearly politically motivated"
