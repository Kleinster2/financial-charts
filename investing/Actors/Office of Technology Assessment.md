# Office of Technology Assessment

Office of Technology Assessment was the US congressional technology-assessment agency created by the Technology Assessment Act of 1972 and defunded in 1995. It matters again because AI, chips, biotech, cyber, space and industrial policy are all technical domains where Congress faces a recurring knowledge deficit; [[ChinaTalk]]'s May 2026 policy-agent experiment used OTA revival as a case for how AI agents might eventually give staffers something closer to personalized technical assessments.

## Why it mattered

OTA existed to give Congress independent technical analysis in areas where legislation depends on complex science and engineering. The FAS OTA Archive preserves the report corpus and the institutional memory around the office's closure. The archive frames OTA as a bipartisan source of competent advice on science and technology policy, with hundreds of reports across environment, health, national security, intellectual property, communications and emerging technologies.

The modern relevance is larger than nostalgia. In the 2020s, Congress is legislating across AI, semiconductor export controls, biotech, cyber vulnerabilities, space commercialization, energy infrastructure and platform governance. Those domains create the same institutional problem OTA was designed to solve: elected officials and generalist staff need a way to evaluate technical claims without depending entirely on lobbyists, companies or executive-branch agencies.

## AI-agent angle

[[ChinaTalk]]'s May 29 2026 "Adventures in Vibecoding Policy" uses OTA as a forward-looking policy-tooling case. The claim is not that a model can fully replace a congressional support agency. The useful idea is that agents can lower the cost of first-pass technical assessment: summary, source mapping, policy option comparison, counterarguments, and public-facing explanation. The risk is that cheap generated analysis can also make weak policy proposals look more coherent than they are.

For the vault, OTA sits at the intersection of [[Industrial policy]], [[Agentic AI]], agent governance, and congressional state capacity.
