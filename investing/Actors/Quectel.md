**Quectel** — Shanghai-based wireless-module supplier and the scale leader in global cellular IoT modules.

Quectel matters because the embedded-module layer is where a mundane supply-chain component turns into a strategic-control question. The company generated RMB 18.59 billion of revenue in 2024, held about 31% of global cellular IoT module revenue in Q1 2024, and now sits at the center of a U.S. policy debate over whether Chinese connectivity modules should be treated like other high-risk communications hardware. The commercial case is scale, breadth, and deep OEM integration across [[5G]], LPWA, GNSS, Wi-Fi/Bluetooth, and satellite modules. The policy case against it is that once the module layer is seen through the lens of [[Military-civil fusion]], a commercial supplier can become a target for [[Pentagon]] audits, procurement bans, and eventually [[Covered List]] escalation.

## Why it matters

Quectel is not just another China hardware exporter. It sits in the hidden communications layer inside routers, meters, vehicles, industrial sensors, gateways, cameras, and medical devices. That makes it economically important in the normal way, through scale and design wins, and strategically important in the Washington way, because the module is the path through which firmware, remote management, and connectivity updates reach the device.

## U.S. policy pressure

- January 2025, the [[Pentagon]]'s Section 1260H list included Quectel as a Chinese military company operating in the United States.
- September 2023, [[Reuters]] reported that the [[FCC]] chair had circulated a congressional request to consider Quectel and [[Fibocom]] for the [[Covered List]].
- April 2026, the policy debate shifted from telecom gear in plain sight to embedded modules inside connected systems, tying Quectel directly to the broader [[Chinese cellular module risk analysis April 2026]] push.

## Evolution

- 2010, Quectel was founded in Shanghai to build wireless modules for the expanding machine-to-machine and IoT stack. The original bet was that connectivity would become a standard feature inside ordinary hardware, which made the module vendor more important than the end-brand in many deployments.
- 2019, the Shanghai listing gave the company public-market financing just as 5G and low-power IoT categories were broadening. That mattered because module scale is partly a working-capital game, not just an engineering game.
- 2023, the company ran into a margin squeeze even before the U.S. policy story fully escalated. The weak profit line showed how quickly pricing pressure can overwhelm module leadership in a crowded market.
- 2024 to 2026, revenue and profits recovered, but the valuation question shifted from cyclical demand to geopolitical durability. Once the [[Pentagon]] and [[FCC]] are in the frame, the key issue is no longer only whether Quectel can win sockets. It is whether some of those sockets remain politically acceptable.
