# David Autor

David Autor is the Daniel (1972) and Gail Rubinfeld Professor of Economics at [[MIT]] and the most influential labor economist working on the consequences of trade, technology, and [[AI]] for US workers. Co-author of the "China Syndrome" paper ([[Autor-Dorn-Hanson]], 2013) that anchored the original [[China trade|China shock]] framework, and more recently of work on AI's potential to compress wage inequality by substituting for high-skill tasks rather than low-skill ones.

## Why he matters to the vault

Autor is the origin point for the [[Second China shock]] vocabulary — the 2013 paper and its follow-ups established that free trade's displacement effects are (a) measurable, (b) geographically concentrated, and (c) do not self-correct on textbook timelines. That empirical finding is the anchor for most modern [[Global imbalances]] analysis and for the political-economy framing of [[Trump]]-era trade policy.

Autor's more recent work on AI and wages argues that the current generation of large language models may compress wage inequality by automating cognitive work that had been a source of skill premiums — a directionally different story from the "robots replace routine manual labor" framing of the 2010s. Vault notes on [[Agentic AI]] and [[February 2026 AI Disruption Cascade]] can anchor themselves in the Autor frame.
