**DRC** — Central African country. World's largest [[Cobalt]] producer (>70% global supply) and a rapidly growing [[Copper]] producer (~2.5Mt/year). Key mining jurisdiction for [[Ivanhoe Mines]] (Kamoa-Kakula), [[Glencore]] (Katanga/KCC, Mutanda), [[CMOC Group]] (Tenke Fungurume), and [[Barrick Gold]] (Kibali).

## Cobalt export restrictions (2025-2026)

In February 2025, the DRC government imposed a blanket ban on [[Cobalt]] exports to curb a global glut and boost prices. The ban was replaced by strict export quotas from October 2025, but procedural delays meant shipments did not resume until early 2026.

Impact:
- Cobalt benchmark prices up 160%+ since the restrictions (Fastmarkets)
- Cobalt hydroxide — the main DRC export product — more than quadrupled
- Global refined cobalt output declined ~20% in 2025, the first drop in five years
- An 82,000-tonne deficit emerged in 2025 (Darton Commodities)
- Deficits projected to persist every year through 2030 (Darton Commodities, March 2026)
- Pre-ban surplus inventories being "structurally depleted"
- First arrivals of DRC raw material in [[China]] expected May/June 2026

The restrictions pushed the market into what Darton Commodities called "a sharp technical deficit," while simultaneously accelerating material substitution ([[LFP batteries]], high-nickel/low-cobalt cathodes) and product diversification. DRC may ease quotas in 2026 to balance revenue maximization against demand destruction risk.

See [[Cobalt]] for full market analysis.

## M23 / tantalum leverage (May 2026)

The eastern DRC mineral story is no longer only a cobalt/copper story. [[Critical Supply]]'s May 27, 2026 post flagged that Rwanda-backed M23 controls tin/tungsten/tantalum territory in the east while Kinshasa controls the cobalt/copper belt in the southeast. Reuters' March 2026 Rubaya reporting gives the hard supply-chain anchor: Rubaya produces around 15% of the world's coltan, processed into tantalum, and has been under AFC/M23 control since 2024.

This creates a recognition-and-security trap. The Congolese state can offer Western governments a minerals-for-security framework, but some strategic minerals sit in rebel-held territory. If foreign buyers need tantalum security, they cannot treat battlefield control as irrelevant. That does not make M23 a legitimate commercial counterparty; it means the supply chain forces the question into diplomacy, sanctions, banking access, and conflict mediation.

For markets, the clean mapping is:
- Katanga / southeast DRC: cobalt and copper, state-controlled, tied to [[Cobalt]], [[Copper]], [[CMOC Group]], [[Glencore]], and [[Ivanhoe Mines]].
- Eastern DRC / Rubaya: coltan/tantalum, tin, tungsten, conflict control, tied to electronics, aerospace, gas turbines, and defense supply-chain risk.

The next risk is strategic mimicry: armed groups elsewhere may learn that controlling the right mineral node creates foreign-policy leverage beyond the local conflict.
