Continental Grain Company — a privately held, Fribourg-family-controlled US food, agriculture and consumer investment-and-operating group, one of the oldest and historically largest grain traders in the world. Founded in 1813 in [[Arlon]], Belgium by Simon Fribourg, it grew into a top global grain exporter, sold its commodity-trading business to [[Cargill]] in 1999 (renaming itself ContiGroup Companies), and reverted to the Continental Grain name in 2008 as it refocused from trading to long-term investing and operating. It is the parent and founding backer of the private-equity arm [[Arlon Group]] — through which it held Brazilian frozen-vegetables leader [[Grano Alimentos]] from 2015 until the 2026 sale to [[Cleam Capital]] — and a co-owner of US poultry major [[Wayne-Sanderson Farms]].

## Why it matters

- The name behind the name: Continental Grain was founded in the Belgian town of [[Arlon]] — the source of the [[Arlon Group]] brand. Understanding Grano's former owner requires this lineage: Arlon Group is Conti's captive food/ag private-equity vehicle, not an independent fund.
- Trading-to-investing pivot: the 1999 sale of the commodity-trading book to [[Cargill]] is the strategic hinge. Conti stopped being a merchant of grain and became a long-horizon owner-operator of food and agriculture businesses — the model that produced both the Grano hold and the Wayne-Sanderson stake.
- Poultry scale: via the [[Cargill]] joint venture, Conti co-owns [[Wayne-Sanderson Farms]], the third-largest US chicken producer, whose 2025 sales jumped ~18% (+$1.3B) to ~$8.7B — a reminder that Conti's reach runs well beyond the mid-market deals Arlon does.

## Funding history

Privately held and family-controlled across six generations of the Fribourg family; not venture- or public-financed. Capital is deployed off the family/holding balance sheet into private equity (via [[Arlon Group]]), public equities, and venture.
