# Jefferies

The largest independent full-service [[investment banking]] and capital markets firm not affiliated with a commercial bank. NYSE: JEF. Headquartered at 520 Madison Avenue, [[New York]]. Founded in 1962 by [[Boyd Jefferies]] as a [[Los Angeles]]-based equity trading shop; survived an insider trading scandal in the 1980s, reinvented itself under [[Rich Handler]] (CEO since 2001, longest-tenured CEO on [[Wall Street]]), and merged with [[Leucadia National]] in 2013 to create a permanent-capital structure. Renamed Jefferies Financial Group in 2018.

---

## Synopsis

Jefferies occupies a distinctive position in investment banking: it is the only firm generating bulge-bracket-level revenue (~$7.3B net revenues in FY2025) without being tethered to a commercial bank balance sheet, a universal bank parent, or post-2008 bank holding company regulation. That independence is the product of the 2013 reverse merger with [[Leucadia National]], a [[Berkshire Hathaway]]-style conglomerate run by [[Joe Steinberg]] and [[Ian Cumming]], which gave Jefferies permanent capital and strategic patience. The firm ranked 6th in global investment banking fees in 2024, up from 23rd in equity fees as recently as 2010 — a market share ascent unmatched by any competitor over that period.

The growth engine is [[Rich Handler]]'s 25-year playbook: hire displaced talent from failing competitors (60 from [[Drexel Burnham Lambert]] in 1990, teams from [[Bear Stearns]] and [[Lehman Brothers]] post-2008, bankers from [[UBS]] and [[Barclays]] in the 2010s), give them economics closer to a partnership than a bureaucracy, and let compound hiring effects build franchise value. FY2024 (ended November 2024) was a breakout: investment banking net revenues hit $3.44B (+52% YoY), the firm ranked 5th globally in M&A and 3rd in global financial sponsor M&A — both records. FY2025 continued the trajectory with ~$7.3B in net revenues and $631M in net income, though the Q4 result was dragged by a $30M pre-tax markdown on the [[Point Bonita]] investment.

The risk profile is asymmetric. At ~$7.5B market cap (March 2026, down from a 52-week high of $71), the stock trades at a discount to tangible book that reflects two concerns: the [[Point Bonita]] litigation (Western Alliance Bancorp sued for $126M in March 2026, triggering a 13% single-day drop), and cyclical exposure to investment banking fee pools. The [[Berkadia]] joint venture with [[Berkshire Hathaway]] (50/50, acquired [[Capmark Financial Group]]'s servicing business in 2009) provides a steady commercial mortgage origination stream. Handler owns ~8% of shares outstanding (~$837M), aligning management incentives with shareholders at a level unusual for financial services CEOs.

## Evolution

The story of Jefferies is the story of a firm that nearly died twice — once from scandal, once from rumor — and each time rebuilt itself into something larger than what came before, by hiring the people its competitors discarded.

1962-1982: [[Boyd Jefferies]] started the firm in 1962 with $30,000 in borrowed capital, enough to buy a seat on the [[Pacific Coast Stock Exchange]]. Operating out of what was essentially a phone booth on the exchange floor, he invented the "third market" — over-the-counter trading of listed stocks with negotiated commissions, providing institutional investors the anonymity and liquidity they couldn't get on the floor of the [[NYSE]]. The concept was radical for the era. By 1964, Jefferies had introduced "split commissions" to attract block order flow. In 1969, [[Investors Diversified Services]] (IDS), then the second-largest US financial services firm, acquired Jefferies for an earnout potentially valued at $45M — a windfall that forced the firm off the NYSE under Rule 318. Boyd Jefferies bought the firm back in August 1973 for just $2.5M when the IDS relationship soured. The 1970s and early 1980s were the firm's golden age of block trading: in 1981, Jefferies executed a $65M buyback for [[Foremost-McKesson]], one of the largest block trades ever at the time. In 1986, the firm traded 25.8M shares for [[Robert Campeau]]'s acquisition of [[Allied Stores]]. Jefferies went public on October 13, 1983, with an IPO of 1.75M shares at $13/share.

1987 — Scandal: The party ended when [[Boyd Jefferies]] pleaded guilty to two felonies — securities "parking" for [[Ivan Boesky]] and margin violations — as part of the broader insider trading scandal that also ensnared [[Michael Milken]] and brought down [[Drexel Burnham Lambert]]. The scheme was simple: Boesky and Jefferies had an oral agreement (March 1985) where Jefferies & Co. would buy and hold stocks for Boesky, who would repurchase them after 31 days, guaranteeing Jefferies against losses and paying double the usual commission. It was textbook parking — concealing true ownership to manipulate market perception. Boyd Jefferies received a fine, probation, and a five-year ban from the securities industry. He resigned as chairman in March 1987. The firm he built survived only because it was bigger than him.

1987-2000 — Rebuilding under Baxter: [[Frank Baxter]] took over as CEO in 1987 and stabilized the firm through a combination of cost discipline (15% expense cuts) and aggressive hiring from the wreckage of [[Drexel Burnham Lambert]], which collapsed in February 1990. Jefferies recruited 60 Drexel bankers and traders, including [[Rich Handler]], then a 28-year-old salesman and trader who had worked in [[Michael Milken]]'s high-yield bond department. The Drexel hires transformed Jefferies from a pure equity trading shop into a firm with high-yield capabilities and investment banking ambitions. Baxter continued expanding through the 1990s — hiring 14 from [[Howard Weil]] in 1993, building convertible and derivative sales desks, and pushing into corporate finance. By 1997, revenues approached $800M with $63.6M in earnings. Baxter stepped down as CEO in 2000.

2001-2007 — Handler era begins: [[Rich Handler]] became CEO on January 1, 2001 — nine months before [[September 11, 2001|9/11]] scrambled every assumption about [[Wall Street]]. Handler, who had risen from Drexel refugee to running Jefferies' high-yield operation, brought a trader's intensity and a partnership-style compensation philosophy. His thesis was that an independent firm unburdened by commercial banking regulation could attract top talent by offering better economics and fewer bureaucratic layers. The firm moved its headquarters from [[Los Angeles]] to [[New York]] in 2002, signaling ambitions beyond the West Coast trading niche. Handler began selectively hiring senior bankers from bulge-bracket firms who were frustrated with post-Sarbanes-Oxley compliance overhead and declining compensation pools. By 2007, Jefferies had roughly tripled revenue from where Baxter left it.

2008-2012 — The hiring spree: The financial crisis destroyed several of Jefferies' larger competitors, and Handler moved aggressively to poach their best people. From [[Bear Stearns]] (collapsed March 2008): Robert Harteveldt (24-year veteran, hired as chairman of fixed income in July 2008), plus nine salespeople, traders, and seven research analysts. From [[Lehman Brothers]] (bankrupt September 2008): John Miller (former head of US investment banking at [[Barclays]]'s Lehman franchise) and multiple high-yield professionals. The logic was elegant — rather than make an expensive acquisition, Jefferies hired dealmakers individually, avoiding the integration risk and overpayment that plagued most Wall Street mergers. The strategy created a near-death moment in 2011: after [[MF Global]] collapsed from European sovereign debt exposure in October 2011, short sellers and rating agencies turned on Jefferies, claiming $2.7B in European sovereign long exposure. The stock dropped 20%. Handler responded with full transparency — disclosing the firm's actual net exposure (~$140M gap after hedges) — and the crisis passed. But it exposed the vulnerability of a standalone broker-dealer without permanent capital.

2012-2018 — The Leucadia merger: The MF Global scare catalyzed the most consequential decision in the firm's history. On November 12, 2012, Jefferies announced a merger with [[Leucadia National]], its largest shareholder. Leucadia was a [[Berkshire Hathaway]]-style conglomerate built by [[Ian Cumming]] and [[Joe Steinberg]] — Harvard Business School classmates (1970) who had gained control of Talcott National Corporation in 1979, renamed it Leucadia in 1980, and compounded returns at ~20% annually for 34 years. Their playbook was pure value investing: buy distressed assets, fix them, hold forever. Key Leucadia deals included buying Colonial Penn insurance from [[Florida Power & Light]] for $150M in 1991 and selling the pieces to [[Conseco]] ($460M) and [[GE Capital]] ($950M) in 1997. By 2012, Leucadia had a net worth of $6.8B. The merger gave Jefferies the permanent capital base it needed — no more existential crises from short-seller attacks on a standalone broker-dealer. Handler became CEO of the combined entity. Cumming retired in 2013 and died in February 2018 at age 77. Steinberg became chairman. In May 2018, Leucadia was renamed Jefferies Financial Group to reflect the reality that the investment bank had become the core operating business.

2019-2024 — Market share ascent: The post-merger years were about converting permanent capital and talent accumulation into market share. The breakthrough was in FY2024 (ended November 2024): net revenues of $7.03B (+50% YoY), investment banking net revenues of $3.44B (+52%), with the firm ranking 5th globally in M&A, 6th in ECM, and 3rd in global financial sponsor M&A — all records. The 50/50 [[Berkadia]] joint venture with [[Berkshire Hathaway]], formed in 2009 to acquire [[Capmark Financial Group]]'s North American loan origination and servicing business, provided steady commercial mortgage revenue alongside the cyclical investment banking business. [[Brian Friedman]], who joined alongside Handler in 2001, has served as president throughout, running the non-banking operations and serving as Handler's operational counterpart.

2025-present — Momentum and headwinds: FY2025 (ended November 2025) delivered ~$7.3B in net revenues (+4.4% YoY) and $631M in net income. The growth rate decelerated from FY2024's breakout, partly due to a $30M pre-tax loss on the [[Point Bonita]] investment in Q4. The Q4 2025 results (announced January 7, 2026) showed investment banking revenues of $1.19B (+20% YoY) and equities net revenues up 18%, offset by a 14% decline in fixed income. In March 2026, [[Western Alliance Bancorp]] sued Jefferies for $126M over the Point Bonita investment, triggering a 13.5% stock decline on March 6. The stock fell further to ~$36 by mid-March, well below the 52-week high of $71 and below tangible book value — creating either a buying opportunity or a signal that the market sees more risk in the asset management side than management has disclosed. The firm declared a quarterly dividend of $0.40/share, raised 14.3% in FY2024, reflecting confidence in the core business even as the Point Bonita overhang persists. Handler's stated ambition: top-five global pure-play investment bank.

## Core thesis

Jefferies is the purest play on the structural shift toward independent advisory in investment banking. The post-2008 regulatory environment (Volcker Rule, bank holding company restrictions, Basel III capital requirements) hobbled bulge brackets' ability to compete aggressively on compensation and risk-taking. Jefferies, which never became a bank holding company, operates with fewer constraints and can offer economics that attract top talent. The Leucadia permanent-capital structure removes the standalone broker-dealer vulnerability that nearly killed the firm in 2011. The risk is cyclicality: investment banking fees are the most volatile revenue line in financial services, and a prolonged M&A drought would hit Jefferies harder than diversified banks.

## Strengths

- Only large independent investment bank not subject to bank holding company regulation
- 25-year track record of hiring displaced talent and converting it into market share
- Permanent capital base from Leucadia merger eliminates standalone broker-dealer risk
- [[Berkadia]] JV with [[Berkshire Hathaway]] provides commercial mortgage diversification
- Handler's ~8% ownership aligns management with shareholders
- Financial sponsor relationships (3rd globally in sponsor M&A) create recurring deal flow

## Risks

- Cyclical revenue concentration in investment banking advisory and underwriting
- [[Point Bonita]] litigation: $126M [[Western Alliance Bancorp]] lawsuit (March 2026) and $30M Q4 markdown
- Stock trading below tangible book value (~$36 vs ~$49 book) may signal market concern about undisclosed asset management risks
- Fixed income revenue declining (-14% Q4 2025 YoY)
- Key-person risk: Handler (born 1961, age 64) has no disclosed succession plan after 25 years as CEO
