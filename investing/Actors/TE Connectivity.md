# TE Connectivity

TE Connectivity is one of the largest global manufacturers of electrical connectors, sensors, and engineered connectivity solutions. It supplies the physical-layer hardware behind [[Automotive|auto]] electrification, industrial automation, data-center networking, aerospace and defense, and medical devices — roughly 16% of its revenue lands in data/compute end-markets that are now growing alongside [[AI infrastructure financing|AI infrastructure]] capex. Spun out of [[Tyco International]] in 2007, re-domiciled from Switzerland to Ireland in 2022 to 2023, and renamed from Tyco Electronics to TE Connectivity in 2011.

## What they do

Two reportable segments, each ~50% of revenue at similar margin profiles:

### Transportation Solutions

Connectors, sensors, relays, heat-shrink tubing, and customized interconnect for [[Automotive|automotive]], [[Commercial transportation]], sensors, aerospace/defense/marine, and medical devices. The segment rides auto production cycles but benefits structurally from content growth per vehicle as EV and [[ADAS]] systems require multiples more connectors than ICE vehicles.

### Industrial Solutions

Connectors and electronic components for industrial equipment, communications data networks, [[Aerospace and defense]], energy, and medical end markets. This is the segment where [[AI infrastructure financing|AI infrastructure]] exposure shows up — TEL makes the high-speed connectors inside AI servers, networking switches, and power-delivery systems. The "digital data networks" sub-vertical grew nearly 50% organically in Q2 FY2026.

## Q2 FY2026 earnings — beat above guidance, stock -10.65% on auto softness (Apr 22, 2026)

A rare case where a genuinely strong print meets a sharp negative stock reaction. TEL beat its own guidance on both revenue and EPS, raised the dividend 10%, and cited record orders — yet the stock fell 10.65% on session, the largest single-day drop in recent memory. The selloff is driven by a sell-the-news reaction combined with deteriorating organic trends in the Transportation segment (the largest) and emerging softness in Medical.

Q3 FY26 guide: revenue ~$5.0B (+10% YoY) and adjusted EPS ~$2.83 (+17% YoY) — both above consensus.

Why the stock fell. Three compounding concerns:

1. Automotive organic -4% is the cleanest negative data point in the quarter. This reverses the +1% print in Q1 and confirms that the [[Automotive]] production softness flagged by [[Aptiv]] and [[Mobileye]] earlier in April is a real industry trend, not a one-quarter anomaly. Read-across to [[Magna International]] and other auto-exposed suppliers.

2. Medical softness — not quantified in the press release but flagged on the call. This tracks with [[Boston Scientific]]'s WATCHMAN deceleration and general mid-single-digit US elective procedure trends.

3. Sell-the-news on AI-connector re-rating. TEL had rallied into the print on the "AI infrastructure physical-layer beneficiary" thesis — +50% data networks growth vindicates it, but the stock had already priced a stronger beat plus raised FY guide. Management kept FY26 guidance at the midpoint, not raised, despite the Q2 beat. In the current market, maintain-guide on a beat-and-call reads as "we don't see it accelerating from here."

The underlying split matters. Industrial Solutions at +17% organic is one of the stronger industrial prints this earnings season; the segment is now a legitimate AI-infrastructure proxy. Transportation at slightly negative organic is the drag — and it is the segment where margins have been the hardest to defend. The Q2 print is the clearest signal yet that within TEL, the "AI beneficiary" narrative and the "auto cyclical" narrative are now pulling in opposite directions.

Read-across to [[Amphenol]], which reports May 1 — the direct peer comparison will test whether the Transportation/auto drag is TEL-specific or industry-wide.

## Evolution

1941: Founded as Aircraft and Marine Products (AMP) in Harrisburg, Pennsylvania to make solderless electrical connections for aircraft and naval ships. The AMP name became synonymous with crimp-style connector technology that replaced soldered joints on production lines.

1960s-1990s: AMP grew into the dominant US connector manufacturer, expanding into automotive and consumer electronics. Revenue passed $1B in the 1970s and $5B in the 1990s.

1999: [[Tyco International]] acquired AMP in a $11.3B hostile deal — one of the largest defense-against-hostile-takeover transactions of the dot-com era. Became Tyco Electronics.

2007: Tyco International split into three companies: [[Covidien]] (healthcare), Tyco Electronics (connectors), and Tyco International (fire and security). Tyco Electronics listed separately as TEL.

2011: Renamed TE Connectivity to signal positioning around connectivity rather than legacy electronics. Acquired Deutsch Group for $2.1B, expanding industrial connector exposure.

2017: [[Terrence Curtin]] became CEO, succeeding Tom Lynch. Focus shifted to portfolio concentration — divesting Broadband Network Solutions, SubCom (undersea cables), and other non-core assets.

2022-2023: Re-domiciled from Switzerland to [[Ireland]] via a corporate restructuring. The move preserved a favorable tax domicile after global tax reform changes while simplifying the legal structure.

2024-2026: Advanced automotive electrification and AI-infrastructure end markets became the dominant narrative drivers. Industrial Solutions segment began posting structurally higher organic growth than Transportation as AI-server connector demand built.

## Competitive position

Global connector market is an oligopoly. TEL, [[Amphenol]], and Molex (privately held, [[Koch Industries]] subsidiary) together hold ~40% of the $80B+ global connector/interconnect market. In specific verticals, concentration is even higher:

- Automotive connectors: TEL and [[Aptiv]] dominate; share ~50% combined
- High-speed data center connectors (AI servers): [[Amphenol]] has historically led, TEL now closing the gap
- Industrial heavy-duty: TEL (via Deutsch brand) and [[Harting]] lead
- Aerospace/defense: TEL, [[Amphenol]], [[Smiths Group]]

---

## Why TEL matters

1. Physical-layer AI infrastructure. Every AI server requires thousands of high-speed connectors. As data rates climb (PCIe 6.0, 224G Ethernet, HBM3E/HBM4), the technical barrier to supplying AI-server connectors rises — TEL and [[Amphenol]] are the only two companies consistently qualified at the top tier. This is a lower-margin exposure than the silicon itself, but it is non-discretionary capex.

2. Auto electrification content growth. EVs require ~30% more connectors by count and ~50% more by dollar value than comparable ICE vehicles. The transition from 12V to 48V and 800V architectures adds high-voltage connector content where TEL has leadership. Even in a soft auto volume environment, content-per-vehicle growth is a structural tailwind.

3. Orderbook as early cycle signal. TEL's sequential order patterns are a closely watched industrial cycle indicator. The record-orders callout in Q2 FY26 is bullish for broader industrial capex, though the auto organic weakness qualifies it.
