**Urenco** — European uranium enrichment company, jointly owned by the UK government (33.3%, via UKAEA), the Dutch government (33.3%, via Ultra-Centrifuge Nederland), and German utilities [[RWE]] and [[E.ON]] (33.3% combined). One of the world's largest producers of enriched uranium, a crucial ingredient in nuclear fuel. Operates centrifuge enrichment plants in four countries.

---

## Synopsis

Urenco is one of only four major commercial uranium enrichment operations globally (alongside Russia's Rosatom/TENEX, France's Orano, and China's CNNC). The company uses gas centrifuge technology — originally developed under the 1970 Treaty of Almelo between the UK, Netherlands, and Germany — to enrich natural uranium from ~0.7% U-235 to reactor-grade levels (~3-5%). Its government ownership structure reflects the strategic sensitivity of enrichment technology, which sits on the proliferation boundary.

The company is riding a structural demand wave: the post-Fukushima contraction is reversing, AI-driven power demand is pulling nuclear back, and the 2026 Middle East energy crisis is accelerating government commitments to energy independence. Urenco's order book hit a record €21.3bn in 2025 — a fourth consecutive year of growth. The company is now expanding into HALEU (High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium, enriched to 5-20%) at its Capenhurst UK facility to fuel next-generation SMRs, positioning it alongside [[Centrus Energy]] as one of the only Western HALEU suppliers.

The key risk is the enrichment market's structure: Russia's [[Rosatom]] still controls ~40% of global enrichment capacity, and Western sanctions have only partially redirected flows. [[France]] imported 39% of enriched uranium from Russia in 2025. Urenco's expansion is necessary but takes years — enrichment capacity can't be surged on short notice.

## HALEU expansion

Urenco is expanding its Capenhurst facility to enrich uranium to higher levels than standard reactor-grade — producing HALEU (High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium, enriched to 5-20% U-235) for next-generation small modular reactors. This puts Urenco alongside [[Centrus Energy]] as one of the only Western HALEU sources.

First HALEU supply deal signed with [[Radiant Industries]], a US microreactor company developing portable 1 MW reactors.

HALEU is a strategic bottleneck: most advanced reactor designs ([[Oklo]] Aurora, [[TerraPower]] Natrium, [[X-energy]] Xe-100, [[Kairos Power]] KP-FHR) require it, and Russia was previously the dominant supplier. US DOE awarded $2.7B in domestic enrichment contracts (Jan 2026) partly to address this dependency.

## March 2026 — Iran war energy shock

CEO Boris Schucht told the *Financial Times* (Mar 12, 2026) that a "nuclear renaissance" was already under way and would accelerate due to the oil and gas supply disruptions from the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure.

Schucht's argument for nuclear fuel supply stability:
- Volumes required by utilities are very small relative to fossil fuels
- Fuel does not require frequent resupply
- Fuel can be easily stored — utilities typically hold ~2 years of fuel stocks
- Supply chain is far more stable than fossil fuels, which depend on continuous maritime transit

*"The supply crisis in the Middle East will refocus policymakers and industry on energy security and the need to have some form of baseload power in countries that is independent from supply threats. This will make deployment of nuclear energy even more important... as countries seek energy independence."* — Boris Schucht, CEO (Mar 12, 2026)

## Competitive landscape

| Company | Type | Geography | Notes |
|---------|------|-----------|-------|
| Urenco | Centrifuge enrichment | UK/NL/DE/US | Government-owned, expanding HALEU |
| [[Rosatom]]/TENEX | Centrifuge enrichment | Russia | ~40% global capacity, sanctions-impacted |
| [[Orano]] (ex-Areva) | Centrifuge enrichment | France | Georges Besse II plant |
| CNNC | Centrifuge enrichment | China | Domestic market focus |
| [[Centrus Energy]] | HALEU production | US (Piketon, OH) | Only US HALEU producer, smaller scale |

The enrichment market is a tight oligopoly. Western sanctions on Russian nuclear fuel are gradually redirecting contracts toward Urenco and Orano, but the transition is slow — enrichment capacity takes years to build.
