**Epogee** was the sole manufacturer of EPG (esterified propoxylated glycerol), a modified plant fat delivering 0.7 kcal/g versus 9 kcal/g for conventional fat — a 92% calorie reduction while maintaining the mouthfeel and functionality of regular fats in food products.

## Synopsis

Epogee occupied a rare position in food technology: it held the manufacturing monopoly on a genuinely novel fat-replacement ingredient with FDA GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) status. EPG works by modifying glycerol with propylene oxide, creating a fat molecule the body cannot fully digest. Unlike earlier fat substitutes (Olestra/Olean, which caused gastrointestinal side effects and commercial failure in the 1990s), EPG claims no such issues and can be used in baking, frying, and processed food formulation. The technology originated from research by the [[USDA]] and was commercialized by Epogee.

The acquisition by [[David]] in May 2025 — using the bulk of David's $75M Series A proceeds — was a vertical integration play born of dependency. David, the protein bar company founded by [[Peter Rahal]] (who previously built [[RXBAR]] and sold it to [[Kellogg's]] for $600M), accounted for roughly 90% of Epogee's revenue pre-acquisition. When your sole customer is also your sole economic lifeline, the acquisition is less a strategic choice than an inevitability. For David, it secures supply of the core differentiating ingredient; for the broader food-tech space, it removes EPG from the open market, making it a proprietary David ingredient rather than a platform technology competitors could license.
