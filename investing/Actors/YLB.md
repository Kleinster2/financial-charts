**YLB — Yacimientos de Litio Bolivianos** (Empresa Pública Nacional Estratégica de Yacimientos de Litio Bolivianos) is the Bolivian state-owned enterprise holding the exclusive development mandate for [[Bolivia]]'s lithium resources, principally in the Salar de Uyuni. [[Bolivia]] holds 21% of the world's known lithium reserves — the single largest share globally — but produces a few hundred tonnes per year. YLB is the reason: state execution capacity has not matched the resource endowment, and political contestation over foreign partnerships has kept large-scale development stalled.

Not directly investable, but load-bearing context for the global lithium supply thesis: YLB's inability to mobilize Uyuni capacity is a structural constraint on Bolivia-sourced supply that cannot be loosened without either state-capacity reform or a political mandate shift.

| Factor | Detail |

| Target stated | **50,000 tpa LCE** export ambition |
| Cumulative state investment in DLE | **$800M+** (2023-2025) |
| Current DLE results | Poor — "underdeveloped for large-scale industrial application" |

The reserves / production gap is the biggest single-country gap in the global lithium market. If YLB ever unlocks capacity, global supply could shift materially. If it does not, Bolivia remains a reserve footnote — the geological equivalent of OPEC's spare-capacity problem but without the institutional infrastructure to deliver.

## The two big partnership tracks

### CBC (Chinese consortium) — $1B deal

A consortium led by Chinese battery producer CBC (with [[CATL]] participation) signed a ~$1B agreement with YLB to build **two lithium plants targeting 35,000 tpa combined capacity**, using [[Direct lithium extraction|DLE]] technology on Salar de Uyuni.

### Rosatom — parallel Russian agreement

A ~$1B Russian agreement (Rosatom-linked) runs in parallel, targeting similar DLE capacity on a different Uyuni section.

### Both stalled, July 2025

Bolivian congress refused to ratify either deal during a chaotic July 2025 session that devolved into shouting matches, water-throwing, and protests. Opposition framing: foreign dependency is being swapped for Chinese/Russian dependency without adequate technology transfer or Bolivian operational control.

The stall means neither project has the legislative authorization to proceed into construction. Bolivia's 2025 presidential transition (Luis Arce out) added a further layer of policy-continuity risk. As of April 2026, ratification remains unresolved.

## DLE technology challenge

The state's $800M+ investment in [[Direct lithium extraction]] pilot plants has not yet produced commercial-scale output. YLB has acknowledged "relatively poor results" from the DLE pilots — the technology works in principle (it is used at scale by [[SQM]] in Chile on legacy brine and by [[Standard Lithium]] in the US Smackover Formation) but Bolivia's salar chemistry has proven less amenable than the Atacama. Uyuni brine contains higher magnesium and other impurities that reduce DLE recovery and raise processing costs.

This is the operational analog of the political stall: even if the Chinese and Russian partnerships clear Congress, the underlying extraction technology has not yet demonstrated that 35,000-45,000 tpa Uyuni DLE is commercially viable at target cost.

## Strategic significance

YLB matters for three threads the vault tracks:

1. **Global supply wildcard.** A functioning 50 ktpa YLB export program would materially loosen the Apr 16 supply-discipline rally. The current price signal ($23,000/t spot) creates economic pressure for Bolivia to unlock supply, but institutional capacity is the binding constraint, not commodity-price incentive.
2. **Chinese supply-chain integration.** If the CBC consortium deal ultimately clears Bolivian congress, another ~20 ktpa of Chinese-integrated lithium output joins the [[Ganfeng Lithium]]-led Argentine pipeline. This extends China's refining chokepoint upstream into Latin American raw-material supply.
3. **Resource-nationalism benchmark.** YLB is the clearest case of resource endowment failing to translate into production due to political fragmentation. Contrast with the [[Codelco]]-[[SQM]] [[Lithium Triangle|Nova Andino Litio]] JV in Chile, which did successfully structure state-majority participation through a functioning public-private framework.
