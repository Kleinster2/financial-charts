[[Mosaic]] (NYSE: MOS) is the world's second-largest producer of concentrated phosphate and a top-tier potash supplier, formed in 2004 from the merger of [[Cargill]]'s crop nutrition division and [[IMC Global]] (itself dating to 1909). Headquarters in Tampa, FL. Three operating segments: Phosphates (North American mining and processing), Potash (Saskatchewan and Carlsbad, NM -- pending sale), and Mosaic Fertilizantes (acquired from [[Vale]] in 2018, the largest fertilizer producer and distributor in Brazil). FY2025 revenue $12.05B, net income $541M, 13,249 employees. Stock at $27.78 (Mar 18, 2026), down from 52-week high of $37.21, pressured by sulfur cost spikes, analyst downgrades, and a DOJ antitrust probe.

## Synopsis

Mosaic is structurally the most exposed pure-play fertilizer stock to the [[Strait of Hormuz]] crisis. Sulfur -- which spiked to >$500/mt in late 2025 vs. a ~$200 historical norm -- is a critical input for [[Phosphoric Acid]] production, and Mosaic consumes more sulfur than any other US producer. The company idled its lowest-margin Brazilian SSP plants at Araxá and Fospar in December 2025, and management guided for ~$250M of Q1 2026 EBITDA headwind from sulfur alone. At the same time, phosphate selling prices are at multi-year highs ($667/t average in 2025, up 13% YoY) because [[Export controls|China's export restrictions]] and [[LFP battery]] demand are tightening global supply. The squeeze is between rising input costs and rising output prices -- the question is which moves faster.

The balance sheet carries $5.05B of debt against $277M cash, with negative free cash flow of -$535M in 2025 driven by a $960M inventory buildup and $1.36B capex (gypsum stack maintenance, tailings dams, Esterhazy K3 completion). The $1.8B Ma'aden shareholding (locked up through 2027-2029) is a hidden asset that doesn't show in operating cash flow. Mosaic Biosciences ($68M revenue in 2025, doubled YoY, 40%+ gross margins) is an emerging growth vector in biologicals. The [[Rainbow Rare Earths]] JV at Uberaba -- extracting rare earths from [[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction|phosphogypsum waste]] -- posted a $916M NPV PEA in March 2026 and could revalue the stock if it reaches FID, but that's a 2030 production story.

The DOJ antitrust probe (March 2026, early stage, Chicago office) into fertilizer price-fixing across [[Nutrien]], [[CF Industries]], [[Yara]], and Koch creates regulatory overhang. Combined with BofA's downgrade to Neutral (March 2026, PT $30, citing margin expansion as "more likely a 2027 story") and Barclays' downgrade to Equal Weight, the stock is trading near trough multiples. The bull case requires sulfur costs normalizing, China maintaining export restrictions, and capex declining toward the $1B/year target by 2030. The bear case is that sulfur stays elevated through the Hormuz crisis, Brazilian credit conditions deteriorate further, and the DOJ probe escalates.

## Evolution

The story of [[Mosaic]] is the story of a commodity giant assembled through merger, nearly swallowed by the 2008 supercycle's collapse, liberated from its parent, then transformed by a Brazilian acquisition that doubled its footprint -- only to find itself squeezed between rising input costs and geopolitical disruption at the worst possible moment.

- 1909-2004: The lineage begins with International Agricultural Corporation, incorporated June 14, 1909, by Walderman Schmidten in New York. By the 1920s it was the largest phosphate owner and distributor in the US. Renamed International Minerals and Chemical Corporation in 1941 after merging with United Potash and Chemical Corporation. The company evolved through decades of consolidation in Bone Valley, Florida, becoming [[IMC Global]] -- a vertically integrated phosphate and potash producer with Florida mines, Louisiana processing plants, and Saskatchewan potash operations. Separately, [[Cargill]] built its own crop nutrition division as part of the world's largest private company. In October 2004, Cargill contributed its crop nutrition business to IMC Global in exchange for ~66.5% of the combined entity's shares. The name "Mosaic" was chosen in June 2004 to represent the combination. Fritz Corrigan became the first CEO. The company listed on the NYSE under MOS.

- 2006-2008: Fritz Corrigan retired effective January 2007. Jim Prokopanko, a Cargill veteran, took over as CEO and steered the company into the 2007-2008 commodity supercycle. DAP prices hit $765/t FOB Tampa by January 2008 (up ~400% from 2006). MOS shares rose tenfold, from ~$15 in mid-2006 to an all-time high of $163.25 on June 18, 2008. Quarterly earnings surged 1,134% YoY. Then the financial crisis hit. Fertilizer demand collapsed as farmers deferred purchases. By year-end 2008, MOS had fallen roughly two-thirds from its peak. The lesson: commodity supercycles are real, but the downside is symmetrical.

- 2011: The defining corporate event. [[Cargill]] divested its 64% stake (~286 million shares) in a tax-free transaction valued at ~$24.3B. The structure was a split-off: 179M Mosaic shares exchanged for Cargill stock held by investors, 107M shares exchanged for Cargill debt. Mosaic's shares were recapitalized into three classes (Common, Class A, Class B) to manage voting rights. The divestiture was driven by Cargill's need to maintain private company status while meeting the distribution needs of the Margaret A. Cargill charitable trusts. For Mosaic, it meant full independence -- no more 64% controlling shareholder. Strategic flexibility and stock liquidity improved dramatically. The dispersed shareholder base that emerged (now ~86% institutional, dominated by Vanguard/BlackRock/T. Rowe Price) persists today.

- 2014: Mosaic acquired [[CF Industries]]' phosphate business for ~$1.2B, adding the Plant City, Florida mine and processing plant. This consolidated Mosaic's dominance in North American phosphate, pushing its share of NA concentrated phosphate capacity to ~60%. Plant City was later idled in 2017 as economics shifted.

- 2015-2018: Joc O'Rourke (ex-[[Barrick Gold]], President of Australia Pacific) succeeded Prokopanko as CEO in August 2015. O'Rourke's tenure was defined by two strategic bets. First, the Esterhazy K3 potash mine in Saskatchewan -- a $3B+ project to sink twin shafts to 3,350 feet, replacing the aging K1/K2 mines that suffered from brine inflows. K3 reached first production in December 2018, with nameplate capacity audited at 7.8Mt (up from 6.3Mt). K1 and K2 shafts were permanently closed in 2021. Second, and more transformative: in December 2016, Mosaic announced the acquisition of [[Vale]] Fertilizantes for $2.5B.

- 2018 (Vale Fertilizantes): The deal closed January 8, 2018, at a modified price of $1.15B cash plus 34.2M Mosaic shares (plus up to $260M in contingent earnout). The acquisition brought five phosphate rock mines, four chemical/fertilizer production facilities, a potash plant in Brazil, a 40% stake in the Miski Mayo phosphate mine in Peru, and the Kronau potash project in Saskatchewan. Capacity: 4.8Mt finished phosphate and 500Kt potash. Approximately 8,000 employees joined, bringing the total to ~17,000. Mosaic became the largest fertilizer producer and distributor in Brazil, with an integrated network of mines, chemical plants, blending facilities, and port terminals. The acquisition gave Mosaic access to South America's largest agricultural market and reduced exposure to North American seasonality.

- 2020-2022: COVID initially crushed demand, but 2021-2022 saw a second commodity supercycle driven by post-pandemic restocking, the Russia-Ukraine war disrupting Belarusian/Russian potash exports, and surging food security spending. Revenue hit $19.1B in 2022 (up from $8.7B in 2020). Net income reached $3.6B. EPS hit $10.06. The stock recovered to $79 by April 2022. Mosaic used the cash flow to buy back shares aggressively: $1.07B repurchased (26.7M shares at avg $29.63) under the August 2022 authorization.

- 2023: The supercycle unwound. Revenue fell to $13.7B as potash prices normalized (average selling price $323/t, down from peaks). Net income dropped to $1.17B. The company continued Esterhazy K3 ramp-up and announced the Hydrofloat project to add 400Kt of potash capacity. O'Rourke resigned as CEO effective December 31, 2023, after eight years.

- 2024: Bruce Bodine became CEO on January 1, 2024, the first internal promotion in Mosaic's CEO succession. Bodine joined a predecessor company in 1999 and spent 25 years at Mosaic across engineering, plant management, and executive roles in both Phosphates and Potash. Revenue declined further to $11.1B as fertilizer prices continued normalizing. A fire at the Riverview, FL facility in Q1 forced production purchases to meet demand. Hurricanes in H2 disrupted Florida operations. The Ma'aden transaction closed December 24, 2024: Mosaic exchanged its 25% stake in the MWSPC joint venture (Wa'ad Al Shamal phosphate complex, Saudi Arabia) for 111M [[Ma'aden]] shares valued at ~$1.5B, recording a ~$500M pre-tax gain. The shares are locked up: one-third transferable after each of the 3rd, 4th, and 5th anniversaries. By end of 2025, the stake had appreciated ~20% to ~$1.8B.

- 2025: Revenue recovered to $12.05B (+8.4% YoY). But the year was defined by cost pressures, not revenue growth. Sulfur costs surged 80% YoY to $237/long ton average (and spiked far higher in Q4), compressing phosphate margins despite higher selling prices. Potash was the bright spot: record international sales of 5.5Mt, production of 8.5Mt (highest in 8 years), operating earnings of $638M. Mosaic Fertilizantes operating earnings rose to $277M but deteriorated sharply in Q4 as sulfur costs hit. The company idled Araxá and Fospar in December, sold Patos de Minas ($111M), sold Taquari ($27M, $66M impairment), and signed the Carlsbad sale ($30M, $185M impairment). Issued $900M in bonds (4.35% due 2029, 4.60% due 2030) to address working capital and retire commercial paper. Free cash flow went negative at -$535M, driven by $960M inventory buildup and $1.36B capex. Cost savings of $150M achieved ahead of schedule.

- 2026 (current): Q4 2025 results missed (EPS $0.22 vs. $0.49 est, net loss of $519M including impairments). Guidance: phosphate production at or above 7.0Mt, potash ~9.0Mt, capex ~$1.5B (declining to $1.0B by 2030), additional $100M cost savings targeted. DOJ antitrust probe disclosed March 4. BofA downgraded to Neutral (March 2026), Barclays to Equal Weight (March 3). Stock at $27.78, near 52-week lows. The rare earths JV with [[Rainbow Rare Earths]] at Uberaba published a PEA (March 11) showing $916M NPV and 45% IRR -- a potential revaluation catalyst on a 4-5 year horizon.

## Operations

### North America Phosphates

Mosaic mines phosphate rock in the Bone Valley Formation of central Florida's Peace River watershed -- the largest phosphate rock mining district in the US. Rock is processed into [[Phosphoric Acid]], then into finished products (DAP, MAP, MicroEssentials, animal feed ingredients).

| Facility | Location | Type | Key capacity/notes |
|----------|----------|------|-------------------|
| Four Corners / South Fort Meade | Polk County, FL | Mining + beneficiation | Active phosphate rock mine (since 1995); Ona Mine matrix pumped here |
| New Wales | Lithia, FL | Concentrates plant | ~3Mt processed phosphate/year; largest US phosphate plant |
| Riverview | Tampa Bay, FL | Concentrates plant | 800Kt phosphoric acid, 1.6Mt processed phosphate/year; fire Q1 2024 |
| Bartow | Polk County, FL | Concentrates plant | Active processing |
| Faustina | St. James Parish, LA | Concentrates plant | Ammonia production (supplies ~1/3 of NA needs) + fertilizer production |
| Uncle Sam | St. James Parish, LA | Concentrates plant | Fertilizer production |
| Plant City | Plant City, FL | Concentrates plant | Acquired from [[CF Industries]] 2014; idled 2017 |

North America phosphate rock production: 9.5Mt in 2025 (up from 9.0Mt in 2024). Finished product production: 6.3Mt. Operating rate: 63%.

Total North American operational capacity: ~8.0Mt finished concentrated phosphate.

### Potash

| Facility | Location | Type | Capacity |
|----------|----------|------|----------|
| Esterhazy K3 | Esterhazy, SK, Canada | Underground mine | 7.8Mt nameplate; twin shafts to 3,350 ft; 1,000+ workers |
| Colonsay | Colonsay, SK, Canada | Underground mine | Operational (periodically idled based on demand) |
| Carlsbad | Carlsbad, NM | Underground mine | Pending sale to International Minerals Carlsbad for $30M (H1 2026) |

Potash production: 8.8Mt in 2025 (unchanged from 2024). Operating rate: 76%. Canpotex entitlement: 35.9% (2025). Record international sales of 5.5Mt.

Hydrofloat project at Esterhazy: adds 400Kt capacity; commissioning mid-2025, ramp-up by end of 2025.

### Mosaic Fertilizantes (Brazil)

Acquired from [[Vale]] in January 2018. The largest fertilizer producer and distributor in Brazil. Vertically integrated: phosphate rock mines, chemical plants, blending/bagging facilities, port terminals, warehouses. All production consumed domestically.

Phosphate rock mines (5 properties in 2025): Araxá, Patrocínio, Tapira (Minas Gerais), Catalão (Goiás), Cajati (São Paulo). Annual rock capacity: ~4.6Mt. 2025 production: ~4.2Mt (71% of Brazilian annual production). Finished phosphate production: ~2.6Mt (46% of Brazilian output).

SSP plants at Araxá and Fospar idled December 2025 due to sulfur costs (>$500/mt). Blended rock cost fell to $97/t in Q4 2025 -- lowest since 2021.

Asset sales in 2025: Patos de Minas mine ($111M, $94M gain), Taquari potash mine ($27M, $66M impairment).

Land holdings: over 34,000 hectares (84,000 acres) across 8 Brazilian states.

### Other

China and India distribution businesses: blending plants in China (two 300Kt/year capacity); distribution in India. 2025 volumes: ~383Kt blends + 862Kt phosphate/potash in China; ~189Kt potash in India.

Miski Mayo phosphate mine (Peru): 75% owned, results consolidated. 719 salaried employees included in headcount.

## Rainbow Rare Earths JV (Uberaba)

Mosaic and [[Rainbow Rare Earths]] (LSE: RBW) signed a Joint Project Development Agreement in March 2026 to advance rare earth extraction from [[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction|phosphogypsum]] at Mosaic's Uberaba phosphoric acid facility in Minas Gerais, Brazil.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Post-tax NPV (10%) | $916M |
| Post-tax NPV (13.5%) | $609M |
| Post-tax IRR | 45% |
| Capex | $279M |
| Payback | 1.7 years |
| Annual throughput | ~2.7 Mtpa phosphogypsum |
| Average TREO grade | 0.51% |
| NdPr oxide output | 1,971 tpa at +99.5% purity |
| SEG+ output | 659 tpa (60t Dy, 14t Tb, 188t Y) |
| Annual revenue | $319M |
| Average EBITDA | $217M |
| Operating cost | $38/kg |
| Basket price (assumed) | $128/kg |
| Project life | 30 years (extendable) |

### Structure and timeline

Proposed JV: 51% [[Mosaic]] / 49% [[Rainbow Rare Earths]]. Currently in Pre-Feasibility Study. DFS targeted late 2026. Construction start: 2027. First production: 2030.

### Strategic angle

Mosaic already produces [[Phosphoric Acid]] at Uberaba, generating phosphogypsum as waste. The JV treats waste into a revenue stream -- 1,971 tpa of NdPr oxide is meaningful supply in a market where China controls 90%+ of [[Rare earth leverage|rare earth processing]]. At $128/kg basket price, the project generates $319M/year revenue on a $279M upfront investment. For Mosaic, it's a zero-incremental-mining optionality play: no new mining permits, no rock extraction -- just reprocessing existing waste stacks. The chemically processed stream returns to Mosaic's acid circuit. See [[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction]] for the full technology analysis.

## Brazil plant idling (late 2025)

Mosaic idled SSP production at Araxá and Fospar in Brazil on December 16, 2025, initially for 30 days, then extended indefinitely. [[Elemental Sulfur|Sulfur]] prices exceeded $500/mt -- more than 2.5x the ~$200 historical norm. Sulfur is a critical input for [[Sulfuric Acid]] production in phosphate processing.

The idling was preceded by a halt in sulfur purchases. Management cited a need to assess market conditions. Q1 2026 EBITDA headwind from sulfur alone: ~$250M. This was the first operational casualty of the sulfur price spike linked to [[Strait of Hormuz]] disruption.

Mosaic Fertilizantes Q4 2025 EBITDA fell to ~$50M despite full-year operating earnings of $277M. Blended rock costs at $97/t were the lowest since 2021 -- the problem was entirely on the sulfur/ammonia input side.

*Source: Mosaic IR, FT (Mar 11, 2026), Q4 2025 earnings call*

## March 2026 headwinds

MOS dropped -6.5% (-2.3 sigma) on March 13, 2026, then fell another ~10% on March 20 after BofA's downgrade, closing at $23.59. Multiple negative catalysts converging:

- Barclays downgraded to Equal Weight from Overweight (March 3, PT $31). Cited Iran strikes driving ammonia input cost inflation and asset underperformance.
- DOJ antitrust probe into fertilizer price-fixing disclosed (March 4, Bloomberg). Early-stage investigation run out of Chicago office. Targets: [[Mosaic]], [[Nutrien]], [[CF Industries]], Koch, [[Yara]]. No charges filed. Civil and criminal violations under review. Related class-action lawsuits filed by Iowa farmers.
- JPMorgan downgraded to Underweight (Feb 26, PT $24).
- BofA downgraded to Neutral from Buy (March 20, PT cut to $30 from $33). Cited margin expansion as "more likely a 2027 story." Lowered 2026 EBITDA estimate by $129M to $1.834B.
- Scotiabank cut PT to $35 from $36 (March 2, maintained Sector Outperform).
- Freedom Capital downgraded to Strong Sell (March 21).

## Environmental / regulatory

The phosphate industry generates [[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction|phosphogypsum]] as a byproduct -- a mildly radioactive, acidic waste stored in massive "gypsum stacks" (gyp stacks) across Florida. Mosaic operates multiple gyp stacks, which require ongoing closure and water treatment.

Key incidents:
- 2016: New Wales sinkhole sent 215 million gallons of acidic water into the Floridan aquifer. Community not alerted for three weeks.
- 2021: Piney Point crisis (not Mosaic-owned, but same industry). 460M gallons wastewater discharged into Tampa Bay from a former phosphate plant.
- EPA/DOJ settlement: Mosaic agreed to spend $170M on environmental projects, $2.2M on local projects, and paid $8M in civil penalties ($5M federal, $1.55M Louisiana, $1.45M Florida) to resolve RCRA violations at six FL facilities and two LA facilities. The 60 billion pounds of hazardous waste addressed was the largest amount ever covered in a federal RCRA settlement.
- EPA road construction pilot: Mosaic testing phosphogypsum use in road construction (Polk County, FL). EPA initially approved (2020), withdrew approval (2021) after environmental lawsuits, then Mosaic and EPA challenged a subsequent lawsuit (Sept 2025).

2026 capex elevated partly due to gyp stack closures, clay settling areas, and tailings dam projects. ARO and environmental spending expected to decline ~$50M YoY.
