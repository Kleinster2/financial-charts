**Bolivia** — Landlocked Andean country (population ~12M, GDP ~$45B). Market-relevant primarily for lithium: holds **~21% of the world's identified lithium reserves** (the single largest share globally), concentrated in the Salar de Uyuni salt flats in Potosí and Oruro departments. Production has been structurally blocked by political fragmentation — an idle resource endowment larger than [[Chile]] and [[Argentina]] combined. The November 2025 political transition from Luis Arce to [[Rodrigo Paz]] is the first major inflection point for Bolivia's lithium thesis in a decade.

---

## Synthesis

Bolivia's lithium story is the cleanest natural experiment in institutional capacity as a commodity constraint. Reserves alone would make Bolivia the world's pivot producer — at ~23M tonnes of measured and indicated resources, exceeding [[Argentina]]'s 28M (where capacity has tripled to 186 ktpa under Milei) and [[Chile]]'s 13M (where ~49 ktpa is already being produced under the [[Codelco]]-[[SQM]] Nova Andino Litio joint venture). Yet 2024 production was a few hundred tonnes. The gap is not geology; it is state execution. The [[YLB]] state enterprise has absorbed $800M+ of state investment in direct lithium extraction pilots since 2023 with admittedly "relatively poor results."

The constraint has been political. The Arce administration (2020-November 2025) signed ~$1B agreements with China's CBC consortium and Russia's Rosatom-linked Uranium One for Uyuni DLE development, but Bolivian Congress refused to ratify either deal in a chaotic July 2025 session — opposition framing was that foreign dependency would be swapped for Chinese/Russian dependency without adequate technology transfer or operational control. The structural problem is real: Bolivia lacks the domestic engineering depth to run advanced DLE operations independently, and the 1952 nationalist tradition makes foreign-operator agreements politically fragile. Arce left office at the end of the country's worst economic crisis in 40 years; lithium remained the story that didn't happen.

The counter-example is the November 8, 2025 transition to [[Rodrigo Paz]], who won the October 2025 election as the first non-MAS president in two decades. Paz's stated framework is "modernization without privatization" — he committed to review the opaque Arce-era Chinese and Russian contracts for transparency but pledged not to "sell out" Uyuni, a nationally symbolic geography. The Paz administration is early enough that its operational approach is unproven, but the posture is clearly distinct from both MAS-era resource nationalism and full privatization: partnership-based with local-community engagement and transparency requirements. Whether that produces actual Uyuni output remains the binary question — the test is whether the CBC and Rosatom contracts get renegotiated rather than cancelled, and whether the resulting DLE projects reach commercial scale before the 2030s lithium deficit forecasts price through. At $23,000/t spot lithium carbonate, Bolivia's opportunity cost of non-production is running at ~$10B/year of foregone revenue at plausible production targets.

One line: Bolivia is the lithium market's largest wild card — the only producer whose output could materially loosen the 2028-2030 supply picture if political execution matches the resource endowment, and every year of delay is a year where [[Argentina]] and [[Brazil]] capture the capacity Bolivia could have built.

## Political economy — Paz transition

### Rodrigo Paz (president, Nov 2025 — )

Paz won the October 2025 election, succeeding Luis Arce on November 8, 2025 — the first non-MAS president in two decades. MAS (Movimiento al Socialismo, Evo Morales's party) had governed 2006-2019 and 2020-2025 under Arce.

**Stated lithium policy:**

- **Modernization without privatization** — explicitly rejects both full-state-operation model (YLB alone) and foreign-operator-ownership model (Chilean Nova Andino Litio style)
- **Review of Arce-era contracts** — the CBC (Chinese) and Rosatom (Russian) DLE agreements are being scrutinized for transparency and local-community benefit
- **Local community engagement** — Potosí and Oruro residents have veto-class political weight on Uyuni operations
- **"Won't sell out Uyuni"** — national-symbolism constraint on structure of any deal

### The stalled Chinese and Russian deals

Arce's late-term lithium agreements:

| Partner | Deal value | Target | Status |
|---------|-----------|--------|--------|
| **CBC consortium** (Chinese, includes [[CATL]]) | ~$1B | Two DLE plants, 35,000 tpa combined | Blocked in Congress July 2025; under Paz review |
| **Rosatom / Uranium One** (Russian) | ~$1B | DLE project on separate Uyuni section | Blocked in Congress July 2025; under Paz review |

Both were DLE-based — see [[Direct lithium extraction]] for the technology context. Neither has received legislative authorization. The Paz administration is expected to renegotiate rather than cancel, but terms and timelines are unresolved as of April 2026.

### Congressional math

Paz's center-right coalition does not have majority control — lithium legislation requires coalition-building with the center-left opposition. The renegotiated foreign-partnership terms are the gatekeeping issue. A deal structure closer to Chile's Nova Andino Litio (majority state-margin capture + foreign operational control) is the plausible landing, though the Potosí political constraint may push it toward higher state share.

## Structural context

### Why Bolivia is the production wildcard

[[Bolivia]] holds the biggest reserve endowment but produces the least:

| Country | Reserves (Mt M&I) | 2024 production (t) | Production intensity |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| [[Bolivia]] | **23** | Few hundred | Near-zero |
| [[Argentina]] | 28 | 18,000 | Moderate (Milei-era accelerating) |
| [[Chile]] | 13 | 49,000 | High (Nova Andino Litio) |

The 3-country [[Lithium Triangle]] region holds ~56% of known global reserves. Bolivia is the unused share — the potential supply that could materially loosen the 2028-2030 deficit forecasts if political execution unlocks it.

### Uyuni chemistry constraint

Uyuni brine has high magnesium and other impurities relative to the [[Atacama]] (Chile) — meaning DLE recovery rates are lower and processing costs higher than at SQM's operations. This is why the YLB pilot plants have underperformed targets. Technology-side, the chromatography-based DLE approaches (see [[Battery recycling]] for parallel hydrometallurgy discussion; [[Direct lithium extraction]] for DLE technology landscape) may handle Uyuni better than conventional ion-exchange DLE.

## Market implications

Bolivia's production trajectory over 2026-2030 is the single largest forecast uncertainty in global lithium supply. Three scenarios:

1. **Status quo** — Paz cannot resolve the CBC/Rosatom deadlock; YLB continues marginal-output operation. Bolivia produces sub-10 ktpa through 2030. Impact on global supply: immaterial.
2. **Partial unlock** — Paz renegotiates one of the two contracts with improved transparency/local-community terms; one consortium begins commercial operation 2028-2029. Bolivia produces 15-25 ktpa by 2030. Impact: moderate, closes ~5-10% of projected deficit.
3. **Full unlock** — Both contracts restructured; Uyuni reaches 50+ ktpa by 2030. Impact: material — adds roughly an [[Argentina]]'s worth of capacity to the global supply curve, plausibly eliminating the 2028 deficit forecast.

The probability weighting across these scenarios is the binary question for the lithium bear case.
