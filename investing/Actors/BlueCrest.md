## Synopsis

BlueCrest is the canonical post-LP hedge-fund-founder vehicle. After building the firm into a >$30bn multi-strategy hedge fund through the 2000s and 2010s, [[Michael Platt]] returned all external client capital in 2015-2016 and restructured as a family office. Post-conversion returns have been extraordinary: multi-year sequences of +50% to +150% returns, including +73% in 2025 on ~$50-60bn of proprietary capital. The structure removes LP fee pressure, drawdown-trigger constraints, and most regulatory disclosure — leaving Platt to deploy personal wealth at scale comparable to [[Millennium]] / [[Citadel]] / [[Point72]] without the LP-relations overhead. The [[Financial Times|FT]]'s September 2025 [[Pod shop compensation]] explainer named BlueCrest specifically as one of the firms that may absorb hedge-fund trading talent if multi-manager pay packages cool, because the family-office structure can offer compensation formats (partnership equity, private terms, jurisdictional tax efficiency) that LP-funded pod shops cannot match.

## Structure

### Family office since 2015

In 2015, citing fee compression and regulatory burdens, Platt returned all external client capital — about $8bn — and restructured BlueCrest as a family office investing only his personal fortune. The shift completed in 2016. The move freed the firm from LP fee scrutiny, [[ERISA]] and similar regulatory regimes, and the operational drag of marketing and investor relations.

The trade-off: BlueCrest stopped being a "hedge fund" in the regulatory sense and became a proprietary trading firm investing one person's capital, even if that capital is now larger than most traditional hedge funds.

The post-family-office returns have been substantial. Without LP fees, the gross-equals-net structure means the headline return numbers translate directly into Platt's personal wealth. The 2025 +73% gain, on a $50-60bn capital base, would mean tens of billions in compounded wealth gain.

### Geographic expansion

BlueCrest registered with the Dubai Financial Services Authority in 2025, allowing the firm to manage capital and hire traders from the Dubai International Financial Centre. The Dubai move is consistent with Platt's earlier relocation patterns and the broader hedge-fund migration to lower-tax jurisdictions.

## Relevance to the talent war

The [[Financial Times|FT]]'s September 2025 [[Pod shop compensation]] piece cited BlueCrest specifically as one of the "trading firms such as Jane Street and Citadel Securities or family offices such as Michael Platt's BlueCrest" that may step in to bid for top trading talent if the multi-manager hedge funds cool. The structural argument: BlueCrest has proprietary capital, no LP scrutiny, no public fee schedule, and Platt's personal wealth as the funding base — meaning the firm can offer partnership-equity-style packages outside the [[Pod shop compensation|standard pod-shop comp template]].

BlueCrest does not publish its talent strategy, but the scale ($50-60bn) and the demonstrated returns mean the firm is structurally capable of competing with [[Millennium]] / [[Citadel]] / [[Point72]] for the same PM profiles, with potentially more attractive structures (partnership upside, no public disclosure, family-office tax efficiency).
