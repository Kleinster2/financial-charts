## Electricity price resilience — Iran conflict (March 2026)

Spain is among the least exposed European economies to the electricity price spike from the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure and gas price doubling. The combination of nuclear baseload, rapid renewables rollout, and favorable weather has kept power costs well below continental peers.

| Metric | Spain | [[Italy]] | [[Germany]] | [[Netherlands]] | [[UK]] |
|--------|-------|-------|---------|-------------|-----|
| Hours gas set electricity price (2026 YTD) | 15% | 89% | ~40% | ~43% | ~66% |
| Avg electricity price forecast (rest of 2026) | ~€66/MWh | ~€132/MWh | — | — | — |
| Nuclear share of electricity (2025) | 20% | 0% | — | — | ~10% |

The mechanism: at any given time, if the preferred energy source (nuclear, solar, wind, hydro) can't supply all demand, gas-fired plants make up the slack and set the price for all generators. Spain's mix of nuclear (20% baseload), solar, wind, and hydro covers enough hours that gas only sets the marginal price 15% of the time. Italy, with no nuclear, relies on gas for 89% of price-setting hours. The UK has >50% renewables+nuclear but still lets gas set the price ~2/3 of the time — intermittent renewables alone don't solve this without nuclear baseload.

A particularly blustery, rainy winter boosted Spanish wind and hydropower output — partly fortuitous. But the underlying infrastructure (rapid solar/wind rollout + 20% nuclear + hydro) is structural.

FT: Spain's average electricity price for the remainder of 2026 is forecast at ~€66/MWh — half Italy's level. The political implication: Spain has less pressure to engage militarily in Hormuz reopening, bolstering its diplomatic independence.

[[Aurora Energy]] (consultancy): UK gas-fired plants set the electricity price ~2/3 of the time despite >50% of power from renewables and nuclear.

*Source: FT, "Spain is a role model in weathering Iranian oil shocks," March 19, 2026*
