**Nikkei 225** — [[Japan]]'s benchmark stock market index, a price-weighted average of 225 large-cap companies listed on the [[Tokyo Stock Exchange]]. The index is the single most referenced barometer of Japanese equity markets globally and carries symbolic weight beyond its analytical utility: its 34-year journey from the 1989 bubble peak to recovery tells the story of Japan's lost decades and subsequent renaissance.

## Synopsis

The Nikkei peaked at 38,957 on December 29, 1989, at the apex of Japan's asset bubble. It then spent 34 years below that level — the longest bear market in modern financial history. The index finally broke above in February 2024 (hitting 39,098), and has since accelerated to 58,000 by February 2026. The rally reflects a confluence of structural catalysts: the [[Japan corporate governance revolution|corporate governance revolution]] (unwinding cross-shareholdings, increasing buybacks), the end of deflation under [[Bank of Japan]] policy normalization, [[Sanae Takaichi]]'s landslide election victory and pro-growth agenda, and the institutional "Japan instead of China" rotation as foreign allocators de-risked from [[China]].

At 58,000, the Nikkei trades at ~16.2x P/E — a fraction of the 60x+ multiple at the 1989 peak — suggesting the rally is earnings-driven rather than speculative. The index is price-weighted (like the Dow), meaning high-priced stocks like [[Fast Retailing]] and [[Tokyo Electron]] have outsized influence, which can distort sector representation versus the broader market-cap-weighted TOPIX.
