# Vitol

World's largest independent energy trader, headquartered in [[Geneva]], [[Switzerland]]. Employee-owned, founded 1966 in [[Rotterdam]]. Trades ~8M bbl/day of crude and products. #7 global bunker supplier (~13M MT physical bunker supply in 2024).

## Funding history

Private; no external funding rounds. Employee-owned since founding — roughly 450 partner-employees hold equity. Capitalization is internal equity plus revolving credit facilities (syndicated across Gulf, European, and Asian banks) — the industry-standard working-capital structure for [[Commodity trading houses]].

## Evolution

- 1966–1989: Formation phase. Founded in [[Rotterdam]] by Viëtor and Detiger trading refined products across Northwest [[Europe]]. Grows alongside the ARA refined-products hub.
- 1990–1999: Internationalization. HQ effectively moves to [[Geneva]]; crude desk matures; Ian Taylor era begins (Taylor becomes CEO 1995, leads through 2018). Physical crude flows globalize.
- 2000–2014: Scale phase. Crude + products volume surpasses 5M bbl/day; African, Middle Eastern, and Asian trading desks build out. Acquires stakes in refineries and terminals. Vitol emerges as the world's largest independent energy trader.
- 2015–2019: Asset-light refinement. Core trading franchise consolidates; Vitol Investment Partners vehicle launched for selected downstream assets. Russell Hardy succeeds Taylor as CEO (2018).
- 2020: Pandemic dislocation. Record $3B profit as oil-price volatility spikes; contango storage plays produce outsized returns.
- 2021–2022: Record volatility. 2022 profit ~$15B — the largest single-year profit ever reported by a commodity trader — driven by Russia-Ukraine dislocation.
- 2023–2024: Normalization + diversification. Net profit settles back to $8B+ range; renewables and [[LNG]] desks grow.
- 2026 Feb–Apr: Iran war response. Two stranded naphtha cargoes destroyed in Gulf on March 12 (one crew fatality); $3B revolving credit facility added for margin-call buffering.

## 2026 [[Iran]] war impact

Vitol was among the hardest-hit traders in the first weeks of the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] because of its outsized Middle East oil book. Losses arose through two channels described by [[Oliver Wyman]]'s head of risk and trading Alexander Franke in the firm's 2026 commodity trading report:

1. Short futures positions held as hedges against physical cargoes were crushed when [[Brent crude]] spiked from ~$70 to $100+ within three weeks of the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure. The shorts were structurally correct (they hedge underlying physical cargo ownership), but the intervening mark-to-market losses were substantial and triggered margin calls.
2. Pre-sold cargoes caught inside the closed strait had to be replaced at elevated prices — Vitol had already agreed to deliver those barrels at pre-war prices but could not load them.

Per FT reporting on March 12, 2026, two vessels carrying Vitol naphtha cargoes were stranded inside the Gulf and set on fire in Iranian retaliatory attacks, killing one crew member. Vitol's Middle East-heavy physical book meant exposure beyond any single attacked cargo: the complex of stranded tanker weeks, insurance-cost escalation (Gulf war-risk premiums up more than sixfold), and margin-call liquidity drain compounded into industry-level "billions" in early-war losses before positions began to reverse in April.

### $3B additional credit facility (March 2026)

In early March 2026, Vitol secured an additional $3B revolving credit facility alongside parallel arrangements at [[Trafigura]] ($3B) and [[Gunvor]] ($1.5B). The facilities were explicitly framed as liquidity buffers for margin calls rather than evidence of distress — commodity price volatility required more cash posted to exchanges even when the underlying physical-trade P&L was sound. See [[Commodity trading houses]] for the working-capital mechanics.
