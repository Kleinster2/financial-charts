# AeroVironment

**AeroVironment** (AVAV) — the dominant US maker of small tactical drones and loitering munitions, now transformed by the $4.1B [[BlueHalo]] acquisition (May 2025) into a multi-domain defense platform spanning air, space, cyber, and directed energy. FY2025 standalone revenue hit a record $820.6M (+14%), with LMS (Switchblade) the explosive growth engine (+87% YoY). Post-BlueHalo, FY2026 guidance is $1.95-2.0B revenue and $300-320M adjusted EBITDA. Funded backlog reached $1.1B as of November 2025, with $1.4B in bookings in Q2 FY2026 alone. The bull case is straightforward: [[Ukraine war]]-validated products, $990M Army Switchblade IDIQ, $874M FMS IDIQ, [[Golden Dome]] positioning, and NATO rearmament creating a secular demand wave for exactly what AV makes. The bear case crystallized on March 2, 2026: the [[US Space Force]] reopened the $1.4B SCAR contract (BlueHalo's crown jewel) for competitive bidding, triggering a triple-downgrade from Raymond James and a 17.4% single-day stock crash. SCAR represented ~50% of combined backlog — single-program concentration risk that the market had been ignoring until it couldn't.

## Business segments

Post-BlueHalo, AV operates in two reporting segments:

### Autonomous Systems (legacy AeroVironment)

Contains three operating units:

UxS (Uncrewed Systems) — small and medium tactical UAS for ISR. The bread-and-butter business. Products: Puma 3, Raven, Wasp, JUMP 20, P550, Quantix Recon.

LMS (Loitering Munitions Systems) — precision strike. Switchblade 300, Switchblade 400 (new), Switchblade 600 Block 2, Red Dragon. The growth engine — FY2025 segment revenue grew 87% YoY.

MW (MacCready Works) — R&D and advanced programs. Named after founder [[Paul MacCready]]. Grew 24% YoY in FY2025.

### Space, Cyber and Directed Energy (legacy BlueHalo)

Brought in via the May 2025 acquisition. Capabilities: space ground systems (BADGER phased-array antennas), laser weapons (LOCUST LWS), RF counter-UAS (Titan family), electronic warfare, cyber, satellite communications. BlueHalo had ~$900M revenue in 2024 and $600M funded backlog pre-acquisition. 260+ systems flying on-orbit.

## Evolution

The story of AeroVironment is the story of a visionary engineer's ultralight aircraft company becoming, through four distinct pivots — human-powered flight to solar vehicles to military micro-drones to loitering munitions — the arsenal of the drone age.

- 1971: [[Paul MacCready]], a Caltech aerodynamicist and competitive glider pilot, founds AeroVironment in Pasadena, California to work on wind energy and atmospheric science. MacCready had won the first World Soaring Championship in 1956 and held multiple altitude records. The company begins as a small engineering consultancy applying aerodynamic principles to environmental problems — wind engineering for buildings, wind farm design, pollution dispersion modeling. Revenue stays small; MacCready is chasing ideas, not scale

- 1977-1979 (the Kremer Prizes): The breakthrough that puts AeroVironment on the map. MacCready's Gossamer Condor completes the first sustained, controlled human-powered flight on August 23, 1977, winning the $95,000 Kremer Prize that had gone unclaimed for 18 years. The aircraft has 96-foot wings of aluminum tubing, corrugated cardboard, and balsa wood, covered in Mylar — weighing just 70 lbs. In 1979, the Gossamer Albatross wins the second Kremer Prize by flying across the English Channel on pedal power. These are engineering spectacles, not businesses — but they establish MacCready's core insight: radical weight reduction and unconventional materials can achieve what brute-force engineering cannot. This philosophy becomes the DNA of every AeroVironment product that follows

- 1980s (solar vehicles): MacCready pivots to solar-powered flight. The Gossamer Penguin and Solar Challenger demonstrate photovoltaic-powered aircraft. Then the work shifts to ground vehicles: AeroVironment builds the GM Sunraycer, which wins the 1,867-mile World Solar Challenge across Australia in 1987 at an average 41.6 mph. GM is impressed enough to commission AeroVironment to build the GM Impact, a high-performance battery-powered car — the direct precursor to the [[General Motors]] EV1, one of the first modern production electric vehicles. AeroVironment is decades ahead of the EV wave but has no way to monetize it. The solar and EV work generates press and prestige but not recurring revenue

- Late 1980s-1990s (the military pivot): MacCready's ultralight design philosophy — proven by the Gossamer Condor's radical weight-to-performance ratio — turns out to be perfectly suited for small, battery-powered military reconnaissance drones. AeroVironment develops the Pointer, a hand-launched tactical UAS, and then the Raven, which becomes the most widely deployed small drone in US military history. The insight: infantry units need persistent ISR that fits in a backpack, launches from a hand throw, and costs a fraction of a Predator. The company carves out a monopoly position in Group 1 tactical UAS for the DoD. The Raven is followed by the Puma (larger, longer endurance) and Wasp (micro). MacCready dies in 2007 at age 81, but the company he built has already made the transition from engineering curiosity to defense contractor

- 2001-2010 (post-9/11 scaling): The wars in Afghanistan and Iraq create massive demand for exactly what AeroVironment makes. Every infantry platoon wants eyes in the sky. Raven becomes standard issue across the US Army and Marine Corps, with tens of thousands produced. AeroVironment goes public on January 23, 2007 on NASDAQ at ~$18/share, raising approximately $113 million. Revenue is ~$250M and growing. The company is profitable but small — a niche player in the larger defense ecosystem dominated by [[Lockheed Martin]], [[Northrop Grumman]], and [[General Atomics]]

- 2011-2019 (Switchblade development, pre-Ukraine): AeroVironment develops the Switchblade — a tube-launched, man-portable loitering munition that gives a single soldier precision strike capability. The Switchblade 300 (anti-personnel) enters production first; the Switchblade 600 (anti-armor) follows. This is the second major pivot: from ISR-only platforms to weapons systems. Revenue plateaus in the $300-370M range as the drawdown from Afghanistan reduces demand. The stock trades sideways. Investors see a one-trick pony tied to ground-war budgets

- 2021 (Arcturus acquisition): AeroVironment acquires Arcturus UAV for $405M ($355M cash + $50M stock), expanding into Group 2-3 medium UAS. The prize asset is the JUMP 20, a VTOL fixed-wing platform with 14-hour endurance competing for the Army's billion-dollar FTUAS program. The deal fills a portfolio gap — AV had nothing between backpack drones and the Switchblade. Revenue for FY2021: $394.9M (+8% YoY). The Army selects JUMP 20 for FTUAS Increment 1 interim capability, but AeroVironment is later eliminated from the FTUAS design phase in 2023, with contracts going to [[Griffon Aerospace]], [[Northrop Grumman]], [[Sierra Nevada Corporation]], and [[Textron Systems]]. A significant competitive setback, though JUMP 20 continues fielding internationally

- 2022-2023 (Ukraine validation): The [[Ukraine war]] transforms AeroVironment's trajectory. The US sends 700+ Switchblade 300s to Ukraine as part of an $800M military aid package. Results are mixed initially — learning curve, Russian EW/jamming — but the concept is validated. Global demand for loitering munitions explodes. Switchblade becomes the reference platform. FY2023 revenue hits $540.5M (+21%), funded backlog doubles to $424.1M. But there's a $156M goodwill impairment charge (likely Arcturus-related given the FTUAS elimination), producing a $160.5M net loss for Q4 FY2023

- 2024 (the breakout year): FY2024 revenue surges to $717M (+33%), driven by LMS. The Army awards the massive $990M five-year IDIQ for Switchblade 300 and 600 systems in August 2024. [[Wahid Nawabi]], CEO since 2016, has overseen an eightfold revenue increase from the ~$100M level when he took over (though the company was closer to $260M in FY2016). Stock rises from ~$100 to ~$200+ range

- 2024-2025 (BlueHalo acquisition): In November 2024, AeroVironment announces an all-stock deal to acquire [[BlueHalo]] for $4.1B enterprise value, issuing ~18.5M shares (AV shareholders retain 60.5%, BlueHalo holders get 39.5%). BlueHalo brings space (BADGER ground stations, 260+ on-orbit systems), directed energy (LOCUST laser), counter-UAS (Titan, CORIAN), cyber, and EW capabilities. Revenue combination: $820.6M (AV FY2025) + ~$900M (BlueHalo) = ~$1.7B+ pro forma. The deal closes May 1, 2025. AV rebrands, drops "AeroVironment" from marketing in favor of just "AV." New post-merger operating segments: Autonomous Systems and Space, Cyber and Directed Energy. FY2025 standalone: $820.6M revenue, $146.4M adjusted EBITDA, $43.6M net income, $726.6M backlog, $1.2B in bookings

- 2025-2026 (the combined company): First combined quarter (Q1 FY2026, ended Aug 2, 2025) shows the scale jump. Q2 FY2026 revenue: $472.5M (+151% YoY, with BlueHalo contributing $245.1M). But GAAP net loss of $(17.1)M due to acquisition-related amortization and integration costs. Bookings hit a record $1.4B in Q2 alone. Key contract wins stack up: $874M FMS IDIQ (Dec 2025), $186M Army Switchblade delivery order (Feb 2026), $13.2M P550 LRR contract, partnership with [[Sierra Nevada Corporation]] for [[Golden Dome]]. Production ramps: Switchblade 600 from 40 to 240 units/month, targeting 1,200/month. New Salt Lake City factory under construction. Kevin McDonnell (CFO since 2020) announces retirement effective July 2026

- March 2, 2026 (the SCAR crisis): The stock surges +20.1% premarket on [[Operation Epic Fury]] (US-Israel strikes on Iran) defense sentiment. Then mid-morning: Space News reports the [[US Space Force]] has formally reopened the SCAR contract (Satellite Communications Augmentation Resource, $1.4B) for competitive bidding — BlueHalo's largest program of record, ~50% of combined backlog. Raymond James analyst Brian Gesuale issues a triple-downgrade (Strong Buy to Underperform, bypassing two levels). The stock reverses ~40% from peak to trough, closing at $208.32 (-17.4%). The SCAR recompetition crystallizes the bear case: the BlueHalo acquisition concentrated enormous backlog risk in a single contract that AV may not retain

## Golden Dome role

AV has positioned itself as a foundational layer of the [[Golden Dome]] missile defense architecture through two key moves:

1. SNC partnership (Aug 2025): Strategic partnership with [[Sierra Nevada Corporation]] to build the next-gen "Limited Area Defense" architecture — integrating passive/active sensing, RF, directed energy, kinetic, EW, and cyber to counter Group 1-4 UAS, advanced cruise missiles, and next-gen aerial threats

2. Grand Forks deployment (Oct 2025): Deploying inner-layer distributed C-UAS capabilities at Grand Forks AFB in North Dakota — the backbone of a layered defense architecture. Uses Titan and Titan-SV systems with AV Halo Command software for fused sensor data and single operating picture. Part of Project ULTRA, a $110M public-private initiative to make Grand Forks the national proving ground for counter-drone defense

AV's pitch: it provides the inner layer (close-in C-UAS) of Golden Dome, while larger primes handle outer layers (ballistic/hypersonic intercept). The counter-UAS portfolio (Titan, CORIAN, LOCUST) maps directly to the threat set Golden Dome is designed to address.

## SCAR contract recompetition (March 2026)

In January 2026, the Space Force issued a stop-work order on BlueHalo's BADGER unit deliveries — initially treated as routine. On March 2, 2026, Space News reported the Space Force had officially reopened SCAR for competitive bidding. On March 10, 2026, AV confirmed in Q3 earnings that the Space Force formally terminated the SCAR contract for convenience — paying allowable costs with fee but ending the program of record. AV took a $151M non-cash goodwill impairment on the Space, Cyber & DE segment. Management plans to recompete under revised requirements and commercialize BADGER as a commercial item. Potential recompetition competitors include [[Northrop Grumman]] and [[L3Harris]].

The timing was devastating — March 2 was also the first trading day after [[Operation Epic Fury|joint US-Israeli strikes on Iran]], and AVAV had surged +20.1% in premarket on defense sentiment. The SCAR recompetition news hit mid-morning, triggering a ~40% intraday swing from peak to trough. AVAV closed at $208.32, down 17.4% from Friday's $252.25.

Raymond James analyst Brian Gesuale issued a rare triple-downgrade — from Strong Buy directly to Underperform, bypassing two rating levels. Prior $348 price target removed. Gesuale cited SCAR recompetition risk and longer defense procurement sales cycles.

BTIG maintained Buy with a $415 target. ~85% of analysts still rate AVAV as Buy (FactSet).

## Investment case

Bull:
- Ukraine validated loitering munitions as essential infantry weapons — Switchblade is the reference platform
- $990M Army Switchblade IDIQ + $874M FMS IDIQ = multi-year revenue visibility
- Production scaling: 40 to 240 to 1,200 Switchblades/month — operating leverage at scale
- [[Golden Dome]] and NATO rearmament as secular C-UAS/drone defense tailwinds
- BlueHalo adds directed energy (LOCUST), space (BADGER), and electronic warfare — diversification beyond ISR/strike
- 50+ allied governments as customer base, expanding via FMS
- New products filling gaps: P550 (Group 2 recon), Switchblade 400 (medium anti-armor), Red Dragon (autonomous, GPS-denied, 400 km)
- Post-selloff at $208 vs. $370 median target — market overreacting to one contract risk
- [[Iran]] escalation creates immediate demand catalyst for counter-UAS and loitering munitions

Bear:
- SCAR recompetition — $1.4B (~50% of backlog at acquisition) at risk of loss to [[Northrop Grumman]] or [[L3Harris]]
- Single-program concentration risk from BlueHalo deal — the acquisition created the vulnerability
- GAAP profitability impaired by acquisition amortization ($17.1M net loss in Q2 FY2026)
- JUMP 20 eliminated from FTUAS design phase — lost the medium UAS franchise for the Army
- Ukraine demand may normalize as war stabilizes or ends
- Competition intensifying: [[Anduril]], [[Shield AI]], [[Skydio]] all well-funded, with AI-native architectures
- Cheap FPV drones (Ukrainian model) may commoditize the loitering munition category at the low end
- Defense procurement timelines lengthening — Raymond James cited this in the downgrade
- Valuation still not cheap even post-crash (~55x trailing P/E)
- CFO retirement during integration adds execution risk
