## Automotive industry crisis (Mar 2026)

The auto sector is the largest share of SA manufacturing and rivals mining in GDP contribution. Two-thirds of SA-made cars are exported. But the industry is buckling under cheaper imports from [[India]] and [[China]], with companies, unions, and investors aligned in calling for urgent policy change.

### Import surge

| Metric | Then | Now |
|--------|------|-----|
| Top-10 sellers SA-built | 9 of 10 (decade ago) | Fewer than 1 in 3 |
| [[China]] car import share (volume) | 21% (2024) | 27% (2025) |
| [[China]] truck import share | ~30% (2024) | ~50% (2025) |
| Imported car prices | — | -4.5% (2025) |

[[Suzuki Motor]] (importing from [[India]] via [[Maruti Suzuki]]) is now the #2 car seller in [[South Africa]], after [[Toyota]]. [[Chery]] entered the market in 2021 and by 2025 displaced [[Volkswagen]] as the country's #3 automaker. [[BYD]] also expanding with showrooms in the Eastern Cape.

### Cost disadvantage

SA labor costs are 2x [[India]]'s. [[Maruti Suzuki]]'s Indian facilities produce 4x more vehicles per year than SA's entire industry combined. Infrastructure compounds the gap: energy is expensive despite post-load-shedding stabilization, municipal water and electricity networks are crumbling, and state-owned [[Transnet]] port/rail logistics add cost. [[Ford]] SA CEO Neale Hill: costs $37 more per vehicle to use rail than trucking from its Silverton plant to the nearest export terminal (600 km away).

### Factory closures and job losses

| Company | Action | Detail |
|---------|--------|--------|
| [[Volkswagen]] | "Make-or-break year" | In SA since 1951, ~4,000 employees. Deciding on major investment for new light pick-up truck. VW Group Africa chair Martina Biene: current environment doesn't justify it |
| [[Ford]] | Cut 470 jobs | Silverton (Pretoria) and Nelson Mandela Bay plants. Ranger pick-up, 200,000 units/year capacity |

### Nelson Mandela Bay — "Motor City"

The coastal city accounts for 40% of SA's automotive jobs. The municipality lost 41,000 jobs in 2024; unemployment jumped from <22% to >28% (Q4 YoY). Gang violence, drugs, and the highest murder rate in SA compound the crisis. Denise van Huyssteen, head of the local business lobby (and former GM employee when it exited): *"We're at a tipping point now. If we don't move with speed and action, we'll go down the same road"* — referring to Detroit's municipal bankruptcy.

NUMSA provincial secretary Mziyanda Twani: without drastic action, *"this area is soon going to be defined as a ghost town."*

### Policy demands

Industry CEOs, unions, and investors want:
- Better governance at [[Transnet]] and faster private investment in port, rail, electricity
- Reforms to the Automotive Production and Development Programme (APDP) voucher system
- Tax changes on new car purchases
- End to state support for semi knock-down (SKD) assembly operations (which employ far fewer workers than full manufacturing)
- Black Economic Empowerment regulations applied to importers (currently only manufacturers)
- Tariff increase — Donald Mackay (XA Global Trade Advisors founder): max 30%, *"anything which goes beyond that has massive effects on the price of cars"*

UCT emeritus professor Anthony Black: *"Some of their requests have some validity. The growth in imports is a problem."*

### Political constraints

Tariff increases are politically difficult. SA is growing trade ties with [[China]] and [[India]] as the [[Donald Trump]] administration takes a hostile stance, falsely accusing the GNU government of perpetuating a "genocide" against white citizens. Tariffs would also add inflationary pressure (Bloomberg Economics). DTIC Director: Automotive Frank Stevens confirmed the government is "reviewing the masterplan" — the SA Automotive Masterplan running until 2035.

### Long-term: [[AfCFTA]]

Progress on the pan-African free trade agreement could open regional markets, but implementation is slow.

*Source: Bloomberg (Alexander Parker, Matthew Hill), March 18, 2026*
