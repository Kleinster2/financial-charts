**Carl Icahn** — Corporate raider turned activist investor. Built a $20B+ fortune through hostile takeovers, greenmail, and concentrated bets on undervalued or poorly managed companies. Vehicle: [[Icahn Enterprises]] (IEP, [[Nasdaq]]). Net worth collapsed from peak after [[Hindenburg Research]] short report in May 2023 exposed leverage and dividend sustainability problems. Age 89, still active.

---

## Synopsis

Carl Icahn invented the modern corporate raider playbook in the 1980s — buy a large enough stake, threaten a hostile takeover, extract value through restructuring, greenmail, or asset sales. TWA, Texaco, RJR Nabisco, Phillips Petroleum, USX. The 1980s raids were brutal, often stripping companies for parts, and earned Icahn a reputation as Wall Street's most feared predator.

He evolved into an activist investor in the 2000s-2010s, running campaigns at [[Apple]] ($2B+ profit on buyback push), [[eBay]] (forced [[PayPal]] spinoff), [[Netflix]] (2x in 7 months), and dozens of others. The approach softened — less asset-stripping, more shareholder value — but the aggression didn't. He maintained positions in 15-20 companies at a time through Icahn Enterprises, a publicly traded holding company that gave retail investors exposure to his portfolio.

The [[Ackman-Icahn Herbalife feud]] (2013-2018) was the most public hedge fund battle in history — Icahn took the opposite side of [[Bill Ackman]]'s $1B short, built a 16.5% long position, and made ~$1.3B while Ackman lost ~$1B. The personal animosity traced back a decade to the Hallwood Realty "schmuck insurance" dispute (see below).

The Hindenburg short report in May 2023 was devastating. [[Hindenburg Research]] alleged IEP was trading at a 218% premium to NAV, funded by a Ponzi-like dividend structure (paying distributions from new capital, not earnings). IEP stock fell ~50% in weeks. Icahn cut the dividend 50% and later pledged personal shares as collateral after margin calls. Net worth fell from $20B+ to ~$5B. The SEC opened an investigation and in December 2023 charged Icahn with failing to disclose $5B+ in personal margin loans against IEP shares over 12 years. He settled for $2M without admitting wrongdoing.

## Career

### Wall Street origins (1961-1978)

Started as a stockbroker at Dreyfus & Co. in 1961 after dropping out of NYU medical school. Moved to options trading at Tessel, Patrick & Co. In 1968, bought a seat on the NYSE and founded Icahn & Co., focused on options arbitrage and risk arbitrage. Built capital through the 1970s trading undervalued closed-end funds — buying at a discount to NAV, then agitating to liquidate.

### Corporate raider era (1978-1990s)

The Tappan Company (1979) was the template — Icahn bought a controlling stake, forced a sale to Electrolux at a premium. Then escalated:

| Target | Year | Outcome |
|--------|------|---------|
| ACF Industries | 1983 | Took control, took private |
| Phillips Petroleum | 1985 | Greenmail — paid to go away |
| TWA | 1985 | Full takeover, stripped assets, airline bankrupted twice |
| Texaco | 1988 | ~$1.5B profit on distressed debt play during Pennzoil lawsuit |
| RJR Nabisco | 1988 | Lost bidding war to KKR |
| USX (US Steel) | 1990 | Forced separation of steel and Marathon Oil units |

TWA was the defining deal of the era. Icahn took over the airline in 1985, sold London routes to [[American Airlines]] for $445M, loaded the company with debt, and extracted ~$469M in personal gains through asset sales and fees. TWA filed for bankruptcy in 1992 and again in 1995. Employees and creditors were devastated. The deal remains a textbook case of value extraction at stakeholder expense.

### Activist evolution (2000s-2010s)

Shifted from hostile takeovers to public activist campaigns — still aggressive, but working through proxy fights and public pressure rather than outright acquisitions:

| Target | Years | Outcome |
|--------|-------|---------|
| [[Apple]] | 2013-2016 | Pushed for $150B buyback, made $2B+ |
| [[eBay]] | 2014 | Forced [[PayPal]] spinoff |
| [[Netflix]] | 2012-2015 | ~2x return in 7 months (initial trade) |
| [[Dell]] | 2013 | Opposed Michael Dell's $24.4B buyout (lost) |
| Herbalife | 2013-2018 | ~$1.3B profit, beat [[Bill Ackman]]'s short |
| Xerox | 2020 | Installed new board, blocked HP merger |

The [[Apple]] campaign was his largest and most profitable late-career trade. Bought ~$3.6B in shares starting August 2013, publicly demanded $150B in buybacks, tweeted about dinner with Tim Cook. Apple did accelerate buybacks. Exited April 2016 on China growth concerns. Profit: $2B+.

## Hallwood Realty and the Ackman feud

In March 2003, [[Bill Ackman]]'s collapsing Gotham Partners fund sold its Hallwood Realty stake to Icahn. The deal included "schmuck insurance" — Ackman would receive 50% of any profits above a 10% annual return if Icahn resold within 3 years.

Hallwood was acquired 13 months later at $136/share (vs. $80 sale price). Icahn refused to pay, reportedly telling Ackman: "I don't pay schmuck insurance." Ackman sued. Courts sided with Ackman in 2005; Icahn paid ~$9M in 2011 after years of appeals.

This was the origin of the personal animosity that exploded into the [[Ackman-Icahn Herbalife feud]] a decade later.

## Icahn Enterprises (IEP)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Ticker | IEP ([[Nasdaq]]) |
| Structure | Diversified holding company (MLP) |
| Sectors | Energy, automotive, food packaging, real estate, pharma |
| Pre-Hindenburg price | ~$52 |
| Post-Hindenburg low | ~$17 |
| Dividend | Cut 50% (Aug 2023), cut again (2024) |

IEP is structured as a master limited partnership — Icahn personally controls the general partner and owns ~85% of units. The structure gives him effective control with minimal outside accountability.

### Hindenburg short (May 2023)

[[Hindenburg Research]] published "Carl Icahn: The Corporate Raider Losing His Edge" on May 2, 2023, alleging:
- IEP traded at a 218% premium to indicative NAV
- The dividend ($2/unit quarterly) was funded by selling new units — a Ponzi-like structure
- Icahn had pledged ~60% of his IEP units as loan collateral (undisclosed margin loans)
- Net investment returns had been negative for years

IEP stock fell ~50% in the weeks following. Icahn cut the quarterly dividend from $2 to $1 in August 2023, then cut again in 2024. The SEC investigated and in December 2023 charged Icahn and IEP with failing to disclose over $5B in personal margin loans against IEP units over 12 years. Icahn settled for $2M without admitting wrongdoing.

Net worth fell from $20B+ to ~$5B. Indian Creek Island compound reportedly also pledged as collateral.

## Investment style

Icahn has no investment team in the traditional sense — no analysts, no investment committee. He makes decisions personally, often on instinct. Concentrations are extreme: single positions have been 20-30% of IEP's portfolio. The style is confrontational by design — Icahn wants management to know he will make their lives difficult if they don't create shareholder value.

Key traits: hostile posture (default to confrontation), heavy leverage (margin loans, IEP debt), public pressure (CNBC, Twitter, open letters), and a willingness to hold indefinitely or exit immediately depending on the trade.
