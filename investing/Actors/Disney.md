## Iger era (2005-2026): scale and strategy

[[Bob Iger]] set three priorities on arrival in 2005 — quality content, technology, and theme parks — and ran roughly $90bn of acquisitions plus close to $100bn committed to the parks business. Revenues tripled; from 2005 to 2020 the share price increased fivefold, outpacing the S&P 500. [[Blair Effron]] of [[Centerview Partners]] recalls pre-Iger Disney as "basically irrelevant."

The four megadeals — [[Pixar]] ($7B, 2006), [[Marvel Entertainment]] ($4B, 2009), [[Lucasfilm]] ($4B, 2012), [[21st Century Fox]] ($71B in 2019, ~$54B net after disposals including the Sky stake) — each added content, technology, and ride/merchandise IP. Iger won over [[Steve Jobs]], [[George Lucas]], [[Ike Perlmutter]], and [[Rupert Murdoch]] in the process.

---

## Deals that did not happen

Iger told the FT he nearly bought [[Twitter]] from [[Jack Dorsey]] "at a very attractive price" as a distribution platform but pulled out fearing "a horrible distraction." Exploratory talks about merging Disney with [[Apple]] — "truly transformational and equal," in Iger's words — went nowhere because "Apple didn't show that much interest." [[James Bond]] was on the acquisition target list but escaped, later landing at [[Amazon]].

---

## 2022 return and restructuring

Iger's November 2022 return (after "10 months and three weeks" away) was triggered by chair [[Susan Arnold]]'s Friday call. He calls the crisis he returned to "significantly" worse than the one he inherited in 2005 — Effron said Disney had "lost its culture" and needed "emergency room fixing." Iger reversed [[Bob Chapek]]'s changes and launched a $6bn cost-cutting plan that cut 7,000 jobs.

---

## 2025-26 box office and the sequel question

Disney topped the 2025 global studio rankings with $6.58bn at the box office — still well below pre-Covid levels. The 2026 slate is sequel-heavy: The Devil Wears Prada 2, Toy Story 5, Avengers: Doomsday. Sequel fatigue is a live concern; The Mandalorian and Grogu spin-offs have struggled at the cinema, and critics are brutal on the wider franchise's prospects.

---

## Profit mix and streaming margins

Parks now make up about 60 per cent of Disney's profits — historically studios, TV, and parks each contributed roughly a third. Streaming has finally turned profitable, but margins remain far below [[Netflix]]. Iger concedes Disney needs to lift streaming profitability to rebalance, and admits he was "not more successful in selling [Disney] to the street," attributing that to the conglomerate's complexity for investors. He signed off [[Josh D'Amaro]]'s $60bn parks investment plan in 2023; four new cruise ships are being built.

---

## Peltz proxy fight (2024)

[[Nelson Peltz]]'s [[Trian Partners]] disclosed a significant Disney stake in 2023 and attacked the company for having "lost its way," criticizing sub-[[Netflix]] streaming margins and complaining Disney had gone "woke" ("Why do I need an all-Black cast?"). [[Ike Perlmutter]], a shareholder since selling Marvel, backed the push. Iger met nearly two dozen large investors and defeated the challenge in an April 2024 shareholder vote. Peltz has not halted his criticism, telling a conference Iger pushed for D'Amaro's appointment as "a reason to stay on," implying continued behind-the-scenes influence.

---

## Trump, Kimmel, and the FCC (2025-26)

Disney's ownership of [[ABC]] has made it a political target. After [[Jimmy Kimmel]] joked about the shooting of rightwing figure [[Charlie Kirk]], Disney took Kimmel off the air and asked him to apologize — hours after an FCC threat; Iger said the move was about taste, not politics. When Kimmel later joked about "Lady Trump," [[Donald Trump]] demanded ABC fire him and the FCC ordered Disney into an early ABC licence-renewal process. Disney stood firm this time; Iger said he "wholeheartedly" endorsed the stance. The episode is an early test of [[Josh D'Amaro]]'s handling of political pressure on Disney's news and broadcast assets.

## Big Tech M&A speculation (FT Lex, Feb 8)

FT Lex framed D'Amaro succession as opening the door to a potential sale to Big Tech — where Iger expanded through acquisitions (Pixar, Marvel, Lucasfilm, Fox), D'Amaro could end up selling.

Candidate logic:
- [[Alphabet]]: 2026 planned capex alone ≈ Disney's entire market cap (~$200B). Currently spending on data centers but developing real-world AI applications — movie/TV production a logical fit. [[Google]] Project Genie "world model" for video game builders signals AI encroachment on traditional studios.
- [[Meta]]: similar financial firepower, same AI application ambitions.
- [[Apple]]: fantasy deal that has circulated for years. Having sat out the AI infrastructure boom, Apple has capital to spare and a strategic reason to zag. Would bolster Apple TV+ with Disney's customers and content. Iger confirmed to the FT (Jun 2026) that exploratory merger talks had occurred but "Apple didn't show that much interest."

Obstacles: [[Netflix]] at $340B market cap is 80% larger than Disney but would still need an enormous premium. Hollywood's most storied asset passing to a tech company would face shareholder resistance.

Near-term D'Amaro moves more likely include spinning off declining cable TV assets — rivals have done similar deals.

HundredX data: households score Disney+ higher than [[Warner Bros Discovery|WBD]]'s HBO Max on original content, new releases, and quantity of programming.
