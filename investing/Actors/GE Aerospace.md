## Q1 2026 earnings — big beat, guidance unchanged, Middle East drags departures outlook (Apr 22, 2026)

The quarter was objectively excellent — revenue $12.39B (+29% YoY) blew past the $10.71B consensus by 16%, adjusted EPS of $1.86 versus $1.60 estimate (+16%), orders up 87% YoY to $23B. Yet the stock fell 5% because management held FY2026 guidance flat "given the dynamic macro environment" and explicitly reduced the full-year departures outlook on Middle East conflict drag.

| Metric | Q1 2026 | Q1 2025 | Growth / vs consensus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $12.39B | $9.61B | +29% YoY; beat by 16% |
| Adjusted EPS | $1.86 | $1.49 | +25% YoY; beat by $0.26 |
| Orders | $23B | $12.3B | +87% YoY (record defense orders) |
| Operating margin | — | — | Declined YoY — the key qualifier |

The Middle East departures cut is the core disclosure. Management lowered the full-year departures outlook from mid-single-digit growth to flat-to-low-single-digit growth, explicitly citing the [[Operation Epic Fury|Iran war]] and [[Iran conflict airline disruption|airline disruption]] dynamics. Departures drive aftermarket service revenue, which is the structural profit engine — LEAP engines and CFM56 shop visits are where GE Aerospace makes most of its margin. A 3-5 point departures cut flows through to services revenue with a lag, which is why the market treated the otherwise-great quarter as a downgrade.

What the market wanted vs got:

| Expectation | Reality |
|---|---|
| Guidance raise on Q1 beat | Guidance maintained ("dynamic macro environment") |
| Departures growth +mid-single-digit | Flat to low-single-digit |
| Clean operating margin expansion | Margins declined YoY |
| Services mix stable | Middle East drag beginning to flow through |

The split with [[GE Vernova]]. The Apr 22 print created a rare GE-family earnings day divergence: GEV +13.7% (guidance raise on power) versus GE -3.6% (guidance held, Middle East headwind). The story is clean — GE Vernova is a pure-play AI-power infrastructure trade, while GE Aerospace is exposed to an Iran-war demand channel (Middle East departures) that GE Vernova is not. The split is the market's way of saying the two businesses were better off separated than as a conglomerate: the durations are different, the end-markets are different, and the macro exposures are now priced separately.

Record defense orders is the most underappreciated positive — T901 engine progress for the Army and broader international defense demand remain strong tailwinds. If commercial departures remain constrained in 2026, defense becomes a bigger share of the growth mix.

Read-across. [[Boeing]] (same day, +5.2%) is the positive counter — 737 MAX ramp to 47/mo in summer 2026 means more engines shipped on new aircraft. [[RTX]] and [[Honeywell]] (both reporting in Q2) will now face pressure to disclose Middle East departure exposure separately, as the market has now priced this as a distinct risk factor.
