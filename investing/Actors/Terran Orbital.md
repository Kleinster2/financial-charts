**Terran Orbital** -- small-satellite manufacturer acquired by [[Lockheed Martin]] in October 2024. It designs and produces modular spacecraft, satellite buses, mission services, and related subsystems for aerospace, defense, civil, and commercial customers.

## Why it matters

Terran Orbital is a supply-chain node for proliferated satellite architectures. Its value is less a standalone public-equity story now that Lockheed owns it, and more a manufacturing capability inside the US defense-space stack: bus production, spacecraft platforms, launch planning, mission operations, and in-orbit support.

Lockheed's acquisition release emphasized Terran's modular spacecraft platforms, interchangeable components, automation/robotics in manufacturing, and advanced testing. That makes the company a useful reference point for the industrialization of small and medium satellites, especially programs where defense customers want faster refresh cycles than legacy bespoke spacecraft procurement.

---

## Lockheed acquisition

[[Lockheed Martin]] completed the acquisition on October 30, 2024, after announcing the deal in August 2024. Lockheed said Terran would report through Lockheed Martin Space, remain recognized as "Terran Orbital, a Lockheed Martin Company," and continue as a merchant supplier to the broader industry.

The deal followed years of Lockheed-Terran collaboration on [[Space Development Agency]] programs and technology demonstrations. Terran had supplied satellite buses for multiple SDA tranches through Lockheed, making the acquisition partly a vertical-integration move around proliferated LEO defense constellations.

*Source: Lockheed Martin acquisition completion release, October 30 2024.*

## Pax Silica supplier-screening exposure (May 2026)

The May 28, 2026 [[Journal of Space Commerce]] / Ex Terra Media excerpt names companies in the Terran Orbital supply network as a concrete reference point for [[Pax Silica]] qualification risk. The issue is not Terran's parentage alone; it is whether sub-tier suppliers for satellite components can document non-China redundancy before US-aligned procurement teams treat trusted-vendor compliance as a de facto award criterion.

This matters for Terran because small-satellite buses are aggregation points for RF systems, printed circuit board assemblies, power electronics, electro-optical payload interfaces, precision structures, and rare-earth magnet-dependent actuators/reaction wheels. A single China-anchored sub-tier supplier can become a qualification problem even when the prime or parent company is US-aligned.
