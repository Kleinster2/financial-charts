# Debevoise

## Synopsis

[[Debevoise]] is one of the elite-NY corporate law firms historically anchored to lockstep partner compensation, alongside [[Wachtell Lipton]], [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]], [[Sullivan & Cromwell]], and [[Davis Polk]]. The firm was founded in 1931 in New York by Eli Whitney Debevoise; the name has carried "& Plimpton" since the 1981 merger that absorbed the Plimpton, Lieberman & O'Connell partnership. As of FY 2025 the firm reported revenue of approximately $1.6B (+9% YoY) and profits per equity partner of approximately $6.0M (+12.5% YoY) per the April 2026 Am Law 100 release. Debevoise has 982 attorneys per current Am Law 200 data and ranked #33 by gross revenue in the latest cycle.

The investing-vault relevance is the [[Big Law transformation]] thread. Debevoise was widely identified as the second-most-pure lockstep US firm behind [[Wachtell Lipton]] until June 2025, when the firm announced a non-equity partnership tier (effective July 1, 2025) while reaffirming lockstep for equity partners — the same dual-structure compensation framework that [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore|Cravath]] adopted between December 2021 and November 2023. The June 2025 announcement removes Debevoise from the pure-lockstep cohort but distinguishes it from the [[Kirkland & Ellis]] / [[Latham & Watkins]] / [[Paul, Weiss]] scale-plus-pay model. With the modification, [[Wachtell Lipton]] becomes the only US firm of comparable scale operating a pure-lockstep equity partnership.

## June 2025 — non-equity partner tier added

On June 9, 2025 (effective July 1, 2025), Debevoise announced the addition of a non-equity partnership tier alongside the firm's continued lockstep model for equity partners. Per Above the Law and Bloomberg Law coverage:

- The non-equity tier is positioned as "a relatively small percentage of the overall partnership," reserved for "attorneys who have shown excellence in their legal acumen and also show potential for further development."
- The partnership voted "overwhelmingly" to reinforce and recommit to the lockstep equity model alongside the new tier — framing the move as additive rather than substitutive.
- The deliberation period ran close to a year before implementation.
- Presiding Partner Peter Furci framed the move as a defence of lockstep rather than a concession: "We may be [one of] the last lockstep firms, but just because we are last doesn't mean we are wrong."
- The announcement positioned Debevoise as one of the final Am Law 100 firms that had maintained an all-equity partnership structure prior to the change.

This is the same dual-structure approach that [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]] adopted in two steps (December 2021 modified-lockstep memo by William Saeed; November 2023 salary-partner tier addition) — lockstep retention for equity partners, non-equity tier added as a flexibility-and-retention release valve. The pattern is most usefully read as a *partial* concession to [[Big Law transformation|Kirklandisation]] pressure rather than a full break — equity-partner economics remain lockstep, but the firm gains the ability to compete for retention against Kirkland-style outside packages by promoting selected attorneys to a tier without lockstep dilution.

## February 2026 — restructuring lateral push

Per Law.com, "Making Restructuring Push, Debevoise Adds Partners from DLA, Sidley, Cooley" (February 12, 2026): Debevoise made a series of lateral partner hires from [[DLA Piper]], [[Sidley Austin]], and Cooley to build out its restructuring practice. The pattern is consistent with the [[Liability management exercises|LME]] cycle volume that [[Kirkland & Ellis]] dominates legally — independent restructuring desks at every elite firm have grown headcount to capture mandate flow as the cycle accelerates.

The February 2026 hires also signal that Debevoise is now *competing* in the lateral market it long resisted, rather than only acting as a target. This is consistent with the post-June-2025 dual-tier compensation structure: a non-equity tier offers a vehicle for absorbing laterals at competitive pay without diluting equity-partner lockstep economics.

## Position in Big Law transformation

The June 2025 modification updates Debevoise's status in the [[Big Law transformation]] taxonomy. The firm is no longer the second-most-pure lockstep holdout (that role now belongs only to [[Wachtell Lipton]]). Instead, Debevoise sits in a "modified-lockstep + non-equity tier" cohort alongside [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore|Cravath]], distinct from the pure-lockstep [[Wachtell Lipton]] on one side and the scale-plus-pay [[Kirkland & Ellis]] / [[Latham & Watkins]] / [[Paul, Weiss]] cohort on the other.

The forward question is whether the modified-lockstep cohort holds — i.e., whether the equity-partner lockstep tier persists as the partnership's economic core — or whether the non-equity tier expands, the equity tier shrinks, and the firm follows the post-2016 [[Paul, Weiss]] trajectory toward de facto scale-plus-pay. The Cravath data (2021-26 ~8 partner departures in 12 months ending early 2026) suggests modified lockstep does not fully arrest defection pressure; whether Debevoise's structurally tighter restructuring lateral push (Feb 2026) is enough to retain partners under modified lockstep is the binding test case.

## Practice profile

- M&A — historically a top-tier M&A firm, frequently appearing in US deal-counsel league tables
- Banking and financial institutions — long-standing strength
- Litigation — appellate, securities, white-collar
- Insurance — long-running specialty
- Restructuring — recently expanded via Feb 2026 lateral hires from DLA, Sidley, Cooley; overlap with the [[Liability management exercises|LME]] practice area where [[Kirkland & Ellis]] dominates
- Private equity — sponsor-side mandates

The firm has notable historical white-collar / regulatory presence; alumni include Mary Jo White ([[SEC]] chair 2013-17) and other senior public-service figures, though firm-by-firm post-government revolving-door dynamics are not yet captured here.
