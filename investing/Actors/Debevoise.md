# Debevoise

## Synopsis

[[Debevoise]] is one of the last remaining pure-lockstep US elite law firms alongside [[Wachtell Lipton]]. The firm was founded in 1931 in New York by Eli Whitney Debevoise; the name has carried "& Plimpton" since the 1981 merger that absorbed the Plimpton, Lieberman & O'Connell partnership. Debevoise is positioned in the same elite-NY-corporate cohort as [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]], [[Sullivan & Cromwell]], [[Davis Polk]], and [[Wachtell Lipton]] — firms that historically resisted the partner-poaching arms race that [[Big Law transformation|Kirklandisation]] now imposes on the high end.

The investing-vault relevance is the [[Big Law transformation]] thread. Debevoise and [[Wachtell Lipton]] are the two firms most often identified as the last pure-lockstep holdouts — the lockstep model that the [[Kirkland & Ellis]] / [[Latham & Watkins]] / [[Paul, Weiss]] scale-plus-pay model is structurally pricing out of the high end of the talent market. Whether and when Debevoise modifies its compensation system — the way [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore|Cravath]] did in December 2021 and again in November 2023 — is one of the cleanest forward indicators for whether the Kirklandisation cycle has reached its terminal lockstep targets.

## Position in Big Law transformation

The 2025-26 [[Big Law transformation]] thread treats lockstep as a binary structural variable: a firm either runs lockstep (predictable progression, no individual partner pricing) or it runs scale-plus-pay (variable compensation, market-rate poaching, multi-million-dollar outside packages). The transformation cycle is the systematic conversion of the former cohort into the latter, beginning with [[Scott Barshay|Barshay]]'s 2016 [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore|Cravath]]→[[Paul, Weiss]] departure and accelerating through the 2025 [[Wachtell Lipton]] partner exodus.

Debevoise is the canonical second name (after [[Wachtell Lipton]]) when journalists, recruiters, and Am Law analysts identify the remaining pure-lockstep firms. A defection event from Debevoise — comparable in scale to the [[Josh Feltman]] $80mn / 3yr [[Wachtell Lipton]]→[[Kirkland & Ellis]] move documented in the May 2026 FT — would mark the cycle reaching the lockstep cohort's last holdouts. As of the May 2026 FT cluster, no such event has been publicly reported for Debevoise.

The structural drivers that imposed the [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore|Cravath]] modifications (December 2021 modified-lockstep memo by William Saeed; November 2023 salary-partner tier addition; 2021-26 ~8 partner departures in 12 months ending early 2026) all apply to Debevoise: senior-partner underpayment relative to outside Kirkland packages; junior-partner equity dilution under traditional lockstep; recruiter pressure on practice-area specialists where outside firms can offer step-change compensation. The forward question is whether Debevoise modifies first, defends lockstep through partner exits, or follows [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s 2025-26 quiet modifications (first-year equity halved; transparency reduced).

## Practice profile

Public-source overview of practice strengths:

- M&A — historically a top-tier M&A firm, frequently appearing in US deal-counsel league tables
- Banking and financial institutions — long-standing strength
- Litigation — appellate, securities, white-collar
- Insurance — long-running specialty
- Restructuring — overlap with the [[Liability management exercises|LME]] practice area where [[Kirkland & Ellis]] dominates
- Private equity — sponsor-side mandates

The firm has notable historical white-collar / regulatory presence; alumni include Mary Jo White ([[SEC]] chair 2013-17) and other senior public-service figures, though firm-by-firm post-government revolving-door dynamics are not yet captured here.
