**Marathon Asset Management** — New York-based credit specialist founded in 1998 by Bruce Richards and Lou Hanover, managing ~$24B across public and private credit. Being acquired by [[CVC Capital Partners]] for up to $1.2B (expected close Q3 2026), after which it rebrands to CVC-Marathon. Richards has become the most prominent voice warning about [[Private Credit Software Concentration|software concentration risk]] in private credit, predicting 15% default rates while the rest of the industry debates whether the problem is real.

---

## Synopsis

Marathon is a pure-play credit shop that has deliberately avoided the software lending boom that consumed its peers. While the average direct lending fund has 23% software exposure, Marathon carries just 1% — a positioning that looked contrarian until the [[SaaS stock meltdown 2026]] and [[Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate]] validated the thesis. Richards' framing — software is 23% of direct lending but only 1% of private companies, meaning the industry massively over-indexed on a single sector — has become the standard critique. His 15% default rate prediction for software sits at the hawkish end of the spectrum, roughly 3x what he expects for direct lending overall and consistent with UBS's worst-case scenario (which [[Ares Management]]'s Arougheti called "absolutely wrong").

The CVC acquisition (Jan 2026, up to $1.2B) is the bigger structural story. CVC gets a scaled US credit platform with $24B AUM, combining with CVC Credit to create ~EUR61B in fee-paying AUM. Richards gets permanent capital and CVC's global distribution. The deal values Marathon at a meaningful premium — $400M cash plus up to $800M in CVC equity, with an additional earn-out of up to $400M over FY2027-29. Richards joins CVC's Partner Board and remains co-head of credit strategies alongside Hanover.

Marathon's alternative to software lending is the HALO strategy — Hard Assets, Low Obsolescence. The firm lends against aircraft, engines, cranes, and other physical assets where AI disruption is irrelevant and collateral provides downside protection. The $615M MAST 2026-1 aircraft securitization (27 [[Airbus]] and [[Boeing]] aircraft) is the latest example. Richards frames the choice starkly: why lend to 10x-levered software companies facing existential AI risk when you can lend against a Boeing 737 that will fly for 25 years?

## Software default warnings (Feb-Mar 2026)

Richards has been the loudest and most specific voice warning about software concentration in private credit. Key claims across CNBC (Feb 12), Bloomberg (Feb 26, Mar 4):

The core argument:
- Software = 23% of direct lending but only 1% of private companies — massive over-indexing
- Software borrowers are ~10x levered
- Predicts 15% default rate for software in private credit over two consecutive years — 3x the overall direct lending default rate he expects
- Compared to the energy sector post-fracking — a structural disruption, not a cyclical downturn
- Called it *"a train coming down the tracks that you could see from some distance"*
- *"I think the shoe for financing has dropped, but I don't think the other shoe has fallen in terms of defaults"*
- Criticized peers for *"rearranging the furniture"* — relabeling software exposure as hospitality or travel to obscure concentration

Marathon's positioning:
- Only 1% software exposure vs 23% industry average
- HALO strategy: lend against aircraft, engines, cranes — assets AI can't disrupt
- $615M MAST 2026-1 aircraft securitization (27 [[Airbus]]/[[Boeing]] aircraft)

Industry response (from [[Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate]] coverage):

| Manager | Response |
|---------|----------|
| [[Apollo]] (Rowan) | Software <2% of total AUM; 30% exposure = poor risk management |
| [[Blackstone]] (Gray) | BCRED software portfolio had double-digit cash-flow growth; 37% LTV provides cushion |
| [[KKR]] (Nuttall) | Advocates linear deployment to avoid overdeployment |
| [[Ares Management]] (Arougheti) | Diversification benefits; [[UBS]] 15% default forecast "absolutely wrong" |
| [[Blue Owl]] (Lipschultz) | "We don't have red flags... we actually have largely green flags" |

Richards is on the hawkish end with Rowan. Arougheti is on the dovish end. The market hasn't decided who's right yet — but the [[Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate]] and [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]] are scoring points for the bears.

## HALO strategy

Hard Assets, Low Obsolescence — Marathon's investment philosophy and the antithesis of software lending.

Core logic: lend against physical assets that (a) hold value through economic cycles, (b) can't be disrupted by AI, and (c) provide real collateral for recovery in default. The downside differential between a loan backed by a [[Boeing]] 737 engine and a loan backed by a SaaS company's recurring revenue is enormous — the engine exists regardless of what happens to the borrower.

Asset classes:
- Aircraft and aircraft engines (MAST securitization program)
- Cranes (wind turbine construction)
- [[Commercial real estate]] restructuring
- Other hard-asset ABL

Recent deal: $615M MAST 2026-1 — 27 [[Airbus]] and [[Boeing]] aircraft securitization (early 2026).
