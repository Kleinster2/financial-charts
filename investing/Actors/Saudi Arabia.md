## Strategic geography

[[Saudi Arabia]] sits on or adjacent to three critical maritime chokepoints: the [[Strait of Hormuz]] (through which ~20 million barrels/day of oil transit, roughly 20% of global supply), [[Bab el-Mandeb]] (the entrance from the Indian Ocean into the [[Red Sea]]), and the [[Suez Canal]] (connecting the Red Sea to the Mediterranean). The kingdom controls 23-25% of the world's proven oil reserves (Haykel, March 2026) and is 1.5 times the size of Western Europe. It is the center of Islam — the location of Mecca (toward which all Muslims turn five times daily for prayer) and Medina (containing the prophet Muhammad's grave). The official title of the Saudi king is Guardian of the Two Holy Mosques, not King of Saudi Arabia.

## Political economy

Saudi Arabia is a redistributive state, not a tax state. The [[GCC]] countries do not tax their citizens. The government takes oil revenues and redistributes them: approximately 70% of the working population is employed in the public sector, most in functitious jobs that serve as wealth distribution mechanisms. Healthcare is free, education is free, and the government pays for citizens to receive specialized medical treatment abroad (Haykel cites Sloan Kettering as an example). This generosity is what creates the $90+ fiscal breakeven — the kingdom needs oil at that level to fund the redistributive model while simultaneously investing in [[Saudi Vision 2030]] diversification. See [[Mohammed bin Salman]] for the transformation agenda and [[Saudi PIF]] for execution.

## Social modernization

Social liberalization without political liberalization. The religious police (mutaween) have been removed from the streets. Women drive, work, and appear unveiled. Entertainment infrastructure is expanding (concerts, gaming events, international tourism). E-government is among the most advanced globally. The ideological identity has shifted from Islamism to nationalism centered on the Al Saud family. See [[Mohammed bin Salman#Social modernization]] and [[Mohammed bin Salman#Ideological pivot]] for full detail. For investment purposes, this normalization unlocks tourism, FDI, and labor force expansion — the three enablers of Vision 2030's non-oil economy.

## Energy transition

Domestic power mix target: 50% renewables + 50% gas (eliminating crude burn). Key projects:
- [[Jafurah]] — flagship unconventional gas ($100B+)
- [[Ghawar]] — conventional oil mainstay (~3.8M bpd)
- NEOM renewable megaprojects

## Iran conflict equity performance (March 2026)

[[Tadawul|Tadawul All Share Index]] +1.7% since [[Operation Epic Fury|conflict start]] (~Feb 28), outperforming [[Dubai Financial Market]] (-17%) and [[Qatar]] exchange. Key drivers:

- Domestic investor bid: Saudi investors' free-float share rose to 85.81% (week ended Mar 5). Saudi corporates/GREs net buyers of ~3B SAR ($800M) — a reversal from 12 months of net selling ([[Jefferies]], Mar 13).
- [[Saudi Aramco]] anchor: +7.6% in March, 16% of Tadawul benchmark. Oil economy benefits from higher crude.
- Red Sea advantage: Saudi exports can bypass [[Strait of Hormuz]] entirely via Red Sea coast, unlike [[UAE]]/[[Qatar]] whose economies depend on Hormuz-transiting trade.

See [[2026 Iran conflict market impact#Day 14 — March 13 (Gulf equity divergence)|full data]].

## Gaming strategy

[[Saudi PIF]] earmarked ~$40B for gaming via [[Savvy Games|Savvy Games Group]]. Key milestones:
- [[Scopely]] acquired for $4.9B (2023) — [[Monopoly Go!]] (~$2.2B player spend, 2024)
- [[Scopely]] acquired [[Pokémon Go]] from [[Niantic]] for $3.5B (March 2025)
- Portfolio stakes in [[Nintendo]], [[Electronic Arts]], [[Activision Blizzard]], [[Take-Two]]
- Hosting Olympic Esports Games 2027 (12-year [[IOC]] deal)
- Target: 250 gaming companies, 39,000 jobs by 2030
