Oxford Nanopore Technologies is a UK-based DNA/RNA sequencing company that pioneered portable nanopore-based sequencing technology. Listed on the [[LSE]] (ticker: ONT).

## Synopsis

Founded in 2005 as a spinout from the University of Oxford, Oxford Nanopore developed a fundamentally different approach to DNA sequencing: passing single molecules through protein nanopores and reading the electrical signal changes, rather than the optical "sequencing by synthesis" method used by [[Illumina]]. The key product, MinION, fits in a user's palm and costs under $1,000 — a radical departure from Illumina's room-sized machines that cost $500K-$1M+. This portability enables sequencing in field settings (pandemic surveillance, environmental monitoring, food safety) that were previously impossible, and the technology gained global visibility during COVID-19 for rapid pathogen identification.

The investment tension is between technological differentiation and commercial scale. Oxford Nanopore generates roughly $200M+ in annual revenue but remains loss-making, burning cash to fund R&D and market expansion. [[Illumina]] controls ~80% of the sequencing market and has the installed base, reagent lock-in, and clinical validation that ONT is still building. The bull case is that nanopore sequencing becomes the standard for long-read sequencing (which reveals structural variants that short-read methods miss), clinical diagnostics, and point-of-care testing. The bear case is that Illumina's own long-read efforts and the entry of [[PacBio]] compress ONT's differentiation before it achieves profitability. [[Edinburgh Worldwide]], the Baillie Gifford trust, holds a position — consistent with its mandate for early-stage, high-growth businesses.
