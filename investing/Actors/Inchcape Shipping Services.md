**Inchcape Shipping Services** is the world's largest maritime services company, providing port agency, marine logistics, and crew management across 60+ countries — and a co-operator of the proposed [[Chile-China Express]] submarine cable.

## Synopsis

Inchcape operates in the invisible infrastructure layer of global shipping: when a vessel arrives at port, someone needs to handle customs clearance, arrange pilotage and towage, manage cargo documentation, coordinate with terminal operators, and handle crew logistics. Inchcape does this across 2,400+ ports in 60+ countries, making ~68,000 port calls per year. The business model is high-volume, low-margin services that benefit from network effects — the more ports Inchcape covers, the more attractive it becomes for shipping lines that want a single global provider. The company was originally part of the Inchcape Group conglomerate (a British trading company dating to the 1800s), then went through various ownership changes before being acquired by private equity ([[Istithmar World]], then others). As of 2025, it remains privately held.

The submarine cable angle is the more interesting vector for the vault. Inchcape is co-operator (with [[China Mobile]]) of the proposed [[Chile-China Express]] cable from Valparaiso to Hong Kong — a project that landed at the center of US-China tech decoupling tensions. The [[United States]] pressured [[Chile]] to exclude Chinese participation in subsea cable infrastructure, citing espionage risk, leading to diplomatic fallout documented in [[US Chile cable visa sanctions]]. Inchcape's involvement bridges the maritime logistics world (where it has deep port infrastructure relationships) and the telecom infrastructure world (where subsea cables are the critical connectivity layer). The company itself is not investable, but its role in the Chile-China cable illustrates how maritime infrastructure companies are being pulled into geopolitical competition over data routes.
