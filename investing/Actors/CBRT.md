**CBRT** (Central Bank of the Republic of Turkiye) conducts monetary policy for [[Turkey]]. Its modern history is defined by political interference under Erdogan — multiple governor dismissals between 2019-2021 that destroyed credibility — followed by a dramatic orthodox pivot in mid-2023 that took the policy rate from 8.5% to 50%, the most aggressive tightening cycle in emerging markets.

## Synopsis

The CBRT became a global case study in central bank credibility destruction when Erdogan fired three governors in under two years (2019-2021), each time because they raised rates against his heterodox belief that high interest rates cause inflation. The result was a lira collapse from ~5.5/USD in 2019 to ~30/USD by mid-2023, with inflation peaking above 85% in October 2022. The appointment of Hafize Gaye Erkan as governor in June 2023, followed by her replacement with Fatih Karahan in February 2024, marked the return to orthodox policy — rate hikes from 8.5% to 50% in under a year.

The cautious easing cycle that began in late 2024 is the current investable question. With inflation declining from 85%+ to ~31% by December 2025 and the policy rate at 45% (after a 100bp cut in February 2026), the CBRT is navigating between maintaining hard-won credibility and responding to the economic drag of ultra-high real rates. The risk is always political: Erdogan tolerated orthodoxy because the lira stabilized, but if easing proceeds too slowly and growth suffers ahead of elections, the pattern of interference could reassert. For EM fixed income investors, [[Turkey]] sovereign and corporate bonds price this ambiguity — real rates above 10% offer carry, but the tail risk of another policy reversal never fully leaves the distribution.

## Gold reserve liquidation (March 2026)

The [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] forced the CBRT to liquidate gold reserves at scale to generate dollar liquidity and defend the lira. Between February 27 and March 27, the CBRT:

| Action | Volume | Value |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Gold sales | 52 tonnes | ~$7.7bn |
| Gold swaps (leased out) | ~79 tonnes | ~$11.7bn |
| Combined | ~131 tonnes | ~$20bn |

![[ft-turkey-bullion-holdings-2020-2026.png]]
*CBRT net gold reserves (tonnes), 2020–2026. Annotations: Covid recovery (2021), election drawdown (2023), Iran war selloff (2026). Source: FT/Metals Focus, data as of Mar 27, 2026*

Net holdings fell to 440 tonnes — lowest in more than two years. The CBRT had been holding 60–70% of reserves in gold, necessitating sales to raise dollar liquidity when the lira came under pressure from the energy shock.

The pace accelerated toward month-end: 31 tonnes sold in the week ending March 27 alone (Metals Focus).

Gold swaps involve leasing out bars to generate income and add downward pressure on [[Gold]] prices by increasing the supply available to markets.

Uğur Gürses (former CBRT gold reserve management): the bank now has sufficient liquidity and no immediate need to sell more gold.

The CBRT was also among global central banks selling US [[Treasuries]] to support its currency.

Turkey's net international reserves fell by almost half to ~$46bn since the Iran war began (Bürümcekçi Research and Consulting, based on official data).

The gold sales underscore Turkey's commitment to currency stability — a central plank in the country's campaign to curb inflation from 85%+ (2022) to ~31% (Dec 2025). Any lira instability risks unraveling the credibility built during the [[#Synopsis|orthodox pivot]].
