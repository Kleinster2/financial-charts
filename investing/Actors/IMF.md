# IMF

The International Monetary Fund is a Bretton Woods institution (founded 1944) that serves as the global lender of last resort for sovereign nations. With 190 member countries and ~$1T in lending capacity, the IMF provides financial stability surveillance, balance-of-payments support, and structural adjustment programs. Its lending toolkit includes Stand-By Arrangements (short-term crises), Extended Fund Facilities (structural reform programs), and Special Drawing Rights allocations. The $15.6B Extended Fund Facility for [[Ukraine]] is its largest active program.

## Investment relevance

IMF programs are a leading indicator for EM sovereign credit. A program signals both distress (the country needs external support) and stabilization (the IMF imposes fiscal discipline and unlocks follow-on multilateral/bilateral funding). For investors, the key question is whether conditionality will stick — [[Argentina]]'s serial IMF programs (11 since 1958) demonstrate that lending without structural reform creates repeat crises. The IMF's role in [[Egypt]]'s stabilization (repeated program expansions alongside Gulf sovereign wealth bailouts) and emerging market debt restructuring (Zambia, Sri Lanka, Ghana) makes it a central actor in EM fixed income.

## Global growth downgrade (Apr 2026)

[[Kristalina Georgieva]] confirmed on Bloomberg (Apr 7, 2026) that the IMF's forthcoming [[World Economic Outlook]] will downgrade the global growth forecast. The announcement came amid the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] escalation, with oil at ~$113-115 and Trump's 8 PM ET deadline on [[Iran]] hours away.

## Chief economist and the WEO forecast record

The Fund's macro forecasts run through its Research Department, led by the Economic Counsellor / chief economist — [[Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas]] from 2022 until his departure for UC Berkeley on July 1 2026. His tenure's signal call was a contrarian one: after [[Liberation Day tariffs|Liberation Day]] the IMF resisted the sharp 2025 US-growth downgrades other forecasters made, reasoning that a slowdown that severe was hard to picture absent a financial crisis and that the dollar's continued stability was a buffer. The call was vindicated — US real GDP grew 2.1% in 2025 (BEA), slower than 2024's 2.8% but well above the recession-adjacent fears. His parting framing in June 2026 was that the defining thread of his years in Washington was [[Geoeconomic fragmentation|geoeconomic fragmentation]], with the US — "one of the main architects" of the postwar system — disengaging from the rules it built. See [[World Economic Outlook]].
