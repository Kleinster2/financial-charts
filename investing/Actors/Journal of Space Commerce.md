## May 27, 2026 - Starlab / 1789 Capital

Tom Patton's May 27 piece on 1789 Capital's Starlab investment is useful less for the undisclosed funding amount and more for the framing. Ex Terra treated the deal as evidence that post-ISS commercial space stations are being pitched as critical infrastructure: an investable platform where national-security continuity, microgravity science, and private capital meet.

The source tracks the official May 21 Voyager / Starlab announcement: 1789 Capital made a strategic investment in Starlab; Voyager president Matt Kuta framed the risk as a US LEO-presence gap while [[Tiangong]] expands; [[Omeed Malik]] framed orbital infrastructure as aligned with American strategic interest. This makes the piece a clean source for the political-capital layer around [[Commercial LEO Destinations]], [[Voyager]], and [[Star Lab]].

## May 27, 2026 - Rocket Lab GEO SDA award

Tom Patton's May 27 piece on [[Rocket Lab]]'s $90M [[US Space Force|Space Systems Command]] award is useful because it reads the contract through supply-chain architecture. The headline facts come from Rocket Lab's May 21 release: two geostationary satellites, Heimdall space-domain-awareness payloads, Lightning bus, launch integration onto a government-furnished launch vehicle, and up to five years of operations. The Journal of Space Commerce emphasis is that Rocket Lab can now frame itself as an end-to-end mission provider in a GEO national-security orbit, not only a small-launch provider with a growing components business.

The key analytical point is the [[GEOST]] path. The Heimdall payload started as a GEOST / [[US Space Force|Space Systems Command]] prototype payload program; after Rocket Lab acquired GEOST in 2025, the prototype line became an internal Rocket Lab Optical Systems payload and then transitioned into a full operational spacecraft-delivery award. That makes the Ex Terra read a clean example of M&A becoming prime-contract eligibility.

## May 28, 2026 - NASA mission-directorate realignment

Tom Patton's May 28 piece on NASA's mission-directorate reorganization is useful because it translates the official org-chart release into the commercial-space question: whether Isaacman's contractor reset can be executed faster through a tighter agency structure. The official facts are NASA's consolidation of four mission directorates into three, direct mission-directorate reporting to the administrator, and named program offices for [[Artemis]], Moon Base, LEO, and the Space Reactor Office.

The source's value is the linkage. The realignment makes [[Commercial LEO Destinations]], Moon Base, and [[Artemis]] part of one human-spaceflight execution stack, which matters for [[Voyager]], [[Vast Space]], [[Axiom Space]], [[Blue Origin]], and the commercial-station field.

*Sources: [Tom Patton, "NASA Reorganizes Mission Directorates"](https://www.exterrajsc.com/p/nasa-reorganizes-mission-directorates), Journal of Space Commerce, May 28 2026; [NASA official announcement](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-announces-realignment-to-accelerate-mission-delivery/), May 22 2026.*

## May 28, 2026 - Blue Origin upper-stage facility

The queued May 28 Journal of Space Commerce item on Blue Origin's upper-stage facility was ingested with official and major-wire confirmation because the core facts come from the public Florida / Space Florida economic-development release. The useful investing-vault point is that Blue Origin is trying to solve New Glenn's upper-stage production bottleneck with a roughly $600M, 830,000-square-foot Cape Canaveral facility.

The follow-on risk is immediate: AP and Reuters reported on May 29 that a New Glenn vehicle exploded during hotfire testing at Launch Complex 36. The source sequence therefore belongs in the vault as a capacity-versus-cadence pair, not as a simple Florida jobs announcement.

*Sources: Space Florida / Florida governor announcement, May 22 2026; Richard Tribou, Orlando Sentinel via The Spokesman-Review, May 26 2026; AP, May 29 2026; Reuters via GMA News, May 29 2026.*
