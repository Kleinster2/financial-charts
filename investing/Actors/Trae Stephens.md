## Career arc

### Intelligence agency (pre-2008)

Worked in an intelligence agency in a basement, "getting nothing done, talking about sports." Saw a [[Palantir]] demo from [[Shyam Sankar]] and realized the software could be transformative. Applied to Palantir, flew to Palo Alto in a full suit with CIA cufflinks. A receptionist intercepted him in the lobby and told him to ditch the tie. Hired immediately — helped build the government business.

### Palantir (2008–~2015)

Joined early 2008, employee ~13 (25-30 people at the time). Built [[Palantir]]'s government business through the period when the company was rejected by DC and validated only from the field backwards. Palantir took ~5 years to reach $10M ARR. Lessons learned:

- Government procurement is a monopsony — enormous buyer power, often wrong about innovation needs
- Validation comes from the field (deployed users), not from DC (procurement bureaucracy)
- Palantir had to sue its own customer (US government) for the right to compete
- Quiet communications strategy was a mistake — should have told the story publicly. "When I started Anduril, the one lesson I learned that we didn't implement at Palantir is we're going to go out there and tell our story."

### Founders Fund (~2015–2017)

[[Peter Thiel]] invited Stephens to join [[Founders Fund]]. Spent three years looking for defense companies to invest in — "the only thing I knew to do." Method: pulled federal contract bid lists, cross-referenced with Crunchbase, outbound to companies. Met with hundreds of defense companies in three years. Made one investment: KDM (renamed Expanse, acquired by [[Palo Alto Networks]]). Otherwise nothing worth backing. "Even looking in retrospect, we didn't miss anything. There was just nothing worth investing in."

Went back to the FF investment team: "Someone should be building a next-gen prime that builds hardware, but it's software-defined and hardware-enabled rather than being hardware-defined and software-enabled." The team responded: "Sounds like you're probably the guy. Maybe you should start a company." Accidental founding.

### Anduril (2017–present)

Co-founded [[Anduril]] with [[Palmer Luckey]] and [[Brian Schimpf]]. Pitched [[Shyam Sankar]] before officially starting: "We know how hard software is. We don't want to do that again. We believe hardware would be less hard." The bet was that government understood hardware economics (bill of materials, margin) better than software (where cost-plus doesn't capture R&D value).

Anduril reached $10M ARR in 22 months — vs Palantir's ~5 years. Stephens credits Palantir's trailblazing: "I don't think that was a credit to Anduril... It was mostly a credit to me, Matt, and Brian living the 5 years at Palantir." But: "It's not like the government has fixed all of their problems."

Key contributions:
- Building [[Arsenal-1]] factory campus in Columbus, Ohio (5M sq ft) — tapping into the underemployed manufacturing workforce from closed factories. "We have the benefit of going back and tapping into that knowledge base."
- Valuation discipline: climbing down the multiples tree every round. Series H (~$60B) lower revenue multiple than Series G. "Not because investors wouldn't pay higher multiples — discipline is really, really important, especially heading into a medium-term IPO."
- Public advocacy for defense tech — learned from Palantir's early mistake of staying quiet

## Key views

On autonomous weapons: "Fully autonomous weapons are not new. [[SeaWiz]] has been deployed on naval vessels for years — shoots down aerial threats fully autonomously." Accountability is the key: every autonomous system will have a person responsible for its actions, just as a captain is responsible for SeaWiz or a soldier for their gun.

On [[Anthropic]]/[[Project Maven|Maven]]: "I don't think abstention from participating in the building of technology for national security is a morally neutral decision. You are making a moral decision when you decide to abstain."

On readiness: US has 8 days of munitions on hand for a major [[China]] conflict vs ~800 days needed. "If we were to start today with unlimited cash, over the next 18 months we could get on track for having a sustainable industrial base. But we're not going to do that — we're going to trickle it out."

On defense VC: references the HBO Silicon Valley scene about raising less at a lower price. Warns defense startups against inflated valuations: "Your product might be awesome... but you can also raise less at a lower price."

On America's industrial hollowing: "In my immediate family — grandparents, aunts, uncles — I have family members that worked at GM, at Ford, at Frigidaire, at National Cash Register, at Armco Steel. Every single one of these factories in Ohio closed. Not a single one still exists."

On the only at-scale manufacturer started this century: [[Tesla]]. "That's it."

*Source: All-In Podcast interview with [[Shyam Sankar]], Apr 6, 2026*
