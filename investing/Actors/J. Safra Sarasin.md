# J. Safra Sarasin

**J. Safra Sarasin** — Swiss-Brazilian private banking group, one of the world's largest privately held banks. Formed in 2013 when the [[Safra family]]'s [[J. Safra Group]] acquired [[Bank Sarasin]] (founded Basel 1841) for ~$1.13B and merged it with Bank Jacob Safra Switzerland. CHF 224.2B AUM at end-2024, CHF 504.5M net profit, 2,550 employees. Controlled by [[Vicky Safra]] and sons [[Jacob Safra]] (international operations) and [[David Safra]] (Banco Safra in Brazil) after [[Joseph Safra]]'s death in December 2020. The group just completed its largest deal — a 71% acquisition of [[Saxo Bank]] for ~EUR 1.1B (closed March 2, 2026), combining private banking stability with fintech/digital trading, bringing combined client assets above $460B. Known for its sustainable investing franchise (CHF 45.5B in ESG-integrated strategies, founding PRI signatory, carbon neutral by 2035 pledge). The controversies are real — CHF 3.5M fine for Petrobras/Lava Jato money laundering, $85.8M DOJ settlement for U.S. tax evasion, and Cum-Ex exposure in Germany — but the family keeps growing the empire through selective acquisitions and conservative balance sheet management (CET1 ratio: 42.7%).

## Synopsis

The J. Safra Sarasin story is really two stories merged into one. The Sarasin side is a 185-year-old Basel private bank — old Swiss money, institutional asset management, and an early mover in ESG. The Safra side is a Lebanese-Syrian Jewish banking dynasty that fled Aleppo, rebuilt in Beirut, expanded to Brazil in the 1950s, and became one of the world's wealthiest families through conservative leveraging of private banking across continents. Joseph Safra — the patriarch who died in 2020 worth ~$25.5B — was the richest banker on Earth. His brother [[Edmond Safra]] built Republic New York Corporation before selling it to [[HSBC]] for $10.3B in 1999, months before dying in a Monaco fire.

The merger created a platform with deep Swiss roots and global Safra family capital. AUM rose from ~CHF 130B at merger (2013) to CHF 224.2B at end-2024, with net profit climbing to CHF 504.5M. The CET1 ratio of 42.7% is extraordinarily high — this is a bank that runs with fortress-level capital, reflecting the family's conservative philosophy. The March 2026 Saxo Bank acquisition is the biggest strategic bet in years: it adds digital trading infrastructure and retail/institutional brokerage to a pure private banking franchise, with combined client assets exceeding $460B. The risk is execution — Saxo has had its own regulatory issues (an administrative fine reduced the deal price by ~EUR 8M), and integrating a Copenhagen fintech into a Swiss-Brazilian family bank is non-trivial.

The inheritance drama matters for the investment case because it determined control. After Joseph's death, son Alberto challenged the will, claiming his father lacked mental capacity and that he'd been deliberately disinherited for launching a competing firm ([[ASA Investments]]). The feud settled in July 2024 — Alberto divests from J. Safra Group by end-2025, and sister [[Esther Safra Dayan]] sold her stake to Jacob and David in January 2025. Control is now consolidated: Jacob runs international (J. Safra Sarasin, Safra National Bank), David runs Brazil (Banco Safra). Vicky Safra — Forbes-estimated $26.9B, Brazil's richest person — sits at the top.

## Evolution

The story of J. Safra Sarasin is the story of two banking traditions — one anchored in Basel Protestantism, the other in Aleppo's Sephardic merchant networks — converging in the 21st century to create one of the last great privately held banking groups.

- 1841 — Sarasin founding. Johannes Riggenbach-Huber, son of a tape factory worker who'd risen to partner at Bank Ehinger & Cie, founded Riggenbach & Cie in Basel on February 20, 1841. The firm focused on trade, shipping, and banking — the classic Basel merchant-banking model. It would remain a Basel institution for over 170 years.

- 1900 — The Sarasin name. Alfred Sarasin-Iselin purchased the firm from Fritz Riggenbach and refounded it as A. Sarasin & Cie with partner Arthur Streichenberg-Mylius. The Sarasin family — one of Basel's old patrician families — would give the bank its identity for over a century, steering it toward private banking and institutional asset management.

- 1841–1920 — Safra origins in Aleppo and Beirut. Meanwhile, the Safra family had been bankers and gold traders in Aleppo for generations, financing caravan routes across the Ottoman Empire. After the empire's collapse, patriarch [[Jacob Safra (banker)|Jacob Safra]] (1891–1963) relocated to Beirut in 1914 and founded Banque Jacob E. Safra in 1920. The bank specialized in trade finance, currency exchange, and gold trading, capitalizing on Beirut's role as a commercial hub connecting Europe, the Middle East, and Asia.

- 1952–1955 — Migration to Brazil. Jacob Safra moved to São Paulo in 1952 with his four sons: Elie, [[Edmond Safra]], [[Joseph Safra]], and Moise. They founded a Brazilian financial institution in 1955, which would become [[Banco Safra]] — today Brazil's 6th-7th largest private bank. The move was prescient: Brazil's postwar industrialization created enormous demand for banking services, and the Safras' expertise in trade finance and private wealth translated directly.

- 1966–1999 — Edmond's American empire. Edmond Safra, the family's most publicly prominent member, founded [[Republic New York Corporation]] in 1966, building it into a major American bank. He also established [[Trade Development Bank]] in Geneva. In 1999, Edmond sold Republic and Safra Republic Holdings to [[HSBC]] for $10.3B in cash — one of the largest private banking transactions in history at that time. On December 3, 1999, Edmond died of smoke inhalation in a fire at his Monaco penthouse, set by a nurse attempting a staged rescue. The fire also killed nurse Vivian Torrente; the perpetrator, Ted Maher, was convicted of arson causing death and sentenced to 10 years.

- 1987–2002 — Sarasin goes public and gets a Dutch partner. Bank Sarasin listed on the SIX Swiss Exchange in 1987. In 2002, it converted into a joint-stock company, with Dutch [[Rabobank]] taking a stake. By late 2006, Rabobank held 46.1% of capital and 68.6% of votes. This Dutch connection would prove pivotal — Rabobank's decision to sell its stake in 2011 opened the door for the Safra acquisition.

- 2011–2013 — The Safra takeover of Sarasin. In November 2011, J. Safra Group bought Rabobank's 46% stake in Bank Sarasin for $1.13B. Through 2012, the group acquired remaining minority stakes, taking full control. In 2013, the bank was delisted from SIX Swiss Exchange and merged with Bank Jacob Safra Switzerland to form Bank J. Safra Sarasin. The combined entity started with roughly CHF 130B in AUM, creating one of the largest privately owned banks in Switzerland.

- 2014–2020 — Joseph Safra's final years and peak. Joseph Safra ran the combined empire — Banco Safra in Brazil, J. Safra Sarasin in Switzerland, Safra National Bank of New York, plus 200+ commercial and agricultural properties worldwide (including London's [[Gherkin Building]] and New York's [[660 Madison Avenue]]). Forbes estimated his wealth at $25.5B in 2020, making him the richest banker in the world. He suffered from Parkinson's disease in his later years. He died on December 10, 2020, at age 82.

- 2020–2024 — Succession drama. Joseph's will left control to wife [[Vicky Safra]] and sons Jacob and David, with reduced allocation to son Alberto — reportedly because Alberto had launched [[ASA Investments]], a competing firm, poaching Banco Safra's CEO and senior executives. Alberto sued, claiming Joseph lacked mental capacity to execute the will. The feud spanned courts in Brazil, New York, and Switzerland. Settlement came July 19, 2024: Alberto divests from J. Safra Group by end-2025, pursues ASA independently. In January 2025, daughter [[Esther Safra Dayan]] also sold her stake to Jacob and David. Control consolidated to a clean three-person structure: Vicky at the apex, Jacob running international, David running Brazil.

- 2024–2026 — Growth and the Saxo bet. AUM hit CHF 224.2B at end-2024, net profit CHF 504.5M. The group expanded aggressively in Asia — hiring 18+ private wealth professionals from [[Hang Seng Bank]] in a single sweep. The marquee move: in March 2025, J. Safra Sarasin announced a ~EUR 1.1B acquisition of 71% of [[Saxo Bank]], the Copenhagen-based digital trading platform. The deal closed March 2, 2026. Saxo founder [[Kim Fournais]] retains 28% and becomes chairman; J. Safra Sarasin CEO [[Daniel Belfer]] becomes Saxo CEO. Combined client assets exceed $460B. This is the group's biggest strategic pivot — adding fintech/digital trading to a pure private banking model.

## Sustainable investing

J. Safra Sarasin has built one of the most established ESG franchises in European private banking:

- Founding PRI signatory — signed the UN Principles for Responsible Investment at inception
- CHF 45.5B in sustainable asset management strategies; 68% classified as SFDR Article 8 or 9
- Proprietary Sustainability Matrix — combines data from MSCI ESG, VigeoEiris, and RepRisk into industry and company ESG ratings
- 2020 Climate Pledge — carbon neutral across all AUM by 2035
- Net Zero Asset Managers initiative signatory
- Active stewardship — direct company engagements, collaborative investor campaigns (PRI, CDP), public policy work through Eurosif and Swiss Sustainable Finance
- Proxy voting aligned with custom ESG guidelines

This is not greenwashing-as-marketing. The Sarasin heritage bank was an ESG pioneer before the term existed — the sustainable investing division predates the Safra acquisition and remains a core institutional identity.

## Controversies

### Petrobras / Lava Jato money laundering

Switzerland's Office of the Attorney General fined Bank J. Safra Sarasin CHF 3.5M for aggravated money laundering connected to Brazil's Lava Jato corruption scandal. The bank's internal disorganization enabled ~$42.5M in corrupt transfers across accounts linked to companies in the oil and construction sectors. A former relationship manager was convicted and sentenced to 6 months (suspended, 2-year probation).

### U.S. tax evasion settlement

Bank J. Safra Sarasin paid $85.809M to the U.S. DOJ based on the aggregate value of offshore U.S. accounts — approximately $2.2B across 1,275 accounts since August 2008. Part of the broader Swiss bank settlement program.

### Cum-Ex trades in Germany

A former managing director of J. Safra Sarasin-operated Luxembourg funds was convicted of serious tax evasion linked to Cum-Ex dividend-stripping trades. German authorities conducted raids across German-speaking Switzerland; the alleged fraud totaled ~EUR 462M. Swiss authorities raided J. Safra Sarasin premises as part of the investigation.

### German espionage allegations

A German client, Erwin Mueller, alleged that J. Safra Sarasin shared his account information with German tax authorities — leading to a spy scandal implicating cross-border information sharing between Swiss banks and German prosecutors.
