#actor #person #politician #russia #geopolitics

**Vladimir Putin** — President of [[Russia]] since 2000 (with a four-year interlude as Prime Minister 2008-2012 under the Medvedev tandem). In office longer than any Russian or Soviet leader since [[Joseph Stalin|Stalin]]. Born October 7, 1952 in Leningrad. Former KGB foreign-intelligence officer (1975-1991), Saint Petersburg deputy mayor (1991-1996), and successively Yeltsin administration deputy chief of staff, FSB director (1998-1999), and Prime Minister (Aug 1999) before assuming the presidency on Yeltsin's December 1999 resignation. The dominant counterparty in nearly every Russia-related thread in the vault: [[Ukraine war]], oil/gas geopolitics, Russian sovereign credit, [[Russian sovereign drone ecosystem]], sanctions architecture, and the China-Russia partnership.

The investing-vault relevance routes through five primary channels: (1) Ukraine-war duration and territorial demands as binding variable for sovereign-risk pricing; (2) Russian oil/gas production decisions and pricing positioning vs OPEC+; (3) shadow-fleet maintenance and sanctions-evasion architecture decisions; (4) Russia-China energy and trade arrangements that route the world's second-largest oil exporter into Chinese pricing; (5) succession question (Putin is 73; constitutionally cleared to run through 2036) which becomes operative on any health event and represents a discontinuous tail-risk in Russian sovereign and FX markets.

## Doctrine and posture

The Putin doctrine on Russia's place in the international order has evolved in three phases:

- **2000-2007** — pragmatic integration with the West, market-oriented economic reforms under Kasyanov government, post-9/11 US cooperation. Russia's GDP roughly tripled in dollar terms over this period on oil-price tailwind.
- **2007-2014** — Munich Security Conference speech (Feb 2007) marks the rhetorical turn; Georgia war (2008) the first operational test; sustained pushback against NATO eastward expansion. Annexation of Crimea (Feb-Mar 2014) is the structural pivot.
- **2014-present** — explicit revisionism on post-Cold-War order; Syrian intervention (Sep 2015); election interference allegations (2016); Belarus stabilization (2020); full-scale invasion of [[Ukraine]] (Feb 24, 2022); current sustained war footing. Domestic repression has tightened in parallel — Navalny death (Feb 2024), media closures, exile of opposition.

The 2022 invasion of Ukraine is the doctrine's furthest-reaching test. As of May 2026 (per [[Ukraine war#May 17, 2026 — escalating drone war + peace talks stalled (FT)]]), Putin's "determination to take by force" the [[Donbas]] region is one of the two named reasons peace talks have stalled (the other being [[Trump II|Trump]]'s [[2026 Iran conflict]] diplomatic-bandwidth absorption). His territorial demand is non-negotiable; Zelenskyy's refusal to concede is symmetrically firm.

## Succession risk

The structural tail-risk in Russian sovereign and FX markets is what happens if Putin exits the stage. Key facts:

- 2020 constitutional amendments reset his term-limit count, allowing him to run again in 2024 (which he did) and 2030. Constitutionally eligible until 2036.
- No designated successor. The major power-bloc figures — [[Sergey Lavrov]] (Foreign Minister; 76), [[Sergei Shoigu]] (former Defense Minister, now Security Council secretary; 70), [[Dmitry Medvedev]] (former President; 60), [[Mikhail Mishustin]] (Prime Minister; 60), and various siloviki — have no clear hierarchy among themselves.
- Health rumors recurrent but never confirmed. The opacity of Russian elite-health information is itself an investing-relevant variable — there is no observable signal until there is a discontinuous event.
- Historical pattern: Russian transitions are typically destabilizing. The 1991 Yeltsin transition, the 1999 Putin handover from Yeltsin, and the 2008-2012 Medvedev tandem all produced significant volatility in sovereign credit and FX. A non-orderly Putin succession would likely produce more.

The investing-vault read: Russian sovereign credit spreads, RUB pricing, and Russian-exposed equities (Russian-listed names, plus US/EU-listed entities with Russian operations) all carry an implicit Putin-continuity premium that would reprice on a succession event. The premium is not easily quantifiable because it manifests on a discontinuous tail rather than in observable spreads day-to-day.
