**Atoms** — [[Travis Kalanick]]'s post-[[Uber]] company, rebranded from City Storage Systems on March 13, 2026 after eight years in stealth. Three divisions: Atoms Food (ghost kitchens, restaurant robotics), Atoms Mining (autonomous haulage via [[Pronto]] acquisition), Atoms Transport (wheeled robot platforms). Operating in 30+ countries with ~3,300 employees. Last valued at $15B (Nov 2021). The thesis is "digitizing the physical world" — applying CS primitives (CPU/storage/network) to atoms (manufacturing/real estate/transport).

---

## Synopsis

Atoms is what happens when an operator who built the world's most valuable startup goes underground for eight years and builds infrastructure nobody can see. Kalanick acquired a controlling stake in City Storage Systems (originally "Urban Kitchens," founded 2016 by Sky Dayton and Diego Berdakin) for ~$150M in 2018, seven months after leaving [[Uber]]. He ran it in total stealth — employees couldn't put the company name on LinkedIn, facility addresses were listed as cross-streets only, and he chose the most generic name possible on purpose. The company raised $400M from [[Saudi Arabia]]'s PIF at a $5B valuation (Jan 2019) and $850M from [[Microsoft]] and King Street Capital at $15B (Nov 2021). Total raised: ~$1.3B.

The ghost kitchen business (CloudKitchens) is the survivor of a category that killed its competitors — [[Kitchen United]] shut down Nov 2023, Reef shut down Aug 2024. But CloudKitchens has a serious problem: 65% tenant churn within one year across studied locations. The real moat isn't the kitchens themselves but the owned real estate (hundreds of millions in property across major cities globally), the Otter restaurant software stack (100,000+ restaurants, 18% of US delivery orders), and the Picnic corporate lunch marketplace that bundles 100 orders per courier. The March 2026 rebrand to Atoms signals Kalanick's pivot from "food infrastructure company" to "physical AI conglomerate" — adding autonomous mining ([[Pronto]] acquisition) and general-purpose wheeled robot chassis to the portfolio. [[Uber]] is reportedly considering "major backing" for the robotics play.

The risk is execution across three wildly different verticals simultaneously, and whether the ghost kitchen economics actually work at scale given the churn numbers. The bull case is that Kalanick already has 30+ countries of physical infrastructure, a robotics team ([[Eric Meyhofer]], formerly [[Uber]] ATG) building production food robots, and an autonomous mining company ([[Anthony Levandowski]]'s [[Pronto]]) with live commercial deployments for [[Heidelberg Materials]].

## Three divisions

### Atoms Food — "Infrastructure for better food"

The original business. Ghost kitchens (delivery-only restaurant locations) plus restaurant technology.

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| CloudKitchens | 400+ facilities, 5,000+ kitchens, 30+ countries, 110+ cities |
| Kitchen rental | $3,500–$6,000/month per unit |
| Top location AUV | $2.5M |
| Otter (software) | 100,000+ restaurants, 18% of US delivery orders, POS $19–$59/mo |
| Lab37 (robotics) | [[Eric Meyhofer]]-led, Pittsburgh. Bowl Builder robot: 18 ingredient dispensers, ~80 bowls/hour, <60 sq ft |
| Picnic (marketplace) | Corporate lunch delivery — 100 orders per courier, no customer fees |

Competitive landscape: CloudKitchens is the category survivor. [[Kitchen United]] shut Nov 2023. Reef shut Aug 2024. But tenant churn is a problem — 65% within one year across 20 studied locations (2022 data). Enterprise locations with $1.2M+ AUV perform well; small operators struggle.

Picnic litigation: Sued by Mixt (Bay Area salad chain, Dec 2024) for reselling food without permission and misleading pricing. Judge moved to arbitration Jan 2025. Picnic countersued for trade libel.

### Atoms Mining — "More productive mines for Earth's industries"

Autonomous mining haulage via [[Pronto]] acquisition (closing Mar 2026). [[Travis Kalanick]] was already [[Pronto]]'s largest investor.

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Technology | Camera-only AHS (autonomous haulage system), OEM-agnostic |
| Key customer | [[Heidelberg Materials]] — 100+ autonomous trucks, exclusive N. America AV provider |
| Milestone | 2M+ tons hauled autonomously at Lake Bridgeport quarry (TX) |
| SafeAI acquisition | Jul 2025 — competitor absorbed, added ASIL D safety framework + multi-sensor |
| Competitors | [[Caterpillar]] (incumbent, captive ecosystem), Komatsu, Hexagon/Wenco |

Kalanick's pitch: mines can run 24/7 with autonomous fleets, and demand for minerals only increases in the "new golden age." Off-road autonomy is a more tractable problem than on-road — controlled environments, no pedestrians, lower regulatory bar.

### Atoms Transport — "Wheelbase for robots"

Modular wheeled robot chassis for industrial applications. Earliest stage of the three divisions.

Kalanick's thesis: specialized wheeled robots, not humanoids. "Could you imagine if that humanoid had wheels? That'd be crazy." The food delivery robot (built by Lab37) is the first application; mining haul trucks are the second. The chassis platform is intended to serve multiple industries.

Competitors in food delivery robotics: [[Serve Robotics]], Bear Robotics, Miso Robotics. In broader wheeled autonomy: [[Tesla]], [[Aurora]], [[Waymo]].

## Evolution

The story of Atoms is the story of a founder who got the direction of history right at [[Uber]] — that software would eat transportation — then bet his second act on the harder version: software eating the physical world. Whether the execution matches the vision is the open question.

- 2016: Sky Dayton (EarthLink founder) and Diego Berdakin co-founded Urban Kitchens in LA to build delivery-only restaurant locations. The premise: commercial real estate is mispriced for food — a parking lot in a dense neighborhood is more valuable as a ghost kitchen than as parking. Kalanick, freshly forced out of [[Uber]] in June 2017, saw an opportunity to do to kitchens what [[Uber]] did to cars. He acquired a controlling stake for ~$150M in 2018, becoming CEO and renaming the company City Storage Systems. The generic name was intentional — maximum stealth.

- 2018–2019: CloudKitchens (the ghost kitchen subsidiary) expanded aggressively. The model: buy or lease underutilized commercial real estate (parking lots, warehouses, strip malls), build out kitchen infrastructure, rent individual units to restaurateurs for $3,500–$6,000/month. Delivery-only — no storefronts. International expansion via acquisitions: FoodStars (UK, $5M, Mar 2020), Nano (Mexico, $20M), Cocinas Ocultas (Colombia, 2019). The $400M Saudi PIF investment (Jan 2019, $5B valuation) was controversial — finalized weeks after the Khashoggi murder — but it funded the global land grab. By year two, CSS was operating in dozens of countries with virtually zero public awareness.

- 2019–2021: Kalanick sold ~$2.5–2.8B of [[Uber]] shares between November and December 2019 (lockup expired Nov 6), liquidating ~90% of his holdings. He left the [[Uber]] board Dec 31, 2019. The cash fueled CSS. In November 2021, the company raised $850M (debt + equity mix) from [[Microsoft]] and King Street Capital at a $15B valuation. But the ghost kitchen category was already showing cracks — high tenant churn (65% within one year), unit economics dependent on location density, and restaurant operators discovering that delivery-only removes the brand-building that makes restaurants viable long-term.

- 2021–2024: The category consolidated violently. [[Kitchen United]] shut down Nov 2023. Reef Technology shut down Aug 2024. CloudKitchens survived because of owned real estate (competitors mostly leased) and the Otter software stack, which pivoted from internal tool to standalone restaurant SaaS — now processing 18% of all US delivery orders across 100,000+ restaurants. Lab37 (Eric Meyhofer's robotics division, Pittsburgh) began developing the Bowl Builder robot — automating 40% of manual food prep in <60 sq ft. Kalanick started thinking beyond food.

- 2025: [[Pronto]] (Anthony Levandowski's autonomous mining company) acquired SafeAI (Jul 2025), signaling consolidation in off-road autonomy. Kalanick, already Pronto's largest investor, began acquisition talks. The Heidelberg Materials deal (100+ autonomous trucks, exclusive N. America provider) validated the technology commercially. Meanwhile, a Middle East unit IPO was explored and shelved (Dec 2025).

- March 13, 2026: Kalanick emerged from stealth on TBPN, renaming City Storage Systems to Atoms. Three divisions announced: Food, Mining, Transport. Pronto acquisition closing imminently. Published a 1,700-word manifesto at atoms.co/vision articulating the "digitize the physical" framework. [[Uber]] reportedly considering "major backing" for the robotics play — reuniting Kalanick with his former company as a partner rather than competitor.
