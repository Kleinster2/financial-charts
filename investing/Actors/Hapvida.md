**Hapvida** is [[Brazil]]'s largest vertical healthcare operator, formed through the 2022 merger with [[NotreDame Intermédica]]. Listed on [[B3]] as HAPV3. The stock collapsed ~70% post-merger on integration challenges, making it a cautionary tale for vertical healthcare consolidation in emerging markets.

## Synopsis

The Hapvida model is vertically integrated managed care: the company sells health plans and owns the hospitals, clinics, and labs where beneficiaries receive treatment. This structure — common in the US via [[Kaiser Permanente]] — theoretically controls costs by aligning insurer and provider incentives. Hapvida built its network primarily in Brazil's North and Northeast regions, where it dominated with low-cost plans targeting C and D socioeconomic classes. The 2022 merger with [[NotreDame Intermédica]] (which was strong in the Southeast, especially Sao Paulo) was supposed to create a national champion with 15M+ beneficiaries and massive scale advantages.

Integration proved harder than the deal model assumed. The combined entity faced a medical loss ratio (MLR) blowout — costs surged as the two networks struggled to integrate clinical protocols, IT systems, and provider contracts. The stock went from ~R$14 pre-merger to under R$4 at the trough, a market-cap destruction of over R$30B. Management has been clawing back margins through portfolio pruning (dropping unprofitable contracts) and network rationalization, but the stock remains well below merger-era levels. The investment question is whether Hapvida can achieve the cost discipline the vertical model promises, or whether Brazil's healthcare regulatory environment ([[ANS]] oversight, mandatory coverage requirements) structurally limits margin recovery. With ~15M lives under management in a country where private health plan penetration is ~25% and growing, the addressable market is not the issue — execution is.
