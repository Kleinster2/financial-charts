# Richard Koo

**Richard Koo** is the chief economist at [[Nomura]] Research Institute and the originator of the balance sheet recession framework, the most influential macroeconomic theory to emerge from [[Japan]]'s lost decades. The framework explains how simultaneous private-sector deleveraging after an asset bubble renders monetary policy ineffective, leaving fiscal stimulus as the only tool to sustain demand.

## Synopsis

Koo's central insight — developed through decades of studying [[Japan]]'s post-1989 experience — is that when asset prices collapse, firms and households shift from profit-maximizing (borrowing to invest) to debt-minimizing (paying down debt regardless of interest rates). In this state, even zero interest rates cannot stimulate borrowing because the private sector's balance sheet is impaired and its priority is solvency, not growth. The economy enters a "liquidity trap" not because banks won't lend but because creditworthy borrowers won't borrow. Only government spending can fill the demand gap, and premature fiscal austerity (as Japan attempted repeatedly in the 1990s and 2000s) causes GDP to contract, ironically worsening the debt-to-GDP ratio austerity was meant to improve.

The framework gained global relevance after 2008, when the US, Europe, and later [[China]] exhibited balance sheet recession dynamics. Koo argued that the Fed's QE programs were pushing on a string — inflating asset prices without generating real economic activity — and that only the fiscal response (TARP, stimulus packages) prevented a 1930s-style depression. His analysis of [[China]]'s post-2022 property deflation applies the same lens: Chinese households and developers are deleveraging simultaneously, the PBOC's rate cuts are ineffective, and only aggressive fiscal stimulus (which [[Xi Jinping]]'s government has been reluctant to deploy at sufficient scale) can arrest the deflationary spiral.
