# Samuel Slater

English-American industrialist (1768-1835) who is the canonical case of failed technology fencing. Trained in an Arkwright-system cotton mill in England, he memorised the water-frame spinning design, evaded Britain's ban on the emigration of skilled artisans, and rebuilt the machinery from memory in the United States — proving that a producer surplus embodied in tacit knowledge cannot be fenced by statute.

## Relevant analysis

Britain in the 1770s-80s banned the export of textile machinery and drawings and the emigration of skilled mechanics — pure state appropriability engineering. Slater's defection showed why it failed: the know-how was tacit and portable, so the surplus walked out in one person's head. The episode is the two-centuries-earlier mirror of [[China]]'s 2026 battery-tech licensing on [[BYD]]/[[CATL]] (see [[China retaliatory toolkit]]); whether modern fencing holds longer turns on how much know-how now lives in codified process and capital equipment rather than human memory. See [[Technological surplus capture]] and [[Industrial policy]].
