# Poly Auction

Second-largest Mainland [[China]] auction house ($397M in 2025, +20%). Subsidiary of China Poly Group, a state-owned conglomerate founded by PLA generals with interests in defense, real estate, and culture. Rebounded 20% in 2025 after a -67% collapse in 2024, but 72% below its $1.4B 2019 peak. Parent company Poly Culture Group was listed on HKEX (3636.HK) from 2014 until privatization in November 2023 at HK$8.88/share — a 73% discount to the HK$33 IPO price.

A subsidiary of China Poly Group — one of [[China]]'s largest state-owned enterprises, founded by PLA generals and Deng Xiaoping's son-in-law — Poly Auction is the [[Art market]]'s most striking example of state capital intersecting with cultural prestige. The numbers tell a story of extreme cyclicality: from a $1.4B peak in 2019 to a 67% collapse in 2024 (~$331M) and a partial 20% recovery to $397M in 2025. The 72% decline from peak is the sharpest among any major auction house globally, reflecting both the systemic forces hitting all Chinese houses (property crisis, consumer confidence) and the particular vulnerability of an SOE subsidiary to state-sector budget pressures.

The parent's identity matters: China Poly Group was formed in 1992 from PLA arms-trading operations, led by Deng's son-in-law He Ping and Wang Jun (son of former Vice President Wang Zhen). Poly Technologies was sanctioned by the US in 2013 for suspected WMD/missile proliferation to Iran, North Korea, and Syria. The cultural arm — including Poly Art Museum's repatriated bronze zodiac heads — serves as a soft-power vehicle for the Chinese state. The parent's property exposure (Poly Developments is one of [[China]]'s largest developers, ~90% of group revenue) makes this a double dose of China property risk.

## Evolution

The story of Poly Auction is the story of how the Chinese state's most politically connected conglomerate — an entity founded by PLA generals and Deng Xiaoping's own son-in-law — decided that soft power required an auction house, and built one that briefly rivaled [[Christie's]] and [[Sotheby's]] before the economic model underlying it collapsed.

- 1984-2000: Poly Technologies was formed in 1984 as an arms-manufacturing and trading wing of the People's Liberation Army. In February 1992, the State Council and Central Military Commission approved the creation of China Poly Group Corporation. Leadership drawn from the highest echelons of revolutionary royalty: Deng installed Wang Jun (son of former Vice President Wang Zhen) as head of both CITIC and Poly Group. Deng's son-in-law He Ping — a former army major general — was made president and remains honorary chairman. The Rand Corporation called Poly a "front company for the PLA" in 1997. Poly Technologies' most notable deal: sale of CSS-2 medium-range ballistic missiles to Saudi Arabia in 1987 (reportedly $3-3.5B to the PLA). In 1996, a US Customs sting in San Francisco seized 2,000 fully automatic AK-47s that Poly had shipped into Oakland aboard a Chinese vessel, disguised as "hand tools" — the largest seizure of smuggled automatic weapons in US history. In 2013, the US sanctioned Poly Technologies for suspected WMD proliferation to Iran, North Korea, and Syria. Against this backdrop, in 2000, Poly pivoted into cultural soft power: reclaiming Chinese cultural relics from Western collections. Poly acquired three of the twelve Old Summer Palace bronze zodiac heads — ox ($0.98M), tiger ($1.98M), monkey ($1.03M) — and Macau gaming magnate Stanley Ho donated the pig head ($0.77M) in 2003. The Poly Art Museum in Beijing was established to house them.

- 2005: Beijing Poly International Auction Co., Ltd. established July 1, 2005 as a subsidiary of Poly Culture Group (itself under China Poly Group). Inaugural sale generated over $91.5M — a remarkable debut for a house with zero track record. SOE parentage provided instant credibility with Chinese sellers, access to state-owned collections, and an implicit guarantee private competitors couldn't match.

- 2005-2011: From $91.5M to close to $2B in annual sales by 2011 — roughly 20x in six years, a growth rate unmatched by any auction house in history. Coincided with the Chinese art market bubble: new millionaires from the real estate boom treated classical Chinese art as both cultural identity and speculative asset. Poly overtook [[China Guardian]] as the #1 Mainland [[China]] auction house. By 2013, Poly had become the world's 3rd-largest auction house behind [[Christie's]] and [[Sotheby's]].

- 2012-2013: Structural cracks emerged. A CNBC investigation found only 34% of Poly's reported sales for works valued above $1.6M were actually completed by the following May. Two-thirds of high-value hammer prices never converted to cash. Poly Culture acknowledged in its IPO prospectus that "although we actively review the outstanding fees... there is no assurance that we could settle all the amount from relevant buyers in due course or at all."

- 2014: Poly Culture Group Corporation completed its HKEX IPO (3636.HK) on March 6, 2014, raising HK$2.57B ($331M) at HK$33/share. Shares surged 29% on day one to close at HK$42.60. Parent China Poly Group retained majority control.

- 2019: Peak at $1.4B including Beijing and [[Hong Kong]] operations (HK contributed ~HK$1.7B). All-time high.

- 2020: Partnered with [[Phillips (auction house)|Phillips]] to jointly conduct fall auctions in [[Hong Kong]] — combining Poly's Chinese collector base with Phillips' Western contemporary art expertise.

- 2023: Post-lockdown bounce to $993M — still 29% below 2019 peak. Meanwhile, Poly Culture was taken private: Zhongyi Century Cultural Industry Investment Co. acquired the company at HK$8.88/share — a 73% discount to the HK$33 IPO price. Last day of trading: November 6, 2023. The privatization reflected the broader trend of Hong Kong-listed Chinese companies going private amid depressed valuations.

- 2024: Collapse to ~$331M (-67%). The sharpest drop among any major auction house globally. Root cause: the property crisis. Poly Developments and Holdings is one of [[China]]'s largest property developers (~90% of group revenue). When property collapsed, it destroyed both the wealth and the confidence of the collector class that had fueled Poly's boom. The broader Chinese auction market shrank from $4.9B to $1.8B (63% decline). Non-payment rates hit the worst levels since COVID.

- 2025: Partial recovery to $397M (+20%). 29 lots above RMB 10M (+45%). Online sales doubled to 20% of total — most aggressive digital adoption among major Chinese houses. But still 72% below 2019 peak. Parent China Poly Group remains a Fortune Global 500 enterprise with 120,000+ employees, 11 major subsidiaries, 2,000+ controlled entities, total assets ~$89B.

## Investing relevance

- No direct exposure — subsidiary of SOE conglomerate; parent Poly Culture delisted 2023
- SOE cultural policy proxy: Poly's operations reflect Chinese state priorities around cultural patrimony, art repatriation, and soft power
- [[China]] property linkage: parent China Poly Group is a major developer (~90% of group revenue) — Poly Auction's revenue decline mirrors the broader China property crisis
- Settlement risk applies: Mainland [[China]] auction settlement rates = 42% for high-value lots (systemic, not Poly-specific); Poly's own rate was 34% in 2012 per CNBC
- Online pivot: 20% online share (doubled) — most aggressive digital adoption among major Chinese houses
