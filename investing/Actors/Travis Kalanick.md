#actor #individual #tech #usa

**Travis Kalanick** — Co-founder and former CEO of [[Uber]] (2009–2017). Now CEO of [[Atoms]] (formerly City Storage Systems / CloudKitchens), a physical AI and robotics conglomerate operating in 30+ countries. Serial entrepreneur: Scour (P2P file sharing, bankrupted by $250B MPAA lawsuit), Red Swoosh (P2P CDN, sold to [[Akamai]] for ~$18.7M in 2007), [[Uber]] (grew to $68B private valuation before forced resignation), [[Atoms]] (valued at $15B in 2021). Estimated net worth ~$3.6–4B.

---

## Synopsis

Kalanick is one of the defining founder-operator stories of the 2010s — built [[Uber]] from a San Francisco black car app into a $68B global platform, then got pushed out by his own investors in June 2017 after a cascading series of scandals (Susan Fowler's harassment exposé, #DeleteUber, the Waymo trade secrets lawsuit, Greyball). He sold ~$2.5–2.8B in [[Uber]] shares after the November 2019 lockup expiration, left the board Dec 31, 2019, and disappeared. What he did next — acquire City Storage Systems for ~$150M, raise $1.3B (including $400M from [[Saudi Arabia]]'s PIF), build ghost kitchens in 30+ countries, develop food robots, and invest in autonomous mining — was entirely invisible for eight years. The March 2026 rebrand to [[Atoms]] is his re-entry, now positioned as a "physical AI" operator rather than a software platform builder.

The Kalanick pattern is consistent across all four companies: find an industry where incumbents are lazy (CDNs, taxis, restaurants, mining), apply software-era operational intensity, raise capital as a strategic weapon, and outwork everyone. The question for [[Atoms]] is whether the same pattern works when the core business (ghost kitchens) has 65% annual tenant churn and the new businesses (mining autonomy, robot chassis) are pre-scale.

## Evolution

The story of Travis Kalanick is the story of a founder who keeps going back to the same well — peer-to-peer infrastructure, operational warfare, capital as weapon — and who has been right about the structural direction of technology every time, even when the execution or the culture exploded.

- 1976–1998: Born in LA to a civil engineer father (Donald, LA DWP) and advertising executive mother (Bonnie, LA Daily News). Grew up in Northridge. Attended Granada Hills Charter High School. Enrolled at UCLA studying computer engineering and business economics, joined Theta Xi fraternity. Dropped out in 1998 to join Scour Inc. full-time.

- 1998–2000 (Scour): Co-founded Scour Inc. with Michael Todd and Vince Busam, working under Dan Rodrigues. Scour was a multimedia search engine plus Scour Exchange — a P2P file-sharing service that predated Napster. Board included [[Michael Ovitz]] and Ron Burkle. The MPAA, RIAA, and NMPA hit Scour with a $250B copyright infringement lawsuit in 2000. The company filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy in September 2000. Kalanick was 24.

- 2001–2007 (Red Swoosh): Founded Red Swoosh with Michael Todd in 2001 — a P2P content delivery network. Kalanick called it his "revenge business" against the studios. [[Marc Cuban]] was on the board. No salary for four years. He raised money for a networking software company in late 2001, during the post-dot-com wasteland — a VC told him "all the software that could be invented has been invented." Sold Red Swoosh to [[Akamai]] in April 2007 for ~$18.7M (stock-for-stock). Kalanick personally cleared roughly $2–3M after taxes. Moved to San Francisco.

- 2008–2009 (Pre-Uber): Spent 2007–2009 as a CXO/adviser to multiple startups simultaneously. Also blogged as a tech influencer ("Swooshing"). Met Garrett Camp (StumbleUpon co-founder) at the LeWeb conference in Paris, late 2008. Camp's frustration with San Francisco taxis became the [[Uber]] concept. Co-founded UberCab in March 2009 with Camp and Oscar Salazar.

- 2010–2017 (Uber): Became CEO in 2010 (replacing Ryan Graves). San Francisco launch May 2010. Built the most aggressive growth operation in startup history — four fundraising rooms running in parallel for a week, IPO-style book-building where Kalanick personally controlled pricing. City launches run by 20-something GMs with sorting-card aptitude tests. Brought crossover investors (Fidelity-type funds) into late-stage private rounds alongside peers Drew Houston ([[Dropbox]]) and Brian Chesky ([[Airbnb]]). Peak valuation: $68B (2016, [[SoftBank]] era). But the culture that enabled the growth also produced the scandals: Susan Fowler's February 2017 blog post (215 internal complaints investigated, 20 employees fired), #DeleteUber (200K users deleted after Uber appeared to break a taxi strike), the [[Waymo]] v. Uber trade secrets lawsuit ([[Anthony Levandowski]] downloaded 14,000+ files from Google; settled Feb 2018 for 0.34% of Uber equity, ~$245M), and Benchmark Capital suing Kalanick for fraud over board expansion. Five major investors demanded his resignation in a letter on June 20, 2017. He resigned as CEO.

- 2018–2019 (Underground): Launched 10100 fund ("ten-one-hundred") in March 2018, investing in real estate, e-commerce, and China/India tech. Acquired controlling stake in City Storage Systems (originally Urban Kitchens, founded 2016 by Sky Dayton and Diego Berdakin) for ~$150M. Became CEO. Raised $400M from [[Saudi Arabia]]'s PIF at ~$5B valuation (Jan 2019) — deal finalized weeks after the Khashoggi murder. Sold ~$2.5–2.8B of [[Uber]] shares Nov–Dec 2019 (90% of holdings), left [[Uber]] board Dec 31, 2019. CNBC later reported he left ~$1.2B on the table by selling when he did.

- 2020–2025 (Deep stealth): Expanded CloudKitchens to 30+ countries, 400+ locations, 5,000+ kitchens. Raised $850M at $15B (Nov 2021). Built Lab37 robotics with [[Eric Meyhofer]] in Pittsburgh. Invested as largest investor in [[Pronto]] ([[Anthony Levandowski]]'s autonomous mining company). Explored Middle East unit IPO (shelved Dec 2025). Throughout: total information lockdown, thousands of employees forbidden from identifying the company publicly.

- March 13, 2026 (Re-entry): Emerged on TBPN podcast, renamed company to [[Atoms]]. Published manifesto at atoms.co/vision. Announced three divisions (Food, Mining, Transport) and imminent [[Pronto]] acquisition. First public appearance in eight years.
