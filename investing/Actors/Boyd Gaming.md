## Synopsis

Boyd is the anti-Strip casino company. While [[MGM Resorts]], [[Las Vegas Sands]], and [[Wynn Resorts]] chase international expansion and mega-resort development, Boyd runs 27 mid-market properties across 11 US states, targeting locals and regional gamblers who drive repeat visitation. The model works: FY2025 was a record year — $4.1B revenue, $1.4B EBITDA, 40% property-level margins. Those margins are peer-leading for regional operators and competitive with Strip economics, achieved at a fraction of the capex intensity.

The $1.76B FanDuel stake sale (July 2025) was transformative. Boyd had held a 5% equity interest in FanDuel Group — a legacy of early market-access partnerships — and sold it to parent [[Flutter Entertainment]] at what amounted to a ~$35B implied FanDuel valuation. The proceeds halved Boyd's debt from $3.6B to ~$1.9B (2.6x debt/EBITDA), funded $800M+ in shareholder returns (11% of shares retired), and extended the FanDuel strategic partnership through 2038 for mobile sports betting and online casino access in Iowa, Indiana, Kansas, Louisiana, and Pennsylvania. Boyd keeps the cash flows and the partnership without the equity risk.

The Cadence Crossing casino (opening March 25, 2026) is Boyd's first new ground-up casino in 20+ years — a 50,000 sq ft property with 450+ slots in the growing Henderson/Las Vegas suburb market. It signals confidence in the locals model and is small enough ($60-70M) to fund from operating cash flow. Boyd doesn't make $10B bets.

## Evolution

The story of Boyd Gaming is the story of a family that built a casino empire by staying in its lane — locals, regionals, repeat customers, disciplined capex — while every peer chased the next Macau or mega-resort.

- **1941-1974**: [[Sam Boyd]] arrived in Las Vegas in 1941. He worked his way up from bingo caller to pit boss to shift boss, eventually buying into the Sahara Hotel and becoming general manager and partner of The Mint Las Vegas. His son [[Bill Boyd]], a practicing attorney, first acquired his stake in the family's Eldorado Casino (Henderson, NV, 1962) by doing the casino's legal work. The lesson: start with what you can afford, earn your way up.

- **1975**: Boyd Gaming Corporation formally founded January 1, 1975 to develop the California Hotel and Casino in downtown Las Vegas. The Cal targeted the Hawaiian tourist market — a niche play that proved remarkably durable. The company went public in 1993.

- **1996-2004**: Expanded via acquisition: bought Par-A-Dice (Illinois), Sam's Town Tunica, Blue Chip Casino (Indiana). The strategy was consistent — acquire well-located regional properties at reasonable multiples, improve operations, generate steady cash flow. No mega-developments, no international ambitions.

- **2004-2007**: Acquired Coast Casinos (The Orleans, Suncoast, Gold Coast) for ~$1.3B, cementing the Las Vegas locals franchise. Added Borgata as a JV with MGM (later sold Boyd's 50% to MGM for $900M in 2016). Acquired several Louisiana properties.

- **2018-2019**: Early FanDuel partnership for market access — Boyd provided the gaming licenses and physical locations, FanDuel brought the online platform. This would become the most lucrative partnership in Boyd's history, though the value wasn't apparent until FanDuel's valuation exploded.

- **2020**: COVID hit regional casinos hard but recovery was faster than Strip properties — locals markets rebounded as people couldn't fly to destinations but could drive to nearby casinos. Boyd emerged with a leaner cost structure and higher margins.

- **2025**: Sold 5% FanDuel stake to [[Flutter Entertainment]] for $1.76B (July 31, 2025). Extended FanDuel strategic partnership through 2038. Used proceeds to cut debt in half (from $3.6B to ~$1.9B) and return $800M+ to shareholders (retiring 11% of shares). Revenue hit a record $4.1B. Online segment projected ~$50-55M EBITDAR for FY2025.

- **2026**: Cadence Crossing opens March 25, 2026 — first new ground-up casino in 20+ years. 50,000 sq ft, 450+ slots, replacing Jokers Wild. Major renovations underway: Gold Coast (700 rooms), The Orleans (1,800+ rooms), Suncoast, Fremont expansion. 2026 capex guidance: $650-700M ($250M maintenance + growth).
