**Howard Hughes Holdings** — Texas-based real estate and master-planned community developer being transformed by [[Bill Ackman]] into a diversified holding company modeled on [[Berkshire Hathaway]]. [[Pershing Square]] is the largest shareholder. Acquired [[Vantage Risk]] for $2.1B in Dec 2025 — the first major step toward an [[Insurance float]]-funded permanent capital vehicle.

---

## Synopsis

Howard Hughes Holdings is Ackman's platform for building what he calls a "modern-day [[Berkshire Hathaway]]." The company has languished on public markets for over a decade as a pure real estate play — its core business is developing and operating master-planned communities and mixed-use properties across the US (including the Seaport District in lower Manhattan, Ward Village in Honolulu, and The Woodlands near Houston).

The Dec 2025 [[Howard Hughes Vantage Risk acquisition|acquisition of Vantage Risk]] for $2.1B marks the pivot from real estate operator to diversified holding company. [[Vantage Risk]] is a Bermuda-based property and casualty insurer specializing in lawsuits, political violence, and cyber risk — a P&C float model, distinct from the life/annuity float favored by [[Apollo]]/[[Athene]] and [[KKR]]/[[Global Atlantic]]. [[Pershing Square]] financed the deal (cash plus up to $1B investment in HHH stock) and will manage Vantage's investment portfolio, creating a direct analog to [[Berkshire Hathaway]]'s structure: insurance float → asset management → compounding.

The Berkshire aspiration is genuine but the comparison is premature. Buffett spent decades building $170B+ in float across GEICO, Gen Re, and BHRG. Ackman is starting with one specialty P&C insurer and a real estate portfolio — the question is whether he can repeat Buffett's compounding trick at a much earlier stage while managing a concentrated, illiquid real estate book alongside insurance operations.

## Ackman assumes control (May 5, 2025)

[[Bill Ackman]] assumed effective control of Howard Hughes with a further $900M investment, shifting its strategy from real estate to diversified conglomerate — buying controlling stakes in public and private companies under the instruction of Ackman and CIO Ryan Israel.

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Additional investment | $900M |
| Management fee | $15M/year to [[Pershing Square]] for investment team |
| Performance fee | 1.5% on market cap increase above inflation rate |
| Hurdle | Tied to current market cap and share count (no credit for new share issuance) |
| Shareholder vote | Not required |
| Closed | May 5, 2025 |

The deal followed shareholder backlash against the original January 2025 proposal, which had a more aggressive fee: 1.5% on all market cap gains with no hurdle. A special committee softened the terms. Mixed reaction: one large shareholder called it "not the perfect deal, but the special committee listened to some feedback." Others criticized the low hurdle (inflation, not S&P 500) (FT, May 5 2025).

## Origin: General Growth Properties (2008-2012)

Ackman created Howard Hughes in 2012 as a way to exit one of his greatest-ever trades — a large bet on bankrupt mall developer General Growth Properties during the 2008 financial crisis. Instead of selling his shares, Ackman took ownership of a piece of GGP's non-core properties including large residential developments in Houston, Las Vegas, Maryland, and Hawaii. These properties' value "has largely gone unrecognized by public stockholders" (Ackman, May 2025).

## Seaport District (spun off Jul 2024)

The South Street Seaport in lower Manhattan was HHH's most visible — and most expensive — project. ~$1.5B total capital invested over a decade. In July 2024, HHH spun it off as [[Seaport Entertainment Group]] (NYSE American: SEG), effectively admitting the project could not be sustained within the holding company's balance sheet.

**What HHH built:** Pier 17 (~$425M rebuild, SHoP Architects, opened Jul 2018), the Tin Building food hall (~$200M+ renovation, opened Sep 2022), and acquired the 250 Water Street development site ($180M + $40M air rights).

**What went wrong:**

The Tin Building by Jean-Georges Vongerichten was a spectacular failure — 54,000 sq ft food hall that generated $32.4M revenue in 2023 against losses exceeding $100K/day. Total cumulative losses exceeded $100M before it closed on Feb 24, 2026. Remote location, seasonal foot traffic, $7 croissants, $25 cocktails. Now leased to Balloon Museum.

250 Water Street became a years-long legal battle. HHH invested $220M+ (purchase + air rights + remediation). The Seaport Coalition (local residents) sued, alleging HHH won Landmarks Preservation Commission approval through a $40M quid pro quo (museum funding commitment). Justice Arthur Engoron halted construction twice (Oct 2022, Jan 2023) before the Appellate Division and Court of Appeals overturned his rulings (2023-2024). SEG ultimately sold the site to Tavros Capital for $143M (closed Feb 2026) — a significant loss.

HHH took a $672.5M impairment charge on Seaport assets in 2023.

**What survived:** Pier 17's rooftop concert venue (Live Nation 5-year deal), ESPN studios, iPic Theater. The neighborhood was 90% leased at year-end 2025 and losses are narrowing. But the investment returns for HHH shareholders were deeply negative.

**Hurricane Sandy context:** The Seaport was devastated on Oct 29, 2012 — 5-7 feet of water on first floors, $20M+ damage to the South Street Seaport Museum. The rebuild happened in Sandy's aftermath, which added both cost and urgency.

## Context: the Berkshire replication trend

Ackman is not alone in trying to replicate Buffett's insurance-funded holding company model. The broader trend:

| Actor | Vehicle | Insurance type | Status |
|-------|---------|---------------|--------|
| [[Bill Ackman]] / HHH | [[Vantage Risk]] | P&C (Bermuda) | Acquired Dec 2025 |
| [[Apollo]] | [[Athene]] | Life/annuities | $430B, merged 2022 |
| [[KKR]] | [[Global Atlantic]] | Life/annuities | >$200B, 100% owned 2024 |
| [[Third Point]] (Dan Loeb) | [[Malibu Life Reinsurance]] | Life/annuities (Cayman) | Converted 2025 |
| [[Brookfield]] | [[American Equity]] | Fixed indexed annuities | $130B, acquired 2024 |

The distinction: Apollo/KKR went for life/annuity float (long-duration, massive pools, predictable). Ackman chose P&C float (shorter duration, more volatile, but closer to Berkshire's original model). Loeb went Cayman-domiciled life reinsurance targeting US fixed annuities.
