## The 1982 near-bankruptcy

### What happened

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Year | 1981-82 |
| Prediction | US lent more to foreign countries than could be repaid; depression coming |
| What occurred | [[Mexico]] crisis did happen (Aug 1982), but marked market trough |
| Result | Fed eased; 18-year bull market began |
| Dalio's outcome | "Dead wrong" |

### Financial impact

| Consequence | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Lost | Everything |
| Borrowed | $4,000 from father to pay family bills |
| Employees | Had to fire everyone |
| Firm status | Just Dalio remained |

### Lessons learned

| Lesson | Implementation |
|--------|----------------|
| Fear of being wrong | "Shifted from 'I'm right' to 'How do I know I'm right?'" |
| Diversification | Discovered could reduce risk 80% without reducing returns |
| Radical transparency | Built culture of challenging assumptions |
| Stress-testing | Systematize decision-making |

**Dalio quote**: "In retrospect, my crash was one of the best things that ever happened to me because it gave me the humility I needed to balance my aggressiveness."

## Philosophy

See [[Dalio Principles]] for the full framework — radical truth, radical transparency, believability-weighted decisions, and the 1982 bankruptcy that shaped it.

**Summary:** The 1982 near-bankruptcy transformed Dalio from "I'm right" to "How do I know I'm right?" This humility became the foundation for Bridgewater's culture and decision-making systems.
