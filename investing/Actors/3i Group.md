#actor #private-equity #uk

**3i Group** is Britain's oldest private equity firm, founded in 1945 as the Industrial and Commercial Finance Corporation (ICFC) by the [[Bank of England]] and the major clearing banks to fill the "Macmillan Gap" in SME financing. Listed on the LSE (III), FTSE 100 constituent. Unlike traditional GP/LP fund managers ([[Blackstone]], [[KKR]], [[Apollo]]), 3i deploys proprietary capital from its own balance sheet — capturing 100% of returns (and risk) without third-party fund structures.

The firm is effectively a single-asset bet: [[Action]], the Dutch discount retailer, represents 74% of portfolio value at GBP 22.4B.

## Model: proprietary capital

3i is a listed investment company, not a fund manager. The structural distinction matters:

| 3i model | Traditional PE (BX, KKR, APO) |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Deploys own balance sheet | Manages third-party capital |
| 100% of returns to shareholders | 80/20 LP/GP split + fees |
| No forced exits (permanent capital) | Fund life forces exits |
| Concentration risk (74% in one holding) | Diversified across hundreds of deals |
| No management fee revenue | 1-2% AUM fee streams |

This means 3i's share price is essentially a leveraged bet on [[Action]]'s compounding. When Action delivers (26% revenue CAGR since 2011), 3i compounds spectacularly — 10-year total return ~600%. When growth wobbles, the concentration punishes.

## Action: the crown jewel

[[Action]] is a Dutch non-food discount variety retailer founded in 1993 in Enkhuizen. 3i acquired a majority stake in June 2011 for GBP 134 million when Action had ~250 stores and ~EUR 80M EBITDA. That GBP 134M is now worth GBP 22.4B — one of the great PE returns of the century.

### What makes Action work

Average store payback under 1 year (~8 months). Cost-plus pricing — prices like EUR 1.23 or EUR 1.52, not rounded to psychological price points — enables genuinely low prices rather than margin-optimized pricing. 6,000 products across 14 categories, two-thirds of assortment rotates, 150+ new articles weekly. Every store and every SKU profitable. Direct sourcing at 17.6% of procurement (targeting 20%).

### France deceleration

LFL of just +1.3% in 2025, the weakest market. Aggressive supermarket discounting from established players forced ~550 price cuts across Q4 2025 and Q1 2026. France dragged group LFL down — ex-France was +7.2%.

### US expansion (announced Mar 26 Capital Markets Day)

3i is betting Action can crack the US market — a notoriously difficult graveyard for European retailers ([[Tesco]], [[Marks & Spencer]], [[Carrefour]] all failed). First US store targeted late 2027 or early 2028, starting with ~20 stores in the Southeast (North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia). Target: ~100 stores by end of 2030. Investment: EUR 350-400M over 2026-2030. Product mix: 40% European assortment, 60% locally tailored.

Action CEO Hajir Hajji: "We recognize the US market is different, and we are aware of other European retailers having entered the US — some with more success than others."

The announcement triggered a 15-16% share price drop on March 26. Analyst Charles Murphy (Singer Capital Markets): the US investment represents "around 5% of EBITDA a year" for four years — manageable in isolation, but the risk is senior management distraction, not capital. CEO Simon Borrows and family trusts bought 350,147 shares on the day of the selloff.

### European white space

Updated estimate of ~4,650 additional stores possible across current and identified markets (up ~200 from prior year). Long-term European maturity target: ~8,000 stores. 2026 plan: at least 400 net new stores. Croatia and Slovenia entering 2026; Bulgaria 2027.

Early 2026 trading: EUR 3.7B in first 12 weeks, +14.5% Y/Y. LFL +4.0%.

## Share price collapse (2025-26)

The stock peaked near 4,496p in late 2025, trading at a ~70% premium to NAV — the market was pricing Action's compounding as near-certain. The premium has collapsed to an 11% discount:

| Driver | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Action LFL deceleration | 10.3% (2024) → 4.9% (2025) → 4.0% (early 2026) |
| France weakness | LFL +1.3%, competitive discounting |
| US expansion risk | EUR 350-400M commitment into a market where European retailers routinely fail |
| PE/alt manager de-rating | Broader private equity sell-off (BX, KKR, APO, ARES all -20%+ YTD) |
| Premium normalization | From +70% premium to -11% discount — repricing of growth certainty |

CEO insider buying on the March 26 selloff day (350,147 shares) signals management conviction.
