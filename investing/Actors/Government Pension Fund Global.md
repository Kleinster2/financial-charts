# Government Pension Fund Global

The world's largest [[Sovereign Wealth Funds|sovereign wealth fund]] (>$2T AUM as of April 2026, up from ~$1.7T in early 2025), managed by [[Norges Bank]] Investment Management (NBIM) on behalf of the Norwegian Ministry of Finance (currently led by [[Jens Stoltenberg]]). Funded entirely by [[Norway]]'s oil and gas revenues, the fund was established in 1990 to invest surplus petroleum wealth for future generations. The government caps annual withdrawals at 3% of the fund's value (the "fiscal rule"), forcing reliance on non-oil tax revenue including the [[Wealth tax]].

NBIM holds stakes in over 9,000 companies across 70+ countries, making it one of the largest single shareholders globally — owning roughly 1.5% of all listed equities worldwide. The fund allocates ~70% to equities, ~25% to fixed income, and ~5% to unlisted real estate. More than half of total assets are invested in the United States, reflecting the size of US capital markets within the global benchmarks NBIM tracks. It publishes all holdings and voting records, making it one of the most transparent institutional investors. NBIM has increasingly used its ownership position to push ESG standards, particularly on climate disclosure and executive pay, though its ethical exclusion list (coal, tobacco, certain weapons) draws criticism from both sides — too aggressive for free-market purists, too lax for climate activists.

## US allocation debate (April 2026)

In an FT interview published April 15 2026, finance minister [[Jens Stoltenberg]] said the fund has "no plans to reduce our exposure in the US," despite some Norwegian parliament members suggesting GPFG is overexposed to a market increasingly volatile under [[Donald Trump|Trump]] II. The pressure followed the April 2025 [[Liberation Day tariffs]] and the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|US-Israeli war in Iran]] — both cited by European institutional investors as reasons to reconsider US concentration. Stoltenberg's framing: "there is no way to hide [$2tn]" because the US presence in capital markets is too large for any major fund to materially underweight. Any reduction would be a political decision; he does not foresee "big changes."

The contrast point in the same FT piece: [[AkademikerPension]] (Danish academics' fund) sold all of its US [[Treasuries|Treasury]] holdings in January 2026 on US debt and fiscal-outlook grounds. GPFG's stance and AkademikerPension's exit bracket the range of foreign-institutional behavior on US assets in 2026. Central banks globally have hedged the same exposure by accumulating gold (see [[Dollar reserve status erosion]]).
