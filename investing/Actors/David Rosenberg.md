**David Rosenberg** — Founder and President of [[Rosenberg Research]] (est. 2020). Former Chief North American Economist at [[Merrill Lynch]] (2002-2009) and Chief Economist at [[Gluskin Sheff]] (2009-2019). Consistently ranked in [[Institutional Investor]] All-Star analyst rankings. Known as a perma-bear, famous for calling the 2008 [[housing]] crisis early. Publishes "Breakfast with Dave" daily newsletter.

## Key theses & frameworks

### Structural bearishness / cycle focus
Rosenberg's analytical framework centers on the [[business cycle]] and identifying late-cycle dynamics:
- Deep focus on [[leading indicators]]: initial [[jobless claims]], [[yield curve]], [[ISM]], [[housing starts]]
- Believes markets systematically underestimate [[recession]] risk and overestimate [[soft landing]] probability
- Prefers [[bonds]] and defensive positioning until data confirms expansion
- Heavily influenced by the work of economic historian [[Joseph Schumpeter]] and cycle theory

### 2008 housing crisis call
Rosenberg's career-defining call. While at [[Merrill Lynch]], he was one of the few major [[Wall Street]] economists to:
- Warn about the [[housing bubble]] and [[subprime mortgage]] risk starting in 2006-2007
- Argue that the [[housing]] downturn would spill over into a broader [[recession]] and [[financial crisis]]
- Maintain his bearish stance despite pressure from management and clients
- Was later vindicated as [[Lehman Brothers]] collapsed and the [[Great Financial Crisis]] unfolded

### Disinflation / deflation bias
Rosenberg has long argued that structural forces favor [[disinflation]]:
- Demographic headwinds ([[aging population]], slowing [[labor force]] growth)
- Technology-driven productivity gains suppress prices
- [[Debt]] overhang creates deflationary drag
- [[Globalization]] (though waning) still exerts downward pressure on wages and goods prices

### Bond bull thesis
For much of the post-2009 era, Rosenberg was bullish on [[bonds]], arguing:
- The [[Federal Reserve|Fed]] would keep rates lower for longer than consensus expected
- [[Disinflation]]ary forces favored duration
- [[Recession]] risk was persistently underpriced
- [[U.S. Treasuries]] remained the ultimate safe haven

## Track record

### Hits
- 2008 housing/financial crisis: One of the most prescient and early calls on [[Wall Street]]; maintained conviction under pressure
- 2001 recession: Correctly positioned bearish ahead of the [[dot-com]] bust recession
- Post-2008 bond bull: Correctly bullish on [[bonds]] during much of the 2009-2019 period
- Leading indicator focus: His analytical framework has correctly identified multiple turning points
- Canadian housing skepticism: Has consistently warned about [[Canada]]'s [[housing bubble]]

### Misses
- Perma-bear reputation: Has called for [[recession]] numerous times post-2009 that didn't materialize — missed most of the 2009-2019 equity bull market
- 2021-2023 inflation miss: His [[disinflation]] framework was wrong-footed by the post-COVID [[inflation]] surge to 9.1%
- Post-COVID recession call: Warned of imminent [[recession]] repeatedly in 2022-2024; the economy remained resilient
- Bond bear market (2022): His long-duration bond positioning suffered badly during the 2022 selloff — worst [[bond]] market in 150 years
- Missing the equity rally: His bearish stance caused him to miss significant [[equity]] upside in 2023-2024

## Style & influence

Rosenberg is known for thorough, data-heavy analysis delivered in his signature "Breakfast with Dave" format — a daily morning note that became essential reading on [[Wall Street]]. His style is methodical and academic, grounding arguments in historical analogs and leading indicator data. He coined the phrase "this is not your father's cycle" to describe structural shifts. While his bearish bias has been costly at times, his 2008 call cemented his reputation as someone who will stand against consensus when his indicators flash warning. He's widely quoted in financial media and maintains a large following among institutional investors.
