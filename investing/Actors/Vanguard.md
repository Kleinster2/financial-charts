**Vanguard** — the world's second-largest asset manager (~$10.4T AUM as of Jan 2025) and largest provider of mutual funds globally. Pioneer of index investing. Unique mutual ownership structure: the funds own [[Vanguard]], so it operates at cost, passing scale economies directly to investors as lower fees.

[[Vanguard]] is the structural anomaly of the asset management industry. Founded in 1975 by [[John Bogle]] on the thesis that most active managers cannot beat the market after fees, the firm introduced the first retail index mutual fund and built an empire around the idea that cost is the most reliable predictor of investment returns. The mutual ownership structure — where the funds themselves own the management company — means Vanguard has no external shareholders extracting profit. Every dollar of scale goes back to investors as lower expense ratios. This is not a marketing line; it is the firm's legal architecture, and it explains why Vanguard's average expense ratio sits at 0.06% vs. the industry average of 0.50%.

The scale is enormous: ~$10.4T in global AUM as of January 2025, 50+ million investors, ~20,000 employees, offices in 17 countries. [[Vanguard]] is the largest mutual fund provider in the world and the second-largest ETF issuer behind [[BlackRock]]'s [[iShares]], with ~$3.95T in ETF assets alone across 103 ETFs. The flagship [[VTI]] (Total Stock Market ETF) crossed $1.5T in AUM in 2024, making it the largest fund in the world by assets. Under CEO Salim Ramji (appointed July 2024, the first outsider CEO in the firm's history), Vanguard has executed the largest fee cuts in its history — $350M in annual savings in 2025, followed by another round in 2026 covering 53 additional funds — and reversed its long-standing ban on crypto ETFs, opening the platform to [[Bitcoin]] and [[Ethereum]] ETFs in December 2025.

The political dimension cannot be ignored. [[Vanguard]], [[BlackRock]], and [[State Street]] — the "Big Three" — collectively cast proxy votes on ~25% of [[S&P 500]] shares. This concentration has drawn antitrust scrutiny, state attorney general investigations, and a Texas-led multi-state lawsuit (settled February 2026) alleging that coordinated ESG engagement harmed competition. Vanguard has responded by launching proxy voting choice programs and positioning itself as more conservative on ESG resolutions than its Big Three peers, supporting only ~28% of social and environmental shareholder proposals. The tension between passive scale and active governance is Vanguard's defining political challenge of the 2020s.

## Ownership structure

Vanguard has no external shareholders and no funding rounds. The mutual ownership model means the Vanguard funds themselves own The Vanguard Group, Inc. This is unique among major asset managers:

| Structure | Owner | Implication |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Mutual ownership | Vanguard's funds (i.e., fund shareholders) | No external profit extraction |
| At-cost operation | N/A — no equity investors | Fees decline as AUM scales |
| No IPO path | N/A | Cannot be acquired or taken public |
| Board governance | Fund boards (independent of management) | Board appoints CEO, sets policy |

This structure means Vanguard cannot raise equity capital, cannot be acquired, and has no shareholder pressure to maximize short-term revenue. It is the structural reason Vanguard's expense ratios are consistently below competitors. The trade-off: Vanguard has historically underinvested in technology and customer service relative to publicly traded competitors like [[BlackRock]] and Schwab, because there is no equity cushion to fund capex beyond what the at-cost model generates.

## Funding history

Not applicable. Vanguard is a mutual company owned by its funds (and by extension, its fund shareholders). It has never raised external equity capital, issued stock, or conducted funding rounds. The at-cost structure means all operating revenue comes from fund expense ratios, which are reinvested or returned to investors as fee reductions.

## Evolution

The story of [[Vanguard]] is the story of one man's firing becoming the most consequential event in the history of retail investing — and the company he built in response reshaping the entire financial industry around a single, relentless idea: cost matters more than anything.

1951–1974: [[John Bogle]] graduated from Princeton in 1951 with a thesis on mutual fund economics that caught the eye of Walter Morgan, founder of [[Wellington Management]]. Bogle rose through the ranks and eventually became CEO of Wellington Management Company. In 1966, he approved a merger with the Boston firm Thorndike, Doran, Paine & Lewis, which ran the aggressive Ivest Fund — a decision Bogle later called the biggest mistake of his career. When the 1973–74 bear market hammered both Wellington and Ivest, the Boston partners banded together and fired Bogle as CEO on January 24, 1974. The irony was total: being fired by an actively managed fund company pushed Bogle toward the passive investing revolution that would eventually marginalize active management.

1975–1976: Rather than walk away from the industry, Bogle persuaded the Wellington fund boards (which were technically independent of Wellington Management) to create a new entity to handle fund administration. On May 1, 1975, The Vanguard Group was born — named after Lord Nelson's flagship HMS Vanguard. The critical structural innovation was mutual ownership: the funds would own Vanguard, and Vanguard would operate at cost. This eliminated the profit motive that inflated fees at every other asset manager. In August 1976, Bogle launched the First Index Investment Trust (now [[VOO|Vanguard 500 Index Fund]]), the first index mutual fund available to retail investors. It raised just $11M in its IPO against a target of $150M. The underwriting banks urged him to cancel. Wall Street derided it as "Bogle's Folly" and called passive indexing "un-American." Bogle refused to shut it down.

1977–1995: The fund languished for years. By 1980, First Index had just $100M in assets — a rounding error. But the math was relentless: year after year, the index fund beat most active managers after fees, exactly as Bogle's Princeton thesis had predicted. Through the 1980s bull market, money slowly came. Vanguard crossed $100B in total AUM by 1993. Bogle expanded the product line methodically — total stock market, bond, international — always with the same at-cost structure. [[Wellington Management]], the firm that had fired him, remained Vanguard's largest external subadvisor for actively managed funds, a partnership that endures to this day. Bogle served as CEO through 1996, building the firm to roughly $230B in AUM.

1996–1999: Bogle's health forced a transition. He had suffered cardiac arrhythmia since the 1960s and received a heart transplant in 1996 at age 66. He stepped down as CEO that year, handing the role to John "Jack" Brennan, whom he had hired in 1982. Bogle retained the title of senior chairman, but friction emerged quickly. Brennan wanted to expand into brokerage services and online fund supermarkets; Bogle viewed this as mission creep that threatened the low-cost philosophy. When Bogle hit Vanguard's mandatory retirement age of 70 in 1999, he asked the board for a waiver. It refused — a decision widely interpreted as Brennan consolidating control. Bogle departed, establishing the Bogle Financial Markets Research Center on the Vanguard campus but formally separated from the firm he founded. He spent his remaining years as an industry gadfly, publishing books and giving speeches criticizing the mutual fund industry's fee structures. He died on January 16, 2019, at 89.

2000–2007: Under Brennan (CEO 1996–2007), [[Vanguard]] rode the secular shift toward indexing. The dot-com bust vindicated Bogle's philosophy — investors who had chased active growth managers were punished, and Vanguard's steady inflows accelerated. Brennan expanded the ETF lineup, launching Vanguard's first ETFs in 2001 as a share class of existing index funds (a patent-protected structure that provided tax advantages over competitors). AUM crossed $1T by 2006. Brennan also pushed international expansion, opening offices in Tokyo (2000), Singapore (2003), and Melbourne (2005). He retired in 2008, succeeded by Bill McNabb.

2008–2017: McNabb became CEO in 2008, arriving just in time for the global financial crisis — arguably the best thing that could have happened for an at-cost index fund company. As the crisis destroyed faith in active management, money poured into low-cost passive vehicles. McNabb presided over the most explosive growth era in Vanguard's history: AUM nearly quintupled from ~$1.25T in 2008 to ~$5T by the time he stepped down in 2017. He built out Vanguard Personal Advisor Services (crossing $100B in advised assets), expanded target-date funds into the largest suite in the industry, and opened the London office (2009) that became the beachhead for European expansion. The "Vanguard effect" — competitors being forced to cut fees just to retain assets — became an industry term. McNabb became chairman in 2010 and held both roles until handing the CEO title to Tim Buckley in January 2018.

2018–2024: Buckley's six-year tenure (2018–2024) was defined by continued asset gathering ($750B+ per year in net flows), technology investment, and growing political friction. AUM grew from ~$5T to over $9T. He pushed Vanguard's digital advisory tools and expanded internationally. But the ESG backlash caught Vanguard in the crossfire: as one of the Big Three passive giants, Vanguard found its proxy voting under political scrutiny from both sides. Red-state attorneys general accused the firm of using its voting power to advance climate and social agendas at the expense of returns; progressive critics accused it of not going far enough. In December 2022, Vanguard withdrew from the Net Zero Asset Managers initiative, attempting to depoliticize its stance. In February 2024, Buckley announced his retirement after 33 years at Vanguard — six as CEO — surprising the industry with an abrupt departure.

2024–present: Salim Ramji took over as CEO on July 8, 2024 — the first outsider to lead Vanguard in its history. Ramji spent a decade at [[BlackRock]], most recently as Global Head of [[iShares]] & Index Investing, where he oversaw the majority of BlackRock's client assets and helped architect the spot [[Bitcoin]] ETF ([[IBIT]]). Before BlackRock, he was a Senior Partner at [[McKinsey]]. His appointment signaled that Vanguard's board wanted a leader who understood the competitive ETF landscape from the inside. Ramji moved quickly: in early 2025, he executed the largest expense ratio reduction in Vanguard's history, cutting fees across 168 share classes for $350M+ in annual savings, followed by another $250M round in early 2026. In December 2025, he reversed Vanguard's ban on crypto ETFs, allowing clients to trade [[Bitcoin]], [[Ethereum]], XRP, and Solana products on the platform (though Vanguard has no plans to launch its own crypto funds). The crypto reversal was notable given that Ramji had helped build the very product ([[IBIT]]) that Vanguard had previously refused to list.

## Proxy voting and political exposure

The Big Three ([[Vanguard]], [[BlackRock]], [[State Street]]) collectively hold voting power over ~25% of [[S&P 500]] shares. One of the three is the largest shareholder in 88% of S&P 500 companies. This concentration has produced a multi-front political battle:

- 2022: Vanguard withdrew from the Net Zero Asset Managers initiative
- Spring 2023: 21 state attorneys general sent letters warning Vanguard and peers that pursuing ESG goals at the expense of returns may violate fiduciary duties
- 2023–2024: Vanguard launched proxy voting choice programs, allowing institutional clients to direct their own votes
- February 2026: Vanguard settled a Texas-led multi-state lawsuit alleging that coordinated ESG engagement harmed competition, particularly in the coal sector

Vanguard's proxy voting record is notably more conservative than [[BlackRock]] or [[State Street]] on ESG: it supported only ~28% of social and environmental shareholder resolutions, compared to higher rates at its Big Three peers. When [[State Street]] cast a minority vote among the three, it tended to vote "For" resolutions the others opposed; when Vanguard was the outlier, it tended to vote "Against."
