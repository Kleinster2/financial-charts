## Why Grimes matters

The relationship is the asset, but the SpaceX mandate shows its limits. Grimes has been Musk's banker of record across roughly 25 years, from Tesla's pre-IPO financing through the X / xAI / SpaceX consolidations and now the SpaceX listing. The FT's May 15 reporting named him as the central Morgan Stanley figure in the five-bank group; the May 21 follow-up says Goldman had already done years of preparation, drafted the S-1 in December 2025, and ultimately took the role that controls pricing, valuation, roadshow, and allocation decisions.

His one-year stint inside the Trump administration overlapped with the policy environment that produced AI-export-control rollbacks and the index-rule revisions ([[Nasdaq Fast Entry Rule]], the [[S&P 500]] seasoning review) that make a trillion-dollar-scale listing structurally viable. But the same detour seems to have changed his mandate mix. FT says that after returning in February 2026 he spent much of his time on Commerce-linked strategic transactions in rare earths, semiconductors, and other politically sensitive industries rather than daily IPO execution.

For Morgan Stanley, the outcome is a reputational bruise rather than an exclusion. The bank still has the stabilization mandate and remains a senior participant, and FT says [[Kate Claassen]] and [[Colin Stewart]] have been involved every day. For Grimes personally, the SpaceX IPO is now the case study in how quickly a relationship moat can narrow when a rival has embedded itself in the workstream.

*Source: [Financial Times - Goldman Sachs eclipses Morgan Stanley's Michael Grimes on SpaceX IPO](https://www.ft.com/content/10cecc4e-dbce-477d-9f10-068581c6e650), May 21 2026.*
