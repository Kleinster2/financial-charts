## Investment lens

In her Apr 24 2026 Bloomberg Tech interview, Price-Wright framed her thesis around critical industries ("manufacturing, energy, supply chain, critical minerals and mining") that "have not been disrupted by technology for decades" and where the engineering work has to happen on the ground rather than from a San Francisco office. She referenced her [[Palantir]] background as the source of that posture: Palantir's habit of "running toward the fire" — taking on hard, controversial, unsexy problems with users and customers in the field — shaped how she now evaluates portfolio companies.

The translation into [[Ulysses]]: the company built commercial UUV use cases first (seagrass planting, seafloor restoration, energy and port inspection) under tight cost discipline ($40-60K per unit), and only then pivoted to defense applications. Price-Wright's argument is that the cost-curve discipline imposed by commercial customers is what makes the same vehicles deployable at scale for the [[Department of War]] — the opposite of starting with a defense contract and trying to bring costs down later.

She also said the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran war]] has acted as a "forcing function" on Department of War procurement, with the agency now asking early-stage companies "how fast can you deliver 100, 1,000, 10,000" rather than running long acquisition cycles. The undersea cable cuts in the Russia-Ukraine war and the [[Strait of Hormuz]] disruptions are her two cited proof points for why the maritime domain is suddenly a procurement priority.
