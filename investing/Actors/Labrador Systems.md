**Labrador Systems** — Home assistive robot company building Retriever, an autonomous mobile shelf that fetches and carries items for elderly and mobility-impaired users. Founded 2017 by ex-Evolution Robotics engineers. Grant + seed funded (~$5.5M total), subscription pricing ($150/month).

---

## Synopsis

Labrador's bet is radical simplicity. Where [[Hello Robot]] builds a general-purpose manipulator arm and humanoid companies build complex bipeds, Labrador built what is essentially a smart autonomous table on wheels. The Retriever carries up to 25 lbs, navigates tight home spaces, adjusts height to the user's position, and docks itself for charging. No arm, no gripper, no manipulation — just reliable fetch-and-carry.

The business model is subscription: $150/month until the robot is paid off, then a lower ongoing fee for support. First customers are senior living facilities (Masonic Homes of California, Eskaton, Presbyterian Villages of Michigan), with [[Amazon]] Alexa integration for voice control. Nationwide Insurance partnered for a pilot — insurance companies see assistive robots as a way to reduce home care costs and extend independent living.

The founding team came from Evolution Robotics, where they shipped the Mint robotic floor cleaner (later acquired by [[iRobot]]). That pedigree matters — they've built and shipped consumer robots before. Total funding is tiny (~$5.5M from SOSV/HAX, [[Amazon]] Alexa Fund, [[iRobot]] Ventures), but the product is in customers' hands.

## Retriever Pro

Autonomous mobile shelf robot for home assistive use:

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Payload | 25 lbs |
| Navigation | Autonomous, tight-space capable |
| Height adjustment | Automatic based on user position |
| Charging | Self-docking, ~4 hours full charge |
| Connectivity | Wi-Fi + Bluetooth |
| Voice control | [[Amazon]] Alexa integration |
| Multi-user | Pro version supports caregiver logins, scheduling, activity reports |

Designed for people with chronic pain, mobility limitations, or age-related decline. Carries meals, laundry baskets, medications — tasks that require walking back and forth, which is where fall risk concentrates.

## Founders

Mike Dooley (CEO) and Nikolai Romanov (CTO) were part of the team that launched the Mint robotic floor cleaner at Evolution Robotics in 2010. Mint was later acquired by [[iRobot]], becoming the Braava line. [[iRobot]] Ventures' investment in Labrador reflects that relationship.

## Investment case

Bull:
- Simplest possible product for a real need — fetch-and-carry covers a large share of daily mobility challenges
- Subscription model creates recurring revenue
- Insurance company interest (Nationwide pilot) suggests potential for payer-subsidized distribution
- Founders shipped consumer robots before (Mint → [[iRobot]] Braava)
- Aging demographics = structural demand

Bear:
- Tiny funding ($5.5M) limits growth
- No manipulation capability — can't open doors, pick items off shelves, assist with personal care
- Consumer/home robotics graveyard (Jibo, Kuri, Anki)
- Subscription model requires long customer lifetime to recoup hardware cost
- Senior living facilities are slow-moving, price-sensitive buyers
