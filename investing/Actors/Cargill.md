#actor #company #agriculture #commodities #us

Cargill Incorporated — US-based privately held global food and agriculture conglomerate. One of the largest private companies in the world by revenue. Major commodity trader, processor, and distributor across grains, oilseeds, meat, cocoa, sugar, and more. Produces ~11bn lbs of beef annually, making it one of North America's largest beef processors.

## AI in beef processing — CarVe

Cargill has developed an AI-powered computer vision system called CarVe that spots "red pixels" — tiny flecks of meat left clinging to bone and fat as cattle carcasses move down processing lines. Early trials show meat packers can recover an average of up to 0.5% more meat per animal. At Cargill's ~11bn lbs/year scale, that translates to roughly 55mn additional pounds of beef annually — worth approximately $200mn at current wholesale prices (FT, March 21, 2026).

The system scans each carcass in real time, identifying red meat versus bone. Performance data appears instantly on screens at cutting stations, with each workstation color-coded red, yellow, or green to signal how much meat has been left on the carcass. Supervisors can replay video clips to coach workers on extraction technique. Currently deployed at Cargill's beef plants in Friona, [[Texas]] and Fort Morgan, Colorado, with plans for further rollout.

Florian Schattenmann (Cargill head of R&D): *"It's not like assembling a car, right? Every Toyota Camry looks the same and it's all automated. Every cow is slightly different"* (March 2026).

Cargill also uses AI to predict shift attendance at meat packing plants, drawing on employee tenure, weather, holidays, and even local high school football schedules. Per Schattenmann: *"You feed this with an insane amount of internal and external data"* (March 2026).

Industry-wide, the [[USDA]] reports total US beef production exceeds 27bn lbs/year. Cargill estimates a similar 0.5% improvement across the industry would add ~135mn lbs to the food system annually.

## US cattle herd context (2026)

The push for incremental efficiency gains comes as the US cattle herd contracts to its smallest level in 75 years. [[USDA]] inventory as of January 1, 2026 showed the national herd at 86.2mn head, down from a year earlier after years of drought prompted ranchers to thin herds. This has driven cattle prices sharply higher and pushed retail beef to record levels — ground beef prices in January 2026 were up ~17% YoY, far outpacing broader grocery inflation. Demand remains resilient despite higher prices, with steakhouses cutting portion sizes and lifting menu prices to manage margin pressure (FT, March 2026).

## Ownership

Cargill has been privately held since its founding in 1865. Ownership is concentrated among descendants of founder William W. Cargill and the MacMillan family (who married into the Cargill family in the early 1900s). No outside funding rounds — the company has never gone public. By the 2020s, the extended family comprised ~100+ shareholders, with periodic tension between family members seeking liquidity and those favoring long-term reinvestment.

## Cocoa alternatives — dual-track portfolio

Cargill's cocoa and chocolate business is one of the largest global processors, which makes West [[Africa]] supply risk a first-order procurement problem. The 2024-25 cocoa price spike (bean prices to ~$12,000/tonne, cocoa butter over €40,000/tonne; see [[Cocoa]]) prompted a dual-track portfolio hedge:

| Partner | Country | Technology | Role |
|---------|---------|------------|------|
| [[Kokomodo]] ([[Pluri]] subsidiary) | [[Israel]] | Cell-cultured cacao + coffee | Development collaboration — beverages, dairy, confectionery (sensory, scalability) |
| [[Voyage Foods]] | US | Cocoa-free chocolate (grape seed, sunflower protein) | Distribution partnership |

The positioning is instructive: Cargill is not picking a technology. Cell culture (Kokomodo) aims at a drop-in replacement for cocoa butter and wins if biotech scale-up works; ingredient substitution (Voyage) sidesteps cocoa entirely and wins on near-term economics if cell-culture bioreactors stay expensive. Either way, Cargill owns the distribution relationship into its existing chocolate-industry customer base.

Parallel moves from other chocolate-industry processors and brands: [[Mondelez]] backs [[Celleste Bio]] (cell-based, Israel); [[Lindt Spruengli]] backs [[Food Brewer]] (cell-based, Switzerland). See [[Cellular agriculture]] for the full landscape.
