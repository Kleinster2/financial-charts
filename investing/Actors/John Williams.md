# John Williams

President of the [[Federal Reserve Bank of New York]] since June 2018 and permanent Vice Chair of the [[Federal Reserve|FOMC]]. The NY Fed seat is the most market-sensitive Reserve Bank presidency: it executes open-market operations on behalf of the system, manages the Fed's foreign-exchange book, and chairs the [[Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee]] interface. Williams's public commentary therefore operates one rung below the Chair in market-moving weight.

## Why Williams matters in 2026

The 2026 [[Operation Epic Fury|Iran war]] split the Fed into a stagflation debate that has played out in the [[Federal Reserve#April 29, 2026 decision|April 29 hold]] (8-4, with [[Stephen Miran]] dissenting for a cut and three regional presidents dissenting against an easing bias). As the permanent Vice Chair, Williams's framing of the conflict's macro impact carries more weight than any other regional president's because his vote is not contingent on rotation.

## May 4, 2026 — "Wide-ranging" supply shock warning

In comments reported by the [[Financial Times]] on May 4, Williams warned of the risk of a "wide-ranging" supply shock as the [[Operation Epic Fury|Iran war]] drags on with no resolution in sight. The framing is significant on two dimensions:

1. *Channel.* "Wide-ranging" implies the shock is no longer confined to crude/refined-product prices but is propagating into broader supply chains — consistent with the mechanism described in [[Iran conflict supply chain cascade]] (Detroit auto OEMs $5B commodity hit, aluminum +16% LME) and the [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium#Twin with shale supply discipline (Apr 2026)|premium-thesis supply rigidity]].
2. *Reaction function.* A supply-shock framing weakens the case for cuts that [[Stephen Miran]] dissented for on Apr 29 and strengthens the hawkish dissents (Hammack, Kashkari, Logan) that objected to keeping an easing bias in the statement. Williams telegraphing supply-shock risk is the most senior post-Apr-29 signal that the May FOMC is unlikely to revert to a dovish reaction function on energy-driven inflation.

The comment landed on a day when [[United States|US]] and [[Iran]] forces exchanged fire near the [[Strait of Hormuz]] and the [[UAE]] intercepted Iranian missiles and drones (first attack on UAE since the ceasefire). Stocks slipped from record highs; oil and Treasuries sold off on the UAE strike — the kind of cross-asset signature that requires a Fed reaction function adjustment.

*Source: [[Financial Times|FT]], May 4, 2026 (live blog: "Iran war upends popular trades / supply shock"); FT, "Donald Trump's Iran war leaves US with sharpest fuel shock in G7" (May 4).*
