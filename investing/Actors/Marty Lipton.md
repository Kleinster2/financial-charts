# Marty Lipton

Co-founder (1965) of [[Wachtell Lipton]] and inventor (1982) of the [[Poison pill]] / shareholder rights plan — arguably the single most consequential US corporate-law innovation of the late 20th century. Now 94, Lipton still ventures into the firm's Midtown Manhattan office almost daily; the trophy cabinet on the 31st floor — mementoes from grateful clients across six decades — is the public symbol of what the FT (May 2026) calls "monument to dealmaking success."

## Influence

The 1982 [[Poison pill]] gave corporate boards the ability to dilute hostile acquirers and effectively block hostile tender offers. The mechanism reshaped US M&A defence, made boards rather than markets the gatekeepers of corporate control, and is now standard practice globally. Lipton's writings on corporate governance — including the 35 founding principles he set out at Wachtell's 1990 25th anniversary, beginning with "the firm is not a business" — are archived at the [[University of Pennsylvania]] and remain the canonical statement of the lockstep, one-office, bet-the-company-only model that [[Wachtell Lipton]] still operates on.

The [[Big Law transformation]] cycle now testing Wachtell is, in the most direct sense, a test of whether Lipton's model survives him.
