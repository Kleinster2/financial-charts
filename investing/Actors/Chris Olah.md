# Chris Olah

Chris Olah is a co-founder of [[Anthropic]] and the leading figure in mechanistic interpretability — the subfield of AI safety research focused on understanding what neural networks actually compute, rather than just testing their outputs. His work on feature visualization, circuits, and superposition has defined the intellectual framework that [[Anthropic]] uses to argue its safety differentiation against [[OpenAI]] and [[Google DeepMind]].

---

## Synopsis

Olah's career arc tracks the evolution of neural network interpretability from academic curiosity to strategic differentiator. He joined [[Google]] Brain in 2017 after earlier stints at the University of Toronto (working with [[Geoffrey Hinton]]) and [[OpenAI]], where he was part of the original research team. His 2017-2020 "Circuits" thread at Google Brain — a series of papers demonstrating that individual neurons and small circuits inside vision models perform interpretable computations (curve detectors, edge detectors, high-low frequency detectors) — established that neural networks aren't black boxes by necessity. They can be reverse-engineered, neuron by neuron.

In early 2021, Olah left [[OpenAI]] alongside [[Dario Amodei]], [[Daniela Amodei]], and four other researchers to co-found [[Anthropic]]. The departure was driven by disagreements over OpenAI's safety practices and commercialization pace. At Anthropic, Olah leads the interpretability team, which has produced landmark work on superposition (how models compress more features than they have neurons), sparse autoencoders for feature extraction, and scaling monosemanticity. The practical implication: if you can identify what features a model activates when it generates harmful content, you can intervene at a mechanistic level rather than relying on RLHF surface-level patches. This is the technical foundation of Anthropic's pitch to enterprise and government customers — that Claude's safety is architectural, not just behavioral. Still at Anthropic as of March 2026.
