# Masimo

**Masimo** is a medical technology company that pioneered Signal Extraction Technology (SET) pulse oximetry — the first system capable of accurate blood oxygen readings during patient motion and low perfusion. Founded in 1989, the company is being acquired by [[Danaher]] for $180/share ($9.9B enterprise value) in an all-cash deal announced February 17, 2026, expected to close H2 2026.

---

## Synopsis

Masimo is a medtech acquisition story where activist intervention unlocked a sale. The core asset is SET pulse oximetry, a genuine technological moat: conventional pulse oximeters fail during motion artifact and low perfusion, and Masimo's signal-processing algorithms solved that in 1995. The installed base spans hospitals globally, with ~$1.5B in healthcare revenue (FY2025 preliminary) growing at 9% and a record $432M in new contract value in FY2024. A $634M jury verdict against [[Apple]] in November 2025 over Apple Watch blood oxygen IP adds a material one-time tailwind, though Apple is appealing.

The deal catalyst was [[Politan Management]]. After founder [[Joe Kiani]] destroyed ~$680M of value with the $1.03B [[Sound United]] consumer audio acquisition in 2022 (sold to [[Harman International|HARMAN]]/[[Samsung]] for $350M in 2025), Politan ran back-to-back proxy fights in 2023-2024, won four board seats, and forced Kiani out. The reconstituted board — led by interim CEO [[Michelle Brennan]] (ex-[[Johnson & Johnson]], 30+ years) — divested Sound United, refocused on healthcare, and agreed to the Danaher sale within 18 months. [[Danaher]] is paying 40% premium to pre-announcement ($130.15 on Feb 13). Stock trades at $178.44 (March 23), a $1.56 arb spread to the $180 deal price, implying high confidence in closure. Danaher expects $125M annual cost synergies and $50M revenue synergies by year 5. Masimo will operate as a standalone unit in Danaher's Diagnostics segment.

## Evolution

The story of Masimo is the story of a genuine medical innovation nearly derailed by founder overreach, then rescued by activist pressure and a strategic acquirer.

1989: [[Joe Kiani]], an electrical engineer and Iranian-American immigrant, founded Masimo in a garage with $40,000 from a second mortgage on his condominium. Co-developer Mohamed Diab joined to tackle the central problem in pulse oximetry: conventional devices measured blood oxygen by pulsing light through tissue, but motion — a patient shivering, a nurse adjusting a monitor — produced false readings that triggered alarm fatigue in hospitals. The technical insight was that arterial blood signals could be mathematically separated from venous blood, skin, and motion noise through adaptive signal processing. They called it Signal Extraction Technology (SET).

1995-2005: FDA cleared SET pulse oximetry in 1995. The product worked where competitors ([[Philips]] Nellcor, [[Medtronic]]) failed — neonatal ICUs, ambulances, field hospitals. More than 100 independent studies validated SET's superiority. Masimo expanded into rainbow SET (noninvasive hemoglobin, carboxyhemoglobin, methemoglobin measurement) and built a sensor-and-equipment razor/blade model: hospitals bought the monitors, then kept buying proprietary single-use sensors. The installed base grew steadily through clinical evidence and exclusive hospital system contracts.

2007-2019: IPO on NASDAQ in August 2007 at $17/share. Revenue grew from ~$340M (2009) to ~$1.0B (2019), a tripling driven by new measurement parameters (SedLine brain function monitoring, O3 regional oximetry, Root patient monitoring platform) and international expansion. The stock reached ~$246 by late 2019. Kiani cultivated a founder-CEO mystique — part inventor, part philanthropist, part political operator.

2022: Kiani acquired [[Sound United]] (Bowers & Wilkins, Denon, Marantz, Polk Audio, Definitive Technology) for $1.03B in April 2022 — a consumer audio conglomerate with zero overlap with medical devices. The market's verdict was instant: stock dropped 37% in 24 hours, erasing ~$5B of market cap. The strategic logic — that wearable health sensors would converge with consumer electronics — was plausible in theory but premature in execution. [[Politan Management]]'s [[Quentin Koffey]] saw an opportunity.

2023: Politan launched its first proxy contest in May, nominating Koffey himself and [[Michelle Brennan]] (ex-[[Johnson & Johnson]]) to Masimo's board. Kiani fought back with unprecedented defensive tactics, including attempting to force activists to disclose their other investments before seeking board nomination. Both Politan nominees were elected in June. The board now had two activist-aligned directors pushing for Sound United divestiture.

2024: Politan launched a second proxy fight in March, nominating Darlene Solomon and Bill Jellison. Masimo delayed the vote and sued Politan for alleged misstatements. Politan countersued. The September 2024 shareholder vote delivered a decisive result: both Politan nominees won, giving the activist four of the board seats. Kiani resigned as CEO the same day (September 19). Brennan was named interim CEO. Kiani still held ~5% of shares (some sources reported up to 13.2%).

2025: The reconstituted board moved quickly. Sound United was sold to [[Harman International|HARMAN]] ([[Samsung]] subsidiary) for $350M in May 2025, closing September 2025 — a $680M loss on Kiani's $1.03B acquisition. The healthcare business, now unburdened, showed its strength: FY2024 healthcare revenue grew and the company signed a record $432M in new contracts. In November, a federal jury awarded Masimo $634M in its patent dispute with [[Apple]] over blood oxygen monitoring technology in the Apple Watch. Apple intends to appeal, but the verdict validated Masimo's IP portfolio. Shares rose 40% from Politan's September 2024 board victory through year-end.

February 2026: [[Danaher]] announced the $180/share all-cash acquisition ($9.9B EV) on February 17. MASI jumped from $130.15 to $174.69 in a single session (+34%). Danaher — the life sciences conglomerate that had acquired [[Cytiva]] for $21.4B (2019) and [[Abcam]] for $5.7B (2024) — saw Masimo as a way to strengthen its Diagnostics segment in acute care patient monitoring. Deal unanimously approved by both boards. Expected close H2 2026, subject to regulatory clearance and shareholder vote. Politan (~9% stake) stands to collect ~$900M at $180/share.

## Apple patent dispute

Masimo and [[Apple]] have been in litigation since 2020 over pulse oximetry IP used in the Apple Watch. The dispute spanned multiple courts and 25+ patents asserted by Masimo.

- October 2023: US International Trade Commission (ITC) issued an exclusion order forcing Apple to disable the blood oxygen sensor in Apple Watch Series 9 and Ultra 2 sold in the US
- November 15, 2025: California federal jury awarded Masimo $634M in damages, finding Apple infringed a Masimo patent covering low-power pulse oximeter features across ~43M Apple Watches. Apple had argued damages should be $3-6M.
- Apple is appealing. The patent in question expired in 2022, limiting ongoing royalties but not the lump-sum award.

The $634M verdict, if upheld, would accrue to [[Danaher]] post-close — a meaningful bonus on top of a $9.9B acquisition.
