#actor #energy #oil #cartel

**OPEC** — [[Oil]] cartel controlling ~40% of global production. OPEC+ includes [[Russia]] and allies (~55% of global). Sets production quotas to manage prices. Pending May 1, 2026: drops to 11 members with the [[2026 UAE OPEC exit|UAE exit]] from both OPEC and OPEC+ — the third major departure in seven years (Qatar 2019, Angola 2024, UAE 2026) and the first by a member at the peak of its capacity arc rather than the trough.

**Recent departures arc (1992-2026):**

| Member | Joined | Left | Stated reason | Underlying tension |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Indonesia | 1962 | Suspended Jan 2009; rejoined Jan 2016; suspended Nov 2016 | Couldn't meet quota; later quota too restrictive | Net importer dynamics |
| Ecuador | 1973 | Suspended Dec 1992; rejoined Oct 2007; withdrew Jan 1, 2020 | Membership fees; quota restrictions | Production-vs-fiscal trade-off |
| [[Qatar]] | 1961 | Jan 1, 2019 | LNG focus | GCC blockade rift with Saudi (June 2017+) |
| Angola | 2007 | Jan 1, 2024 | Quota dispute (1.46 → 1.11 mb/d) | Production decline + fiscal pressure |
| [[UAE]] | 1967 (via Abu Dhabi) | May 1, 2026 | "Pure policy change," capacity flexibility | Quota frustration + Saudi rift + war reordering |

The pattern across all five departures is the same core tension: OPEC's quota system restrains the highest-capacity producers to support price, and those producers eventually conclude that the cartel premium is smaller than the volume foregone. ## OPEC+ dynamics

**Saudi-[[Russia]] alliance (2016+):**
- Coordinates cuts between OPEC and non-OPEC
- Fragile — competing interests
- [[Russia]] often cheats on quotas
- Ukraine war strained but didn't break alliance

**Tensions:**
- UAE quota frustration crystallized into full exit (Apr 28, 2026 — see [[2026 UAE OPEC exit]])
- Iraq chronic overproducer
- African members struggling
- US shale complicates cuts (fills any gap)
- Saudi must absorb increased "heavy lifting on price stability" with UAE shock-absorber gone ([[Jorge Leon]] / [[Rystad Energy]], Apr 28)

**Russia's OPEC+ trap ([[Sergey Vakulenko]], March 2026):**
OPEC+ membership has become politically valuable for [[Russia]] as "one of Russia's last multilateral forums" — creating incentives to maintain quotas even when they constrain profitable production. Russia's production lagging quota by up to 400K bpd, but the quotas have destroyed Russia's project pipeline by making development uneconomic. The political lock-in prevents Russia from leaving despite economic costs, trapping Russia in a declining production trajectory. OPEC+ quotas are effectively accelerating Russia's structural oil sector decline.

## Iran conflict response (Mar 2026)

OPEC+ decided to unwind only 206K bbl/day during the [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran conflict]] — widely seen as inadequate given the scale of disruption (~20M+ bbl/day of [[Strait of Hormuz]] transit halted). [[FGE]] energy consultant Iman Nasseri (CNN, Mar 2): the response is "insignificant and basically not showing the level of concern or worry from the OPEC member countries." Market expecting emergency IEA stock release from OECD countries instead. If prices continue to reflect supply pressure, [[FGE]] believes both OPEC and OECD can bring new supply — but the signal so far is that OPEC is not stepping in.

### Apr 28 — UAE exit announcement

The [[UAE]] announced exit from both OPEC and OPEC+ effective May 1, 2026 — the cartel's most significant member loss since [[Qatar]] in 2019 and the first by a major exporter leaving at the peak of capacity rather than trough. Energy Minister [[Suhail Al Mazrouei]] explicitly tied the timing to the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure: with the market undersupplied, the exit doesn't immediately move balance. Per [[Jorge Leon]] ([[Rystad Energy]]), Saudi Arabia must now do "more of the heavy lifting on price stability" as the bloc loses a key "shock absorber." UAE production capacity is ~4.8 mb/d today, target 5 mb/d by 2027 via [[ADNOC]]'s $150B 2023-2027 capex; Baker Institute estimated ~$50B/yr revenue upside from unconstrained operation. The exit follows five days after [[Scott Bessent]] publicly defended a $20B currency swap line for the UAE before a Senate appropriations subcommittee — the financial-architecture context that makes the "pure policy change" framing difficult to detach from broader Gulf realignment. Mazrouei denied any political dispute with Saudi; Bloomberg's Versace called it "a blow for their cohesion." Full event detail: [[2026 UAE OPEC exit]].

The forward question is post-Hormuz: whether informal coordination replaces formal quota discipline, or whether unconstrained UAE production toward 5 mb/d at the same time as a rebuilt OPEC+ output triggers a market-share reset. Saudi's silence to date is itself a signal.

### Day 9 reality check (Mar 7)

At **Brent $92.69** (+33% since conflict start) and Hormuz traffic down 86%, OPEC+'s 206K bbl/day response is now comically inadequate — it represents ~1% of the disrupted flow. Key OPEC members are themselves under attack:
- [[Saudi Arabia]]'s largest oil refinery suspended production after Iranian drone strike near [[Saudi Aramco]]'s Ras Tanura
- [[Iraq]] shut Rumaila (world's largest oilfield) on storage constraints — can't produce if you can't ship
- [[Qatar]] (OPEC+ aligned) declared force majeure on LNG
- [[Iran]] itself (~2.5M bpd) is the target of the war

The cartel's traditional levers (quota adjustments, spare capacity deployment) are impotent when the chokepoint itself is closed and member states' infrastructure is being attacked. The crisis has exposed OPEC's structural limitation: it can manage supply volumes but cannot guarantee physical delivery when the shipping lanes are under military threat. The real supply response is coming from outside OPEC — US LNG exports, OECD strategic reserves, and pipeline rerouting.
