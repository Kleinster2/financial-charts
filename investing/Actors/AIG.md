# AIG

**AIG** — Global commercial P&C insurer, post-2008 turnaround now entering a growth phase under CEO [[Peter Zaffino]]. Separated life/retirement business into [[Corebridge Financial]] (CRBG), retaining ~50% stake. FY2025 General Insurance underwriting income $2.2B (+22%), combined ratio 90.1. Q1 2026 revenue $12.8B (+7% YoY), net income $1.4B. In active negotiations to join the [[US Development Finance Corporation|DFC]]/[[Chubb]] $20B Gulf maritime reinsurance program as a reinsurance partner — a reversal of its 2018 exit from Iran-related cargo insurance, enabled by the sovereign backstop removing tail risk. Stock dropped 3% on March 3 as insurers canceled war risk coverage for Gulf vessels.

## Iran conflict exposure (March 2026)

AIG stock dropped 3.02% on March 3 (vs insurance sector -2.13%) as the [[Iran conflict economic disruption|Iran conflict]] drove industry-wide war risk coverage cancellations for Gulf vessels. Drivers:
- Geopolitical tensions led to insurers canceling war risk coverage
- North American retail property and personal NPW already contracting due to "reduced market appetite and higher reinsurance costs"
- Rising Treasury yields linked to inflation/energy price concerns

AIG "acknowledged active participation in negotiations with the DFC" alongside [[Chubb]], [[Liberty Mutual]], and [[Lloyd's]] regarding the $20B Gulf maritime reinsurance facility (Clark Hill analysis). [[Chubb]] was named lead underwriter on March 11, with DFC and Chubb identifying "several American insurance companies" as reinsurance partners behind Chubb — AIG is likely among them, though the full partner list has not been formally disclosed. The government assumes first-loss or extreme risk, allowing private insurers to continue writing policies they would otherwise abandon.

The program structure: DFC provides the sovereign backstop ($20B rolling), Chubb leads the front-end underwriting, and reinsurance partners (AIG, Liberty Mutual, potentially others) provide capacity behind Chubb. Coverage: hull & machinery, cargo, war risk, political risk. London publications speculated the facility could "shift the center of maritime finance from London to Washington" if made permanent.

AIG previously exited Iran-related cargo insurance in 2018 following Trump sanctions reimposition (via Validus Holdings acquisition). Re-entry via a US government-backstopped facility represents a reversal — the sovereign guarantee removes the tail risk that would otherwise make Gulf war-zone underwriting untenable.

## 2008 crisis history

Near-collapse from CDS exposure via AIG Financial Products. $182B government bailout, systemic risk designation, decade-plus restructuring. Repaid bailout with profit to government, shed non-core assets, refocused on core insurance.

## Investment case

Bull:
- NA Commercial turnaround dramatic — combined ratio from 98.8 to 84.7
- Simplified structure post-Corebridge separation
- $7.09 adjusted EPS (+43%) shows earnings power
- Discount to [[Chubb]] despite improving fundamentals
- Share buyback plan ($1B+ for 2026)

Bear:
- Legacy long-tail liabilities
- Global Personal segment shrinking
- International Commercial margin compression (combined ratio worsened 5.7 pts Q4)
- DFC Gulf program: sovereign backstop limits tail risk, but AIG still exposed if losses exceed $20B cap
- Marine book growing (led Q1 International growth) — Gulf war risk adds upside premiums but convex downside
- Brand still carries 2008 stigma with some institutional investors

## Q1 2026 earnings — underwriting income tripled (Apr 30, 2026)

AIG reported Q1 2026 before the open on Apr 30. The General Insurance turnaround story compounded:

| Metric | Q1 2026 | YoY |
|--------|---------|-----|
| Adjusted after-tax EPS | $2.11 | +80% (vs $1.89 est, +11.6% beat) |
| Core operating ROE | 12.2% | — |
| GI net premiums written | $5.6B | +24% reported / +18% constant |
| GI underwriting income | $774M | +3.2x ($243M Q1 2025) |
| Calendar-year combined ratio | 87.3 | improved 8.5 pts (from 95.8) |

Segment NPW growth: NA Commercial +36%, International Commercial +12%, Global Personal +11% — supported by strategic transactions, reinsurance program changes, and organic growth in targeted areas. Capital returns: $760M total in Q1 ($519M buybacks of ~7M shares; $241M dividends). Board declared quarterly dividend of $0.50/share, +11% — fourth consecutive year of double-digit dividend growth.

Stock: AIG closed May 1 at $78.67, +5.2% on the day (+3.8σ on a low-vol name). The market read the print as confirmation that the [[AIG#Business segments|NA Commercial turnaround]] (combined ratio from 95.8 → 87.3) is durable, not a single-quarter blip. Underwriting income tripling YoY with the calendar-year combined ratio improving 8.5 points lifts AIG's earnings power closer to the [[Chubb]] benchmark, narrowing the structural valuation discount that has persisted post-2008.

Iran-conflict context: the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis|Hormuz crisis]]-driven [[Iran conflict insurance impact|war-risk repricing]] has been a tailwind to specialty premiums but the Q1 print pre-dates most of the war-risk premium flow-through (war started late February; the $20B [[US Development Finance Corporation|DFC Gulf reinsurance program]] is just becoming operational). The Q1 calendar-year CR includes minimal Gulf-war reinsurance contribution — the upside scenario is that Q2-Q3 prints layer in war-risk premium income on top of the underlying franchise improvement.
