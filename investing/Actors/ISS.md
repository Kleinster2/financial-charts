#actor #space #international #station

**International Space Station (ISS)** — Multi-agency crewed space station in low Earth orbit since 1998. Largest structure ever built in space (~$150B cumulative cost). Partners: [[NASA]] (US), Roscosmos ([[Russia]]), JAXA ([[Japan]]), ESA ([[Europe]]), CSA ([[Canada]]). Targeted retirement ~end of 2030 — the deadline that drives the [[Commercial LEO Destinations]] commercialization framework. Aging hardware (leak issues), Russia partnership tension, and the structural shift toward commercial alternatives are the three pressures pulling the program toward retirement.

## Why ISS matters

The ISS is the institutional backbone of human spaceflight in low Earth orbit. Its retirement timeline (~2030) is the structural deadline that drives:

1. [[Commercial LEO Destinations]] — NASA's program to transition from owning a station to being a customer of commercial alternatives ([[Vast Space|Vast]], [[Voyager]], [[Axiom Space]], [[Orbital Reef]]).
2. Sovereign-agency demand — [[Japan]], [[Europe]], [[UAE]], and [[Saudi Arabia]] all need a post-ISS LEO destination; this is the customer base commercial stations are designed to serve.
3. [[Boeing Starliner]] and [[Crew Dragon]] crew rotation — both capsules were built for ISS rotation; their futures hinge on what replaces ISS. [[Boeing Starliner]] CFT was reclassified [[Type A mishap]] Feb 2026, materially weakening Boeing's path to ongoing crew rotation.
4. Geopolitical tensions — [[Russia]] partnership has been the most politically fraught element since 2022; the post-ISS architecture is structurally a US-led commercial framework with sovereign-agency follow-on customers, not a US-Russia partnership.
