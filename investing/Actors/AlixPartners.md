**AlixPartners** is a global advisory firm specializing in turnaround, restructuring, and performance improvement consulting — the firm that invented the turnaround business and holds ~25% market share in bankruptcy advisory.

---

## Synopsis

[[AlixPartners]] was founded in 1981 by [[Jay Alix]], a Wharton-trained CPA who built the blueprint for corporate restructuring advisory. The firm's first major engagement — the [[DeLorean Motor Company]] bankruptcy in 1984, where it recovered $100M for creditors — set the template: get inside a dying company, find the money, and either save it or wind it down efficiently. Four decades later, AlixPartners handles 15-20 bankruptcy assignments annually and has advised on the biggest collapses in American corporate history: [[Enron]], [[WorldCom]], [[Lehman Brothers]], [[General Motors]], [[Chrysler]].

The firm is currently owned by a consortium of [[CDPQ]], [[PSP Investments]], and [[Investcorp]], who acquired a majority stake in 2016 at a $2.5B+ valuation. Jay Alix retains ~35% ownership. In January 2025, the owners began exploring a sale at a reported $5-8B valuation — a 2-3x markup in under a decade, driven by the firm's expansion from 1,500 professionals and 24 offices (2015) to ~3,500 employees and 27 offices (2026). The co-CEO transition in February 2025 — [[David Garfield]] (Chicago, ex-PwC, industries focus) and [[Rob Hornby]] (London, doubled EMEA revenues) replacing [[Simon Freakley]] (now Executive Chairman) — looks like pre-sale professionalization. The key risk is that restructuring advisory is cyclical: the firm's value peaks when the economy is weakest, making timing a sale tricky.

## Evolution

The story of AlixPartners is the story of how one man's invention — the corporate turnaround — became a billion-dollar industry, and what happens when the firm that created the playbook has to decide whether its own future lies with private equity or independence.

- 1981: [[Jay Alix]], a 29-year-old CPA with a Wharton BS and Rutgers MBA, founded Jay Alix & Associates PC in Southfield, Michigan (Detroit metro). He'd spent his early career at Price Waterhouse in New York investigating fraud in bankrupt insurers, and saw that companies in crisis needed dedicated operational advisers, not just lawyers and bankers. He essentially created the "turnaround" as a professional discipline. President Clinton later appointed him to the National Bankruptcy Review Commission in 1994 — the only non-attorney on the panel.

- 1984: The firm's first major case — the [[DeLorean Motor Company]] bankruptcy — established the model. AlixPartners found an additional $100M for creditors that nobody else had located. This wasn't just legal restructuring; it was forensic financial work inside the company. The DeLorean engagement proved the concept: a specialized advisory firm could add enormous value in bankruptcy proceedings.

- 1990s–2000s: The firm grew steadily on the back of major engagements — [[Enron]], [[WorldCom]], [[Kmart]] (where [[Al Koch]] served as interim CFO during the 2002 Chapter 11). By the early 2000s, AlixPartners had ~550 professionals across 12 offices. Jay Alix retired in 2000 at age 48 after his wife's accidental death, stepping back from day-to-day operations but retaining ownership. Fred Crawford took over as CEO in 2004.

- 2006: [[Hellman & Friedman]] invested at an ~$800M valuation, the first outside capital. This was a bet that the restructuring advisory model could scale globally — more offices, more industries, more crisis types. By this point the firm had moved beyond pure bankruptcy into performance improvement consulting.

- 2009: The financial crisis was AlixPartners' moment. [[Al Koch]] was appointed Chief Restructuring Officer of [[General Motors]] on May 31, 2009 — the most consequential corporate restructuring in American history. AlixPartners had been paid $40M pre-filing, stood to earn $13M+ through the process, and deployed nearly 70 employees on the case at rates of $180–$995/hr. The firm also advised on [[Chrysler]] and [[Lehman Brothers]] bankruptcies. These three engagements alone cemented AlixPartners' position as the dominant restructuring adviser globally.

- 2012: [[CVC Capital Partners]] acquired a majority stake from [[Hellman & Friedman]] at a reported $1B+ valuation. At the time: 125 Managing Directors, 950+ professionals, 17 offices. The CVC era was about geographic expansion and diversification beyond pure restructuring.

- 2015: [[Simon Freakley]] became co-CEO alongside Crawford in August, then sole CEO in January 2016. Freakley came via the Zolfo Cooper Europe acquisition ($100M, ~200 senior professionals, 19 MDs). Before Zolfo Cooper, he'd been CEO of [[Kroll|Kroll Inc.]] (65 offices, 33 countries, $1B revenue) — so he brought both restructuring credibility and global management experience. Under Crawford's tenure the firm had grown from 700 to 1,500 professionals and from 13 to 24 offices.

- 2016: [[CDPQ]], [[PSP Investments]], and [[Investcorp]] acquired a majority stake from [[CVC Capital Partners]] at $2.5B+ — a 2.5x markup in four years. The investors committed to a minimum 10-year hold, signaling this was a long-term bet on the advisory model, not a quick flip. Jay Alix retained his ~35% stake.

- 2018: Jay Alix filed a RICO lawsuit against [[McKinsey]] in SDNY seeking $100M+ in damages, alleging McKinsey's restructuring practice (RTS) systematically failed to disclose conflicts of interest — including McKinsey's internal hedge fund MIO Partners investing in distressed companies where McKinsey served as bankruptcy adviser. Alix had created Mar-Bow Value Partners in 2015 specifically to buy distressed debt and gain legal standing in bankruptcy courts to challenge McKinsey's disclosures. McKinsey settled with the US Trustee for $15M in February 2019 without admitting wrongdoing. Alix reportedly spent $1M+ in legal fees. The campaign revealed both Alix's combative character and a genuine structural problem in bankruptcy advisory — the big consulting firms were playing in restructuring without the disclosure standards the specialist firms followed.

- 2021: Acquired [[Galt & Company]], a strategy consulting firm (~80-100 employees, offices in NYC, Chicago, London, Singapore). This launched AlixPartners' Corporate Strategy & Transformation practice — a deliberate move to become more than a crisis shop. Clients included Coca-Cola, Clorox, Gillette.

- 2023: Acquired [[THM Partners]] LLP (UK, founded 2001, pioneered the CRO role in the UK, 40 staff in UK, Germany, Singapore). Continued build-out of European restructuring capabilities.

- Jan 2025: Two things happened almost simultaneously. First, [[David Garfield]] (Global Head of Industries, Chicago, ex-PwC) and [[Rob Hornby]] (EMEA leader who doubled EMEA revenues over 4 years, London, ex-Sky, ex-Andersen) were named co-CEOs effective February 1, with Freakley moving to Executive Chairman. Second, Bloomberg reported that CDPQ, PSP, and Investcorp were exploring a sale at a $5-8B valuation. The co-CEO structure and the sale exploration together suggest pre-exit positioning — professionalize leadership, prove the firm doesn't depend on any single figure.

- Mar 2026: Opened 27th office in Sydney, led by Peter Saville (founding partner of Zolfo Cooper). Target: 5 partners, 25 staff by year-end. The firm now generates 60% of income from performance improvement (non-restructuring) work — a meaningful shift from its crisis-only origins. Current engagement: running the [[BrewDog]] accelerated sale/breakup process (4 breweries, 72 bars; GBP 37M loss on GBP 357M turnover, GBP 148M cumulative losses since 2019).

## Jay Alix vs McKinsey

Jay Alix's personal campaign against [[McKinsey]]'s restructuring practice is both a competitive story and a governance one. Starting ~2014, Alix alleged that McKinsey systematically failed to disclose conflicts of interest in bankruptcy proceedings — specifically that McKinsey's internal hedge fund MIO Partners was investing in distressed companies where McKinsey served as bankruptcy adviser. In 2015, Alix created Mar-Bow Value Partners to buy distressed debt and gain legal standing in bankruptcy courts. In May 2018, he filed a RICO lawsuit in SDNY seeking $100M+. McKinsey settled with the US Trustee for $15M (Feb 2019) without admitting wrongdoing. Alix spent $1M+ in legal fees. The campaign mattered because it exposed a real structural gap: the big consulting firms were entering bankruptcy advisory without the disclosure standards specialist firms like AlixPartners followed. McKinsey does ~1 bankruptcy assignment per year vs AlixPartners' 15-20.

## Disruption Index

The AlixPartners Disruption Index (ADI) is the firm's flagship thought leadership product — an annual executive survey measuring the magnitude and complexity of disruption facing global businesses. Launched at Davos in January 2020 by [[Simon Freakley]], released each year at the [[World Economic Forum]] Annual Meeting. AlixPartners is a WEF strategic partner; in 2026 they jointly launched the Disruption Navigator on WEF's Strategic Intelligence platform.

### Methodology

The 2026 edition (7th annual) surveyed 3,200 executives (50% C-level, 50% at $1B+ revenue companies) across 11 countries (US, Canada, UK, France, Germany, Italy, Switzerland, China, Japan, Saudi Arabia, UAE) and 10 industries (~320 per industry). Fieldwork: Aug 14 – Sep 30, 2024, plus a 500-executive post-US-election supplement (Nov 12 – Dec 2, 2024). The composite score combines how many disruptive forces executives report with how powerful they rate each one.

The survey has expanded significantly since inception: the 2020 launch covered 635 companies across 5 industries; by 2024 it reached 3,200 executives, 11 countries, and 10 industries.

### 2026 key findings

- 48% of executives report high disruption (down from 57% in 2025)
- 70% of CEOs report high disruption vs 39% of other C-suite — perception gap
- 45% of CEOs fear losing their jobs; 40% more anxious than last year
- 51% of growth leaders have widely implemented agentic AI vs 14% of slower-growing companies
- 44% of CEOs expect layoffs of 10%+ within 5 years due to AI; 77% expect some AI-driven layoffs
- 46% cite cybersecurity/data privacy as top disruptive threat (healthcare: jumped 22% → 62% in one year)
- 72% say it's increasingly difficult to prioritize which disruptive forces matter (up from 67%)
- China highest country score at 77 (down from 83 in 2024); US at 72; Japan lowest at 64
- Automotive remains most disrupted sector globally (score 72, down from 76.7 in 2025)

### Use and positioning

The ADI is a sentiment/perception survey, not an investable index. It functions as a Davos-timed business development tool for AlixPartners' consulting practice, an annual CEO agenda-setter, and a benchmark that segments companies into "growth leaders" vs the rest. Widely covered by Bloomberg TV, Yahoo Finance, Automotive News. Not a ranking of named companies.
