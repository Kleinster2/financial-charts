# George Lee (Goldman Sachs)

**George Lee (Goldman Sachs)** - Co-head of the [[Goldman Sachs Global Institute]], former Goldman CIO, and former co-chairman of Goldman's global Technology, [[Media]], and Telecommunications investment-banking group. He is the technology-and-markets counterpart to [[Jared Cohen]]'s geopolitics-and-statecraft role.

Lee matters because he makes the Global Institute a Goldman product rather than a detached policy shop. Cohen brings the [[State Department]] / [[Jigsaw]] / geopolitics frame; Lee brings decades of technology banking, internal Goldman technology execution, and client-market translation. The pairing is the thesis: geopolitics only becomes investable when someone can connect it to clients, capex, deal flow, infrastructure, and market structure.

His public work is mostly about AI as a real economic and market transition rather than a narrow model race. Lee has been one of Goldman's more constructive AI voices: not blind to capex and ROI risk, but focused on declining inference costs, model performance gains, enterprise adoption, agentic workflows, and the way AI bottlenecks move across power, chips, cloud, data, talent, and capital.

## Goldman role

Lee's role is to turn the Institute's big geopolitical and technological questions into a markets conversation. When [[Jared Cohen]] frames AI as statecraft or sovereign capital as power projection, Lee is the person who can translate the same topic into Goldman-native questions: which clients need advice, where capex is going, whether the ROI debate is priced correctly, and what the deal-making implications are.

That makes Lee especially important for the AI side of the Institute. Goldman positions the Global Institute around geopolitics, technology, and markets; Lee is the technology and markets bridge. His background as former CIO gives him credibility inside Goldman on implementation, while his TMT-banking history gives him credibility with tech-company boards and investors.

---

## AI framework

Lee's AI view is constructive but not simplistic. In Goldman's June 2025 AI adoption interview, he argued that AI usage and hyperscaler investment were running ahead of prior expectations: leading-model usage had risen sharply, API token costs had fallen roughly 100x from early-2024 levels, [[OpenRouter]] token consumption had risen around 75x, and 2026 capex expectations for the four principal hyperscalers had moved from about $207B to roughly $405B. That is the Lee lens: track the empirical curve, then ask whether enterprise adoption and ROI can catch up.

His February 2025 AI Exchanges discussion after the [[DeepSeek]] shock is the cleaner example. The obvious market question was whether cheaper models undermine the AI capex buildout. Lee's answer was closer to the Jevons-paradox view: falling costs can broaden utility, unlock new use cases, and answer part of the skeptic case that AI would never become cheap enough for enterprise deployment. The risk does not disappear; it migrates from "can models work?" to "who captures value as capability gets cheaper?"

The market signal is that Lee is useful when the AI debate moves from model benchmarks to investment translation. His natural subjects are hyperscaler capex, enterprise adoption, [[Agentic AI|AI agents]], power as bottleneck, coding workflows, and whether AI moves from experimentation to scaled corporate productivity.
