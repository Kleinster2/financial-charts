## Early Palantir

In the early days of [[Palantir]] (2006–), Sankar roamed around giving demos to anyone who would watch. One of those demos reached [[Trae Stephens]], who was working in an intelligence agency. Stephens applied, flew to Palo Alto in a full suit with CIA cufflinks, and was intercepted by a receptionist who told him to dress down. Sankar and the team "loved him immediately" — Stephens became employee ~13 and helped build the government business.

On the early rejection: [[Sequoia]]'s Michael Moritz doodled through the entire Palantir pitch. [[Kleiner Perkins]] lectured the founders about their "inevitable failure." The government was unpopular in the Valley and nobody wanted to fund enterprise software for intelligence agencies. [[Peter Thiel]] bankrolled the initial $30M largely alone.

On building the business: "It was one of these things that was kind of a slow start." The founding thesis was that post-9/11, arguing privacy vs. security as a tradeoff was wrong — both matter, and nobody was pushing out the efficient frontier. Palantir started "myopically focused on solving a handful of problems in counterterrorism for a handful of institutions."

## "Heretics and heroes" framework

Sankar frames defense innovation through historical figures who drove change despite — or against — their institutions:

| Person | Innovation | Context |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Kelly Johnson | [[Skunk Works]] — 41 airframes including SR-71, U-2 | Had to play defense to keep government bureaucrats out. U-2 started because the military didn't want it — Johnson went through the intelligence community. |
| Admiral Rickover | Nuclear navy | One person driving institutional transformation |
| Benny Shriever | ICBMs | Eight competing programs during Cold War (today that would be viewed as "wasteful") |
| Bob Noyce ([[Fairchild Semiconductor|Fairchild]]) | Integrated circuits | 96% of all ICs sold to Apollo program, but Noyce never let more than 4% of R&D be government-funded. "Why should some PM tell me my R&D roadmap? I invented the thing." Chased Moore's Law mercilessly → enabled precision guided munitions in the 1980s at the price performance needed. |
| David Packard | Defense acquisition reform | Wrote the 5000 series on acquisition at 7 pages. Today it's 2,000 pages. Entropy, not design. |

Parallel to [[Jensen Huang]] and [[NVIDIA]]: sold graphics chips as OEM from 1993 until the AI moment arrived. Build the capability first, the defense application follows.

## Views on industrial base

On the loss of dual-use companies: in 1989 (Berlin Wall), only 6% of major weapons spending went to pure-play defense specialists — 94% went to dual-purpose companies. Chrysler made the Minuteman ICBM and minivans. General Mills built torpedoes and inertial guidance systems. Ford built satellites until 1990. Today: 86% goes to defense specialists. "We've actually missed some of those signals."

On deterrence: "When Ukraine went through 10 years of production in 10 weeks of fighting, that probably should have been a five-alarm fire. We thought the stockpile was going to deter our adversaries. It was always the factory."

On the cost asymmetry: "You can't keep shooting $2 million interceptors at $20,000 drones and have that math work very long."

On the DJI counterfactual: "The drone is an American birthright. Abe Karem and the Predator — we built it." Then ITAR + FAA beyond-line-of-sight ban killed the domestic market. "There was a counterfactual world where [[General Atomics]] had a consumer subsidiary called [[DJI]]."

On pharmaceutical vulnerability: 80% of APIs for generic drugs produced by [[China]]. 50% of new clinical trials in China. "What do you think the American people are going to think when they have to choose between defending the free world and their 5-year-old dying of an ear infection?"

On CCP influence operations: "CCP money flowing to organizations that are protesting us." Soviets spent $7B (2026 dollars) funding the Vietnam-era anti-war movement.

On the F-35 as political project: components manufactured in 400 congressional districts. "The objective is the money being spread around."

On 2040 if things go wrong: "A Chinese century we never recover from. Everyone a vassal state to China. Might makes right." China not just competing but actively sabotaging: "smuggling agricultural funguses into the US so that we can't grow soybeans."

On [[SpaceX]] as the model: shuttle was $50K/kg to orbit. Starship heavy reuse: under $20/kg. "No way you achieve that in a cost-plus world — every day you're deleting your cost, which means you'd be deleting your profit." SpaceX also sued the Air Force under the same procurement law as Palantir.
