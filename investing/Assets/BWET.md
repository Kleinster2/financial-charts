# BWET

**BWET** is the Breakwave Tanker Shipping ETF, issued by [[Breakwave Advisors]]. It is a futures-based ETF that provides exposure to near-dated tanker freight futures, offering one of the few listed vehicles for retail and institutional speculators to bet on crude tanker day-rates. During the 2026 [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure it became the single best-performing US-listed ETF of the year — up "multiples" YTD per Bloomberg (April 2026) — yet attracted only ~$25M in net inflows, a small fraction of the ~$720M that poured into the United States Brent Oil Fund (BNO) over the same window despite BNO returning "a fraction as much" (Bloomberg, April 2026).

---

## Synopsis

BWET is the retail-accessible expression of the tanker rate thesis. Its underlying exposure is near-dated freight futures tied to crude tanker routes — the same rates that blew out to record levels during the Hormuz crisis ([[VLCC]] TD3C hit $423,736/day on March 3, 2026, surpassing the 2008 peak). The fund has a 3.5% headline expense ratio, which [[Breakwave Advisors]] founder [[John Kartsonas]] says could drop if AUM scales and which is partially offset by interest earned on cash collateral backing the futures positions.

The flow disconnect is the story. In a year when tanker rates set all-time highs on the back of Hormuz closure, concentrated ownership, sanctions-induced shadow fleet demand, and an aging global fleet, BWET gathered a rounding error of capital. The passive US Brent Oil Fund (BNO) — a far less dramatic Brent-tracking vehicle — pulled in ~29x the inflows while delivering "a fraction" of the return. The gap reveals how narrow tanker-rate exposure is as an investable theme: even ETF speculators who hunt macro dislocations defaulted to the oil ticker they already knew.

Two non-rate factors also dragged flows. [[Todd Sohn]] ([[Strategas Securities]] chief ETF strategist) cited a played-out arbitrage ("already up multiples this year, so the risk-reward from here may not be compelling") and niche exposure ("tanker freight is a more specialized bet"). The 3.5% fee and K-1 tax treatment (typical of futures-based commodity ETFs) are additional frictions for advisory and retirement money.

[[John Kartsonas]] frames a second-leg thesis: "The story right now is war and disruption, but this could transition to a fundamental story" — pointing to concentrated tanker ownership ([[Sinokor Group]]), [[Russia]] / [[Iran]] sanctions shrinking the pool of compliant vessels, and years of underinvestment leaving the global fleet aging and constrained. If the Hormuz aftershocks persist — higher insurance, rerouted supply chains, port congestion at [[Mundra]] / [[Nhava Sheva]] / [[Khor Fakkan]] — the structural bid for tanker capacity outlives the ceasefire.

Mechanical note: "If rates decline, the fund will also decline." BWET is pure freight-rate beta, not a geared war-hedge.
