# M-series chips

[[Apple]]'s system-on-chip line for the Mac and iPad — the desktop/laptop-class counterpart to the iPhone's A-series. The line opened with the M1 in November 2020, the chip that began Apple's two-year transition off [[Intel]] processors, and ran through M2 (2022), M3 (2023, [[TSMC]] 3nm) and M4 (2024). It shares the unified-memory, big-core architecture of Apple's phone silicon, scaled up with more CPU/GPU cores and memory bandwidth across the Pro / Max / Ultra variants.

The open question now is foundry, not design: under the [[Apple-Intel chip-making agreement May 2026|Apple-Intel deal]], [[Apple]] is set to produce its M7 SoC at [[Intel]] — reported to be on the [[Intel 18A|18A-P]] process in late 2027, Apple's first leading-edge silicon outside [[TSMC]] in over a decade, with future smartphone-class chips reported to target Intel's [[14A]] node. [[TSMC]] still holds 90%+ of Apple's volume, so the Intel allocation reads as concentration-risk insurance, not a defection. The strategic point stands: Apple's design organization, not any single foundry's process, is the durable moat. [[Apple Silicon]] covers the umbrella in-housing strategy; this note tracks the Mac/iPad chip line itself. See [[Apple#Innovation gap and the reinvestment question (FT, May 2026)]] for the strategic frame.
