# Naphtha

Light hydrocarbon fraction distilled from crude oil — the primary feedstock for petrochemical production in Asia and Europe. Fed into steam crackers to produce ethylene, propylene, and aromatics (benzene, toluene, xylene) — the building blocks of plastics, synthetic fibers, solvents, and industrial chemicals.

## Why naphtha matters

Naphtha is the feedstock that connects the oil market to the chemical industry. When naphtha prices spike, the cost cascades through: ethylene → polyethylene → plastics → packaging → consumer goods. Asia's petrochemical industry — the world's largest — is overwhelmingly naphtha-fed (unlike the US, which cracked ethane from shale gas). This makes Asian chemical producers acutely sensitive to Gulf crude supply disruptions.

[[JPMorgan]] ([[Natasha Kaneva]] et al., March 20, 2026) identified naphtha as the *most elastic* refined product: petrochemical plants can partially substitute ethane in cracking when naphtha prices spike, giving some flexibility. But this substitution capacity is limited — most Asian crackers are designed for naphtha, not ethane.

## March 2026 — Iran conflict

Naphtha prices went "parabolic" (Will Kennedy, Bloomberg, March 19, 2026). Over 50% of Asian naphtha comes from the Middle East, and the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure severed that supply. The physical absence of feedstock — not just price — forced production cuts across the region.
