# Phosphoric Acid

**Phosphoric acid** (H3PO4) is the second-most-produced industrial acid by volume, the chemical backbone of the global fertilizer system, and an emerging critical material for [[Lithium|LFP battery]] cathode chemistry. It is produced almost entirely via the wet process -- reacting [[Sulfuric Acid|sulfuric acid]] with phosphate rock -- making its supply [[Companionality|structurally captive]] to both phosphate rock availability and sulfuric acid markets.

---

## Synopsis

The global phosphoric acid industry produces ~50 Mt annually (measured as P2O5 — phosphorus pentoxide — the standard unit the fertilizer industry uses to express phosphoric acid output, not a literal product) from ~400 wet-process plants, with a market value in the range of $40-55 billion (2024, depending on methodology). The commodity is dominated by a single geological fact: [[Morocco]] holds ~70% of global phosphate rock reserves (50 billion tonnes), controlled by state-owned [[OCP Group]], which commands ~31% of world phosphate product market share and generated $9.76 billion in revenue in 2024. [[China]] is the largest producer and consumer of phosphoric acid (~4.9 Mt in 2024), but Morocco is the largest exporter (~2.1 Mt). The supply chain is structurally unusual -- phosphoric acid requires two non-substitutable inputs (phosphate rock, which must be mined, and sulfuric acid at ~2.8 tonnes H2SO4 per tonne P2O5), and generates a massive toxic byproduct ([[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction|phosphogypsum]], ~5 tonnes per tonne P2O5) that is weakly radioactive and accumulating globally at 100-280 Mt/year.

Roughly 85% of phosphoric acid goes to fertilizer production -- DAP, MAP, TSP, and liquid fertilizers that underpin global food security. Phosphorus is non-substitutable in agriculture; unlike nitrogen (synthesized from air via Haber-Bosch), phosphorus must be mined, creating a finite-resource dynamic that feeds the "peak phosphorus" debate. The remaining 15% serves animal feed (DCP/MCP), food and beverage (Coca-Cola's signature tang comes from food-grade H3PO4), metal treatment, detergents, semiconductors, and -- increasingly -- [[Lithium|LFP battery]] cathodes. The LFP connection is the energy transition angle: lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4) cathode chemistry requires purified phosphoric acid, and LFP batteries now hold 60%+ market share in China with global adoption accelerating (Tesla, BYD, Ford all shifting to LFP for standard-range vehicles). CRU Group estimates that LFP growth may require the global purified phosphoric acid industry to nearly double in capacity by 2045.

The investment case sits at the intersection of food security (inelastic fertilizer demand from a growing population), geopolitical concentration (Morocco/OCP + China together control supply), the sulfuric acid dependency (acid market cycles transmit directly to phosphoric acid costs), and the LFP battery demand pull (structurally new demand from an industry that barely existed a decade ago). The key risk is [[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction|phosphogypsum]] -- an environmental liability growing at billions of tonnes globally, with Florida alone storing over 1 billion tonnes in 25 radioactive stacks. Any tightening of phosphogypsum regulation could constrain wet-process production.

## Production and supply structure

### The wet process (~90-95% of production)

The dominant production route reacts phosphate rock with sulfuric acid:

Ca3(PO4)2 + 3H2SO4 + 6H2O --> 2H3PO4 + 3CaSO4-2H2O

In practice, fluorapatite (the actual mineral) is used. The process yields phosphoric acid at 26-32% P2O5 concentration ("green acid" or "merchant grade acid"), which is then concentrated to 40-54% P2O5 for fertilizer production or further purified for food/technical/battery grade. The byproduct is calcium sulfate dihydrate -- phosphogypsum -- at ~5 tonnes per tonne of P2O5 produced.

Key inputs per tonne of P2O5:
- ~2.8 tonnes of sulfuric acid (93% concentration)
- ~3.0-3.5 tonnes of phosphate rock (depending on grade)
- Process water and energy for concentration

The wet process produces acid suitable for fertilizer, but requires additional purification (solvent extraction, crystallization) for food-grade, technical-grade, or battery-grade applications.

### The thermal process (~5-10% of production)

Elemental phosphorus is burned to P4O10, then hydrated to produce high-purity H3PO4. This route bypasses the sulfuric acid dependency but is far more energy-intensive and expensive. It produces ultra-pure acid for electronics, semiconductors (78,000 tonnes of 99.999% purity acid consumed by fabs in 2025), and specialty applications. Declining as purification of wet-process acid improves.

## Demand structure

### End-use breakdown (2024)

| Application | Share | Notes |
|-------------|-------|-------|
| Phosphate fertilizers | ~85% | DAP, MAP, TSP, liquid fertilizers; backbone of global food system |
| Animal feed | ~5% | Dicalcium phosphate (DCP), monocalcium phosphate (MCP) |
| Food and beverage | ~3-4% | pH adjustment, acidulant (cola beverages), food additives, sugar refining |
| Industrial | ~3-4% | Metal treatment, detergents, water treatment, flame retardants |
| Electronics/semiconductors | <1% growing | Ultra-high purity (99.999%) for wafer cleaning; 78 kt consumed in 2025 |
| LFP battery cathodes | <1% growing fast | Purified phosphoric acid for iron phosphate precursor; see below |

### Fertilizer demand (dominant)

Phosphoric acid is the intermediate chemical that transforms phosphate rock into plant-available phosphorus. The three main fertilizer products:

- DAP (diammonium phosphate, 18-46-0): Largest single phosphate fertilizer; H3PO4 + NH3 --> (NH4)2HPO4
- MAP (monoammonium phosphate, 11-52-0): H3PO4 + NH3 --> NH4H2PO4
- TSP (triple superphosphate, 0-46-0): phosphate rock + phosphoric acid

Global fertilizer P2O5 consumption was 47.5 Mt in 2024, up from 45.8 Mt in 2023 (USGS). Demand is structurally tied to population growth, changing diets (more meat = more feed crops = more fertilizer), and government food security policies.

### What drives price swings

- China export policy (single largest swing factor -- any restriction immediately tightens global supply)
- Phosphate rock availability and mine output
- Sulfuric acid cost (2.8 tonnes H2SO4 per tonne P2O5 means acid price transmits directly)
- Crop prices and farm economics (strong corn/soy prices = more fertilizer demand)
- India import demand (largest importer; government subsidy policy affects volumes)
- Seasonal planting cycles (Northern Hemisphere spring, Indian Rabi/Kharif seasons)

## The LFP battery connection

### The chemistry

Lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4) cathode material requires purified phosphoric acid as a key input. The primary synthesis route:

1. Iron sulfate (FeSO4) + phosphoric acid (H3PO4) --> iron phosphate (FePO4) precursor
2. Iron phosphate + lithium carbonate (Li2CO3) --> LiFePO4 cathode, baked at ~700C

From stoichiometry (molecular weights: LiFePO4 = 157.76, H3PO4 = 97.99), each tonne of LiFePO4 cathode requires ~0.62 tonnes of pure H3PO4. An LFP battery pack uses ~1.1-1.3 kg of cathode material per kWh, implying ~680-810 tonnes of pure H3PO4 per GWh of LFP batteries.

### Market scale

- LFP battery market share: 60%+ in China (2024-2025); growing globally as Tesla, BYD, Ford adopt for standard-range EVs
- Global LFP demand exceeded 200 GWh in 2022 and is growing rapidly
- In 2023, LxFP (LFP + LMFP variants) consumed ~35% of all purified phosphoric acid produced in China
- CRU Group projects global purified phosphoric acid capacity may need to nearly double by 2045 to meet LFP demand
- Benchmark Minerals identifies a potential purified phosphoric acid deficit as early as 2030

### Supply chain concentration

- ~98% of LFP cathode material and LFP battery cells are produced in [[China]]
- ~90% of global battery-grade purified phosphoric acid is produced in [[China]]
- China also produces ~75% of all purified phosphoric acid globally (all grades)
- This concentration creates supply chain risk identical to the early rare earths dynamic

### Non-China supply chain development

[[First Phosphate]] (CSE: PHOS / OTCQB: FRSPF) is developing battery-grade phosphoric acid from igneous phosphate in Quebec, Canada, partnering with LG Energy Solution and American Battery Factory. The company has completed pilot production of LFP battery cells from North American-sourced minerals. Still pre-revenue, but represents the emerging push to de-risk LFP supply chains from Chinese dominance.

## Morocco/OCP dominance

### The reserve position

[[Morocco]] (including contested [[Western Sahara]] deposits) holds ~50 billion tonnes of phosphate rock reserves -- approximately 70% of the global total. At current production rates (~30 Mt/year), Morocco's reserves would last over 1,300 years. No other country comes close: China has ~3.2 Bt (depleting far faster), the US has ~1 Bt (Florida deposits declining).

### OCP Group

[[OCP Group]] is Morocco's state-owned phosphate monopoly and the world's largest phosphate company:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Revenue (2024) | $9.76 billion (97 billion MAD; record) |
| H1 2025 revenue | $5.7 billion (+21% YoY) |
| Market share (global phosphate products) | ~31% |
| Market share (Africa fertilizer) | ~54% |
| Investment plan (2025-2027) | $14.06 billion (139.1 billion MAD) |
| Phosphoric acid capacity (current) | ~3 Mt |
| Phosphoric acid capacity (2028 target) | ~4 Mt (Mzinda-Safi corridor expansion) |
| Ownership | 95% Moroccan state |
| Status | Private; no public IPO plans |

OCP is investing aggressively: the Mzinda-Safi corridor targets 12 Mt rock, 3 Mt acid, 8.4 Mt fertilizer by 2028; the Meskala-Essaouira corridor targets 20 Mt rock, 1 Mt acid, 2 Mt fertilizer by 2030.

### Western Sahara controversy

[[OCP Group]]'s subsidiary Phosboucraa mines phosphate rock in [[Western Sahara]], a non-self-governing territory occupied by Morocco since 1975. The Polisario Front (representing the Sahrawi people) and multiple UN bodies have challenged the legality of resource extraction without the consent of the indigenous population. In 2002, the UN Under-Secretary General for Legal Affairs stated that exploitation "in disregard of the interests and wishes of the people of Western Sahara" would violate international law. Several sovereign wealth funds and institutional investors have divested from buyers of Western Saharan phosphate. Morocco ships ~1.5 Mt of phosphate rock annually from the territory. Only three companies were known to still purchase conflict phosphate as of recent reporting. The controversy creates ESG risk for downstream buyers and periodic trade disruptions.

### Geopolitical implications

Morocco's phosphate position is analogous to Saudi Arabia's oil or the DRC's cobalt -- a single country controls the resource the world depends on. Unlike oil, phosphorus has no substitute in agriculture. Unlike cobalt, there is no recycling pathway that meaningfully reclaims it. This gives Morocco extraordinary long-term leverage, constrained mainly by OCP's state-owned opacity and the Western Sahara legal cloud.

## China

[[China]] is the world's largest producer and consumer of both phosphate rock and phosphoric acid:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Phosphate rock production (2024) | ~100 Mt (largest globally) |
| Phosphoric acid production (2024) | ~4.9 Mt |
| Global production share (phosphoric acid) | ~61% by some measures |
| Reserves | ~3.2 Bt (modest vs. Morocco's 50 Bt) |
| Reserve life at current production | ~32 years |
| Primary producing provinces | Guizhou, Yunnan, Hubei, Sichuan |

### Export restrictions

China has used phosphate export controls as a food security tool:

- October 2021: Imposed restrictions on phosphate fertilizer exports to ensure domestic supply amid global price spikes
- July 2022: Extended restrictions through end of 2022; quota cut to 3.16 Mt (down from 5.5 Mt, a 42% reduction)
- Result: Chinese phosphate fertilizer exports dropped 42% between 2021 and 2022; six manufacturers with 2.75 Mt/year capacity shut down in December 2021; another 880 kt shuttered February-April 2022
- 2025: China continued to limit phosphorus fertilizer exports, further tightening global supply

### Self-sufficiency strategy

China's phosphate reserves, while large in absolute terms, are being depleted at a rate that implies ~32 years of remaining life -- a fraction of Morocco's 1,300+ years. Beijing's export restrictions reflect a strategic calculus: protect domestic agriculture by keeping fertilizer cheap at home, even if it squeezes global markets. This is the same resource nationalism playbook China has applied to rare earths and gallium/germanium.

### Battery-grade pivot

China dominates LFP battery-grade purified phosphoric acid production (~90% of global supply), creating a dual dependency: the world needs China for both fertilizer-grade and battery-grade acid. Any expansion of export controls to include purified acid would directly hit the global LFP battery supply chain.

## The phosphogypsum problem

### Scale

For every tonne of P2O5 produced via the wet process, ~5 tonnes of phosphogypsum (CaSO4-2H2O) are generated. At ~50 Mt P2O5/year global production, this implies ~250 Mt of phosphogypsum generated annually (estimates range 100-280 Mt depending on methodology). Cumulative global phosphogypsum in storage runs to billions of tonnes.

### Why it's problematic

Phosphogypsum contains naturally occurring radioactive materials (NORM) from the phosphate rock:
- Uranium (U-238) and thorium (Th-232) from the original ore
- Radium-226 (1,600-year half-life) -- the primary regulatory concern
- Radon-222 gas (emitted continuously) -- second-leading cause of lung cancer in the US (~21,000 deaths/year)
- Polonium-210 -- alpha emitter

The material also contains heavy metals (cadmium, arsenic, lead) and residual fluorides.

### Florida: ground zero

Florida is the epicenter of the phosphogypsum problem in the US:
- 1+ billion tonnes stored in 25 stacks across the state
- Industry adds ~40 million tonnes annually
- Stacks sit atop the Floridan Aquifer (drinking water for 10 million people)
- EPA has banned the use of phosphogypsum in road construction since 1992 due to radioactivity (though Florida signed a bill in 2024 allowing pilot "radioactive roads" programs)

### Piney Point disaster (2021)

In March 2021, the containment wall of a 67-acre holding pool at the decommissioned Piney Point phosphate plant in Manatee County, Florida began to fail. The pool held ~480 million gallons of wastewater containing nitrogen, phosphorus, ammonia, and radium. To prevent catastrophic collapse into Tampa Bay, authorities authorized emergency discharge of contaminated water into the bay, triggering ecological damage and a public health scare. The remaining 250 million gallons were eventually injected into a deep well ~1,500 feet below the Floridan Aquifer.

### Investment implications

Phosphogypsum is the industry's unpriced liability. Tighter regulation could increase production costs (requiring lined containment, deep-well injection, or reprocessing), constrain capacity additions in jurisdictions with strict environmental rules (US, EU), and shift production advantage further toward Morocco and the Gulf states where regulatory environments are more permissive. Conversely, phosphogypsum contains trace rare earth elements and uranium, creating a potential secondary recovery play — the DOE has funded research into rare earth extraction from Florida phosphogypsum. [[Rainbow Rare Earths]] has developed a hydrometallurgical process at Phalaborwa ([[South Africa]]) and is advancing a 51/49 JV with [[Mosaic]] at Uberaba, [[Brazil]] — preliminary economics show $916M NPV, 45% IRR on $279M capex. See [[Phosphogypsum rare earth extraction]] for full analysis.

## Food security nexus

### Phosphorus is non-substitutable

Phosphorus is one of three macronutrients essential for plant growth (along with nitrogen and potassium). It is required for DNA, RNA, ATP (energy transfer), and cell membranes. There is no chemical substitute. One tonne of phosphate produces every 130 tonnes of grain. The world mines ~240 Mt of phosphate rock annually (2024 USGS) to sustain global agriculture.

Unlike nitrogen, which can be synthesized from atmospheric N2 via the Haber-Bosch process, phosphorus must be mined from finite rock deposits. This creates a fundamentally different resource dynamic.

### The peak phosphorus debate

The question of when phosphate reserves will be exhausted has been debated since Cordell et al. (2009) estimated peak production around 2033 at ~203 Mt/year. However, the USGS dramatically revised global reserve estimates upward in 2011 (from 16 Gt to 65 Gt), largely reflecting better assessment of Moroccan deposits. Current consensus:

| Estimate | Reserve life | Source |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| Conservative | ~186 years | Various academic |
| Central | ~305 years | USGS-based |
| Optimistic | ~458 years | Including sub-economic resources |
| Morocco-specific | 1,300+ years | At current production rates |

The debate has shifted from "when do reserves run out?" to "when does geographic concentration become critical?" As non-Moroccan reserves deplete, by ~2100 North Africa could hold ~95% of remaining global phosphorus reserves -- an unprecedented concentration of a non-substitutable agricultural input.

### Food price correlation

Phosphoric acid prices correlate with food prices through a feedback loop: strong crop prices --> farmers invest in more fertilizer --> phosphate demand rises --> acid prices rise. The 2008 and 2022 price spikes both coincided with global food crises. Government fertilizer subsidies (India's massive subsidy program, China's domestic price controls) are key demand drivers.
