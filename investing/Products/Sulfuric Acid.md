# Sulfuric Acid

**Sulfuric acid** (H2SO4) is the world's most-produced industrial chemical by volume, a bellwether for industrial activity, and a commodity whose supply is structurally tied to copper smelting, oil refining, and natural gas processing. It is both a primary product (manufactured from elemental sulfur) and a byproduct (from non-ferrous metal smelting), creating an unusual supply dynamic where production is often involuntary.

---

## Synopsis

The global sulfuric acid market produced ~261 million tonnes in 2024, valued at ~$15-17 billion. The commodity sits at the intersection of three structural forces: fertilizer demand (~55% of consumption, driven by phosphoric acid production for DAP/MAP/TSP), the [[Copper smelter squeeze]] (where Chinese smelter expansion floods acid markets with byproduct acid, and where acid revenue now represents 60-70% of Chinese smelter income vs. 0-5% from processing fees), and energy transition demand (lithium spodumene processing, nickel HPAL, rare earth extraction, battery recycling).

The supply structure is what makes this commodity unusual. Roughly 55-60% comes from burning elemental sulfur (itself a byproduct of oil/gas refining via the Claus process), ~30-35% from smelter acid (byproduct of copper, zinc, lead, nickel smelting), and the remainder from pyrites and spent acid regeneration. Because ~90% of supply originates as a byproduct of other processes, production decisions are not driven by acid economics but by the economics of petroleum refining and base metals. This creates boom-bust cycles: when copper smelters expand, acid floods the market; when refineries cut throughput, sulfur tightens. The commodity is also punishing to trade -- corrosive, heavy (1.84 g/cm3), low value-to-weight, requiring specialized chemical tankers and infrastructure -- which segments it into regional markets rather than a single global one.

The investment case centers on the tension between involuntary supply growth (Chinese smelter expansion adding ~1 Mt/year of export acid) and emerging demand from energy transition chemistry. Spodumene lithium processing consumes ~7-8 tonnes of H2SO4 per tonne of lithium carbonate equivalent (LCE). Nickel HPAL in Indonesia is scaling rapidly. Rare earth extraction requires 3.4-4.0 tonnes per tonne of REO. If lithium demand reaches projections, the acid requirement could rival current fertilizer demand growth -- but supply is tethered to oil refining and copper smelting decisions made for entirely different reasons. That mismatch is the thesis.

### The sulfur supply chain

Elemental sulfur is overwhelmingly a byproduct:

1. Claus process (oil/gas) -- hydrogen sulfide (H2S) removed during petroleum refining and natural gas processing is converted to elemental sulfur. The Claus process recovers 95-99.99% of H2S. Approximately 90-95% of all recovered sulfur globally comes from this process. The vast majority of the 68+ Mt of sulfur produced annually is byproduct sulfur from petroleum refining and gas processing.

2. Sulfur to acid -- Elemental sulfur is burned at ~1,300 K, producing 10-12% SO2 gas, which enters the Contact process (vanadium oxide catalyst at ~700 K), achieving >99.5% conversion to SO3, then absorbed into 98% H2SO4.

3. Smelter gas to acid -- Non-ferrous smelters (copper, zinc, lead, nickel) produce SO2 as a mandatory emission control byproduct. Modern flash smelting achieves up to 99% SO2 capture. This SO2 is converted to acid via the same Contact process.

The critical implication: sulfuric acid production is driven by oil refinery throughput and base metal smelter output, not by acid demand. Supply is involuntary.

### Spent acid regeneration (SAR)

The third supply source, serving the petroleum refining industry. Sulfuric acid catalyzes the alkylation process for high-octane gasoline production. Approximately 100 US refineries operate alkylation units using H2SO4 or HF catalyst. Virtually 100% of spent acid from refinery alkylation is regenerated. The SAR business was built by DuPont (spun to Chemours), then acquired by [[Veolia]] for $325M in 2016, then sold to American Industrial Partners for $620M in July 2024 and rebranded as Nexpera (13 US facilities, ~300 employees).

## Demand structure

### End-use breakdown (2024)

| Application | Share | Notes |
|-------------|-------|-------|
| Phosphate fertilizers | ~55% | Phosphoric acid → DAP/MAP/TSP; 1.8-2.2 tonnes H2SO4 per tonne phosphate fertilizer |
| Chemical manufacturing | ~20% | Detergents, dyes, pharmaceuticals, explosives |
| Petroleum refining | ~5-7% | Alkylation catalyst for gasoline blending |
| Metal processing/mining | ~8-10% | Copper SX-EW, uranium ISL, ore leaching, steel pickling |
| Other industrial | ~5-8% | Water treatment, paper, textiles |
| Energy transition (emerging) | <3% growing | Lithium, nickel HPAL, rare earths, battery recycling |

### Fertilizer demand (dominant)

Phosphate rock + sulfuric acid → phosphoric acid. This reaction underpins the global food system. [[OCP Group]] (Morocco) is the world's largest phosphate producer. [[Mosaic]] (US) operates four phosphate facilities (New Wales, Bartow, Riverview in Florida, Uncle Sam in Louisiana) with ~4.5 Mt P2O5/year capacity (7% of world, 60% of North America). [[Nutrien]] and [[Ma'aden]] are also major consumers.

### Mining and hydrometallurgy

| Process | Acid intensity | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| Copper SX-EW (heap leach) | 5-10 g/L H2SO4 in lixiviant | ~18% of global copper output uses SX-EW; Chile and Peru dominant |
| Uranium ISL (in-situ leach) | High; Kazakhstan uses acid leach, US uses alkaline | Kazakhstan is world's largest U producer via acid ISL |
| Uranium heap leach | 500-1,000 kg acid per tonne of ore | Much higher intensity than copper |
| Copper electrowinning | Continuous acid recycling | SX-EW is net acid consumer due to gangue mineral reactions |

[[Freeport-McMoRan]] operates its own acid supply chain: the Miami smelter (Arizona) is the primary source of H2SO4 for FCX's North American leaching operations, supplemented by a sulfur burner at Safford.

### Energy transition demand (the growth thesis)

| Application | Acid consumption | Scale context |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Lithium spodumene processing | ~7-8 tonnes H2SO4 per tonne LCE | Concentrated H2SO4 (93%) at 250 C, 145% stoichiometric excess; >96% Li recovery |
| Nickel laterite HPAL | ~250-1,400 kg per tonne of ore | Indonesian HPAL produced 180 kt Ni in 2023, up from 88 kt in 2022 |
| Rare earth extraction | 3.4-4.0 tonnes per tonne REO | China's Baotou processing; 30+ years of operation |
| Battery recycling (black mass) | ~1.3 kg H2SO4 per kg black mass | Hydrometallurgical route; 2M H2SO4 optimal; 98% Li, 80%+ Co/Mn/Ni recovery |

Lithium is the big number. If global LCE demand reaches 11.2 Mt by 2050 (projected) and the sulfuric acid roast process remains dominant, implied acid demand is ~78-90 Mt -- equivalent to ~30-35% of current total production. Even near-term, each 100 kt of new spodumene-route LCE capacity requires ~700-800 kt of H2SO4. Alternative processes (direct lithium extraction, phosphoric acid leaching) could reduce this but are not yet commercial at scale.

### What drives price swings

- Smelter operations -- unplanned outages tighten supply instantly; new smelter capacity floods market
- Liquid sulfur availability -- driven by refinery throughput and gas processing
- Fertilizer demand cycles -- seasonal planting demand, crop prices, subsidies
- Copper smelter expansion/contraction -- see below
- Trade flow disruptions -- corrosive cargo, port bottlenecks, specialized tanker availability
- Energy costs -- sulfur burner plants are energy-intensive; European acid production fell 4.3% in 2023 due to plant closures driven by energy costs

## Trade flows

### Why acid is hard to trade

Sulfuric acid is among the most difficult bulk chemicals to ship internationally:
- Corrosive (Class 8 hazardous material) -- requires specialized chemical tankers, acid-resistant tanks and infrastructure
- Dense (1.84 g/cm3, ~70% heavier than water) -- low value-to-weight ratio
- Low unit value ($28-185/Mt) vs. high logistics cost
- Regulatory burden -- hazmat classifications, border inspections slow transit

This segments the market into regional rather than truly global. Overland transport (rail, truck) dominates within continents. Seaborne trade exists but is limited to surplus/deficit regions.

## The copper smelter-acid nexus

This is the most important structural dynamic in the sulfuric acid market and connects directly to the [[Copper smelter squeeze]].

### The mechanism

Every tonne of copper smelted from sulfide concentrate produces roughly 2-4 tonnes of sulfuric acid as a mandatory byproduct (SO2 capture is legally required for environmental compliance). Copper smelter expansion therefore directly increases acid supply, regardless of acid market conditions.

### China's smelter buildout

China's copper smelter capacity has expanded aggressively:
- 83% of China's new sulfuric acid capacity (2019-2023) came from new smelter operations
- China produced 9.7% more refined copper YoY through October 2025 despite negative TC/RCs
- China is ~80% reliant on copper concentrate imports

### The revenue inversion

The traditional smelter business model has inverted. TC/RCs (treatment and refining charges -- the fee miners pay smelters to process concentrate) collapsed:

| Period | TC benchmark | Signal |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Aug 2023 | $89.20/Mt | Five-year high; smelters profitable |
| 2024 benchmark | $80/Mt | Comfortable |
| Jan 2024 | $22.70/Mt | Rapid decline |
| May 2024 | -$4.10/Mt | Smelters paying miners for feed |
| Nov 2025 | -$60/Mt | Unprecedented negative territory |
| 2025 benchmark | $21.25/Mt | -73% vs. 2024 |

But Chinese smelters survived. How? Sulfuric acid became the business:
- Processing fees: 0-5% of total revenue
- Sulfuric acid sales: 60-70% of revenue at major Chinese smelters (as of 2025)
- Gold and silver credits provide the remainder

This is a structural shift. Chinese smelters are no longer in the copper processing business -- they are in the sulfuric acid business that happens to produce copper. The acid revenue subsidizes continued operation despite negative TC/RCs, which in turn floods the market with more copper and more acid.

### Implications for fertilizer producers

- When smelter acid is abundant (as in periods of Chinese smelter expansion), fertilizer companies can buy cheap byproduct acid instead of manufacturing from elemental sulfur
- This creates cost advantage for fertilizer producers near smelter clusters (China, Japan, South Korea)
- But it also creates supply dependency: if smelters cut (as happened in Japan, Europe, Korea, Spain -- all reducing capacity or requiring government bailouts), fertilizer producers face acid shortages
- European acid prices spiked to $185/Mt in late 2025 precisely because Nyrstar's Budel smelter went offline (324K tpa acid capacity lost), Aurubis Hamburg took a 60-day maintenance (200K tonnes removed), and Boliden maintenance tightened NW European supply

### China's production evolution

China's acid industry is transitioning from pyrite-based (historically dominant, declining to ~17%) toward smelter acid (growing fastest, driven by copper expansion) and sulfur burning (largest single source at ~43%). Environmental regulations and industrial policy are pushing retirement of older pyrite plants.

### Domestic consumption drivers

Phosphate fertilizer production is the largest downstream, consuming 50.9% of China's acid in 2024. Non-ferrous metals smelting is the second-largest. The combination of expanding phosphate capacity and new smelter commissioning creates both supply and demand growth simultaneously.

### Export dynamics

China's acid exports are growing in both volume and value:
- 2020: 1.72 Mt
- 2021: 2.84 Mt
- 2022: 3.64 Mt (peak)
- 2023: 2.51 Mt
- 2024: 2.68 Mt
- 2025 (projected): 3.6 Mt

Export prices have surged: from $17.5/Mt average in 2023 to $28.2/Mt in 2024 to $60.8/Mt average in the first ten months of 2025 (+115% YoY).

## Environmental and regulatory

### Acid rain program (the precedent)

The US Acid Rain Program under Title IV of the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments was the world's first national cap-and-trade system. It targeted SO2 emissions from power plants, which had reached ~18.9 Mt in 1980.

Results: SO2 emissions from US power plants decreased 94% from 1990 to 2019. NOx fell 86%. This was achieved through fuel switching and flue-gas desulfurization, with the cap-and-trade mechanism proving more cost-effective than command-and-control regulation.

### How regulations shape supply

1. Mandatory SO2 capture at smelters -- environmental laws require non-ferrous smelters to capture SO2 emissions. Converting SO2 to sulfuric acid is the standard compliance method. This makes acid production legally required, not economically optional.

2. Sulfur recovery at refineries -- regulations require 95-99.99% sulfur recovery at gas processing plants and refineries. This generates elemental sulfur that flows into acid production.

3. European energy policy impact -- high energy costs drove European sulfuric acid production down 4.3% in 2023, with fertilizer plant closures in the Netherlands and Germany.

4. China's pyrite phase-out -- environmental concerns are pushing China to retire older pyrite-based acid plants, shifting supply toward smelter and sulfur-burning routes.

## Cyclicality and investment relevance

### Sulfuric acid as economic indicator

Sulfuric acid demand correlates with industrial production. Rising demand signals manufacturing expansion; falling demand signals slowdown. Its breadth of uses -- fertilizers, mining, refining, chemicals -- makes it a cross-sector signal. The US PPI for sulfuric acid (FRED: WPU0613020T1) is a useful proxy.

### The cycle

The acid market cycles on the interaction of three independent drivers:

1. Fertilizer demand (seasonal, food-price sensitive, policy-driven) -- relatively stable base demand
2. Smelter output (metals prices, smelter economics, Chinese industrial policy) -- volatile supply driver
3. Sulfur availability (refinery throughput, gas processing, Middle East output) -- supply constraint

Bull case: smelter cuts (as in Europe 2024-2025) tighten supply; fertilizer demand is inelastic; energy transition adds incremental demand that is structurally new and growing.

Bear case: Chinese smelter overcapacity floods market with byproduct acid; weak fertilizer demand from crop price declines; alternative chemistries (HF alkylation, DLE for lithium) reduce acid intensity.

### Structural signals to watch

- China copper smelter utilization rates (higher = more acid supply)
- European liquid sulfur availability (constrained since 2024)
- TC/RC benchmarks (negative TCs = smelters subsidized by acid sales)
- Lithium spodumene converter commissioning (each 40 kt LCE plant needs ~300 kt acid)
- Indonesian HPAL plant ramp-ups
- Refinery throughput trends (decarbonization → less sulfur recovery → less acid feedstock long-term)

### The long-term structural question

If oil refining declines over decades (energy transition), sulfur recovery from refineries declines with it. Sulfur is currently cheap because it's an unwanted byproduct. If refining declines but acid demand grows (lithium, nickel, rare earths, fertilizers for a growing population), the supply structure inverts: sulfur and acid shift from byproduct surplus to primary-production deficit. That inflection point -- when acid transitions from involuntary oversupply to deliberate undersupply -- is the long-arc investment thesis.
