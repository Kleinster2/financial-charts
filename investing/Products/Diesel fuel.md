#product #energy #commodity #distillate

**Diesel fuel** — the workhorse distillate that powers global freight, agriculture, and industry. ~27-29 mb/d global consumption (~27% of total oil demand), making it the single largest refined product category by volume. Priced via NYMEX ULSD (US) and ICE Low Sulphur Gasoil (Europe). Shares the middle distillate barrel with [[Jet fuel]] and heating oil — the three compete for the same refinery cut.

---

## Synopsis

Diesel (technically No. 2 distillate fuel oil, marketed as ultra-low sulfur diesel / ULSD since 2006) is refined from crude oil at the 250-350C distillation range — heavier than kerosene/jet fuel, lighter than residual fuel oil. A US refinery produces ~11-12 gallons of ULSD per 42-gallon barrel of crude, a yield of ~26-29%. Global demand runs ~27-29 mb/d, with transportation (primarily trucking/freight) consuming ~74% of the total. The US alone consumes ~3.9-4.0 mb/d, roughly 20% of total US petroleum consumption.

The pricing structure is crude oil + crack spread. The diesel crack spread — the difference between ULSD price and crude oil — is the key profitability metric for refiners and cost metric for consumers. Pre-2020, the ULSD-WTI crack averaged ~$15-18/bbl. In 2022, it spiked to $88/bbl as Russian sanctions, refinery closures, and surging post-COVID demand collided. By 2024, it normalized to ~$22/bbl (Q3 2024), still above historical norms due to structural capacity tightening. The refinery closure wave (2020-2025) has permanently removed capacity: [[LyondellBasell]]'s Houston refinery shut in early 2025, [[Phillips 66]] closed its 139,000 b/d Los Angeles refinery in late 2025, and [[Valero]] plans to shutter its 145,000 b/d Benicia refinery by spring 2026.

Diesel demand is a direct proxy for economic activity — trucking volumes correlate tightly with industrial production and GDP. The structural threat is electrification, but heavy-duty trucks electrify far slower than passenger vehicles. ACT Research forecasts only 25% zero-emission truck adoption by 2030 and 50% by 2040. Renewable diesel (HVO) is the nearer-term displacement vector: US capacity reached ~5.1 billion gallons in 2025, led by [[Diamond Green Diesel]] (Valero/Darling JV), [[Phillips 66]]'s Rodeo complex, and [[Marathon Petroleum]]'s Martinez facility. Renewable diesel is a drop-in replacement (chemically identical to fossil diesel) but costs 15-30% more.

## Product classification

| Grade | Sulfur limit | Use |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| No. 2 ULSD | <15 ppm | On-road diesel (trucks, buses) — US standard since 2006 |
| No. 1 diesel | <15 ppm | Winter blend, lighter (kerosene-like), better cold flow |
| No. 2 heating oil | <15 ppm (ULSHO) | Home heating (Northeast US) — chemically identical base to ULSD |
| ICE Gasoil | <10 ppm | European standard (10 ppm vs US 15 ppm) |
| Marine gasoil (MGO) | <500 ppm | Post-[[IMO 2020]] compliant marine fuel |
| Off-road diesel | <15 ppm | Construction, agriculture — dyed red (no road tax) |

ULSD and ultra-low sulfur heating oil (ULSHO) are the same base product. The differences are additives (diesel gets detergents and lubricity enhancers; heating oil gets storage stabilizers) and taxes (heating oil exempt from highway fuel tax). The NYMEX "heating oil" contract (HO) was renamed to "NY Harbor ULSD" in 2013 to reflect this convergence. The contract still serves as the US diesel benchmark.

## Benchmarks and pricing

### Primary benchmarks

| Benchmark | Exchange | Specification | Role |
|-----------|----------|---------------|------|
| NY Harbor ULSD (HO) | NYMEX/CME | <15 ppm sulfur, No. 2 oil | US diesel benchmark |
| ICE Low Sulphur Gasoil | ICE Futures Europe | <10 ppm sulfur, 100 MT lots | European/global diesel benchmark |
| Singapore gasoil | Platts (OTC) | 10 ppm | Asian benchmark |

The NYMEX ULSD contract trades in cents/gallon (1 contract = 42,000 gallons). ICE gasoil trades in $/metric tonne, delivered by barge in ARA (Amsterdam-Rotterdam-Antwerp). The two benchmarks track closely but diverge on regional supply/demand — the transatlantic arb (shipping diesel between US Gulf Coast and Europe) is a key spread trade.

### Crack spread dynamics

The diesel crack spread (ULSD price minus crude oil price) is the refining margin proxy. It drives refiner profitability and is the most-watched metric in distillate markets.

| Period | ULSD-WTI crack | Context |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| 2015-2019 avg | ~$17-18/bbl | Normal range |
| 2020 (COVID) | ~$10-12/bbl | Demand collapse |
| 2021 | ~$18-22/bbl | Recovery |
| Q3 2022 (peak) | ~$57-64/bbl | Russia sanctions + refinery closures + post-COVID surge |
| Oct 2022 (extreme) | ~$88/bbl | US distillate inventories at 1951 lows |
| Q3 2023 | ~$45/bbl | Still elevated |
| Q3 2024 | ~$22/bbl | Normalization, still above pre-2020 |
| Q3 2025 (EIA) | Rising | Refinery margins increasing |

Widening drivers:
- Low distillate inventories (strong inverse correlation with East Coast stocks)
- Refinery outages (planned turnarounds or unplanned shutdowns)
- Geopolitical supply disruptions (Russian sanctions, Hormuz risk)
- Seasonal demand spikes (fall harvest, winter heating)
- Gasoline-mode refining (refiners maximizing gasoline yield reduces diesel output)

Narrowing drivers:
- High refinery utilization and throughput
- Inventory builds
- Demand destruction (economic slowdown)
- Biofuel displacement (renewable diesel substituting for fossil diesel)
- Mild winters (reduced heating oil demand)

## Global demand

### Total consumption

Global diesel/gasoil demand: ~27-29 mb/d (2023-2024), representing the largest single refined product category. IEA reported a 258,000 b/d decline in 2024 gas oil demand, but expected 176,000 b/d growth in 2025. Long-term forecasts project ~32.5 mb/d by 2050 (OPEC).

China's diesel consumption fell 11% YoY in June 2024 (to 3.9 mb/d) due to construction slowdown and growing LNG truck fleet. India is emerging as the growth engine — manufacturing expansion and trucking demand. European gasoil demand dropped 140,000 b/d YoY in Q1 2024, driven by poor industrial activity and mild winters.

## US demand

### Consumption

US distillate fuel oil product supplied averaged ~3.9-4.0 mb/d in 2024. EIA forecasts US distillate consumption to increase ~2% in both 2026 and 2027, reaching record highs in 2027 (driven by domestic manufacturing growth and freight volumes).

Distillate (diesel + heating oil) represents ~20% of total US petroleum consumption. Most consumption is on-road transportation (trucking), followed by off-road (construction, agriculture), and heating.

### Seasonal patterns

US diesel demand seasonality has flattened over the past 30 years as heating oil's share declined and transportation's share grew. Still, identifiable patterns persist:

| Season | Pattern | Driver |
|--------|---------|--------|
| October peak | +4% avg over September (2019-23) | Fall agricultural harvest (Midwest) |
| Winter | Heating demand in Northeast | ULSHO consumption, historically dominant but declining |
| Summer | Steady freight demand | Construction activity + freight |
| Holiday season | Elevated | Freight/logistics for retail |

The older pattern of winter drawdowns and fall inventory builds has nearly disappeared. Distillate inventories in March were higher than the previous October in three of the past five heating seasons (EIA), reflecting the shift from seasonal heating to year-round transportation as the primary demand driver.

### Exports

US distillate exports averaged 1.2 mb/d in H1 2025, 7% above the five-year average. The US Gulf Coast is a structural exporter of diesel to Latin America and Europe, making US inventory levels sensitive to global demand — a point of vulnerability when domestic stocks run low.

### Utilization and spare capacity

US refinery utilization runs 85-95% depending on season and maintenance schedules. Gulf Coast (PADD 3) was at ~93.5% in early October 2025, dropping to 87.1% during fall turnarounds. The system has limited spare capacity — any disruption (hurricanes, unplanned outages) quickly tightens the market.

New global refining capacity is concentrated in Asia-Pacific (3.2 mb/d additions through 2030), Africa (1.2 mb/d), and the Middle East (1.0 mb/d) — over 90% of global additions. US and European capacity is flat to declining.

### Diesel yield per barrel

A US refinery produces ~11-12 gallons of ULSD per 42-gallon barrel of crude (~26-29% yield). Distillate yield peaked at 29.7% in October 2011 and typically runs ~27-28%. Refiners can adjust the cut point to shift output between gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel, but flexibility is limited (~5-10% swing). Light sweet crude produces more distillate than heavy sour crude.

Processing gain adds ~6.3% — refineries produce ~45 gallons of refined products per 42-gallon barrel of crude input (2023 average).

### The 2022 diesel crisis

In October 2022, US distillate inventories hit their lowest level since 1951. Days of supply fell to 25.9 — a 30-year seasonal low. The Northeast (PADD 1) was hit hardest, with inventories down 50% YoY to levels not seen since 1990.

Root causes:
- Refinery capacity loss — US operable capacity shrank in 2020-2021, dropping to 17.9 mb/d as of Jan 2022 — below pre-COVID levels. ~900k b/d of global capacity permanently closed 2020-2023.
- Russia sanctions — War in [[Ukraine]] disrupted global diesel trade flows. Russian diesel exports to Europe halted, forcing European buyers to bid up US and Middle East cargoes.
- Chinese export cuts — China reduced refined product exports to conserve domestic supply, removing volume from global markets.
- Post-COVID demand surge — Freight and industrial demand recovered faster than refining capacity could rebuild.
- Seasonal stacking — Fall harvest + pre-holiday freight + early heating season demand converged.

### Why inventories remain structurally low

EIA forecasts total distillate inventories to end both 2025 and 2026 at multiyear lows. Key structural factors:
- [[LyondellBasell]] Houston refinery shut down early 2025
- [[Phillips 66]] LA refinery (139k b/d) and [[Valero]] Benicia (145k b/d) closing — combined 284k b/d of capacity removal
- Strong export demand (1.2 mb/d, 7% above 5-yr avg in H1 2025)
- Renewable diesel production displacing some petroleum diesel but not fully offsetting refinery closures

Diesel demand is the most GDP-sensitive petroleum product. When industrial production slows, diesel demand falls first (before gasoline, before jet fuel). This makes distillate inventories and crack spreads leading economic indicators.

## Structural trends

### EV threat to diesel demand

Electric trucks are the long-term displacement risk, but the timeline is significantly slower than passenger vehicle electrification:

| Milestone | Timeframe | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Cost parity (small trucks) | Before 2030 | DOE |
| Cost parity (heavy-duty, <500mi range) | ~2035 | DOE |
| 25% zero-emission truck adoption | 2030 | ACT Research |
| 50% zero-emission truck adoption | 2040 | ACT Research |
| 90% of new truck sales zero-emission | 2040 | Industry projections |
| Battery cost reduction | 15-35% in next 5 years | IEA |

As of early 2026, ~38,000 medium- and heavy-duty electric trucks had been deployed across 386 fleets in the US — shifting from pilot to early-scale adoption. But the installed base of diesel trucks is enormous, and fleet turnover takes 10-15 years. Long-haul trucking (>500 miles) remains the hardest segment to electrify due to battery weight and charging infrastructure requirements.

Diesel demand from trucking is not materially threatened before 2035. The risk is real but slow-moving.

### Emissions regulations

US — EPA Phase 3 GHG Standards (finalized March 2024):
- Applies to heavy-duty vocational vehicles and tractors from MY2027
- Targets 60% GHG reduction by 2032 vs baseline
- Projected to prevent 1 billion metric tons of CO2 over program life
- Requires higher capital spending on cleaner diesel engines and aftertreatment

EU — Euro 7 (effective July 2027 for cars; May 2028 for new HD vehicle types):
- NOx limits 56% below Euro VI
- PM limits reduced 20%
- Particle number limits reduced 75% for heavy-duty
- Compliance required for 700,000 km or 15 years
- First regulation to include non-exhaust emissions (brake/tire wear)

These regulations increase the cost of diesel vehicles but do not ban them. They push the technology toward cleaner diesel rather than away from diesel entirely. The aftertreatment complexity (SCR, DPF, DEF/urea) increases operating costs for fleet operators.

### China: LNG truck substitution

A small but growing share of China's trucking fleet is switching from diesel to LNG. While still small relative to the total truck fleet, this substitution contributed to China's 11% YoY diesel consumption decline in mid-2024. This is a China-specific trend driven by LNG price competitiveness and domestic gas infrastructure buildout.

## Renewable diesel (HVO)

### What it is

Renewable diesel (also called hydrotreated vegetable oil / HVO) is produced by hydroprocessing fats, oils, and greases (FOGs) — used cooking oil, animal fats, soybean oil, canola oil — into a hydrocarbon identical to petroleum diesel. Unlike biodiesel (FAME), renewable diesel is a drop-in replacement: same chemical structure, same performance, same infrastructure. No blending limits.

### US capacity

US renewable diesel production capacity reached ~5.1 billion gallons/year in 2025 (~333,000 b/d), with projections remaining flat through 2026 as the major buildout phase is completing.

| Producer | Facility | Capacity | Status |
|----------|----------|----------|--------|
| [[Diamond Green Diesel]] (Valero/Darling JV) | Norco, LA + Port Arthur, TX | ~1.2B gal/yr (~78,000 b/d) | Largest North American renewable diesel producer |
| [[Phillips 66]] | Rodeo Renewable Energy Complex, CA | ~50,000 b/d (800M+ gal/yr) | Converted from petroleum refinery; full production reached mid-2024 |
| [[Marathon Petroleum]] | Martinez Renewables, CA | Ramping to 100% capacity | JV with Neste; was at 50% capacity |
| [[Neste]] | Singapore + Rotterdam + Porvoo | ~5.5M tonnes/yr total (~120,000 b/d) | World's largest renewable fuel producer |
| [[Neste]] (expansion) | Rotterdam expansion | +2.7M tonnes/yr | Expected 2027; will bring total to 6.8M tonnes/yr |

### Economics

- Cost premium: 15-30% over fossil diesel (Zemo Renewable Fuels Guide)
- EU HVO premium: Ranged from EUR5-40/100 liters in 2024
- US subsidy landscape: RFS D4 RINs + state-level LCFS credits (California) — these incentives have been the primary driver of US renewable diesel investment
- RFS obligations: Biomass-based diesel (D4) RVOs rising from 5.36B RINs (2025) to 7.12B (2026) and 7.50B (2027)
- Feedstock competition: Used cooking oil and animal fats are finite — scaling beyond current capacity requires agricultural oilseed expansion or new feedstock pathways

### Biodiesel vs renewable diesel

| Property | Biodiesel (FAME) | Renewable diesel (HVO) |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Process | Transesterification | Hydroprocessing (high temp, high pressure, hydrogen) |
| Chemistry | Fatty acid methyl ester | Hydrocarbon (identical to petroleum diesel) |
| Blend limit | B20 typical (20% max) | No limit — drop-in replacement |
| Cold weather | Poor cold flow | Excellent cold flow |
| Infrastructure | Separate handling | Uses existing diesel infrastructure |
| ASTM spec | D6751 | D975 (same as petroleum diesel) |

## Investment implications

### For refiners
Structurally low inventories + declining US/European refining capacity = elevated crack spreads relative to pre-2020 norms. The $15-18/bbl pre-2020 average crack is unlikely to return durably. California refinery closures ([[Phillips 66]] LA, [[Valero]] Benicia) remove 284k b/d, tightening PADD 5. Gulf Coast refiners (Marathon, Valero) benefit from export demand to Europe and Latin America.

### For renewable diesel
The investment cycle has largely peaked — US capacity is projected flat through 2026 after the 2021-2024 buildout wave. Margins depend on RIN prices and LCFS credits, both politically sensitive. Feedstock (used cooking oil, animal fats) supply constraints limit further scaling without new oilseed cultivation. The policy risk is real: RFS obligations could be weakened under deregulatory administrations.

### For trucking/freight
Diesel price is a direct operating cost. Elevated crack spreads flow through to higher freight rates, which flow through to consumer prices. Fleet operators with fuel hedging or renewable diesel access gain cost advantages. The EV transition for heavy-duty is real but not imminent — 2035+ for meaningful displacement.

### For macro/trading
Diesel crack spreads are a leading indicator. Widening cracks signal refinery stress, supply tightness, or demand surge — all bullish for economic activity reads. Narrowing cracks signal demand weakness or inventory builds. The transatlantic arb (US-Europe diesel trade) is a key spread to monitor for global balance signals.

## Insights

- Diesel is the most economically sensitive petroleum product — crack spreads and distillate inventories are better GDP proxies than gasoline or jet fuel prices
- The 2022 inventory crisis ($88/bbl crack) was a structural event, not a one-off: refinery closures permanently removed capacity, and 2025-2026 closures continue the trend
- China's diesel demand has peaked — LNG truck substitution + construction slowdown. India is now the marginal demand growth engine
- Renewable diesel has gone from fringe to material (~5.1B gal/yr US capacity, ~333k b/d) but remains subsidy-dependent. Without RINs and LCFS credits, the 15-30% cost premium makes it uneconomic
- The EU is further ahead on diesel displacement (Euro 7 regulations, diesel car bans in cities, renewable fuel mandates) than the US, where diesel's dominance in trucking faces no near-term regulatory challenge
- Winter heating oil demand — historically the key seasonal driver — has become irrelevant as natural gas substitution reduced its share. Transportation is now the year-round demand driver, flattening seasonality
- [[IMO 2020]]'s feared diesel market disruption largely didn't materialize — shipping adopted VLSFO blends rather than competing directly for diesel/gasoil molecules, though the regulation permanently increased demand for low-sulfur distillates
