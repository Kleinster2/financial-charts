# Urea

The world's most-produced nitrogen fertilizer and the single largest derivative of [[Ammonia]], accounting for ~55% of global ammonia consumption. ~180 Mt/year global production. Produced by reacting ammonia with CO2 at high pressure. [[China]] is the largest producer. Key price benchmarks: Yuzhny FOB (Black Sea), Middle East FOB, US Gulf NOLA.

## Urea-corn correlation

![[urea-corn-futures-correlation-aurelion-mar2026.png]]
*Corn futures (cents/bu, LHS, blue) vs urea futures (USD/t, RHS, olive). The two have tracked closely over six years — nitrogen is the single largest input cost for corn. As of Mar 2026: Corn 467, Urea 662. The 2022 episode showed the full pattern: urea tripled, corn followed above 1,000 cents/bu. The 2026 urea spike is running ahead of the corn response so far — the gap is whether corn catches up. Source: Bloomberg via Aurelion Research.*

Two paths from here: (1) if [[Strait of Hormuz]] disruption fades and alternative supply from Southeast Asia or [[Venezuela]] fills the gap, the urea spike unwinds and corn stays in its range; (2) if the conflict drags on and Chinese exports stay offline, the 2022 playbook repeats — farmers face higher input costs, absorb them, or plant fewer acres. Both outcomes point to higher grain prices.

## Import-dependent buyers

![[urea-imports-india-brazil-china-aurelion.png]]
*Urea imports by major country — [[India]], [[Brazil]], [[China]] (net, with Chinese exports during 2022-23 near-zero period visible). Source: Aurelion Research.*

| Country | 2022 imports | 2025 imports | 2026 expected | Notes |
|---------|--------------|--------------|---------------|-------|
| [[India]] | 6M mt | 9.3M mt | 8-10M mt (could rise higher with domestic cuts) | Gas rationing at 70-75% has cut domestic production ~800kt/month |
| [[Brazil]] | ~7M mt | 7.7M mt | 7-8M mt | ~50% typically sourced from Middle East |
| [[China]] | net importer | 4.9M mt exports | 4-6M mt exports expected | Export capacity at risk from sulfur squeeze |

[[China]]'s swing role is the largest variable. After nearly zeroing exports in 2022-23 to protect domestic supply, China ramped back to 4.9M mt in 2025 and was projected to export 4-6M mt in 2026. The sulfur and energy squeeze from the Middle East conflict has thrown that assumption into doubt; Beijing halted all fertilizer exports on March 16, 2026.

## Capacity pipeline — not keeping up

![[urea-capacity-additions-vs-demand-aurelion.png]]
*Urea capacity additions (M tonnes, dark bars) vs. ~2.3M tonnes/yr consumption growth line. After 4.5M mt came online in 2023 (projects sanctioned during the 2021-22 boom), additions dropped to 0.7M mt (2024) and 0.3M mt (2025). 2026-28 picks up as the next investment wave arrives, but falls back to 1.0-1.3M mt in 2029-30. Current tightness has not yet triggered enough new project approvals to fill the late-decade gap. Source: Aurelion Research.*

| Year | New capacity (M mt) | Vs. ~2.3M mt/yr demand growth |
|------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| 2023 | 4.5 | Surplus (2021-22 investment wave) |
| 2024 | 0.7 | Deficit |
| 2025 | 0.3 | Deficit |
| 2026 | 2.2 | Near balance |
| 2027 | 3.4 | Surplus |
| 2028 | ~2.0 | Near balance |
| 2029 | 1.0-1.3 | Deficit |
| 2030 | 1.0-1.3 | Deficit |

Urea plants are capital-intensive projects with 3-5 year build times. They cluster in waves around high-price periods — the 2023 additions reflect the 2021-22 boom, the 2024-25 gap reflects the low-price investment pause, and the 2029-30 drop-off tells us that current tightness has not yet triggered enough fresh sanctions. By the time the market realizes it needs more capacity, it will be years before it arrives.

## Iran conflict crisis pricing (March 2026)

The [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure disrupted ~49% of globally traded urea (TFI estimate). Prices surged across all benchmarks and have continued climbing:

| Benchmark | Price (Mar 12) | Price (Mar 18) | Change from pre-war |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|---------------------|
| NOLA barges | $600-620/st | $673-675/st | +42% from $475 |
| NOLA spot peak | $683/st | — | +44% |
| Retail urea (DTN) | — | $674/ton | +12% m/m |
| SE Asian granular | >$700/t | $710-750/t fob | +37-45% |
| Australian | >A$1,200/t | >A$1,200/t | +45% from A$830-840 |
| Anhydrous ammonia | $895/ton | $924/ton | First above $900 since May 2023 |

Pre-war analyst consensus had urea at $350-380/t for 2026 — current spot is roughly double.

The crisis simultaneously disrupts Gulf urea production ([[QAFCO]] shut, Iran offline), Gulf-dependent LNG feedstock to South Asian plants ([[India]] at 60% capacity, [[Bangladesh]] 5 of 6 factories shut, Pakistan halted), and Chinese export availability. [[China]] halted ALL fertilizer exports on Mar 16 (Bloomberg) — not just phosphate but nitrogen-potassium blends and urea. As the world's largest urea producer, China's full halt removes the only alternative source large enough to partially offset Hormuz losses. [[India]] formally asked China to ease urea export restrictions (Bloomberg, Mar 12) — the diplomatic ask has gone unanswered.

Non-agricultural urea demand also at risk: AdBlue/diesel exhaust fluid (critical for Australian heavy freight), industrial process inputs. [[Australia]] imports virtually all urea, ~2/3 from the Gulf — National Cabinet convened emergency meeting, appointed logistics coordinator, established priority allocation at WA ports. Officials warn of shortfalls by late May/June without new arrivals.
