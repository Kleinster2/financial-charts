# Ammonia

**Ammonia** (NH3) is the world's largest-volume synthetic chemical, a cornerstone of global agriculture, and increasingly a candidate fuel and hydrogen carrier for the energy transition. Approximately 150 Mt produced annually, ~70% consumed as fertilizer feedstock, with natural gas comprising 70-80% of production cost. The molecule sits at the intersection of food security, fossil fuel economics, and decarbonization policy.

---

## Synopsis

The global ammonia market produced ~150 million tonnes in 2024, valued at an estimated $80-160 billion depending on methodology (wide range reflects regional price dispersion and scope definitions). Production is dominated by the Haber-Bosch process — the century-old synthesis of nitrogen and hydrogen at high temperature and pressure — which consumes ~1-2% of global energy supply and accounts for ~1.2-1.4% of global CO2 emissions. The feedstock split is ~72% natural gas via steam methane reforming (SMR), ~22% coal gasification (overwhelmingly [[China]]), and ~6% naphtha/fuel oil/other. This makes ammonia a natural gas derivative commodity: when gas prices move, ammonia margins move with them. European producers shuttered ~50% of capacity in 2022 when TTF gas spiked above EUR200/MWh, permanently shifting trade flows toward cheap-gas producers in the US Gulf, Middle East, and Trinidad.

The demand structure is fertilizer-dominated. Roughly 70% of ammonia goes into nitrogen fertilizers — [[urea]] (the single largest derivative at ~55% of fertilizer use), ammonium nitrate, DAP/MAP, UAN solutions, and direct application. Industrial uses (~15-20%) include explosives (ammonium nitrate), nylon intermediates (caprolactam), acrylonitrile, and refrigeration. The remaining ~5-10% is the growth story: ammonia as a zero-carbon shipping fuel (the IMO targets net-zero by 2050 for international shipping), as a hydrogen carrier for long-distance energy transport, and as a co-firing fuel in coal power plants ([[Japan]] and South Korea are piloting this). The shipping application alone could create 150-200 Mt of incremental demand by 2050 if ammonia captures 35-50% of the marine fuel mix as projected.

The investment case has two dimensions. Near-term, ammonia is a leveraged play on natural gas prices and agricultural demand — producers like [[CF Industries]] (CF), [[Yara]] (YAR.OL), and [[Nutrien]] (NTR) see margins expand when gas is cheap and fertilizer demand is strong. Long-term, green ammonia (produced from renewable hydrogen + Haber-Bosch) is the energy transition thesis. The $8.4B NEOM Green Hydrogen Complex ([[Saudi Arabia]], operational ~2027) will produce 1.2 Mt/year of green ammonia, the first commercial-scale project. But green ammonia costs 3-4x grey today (~$885-1,050/t vs. ~$250-400/t), and cost parity requires electrolyzer costs to fall 40-80% — optimistically by 2028-2030 in favorable regions, more realistically 2030-2035 globally. The gap between the thesis and the economics is the central tension.

The commodity is also highly traded — unlike [[Sulfuric Acid]], which is too corrosive and dense to ship economically, ammonia moves globally via dedicated tankers and pipelines. Major trade routes connect exporters ([[Russia]], [[Trinidad and Tobago]], [[Saudi Arabia]], [[Indonesia]]) to importers ([[India]], [[Brazil]], the US East Coast, Europe). The Russia-Ukraine war disrupted two critical channels: Russian pipeline exports via the Togliatti-Odessa pipeline (shut since February 2022, physically damaged June 2023) and Black Sea cargo flows. These disruptions, combined with the European gas crisis, accelerated the long-running shift of production toward low-cost gas regions.

## Production and supply structure

### The Haber-Bosch process

The synthesis of ammonia from atmospheric nitrogen and hydrogen — N2 + 3H2 → 2NH3 — was commercialized in 1913 and is arguably the most consequential chemical reaction in human history (without it, an estimated 40-50% of the world's population could not be fed). The process operates at 150-300 atm pressure and 400-500°C over an iron-based catalyst. The energy intensity is ~28-40 GJ per tonne of ammonia (best practice ~28 GJ/t, global average ~36 GJ/t).

The hydrogen feedstock is the carbon-intensive step. Three production routes:

### Feedstock breakdown (global, IEA data)

| Feedstock | Share | Process | Primary regions |
|-----------|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Natural gas | ~72% | Steam methane reforming (SMR) | US, Russia, Middle East, Europe, Trinidad |
| Coal | ~22% | Coal gasification | [[China]] (~79-85% of China's ammonia uses coal) |
| Naphtha/fuel oil/other | ~6% | Partial oxidation | [[India]], parts of Asia |

Natural gas accounts for 70-80% of the variable production cost at a gas-fed plant. At $3/MMBtu Henry Hub, US Gulf production cost is ~$150-200/t. At EUR200/MWh TTF (the 2022 peak), European production cost exceeded $1,500/t — above the selling price, forcing shutdowns.

### Fertilizer demand (dominant)

Nitrogen is the most consumed plant nutrient globally. Ammonia is the precursor for virtually all synthetic nitrogen fertilizers. The Haber-Bosch process is credited with sustaining roughly half the world's current population — without synthetic nitrogen, global agricultural output would collapse.

Key fertilizer derivatives and their ammonia consumption:
- Urea: ~0.58 t NH3 per tonne of urea. Global urea production ~180 Mt/year.
- Ammonium nitrate: ~0.21 t NH3 per tonne of AN (via nitric acid intermediate).
- DAP/MAP: Requires both ammonia and phosphoric acid (connects to [[Sulfuric Acid]] via phosphate rock processing).
- UAN solutions: Blend of urea and ammonium nitrate; liquid application.

Fertilizer demand is structurally underpinned by population growth, declining arable land per capita, and rising protein consumption in emerging markets. It is cyclical around crop prices, planting seasons, and government subsidy programs.

### Emerging demand — the energy transition thesis

| Application | Potential demand (Mt NH3/yr by 2050) | Status |
|-------------|--------------------------------------|--------|
| Shipping fuel | 150-200 | IMO targets net-zero by 2050; ammonia projected 35-50% of marine fuel mix |
| Power generation (co-firing) | 20-50 | [[Japan]] and South Korea piloting; controversial efficiency |
| Hydrogen carrier | 10-30 | Long-distance H2 transport (cracking at destination) |
| Total emerging | 180-280 | Roughly doubles current total production if realized |

The shipping application is the largest potential demand source. International shipping consumes ~300 Mt of fuel per year and emits ~1 Gt CO2 (2.5% of global emissions). The IMO's 2023 revised strategy targets at least 20% emissions reduction by 2030 (striving for 30%), 70% by 2040 (striving for 80%), and net-zero by or around 2050. Zero or near-zero GHG fuels must represent at least 5-10% of energy used by 2030.

[[Japan]] is the most aggressive on ammonia-for-power. JERA (Japan's largest power company, JV of [[Tokyo Electric Power]] and [[Chubu Electric Power]]) is piloting 20% ammonia co-firing at its Hekinan coal plant, with plans for 50% and eventually 100% ammonia. South Korea is pursuing a similar path. Critics (BNEF, E3G) argue co-firing is a costly approach that prolongs coal dependence — renewables are more economic.

### Benchmark prices

The Tampa CFR (cost and freight) price is the most-watched ammonia benchmark globally. It is set monthly through negotiation between [[Yara]] (the dominant global supplier) and [[Mosaic]] (the dominant US phosphate producer/buyer). Effective June 2025, S&P Global Platts formally defined this as reflecting only the Yara-Mosaic contract price.

### What drives ammonia prices

1. Natural gas prices — the dominant variable. Ammonia-gas correlation is the strongest in commodity markets. Henry Hub (US), TTF (Europe), JKM (Asia LNG) all feed through.
2. Unplanned outages — ammonia plants are high-pressure continuous operations; turnarounds and breakdowns create immediate supply gaps. Algerian and Egyptian outages drove 2024 tightening.
3. Fertilizer demand cycles — seasonal (spring planting in Northern Hemisphere), crop-price sensitive, government subsidy driven.
4. Trade disruptions — Russian pipeline/port closures, Suez/Panama Canal disruptions, sanctions.
5. Chinese export policy — China is self-sufficient and can swing between net importer and net exporter based on domestic coal costs and government policy.
6. Energy transition policy — IRA 45V hydrogen production tax credit (up to $3/kg H2), EU CBAM, IMO shipping mandates create demand signals but are not yet material to volumes.

## The green ammonia thesis

This is the big investment story — and the most uncertain.

### The promise

Ammonia is the most practical hydrogen carrier for long-distance energy transport. Pure hydrogen is difficult to store and ship (requires cryogenic -253°C or high-pressure tanks). Ammonia liquefies at -33°C at atmospheric pressure (or at room temperature under ~10 atm) and has an energy density of 12.7 MJ/L — lower than diesel (35.8 MJ/L) but practical for shipping. At the destination, ammonia can be cracked back to hydrogen or burned directly.

### The cost gap

| Metric | Grey ammonia | Green ammonia | Blue ammonia |
|--------|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| Production cost | $250-400/t | $885-1,050/t | $350-550/t |
| CO2 intensity | 1.6-2.9 t/t | ~0 | ~0.3-0.8 t/t |
| Cost premium vs. grey | Baseline | 3-4x | 1.2-1.8x |
| Parity timeline | — | 2028-2035 (region-dependent) | Near-competitive now with CCS incentives |

IRENA projects electrolyzer costs to fall 40-80% by 2030. Unsubsidized green ammonia could reach grey parity in regions with the cheapest renewables ([[Saudi Arabia]], Chile, [[Australia]], [[Norway]]) by 2028-2030. Globally, parity likely requires both cheap renewables and carbon pricing — realistically 2030-2035.

### The reality check

[[Green hydrogen]] economics have faced a brutal reality check. [[Fortescue]] — once targeting 15 Mt/year of green hydrogen by 2030 — cut 1,400+ jobs and wrote down ~$150M in cancelled projects. Multiple projects globally have stalled. The IRA's 45V production tax credit (up to $3/kg H2) was the most significant policy catalyst, but implementation delays and political uncertainty have slowed US project FIDs. Green ammonia's share of total production remains <1%.

## The natural gas nexus

Ammonia is functionally a gas derivative. The price correlation is the most important structural relationship in the market.

### Cost structure at a gas-fed plant

Natural gas serves a dual role: feedstock (hydrogen source via SMR) and fuel (energy for the Haber-Bosch reactor). Combined, gas accounts for 70-80% of variable production cost. At $3/MMBtu Henry Hub, a US Gulf producer has a delivered cost of ~$150-200/t. At $15/MMBtu (European average in 2023), cost jumps to ~$600-700/t.

### The 2022 crisis as case study

When [[Russia]] curtailed gas flows to Europe in 2022 and TTF spiked from ~EUR20/MWh to EUR340/MWh peak (August 2022):

- ~50% of European ammonia capacity shut down
- [[Yara]] halted production at Ferrara (Italy) and Le Havre (France) in March 2022
- European nitrogen fertilizer production fell ~33%
- Tampa CFR ammonia hit ~$1,425/t in May 2022 (vs. ~$250 pre-COVID normal)
- European farmers faced fertilizer shortages; governments intervened with subsidies

The crisis permanently accelerated production migration to cheap-gas regions. New capacity is being built in the US Gulf ([[CF Industries]] Blue Point, [[OCI]] Beaumont), [[Saudi Arabia]], and Qatar — not Europe.

### Regional cost advantage

| Region | Typical gas cost | Ammonia production cost | Competitive position |
|--------|-----------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| US Gulf (Henry Hub) | $2-4/MMBtu | $150-250/t | Global cost leader alongside Middle East |
| Middle East (Qatar, Saudi) | $1-2/MMBtu (subsidized) | $100-200/t | Lowest global cost; captive gas |
| [[Trinidad and Tobago]] | $2-4/MMBtu | $150-250/t | Caribbean export hub; declining gas reserves |
| [[Russia]] | $1-3/MMBtu | $100-200/t | Cheap but sanctioned/trade-restricted |
| Europe (TTF) | $8-15/MMBtu | $400-700/t | Structurally uncompetitive; import-dependent |
| [[China]] (coal-based) | Coal cost equivalent | $250-350/t | Self-sufficient; higher emissions |

## China

[[China]] dominates ammonia production but operates a fundamentally different model from the rest of the world — coal-based rather than gas-based.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Production (2024) | ~43 Mt |
| Global share | ~29% |
| Feedstock | ~79-85% coal gasification |
| CO2 intensity | ~4.0 t CO2/t NH3 (coal route is ~70% higher than gas route) |
| Share of global ammonia CO2 | ~45% (30% of production but 45% of emissions) |
| Self-sufficiency | Near-complete; food security priority |
| Export posture | Swing producer; policy-dependent |

China's coal dependence is an artifact of resource endowment: abundant coal, scarce domestic gas. Pulverized coal entrained flow gasification (PEF) is the dominant and improving technology. Environmental pressure is mounting — China's ammonia industry emits ~70% more CO2 per tonne than gas-based producers — but food security overrides climate policy. China is unlikely to become a significant importer; experts assess the government will prioritize self-sufficiency.

The environmental challenge is creating policy tension. China's inclusion of industrial sectors in its emissions trading system could increase the cost competitiveness of low-carbon ammonia routes domestically. Renewable ammonia projects are being explored, leveraging China's massive solar/wind buildout, but coal remains dominant for the foreseeable future.

### The Togliatti-Odessa pipeline

The world's longest ammonia pipeline — 2,417 km from Togliatti (Russia) to Yuzhny/Odessa port (Ukraine) — was the backbone of Russian ammonia exports to global markets. [[TogliattiAzot]], Russia's largest ammonia producer (~3 Mt/year capacity), suspended transit on February 24, 2022, the day Russia invaded Ukraine. In June 2023, a section in Kharkiv Oblast was physically damaged, releasing 134 tonnes of ammonia.

The pipeline shutdown removed ~2-3 Mt/year from global seaborne supply. Russia attempted to negotiate reopening as a condition of extending the Black Sea Grain Initiative (estimated $2.4B annual export revenue at stake), but Ukraine resisted. As of 2026, the pipeline remains inoperative with no realistic prospect of restoration. TogliattiAzot shuttered four of its seven ammonia lines and reduced operating rates at the remaining three.

## Environmental and regulatory

### The emissions profile

The Haber-Bosch process accounts for ~1.2-1.4% of global CO2 emissions — roughly equivalent to the entire aviation industry. The ammonia sector consumes ~2% of global energy. Per tonne of ammonia:

| Route | CO2 emissions (t CO2/t NH3) |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Natural gas (best practice) | ~1.6 |
| Natural gas (global average) | ~2.4 |
| Coal (China average) | ~4.0 |
| Global weighted average | ~2.9 |

Additionally, nitrogen fertilizer application creates downstream environmental effects: NOx emissions (nitrous oxide is 273x more potent than CO2 as a greenhouse gas over 100 years), nitrate runoff into waterways (dead zones in Gulf of Mexico, Baltic Sea), and ammonia itself is an air pollutant contributing to particulate matter formation.

### Decarbonization pathways

1. Efficiency gains — existing technologies could reduce emissions ~20% (heat recovery, process optimization, catalyst improvements).
2. Blue ammonia (SMR + CCS) — captures 85-95% of CO2; near-competitive with grey; multiple projects under construction.
3. Green ammonia (electrolysis + Haber-Bosch) — zero direct emissions; cost must fall 60-70% to reach parity (see green ammonia section above).
4. Demand-side reduction — precision agriculture reduces fertilizer waste (currently ~50% of applied nitrogen is lost to the environment); better application reduces total ammonia demand without reducing crop yields.

### Regulatory landscape

- EU CBAM — Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism includes fertilizers; European importers will pay carbon levies, potentially narrowing the cost gap between clean and grey ammonia.
- IRA 45V — US production tax credit up to $3/kg for clean hydrogen (applies to hydrogen used in ammonia production).
- IMO GHG Strategy — shipping decarbonization mandates create future demand signal for ammonia fuel.
- China ETS expansion — if ammonia/chemicals are included, coal-based production faces higher costs.

### Ammonia shipping

Ammonia requires dedicated refrigerated gas carriers. The global fleet is relatively small (~70-80 vessels), creating freight rate volatility when demand spikes. Shipbuilders in [[Japan]], [[China]], and South Korea are developing ammonia-fueled vessels and bunkering infrastructure. Japan invested >JPY120B (~$770M) in zero-emission ship construction; Korea's K-Shipbuilding program allocated ~$1.4B over a decade.

Gas is both feedstock and energy source, typically 70-80% of cash cost of ammonia production. ## Nitrogen's share of fertilizer consumption

![[nitrogen-share-of-nutrients-aurelion.png]]
*Nitrogen makes up ~58% of global fertilizer nutrient consumption, vs. ~24% phosphate and ~18% potash. Source: Aurelion Research.*

At 58% of global nutrient consumption, nitrogen dwarfs phosphate and potash. It is applied annually, demand is relatively stable, and farmers cannot skip it without immediate yield consequences. That combination of scale and inelasticity is precisely why the Persian Gulf disruption hits nitrogen prices hardest.
