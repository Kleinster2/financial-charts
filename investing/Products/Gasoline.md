# Gasoline

The most politically sensitive refined petroleum product in the US — the price on the gas station sign that voters see every day. RBOB (Reformulated Blendstock for Oxygenate Blending) is the futures benchmark, traded on NYMEX ([[CME Group]]). The single most direct transmission mechanism from oil markets to US consumer sentiment and, by extension, electoral politics.

## Why gasoline is the political benchmark

[[Robin J. Brooks]] (March 23, 2026) made the analytical case: US gasoline prices track [[WTI]], not [[Brent crude|Brent]] or [[Dubai crude|Dubai]]. This matters because [[Iran]]'s strategy in the 2026 conflict is to spike oil prices high enough to undercut American domestic support for the war. The politically decisive question is not "where is Brent?" but "what does the pump say?"

As of March 21: US gasoline at +32% since hostilities, still below $4/gallon — far below the $5 peak of June 2022 that severely damaged Biden's approval ratings. US energy independence (net exporter status) creates a buffer between Gulf disruptions and the American gas pump that didn't exist in prior oil shocks.

The $4 threshold is approximate but real — historically, sustained pump prices above $4 correlate with declining presidential approval and consumer confidence deterioration. Below $4, the issue doesn't dominate the news cycle. The current crisis hasn't breached that level, which limits the domestic political pressure on [[Trump II|Trump]] to de-escalate.
