# Gasoline

The most politically sensitive refined petroleum product in the US — the price on the gas station sign that voters see every day. RBOB (Reformulated Blendstock for Oxygenate Blending) is the futures benchmark, traded on NYMEX ([[CME Group]]). The single most direct transmission mechanism from oil markets to US consumer sentiment and, by extension, electoral politics.

## Why gasoline is the political benchmark

[[Robin J. Brooks]] (March 23, 2026) made the analytical case: US gasoline prices track [[WTI]], not [[Brent crude|Brent]] or [[Dubai crude|Dubai]]. This matters because [[Iran]]'s strategy in the 2026 conflict is to spike oil prices high enough to undercut American domestic support for the war. The politically decisive question is not "where is Brent?" but "what does the pump say?"

As of March 21: US gasoline at +32% since hostilities, still below $4/gallon — far below the $5 peak of June 2022 that severely damaged Biden's approval ratings. US energy independence (net exporter status) creates a buffer between Gulf disruptions and the American gas pump that didn't exist in prior oil shocks.

The $4 threshold is approximate but real — historically, sustained pump prices above $4 correlate with declining presidential approval and consumer confidence deterioration. Below $4, the issue doesn't dominate the news cycle.

The threshold crossing: the vault's March 21 Brooks reference had US gasoline "still below $4/gallon." The All-In Podcast E222 readout (April 3) described gasoline as already "over $4/gallon across US" — placing the $4 breach somewhere in late March. By April 15 (FT, Jamie Smyth), the AAA national average stood at $4.10/gallon, up approximately $1/gal since the war began. The domestic political constraint that the vault's earlier framing argued "limits the domestic political pressure on Trump to de-escalate" has now activated. Simultaneously, [[Rapidan Energy Group]] (April 15) signaled that if prices continue climbing toward $150/bbl, export restrictions become a live policy option — which would reprice the [[US Energy Independence Six Countries]] framework built over fifteen years.

**[[Amos Hochstein]] assessment (Mar 31):** $4/gallon "already a psychological barrier, not for the White House, but for the American people." California gasoline already over $6/gallon.

US diesel at $5/gallon (Will Kennedy, Bloomberg, March 19) is running well ahead of gasoline — trucking and agriculture absorbing more of the shock than consumers at the pump. The refined product spread (diesel premium over gasoline) widening signals industrial pain arriving before consumer pain.

By April 15, diesel reached $5.63/gallon, within $0.18 of the all-time high of $5.81. Hochstein's March 31 framing of diesel as the more economically consequential product — because trucking costs pass through to every consumer good — is playing out: pump gasoline at $4.10 is the political threshold; diesel near ATH is the goods-inflation transmission channel.

## Trump campaign-promise inversion (April 15)

The FT article (Jamie Smyth) noted Trump's 2024 campaign pledge to halve energy costs within the first year of the second term. As of April 15, US petrol, diesel, and electricity prices are all rising faster than the inflation rate. The campaign-trail framing has inverted — energy is now an inflation accelerant, not a deflator.

The pump price has been above $4 since approximately early April (All-In E222 readout on April 3), but the April 15 FT framing is the first time the press has paired the pump price directly with the campaign pledge. Prior weeks (Mar 6, Mar 19, Mar 21) kept the pain analytical; April 15 makes it electoral.
