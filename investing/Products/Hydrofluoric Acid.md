# Hydrofluoric Acid

**Hydrofluoric acid** (HF) is a solution of hydrogen fluoride in water — and in its anhydrous form (AHF), pure hydrogen fluoride gas/liquid. It is the gateway chemical for virtually all fluorine chemistry: fluoropolymers ([[PTFE]], [[PVDF]]), refrigerants (HFCs, HFOs), aluminum fluoride for smelting, petroleum alkylation catalyst, electronic-grade etchant for semiconductors, and uranium hexafluoride for nuclear fuel. Global production is ~2.2 Mt/year (2024), a small market by volume but with outsized strategic importance because it sits upstream of multiple critical supply chains — and because [[China]] controls ~60% of the fluorspar feedstock that makes it.

---

## Synopsis

The hydrofluoric acid market is valued at ~$2.5-3.0 billion (2024), with global production of approximately 2.2 million tonnes. HF is produced by reacting acid-grade fluorspar (CaF2, >97% purity) with [[Sulfuric Acid]] at 250-300 C: CaF2 + H2SO4 → 2HF + CaSO4. This makes HF supply structurally dependent on two inputs — fluorspar, where [[China]] controls ~60% of global mining output, and sulfuric acid, a commodity with its own involuntary-supply dynamics tied to copper smelting and oil refining. The reaction also produces calcium sulfate (synthetic gypsum) as a byproduct at roughly 3.4 tonnes per tonne of HF, creating a waste disposal challenge.

The demand structure splits across several distinct value chains. Fluorochemicals — refrigerants, fluoropolymers, fluorocarbons — account for the largest share (~40-45% of global HF consumption). Aluminum smelting consumes HF as the feedstock for aluminum fluoride (AlF3) and synthetic cryolite (Na3AlF6), essential flux agents in the Hall-Heroult electrolysis process. Petroleum refining uses HF as an alkylation catalyst for high-octane gasoline blending (~50 US refineries run HF alkylation units). The semiconductor industry requires electronic-grade HF (UP-SSS/G5 purity) for wafer etching and oxide removal — a small-volume but high-value segment dominated by [[Japan]] ([[Stella Chemifa]], [[Daikin]], Morita Chemical) and [[South Korea]] ([[Soulbrain]]). Uranium processing requires HF for the UO2 → UF4 → UF6 conversion chain that feeds enrichment plants.

The strategic chokepoint is fluorspar. [[China]] produces ~3.8 Mt of the global ~9.5 Mt (2024), but is shifting from exporter to net consumer as domestic fluorochemical demand grows. The US has zero domestic fluorspar production and is 100% import-dependent. The EU lists fluorspar as a critical raw material. [[Japan]]'s 2019 export restrictions on hydrogen fluoride to [[South Korea]] — triggered by a forced-labor court ruling — demonstrated that HF supply can be weaponized: Japanese exports of HF to South Korea fell 87.9%, forcing Korean chipmakers to scramble for alternative sources and prompting [[Soulbrain]] to double domestic capacity. That episode was a dry run for the critical-mineral decoupling now accelerating across the semiconductor supply chain.

The investment case is structural. Energy transition demand is additive — PVDF for lithium-ion battery binders, HF for uranium conversion in the nuclear renaissance, growing fluoropolymer needs for chemical-resistant industrial applications. The Kigali Amendment's HFC phase-down forces a refrigerant transition to HFOs, where [[Honeywell]], [[Chemours]], and [[Arkema]] hold key patents. Meanwhile, semiconductor node shrinkage and 3D architectures (gate-all-around, high-aspect-ratio etching) increase HF consumption per wafer. All of this pulls on a fluorspar supply base that is geographically concentrated and increasingly constrained by Chinese domestic consumption and environmental inspections.

## Production and supply structure

### The reaction

CaF2 + H2SO4 → 2HF + CaSO4

Acid-grade fluorspar (>97% CaF2) is reacted with concentrated [[Sulfuric Acid]] (93%) in rotary kilns at 250-300 C. Yield is approximately 0.5 tonnes HF per tonne of fluorspar consumed, plus ~3.4 tonnes of calcium sulfate (synthetic gypsum) waste. The process requires significant energy input and generates corrosive fumes, demanding specialized reactor materials (carbon steel lined with acid-resistant alloys).

The sulfuric acid input links HF production directly to the H2SO4 supply chain — itself driven by copper smelter byproduct acid, elemental sulfur from petroleum refining, and pyrite roasting. See [[Sulfuric Acid]] for the full supply dynamics.

#### China's fluorspar dynamics

China is the world's largest fluorspar producer *and* increasingly its largest consumer. Domestic demand from fluorochemicals, aluminum, and semiconductor chemicals is growing faster than production, transforming China from a net exporter to a supply-constrained domestic market. Country-wide safety inspections in 2024 further tightened supply. The US fluorspar price has risen steadily from $267/t (2017) to $470/t (2024), reflecting this structural shift.

The discrepancy reflects different scope definitions — industrial-grade HF vs. all grades including AHF for fluorochemicals.

### Fluorochemicals and refrigerants (~35-40%)

HF is the starting material for virtually all fluorinated organic compounds. The chain runs: HF → chlorofluorocarbons/hydrochlorofluorocarbons/hydrofluorocarbons/hydrofluoroolefins → end applications. The Montreal Protocol eliminated CFCs (ozone depleting); the Kigali Amendment (2016) now phases down HFCs (high global warming potential, GWP of 1,000-4,000). This forces a transition to HFOs (GWP ~1), which still require HF as feedstock but through different synthesis routes.

Key dynamics:
- HFC phase-down timeline: developed countries must cut 45% by 2024, 85% by 2036 (vs. 2011-2013 baseline)
- HFO transition creates winners: [[Honeywell]] (Solstice), [[Chemours]] (Opteon), [[Daikin]] hold critical patents
- Two-thirds of all low-GWP refrigerant patent litigation involves just three companies: [[Honeywell]], [[Chemours]], [[Arkema]]
- China's Huanxin successfully invalidated one of [[Honeywell]]'s HFO-1234yf manufacturing patents (Sep 2021), opening the door for Chinese generic production
- Net effect on HF demand: roughly neutral near-term (HFOs still need HF), but patent dynamics determine who captures value

### Fluoropolymers (~10-15%)

The global fluoropolymer market is valued at ~$8-10 billion (2024), growing at ~6-7% CAGR. PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene, Teflon) holds ~65% of the fluoropolymer market. HF consumption is approximately 1.8 tonnes per tonne of PTFE produced.

Key fluoropolymers from HF:

| Polymer | Trade names | Key applications | HF link |
|---------|------------|-----------------|---------|
| PTFE | Teflon (Chemours) | Non-stick, seals, gaskets, chemical processing | HF → chloroform → HCFC-22 → TFE → PTFE |
| PVDF | Kynar (Arkema), Solef (Syensqo) | Li-ion battery binders/separators, chemical piping | HF → VDF monomer → PVDF |
| FEP | Teflon FEP (Chemours) | Wire insulation, tubing | HF → HFP + TFE → FEP |
| PFA | Teflon PFA (Chemours) | Semiconductor equipment linings | HF → PPVE + TFE → PFA |

PVDF is the energy transition play. PVDF serves as the primary binder in lithium-ion battery cathodes and anodes (~2-5% of electrode composition), and as separator coating. The lithium-ion battery market is projected to exceed $100B by 2030. [[Arkema]] (Kynar) and [[Syensqo]] (Solef) are the dominant Western suppliers. Chinese producers (Dongyue Group, others) are scaling rapidly — Dongyue completed a 10,000 t/year PVDF expansion in Oct 2022, bringing total capacity to 25,000 t/year.

### Aluminum smelting (~15-20%)

The Hall-Heroult process dissolves alumina (Al2O3) in a molten electrolyte of cryolite (Na3AlF6) and aluminum fluoride (AlF3) at ~960 C. Both cryolite and AlF3 are produced from HF:

- AlF3: HF + Al(OH)3 → AlF3 + H2O
- Synthetic cryolite: 6HF + Al(OH)3 + 3NaOH → Na3AlF6 + 6H2O

Natural cryolite (from Greenland) is commercially extinct. All cryolite used in aluminum smelting is now synthetic, produced from HF. Aluminum smelter demand for HF tracks global primary aluminum production (~70 Mt/year), which is dominated by [[China]] (~60% of global output).

### Petroleum alkylation (~5-8%)

About 100 US refineries operate alkylation units; roughly half use HF catalyst, the other half use [[Sulfuric Acid]]. The HF process converts isobutane and light olefins into alkylate — a clean, high-octane gasoline blending component. HF alkylation does not require refrigeration (unlike H2SO4), making it cheaper to operate, but presents far greater safety risk: a release of HF at ambient temperature produces a ground-hugging toxic vapor cloud. Public pressure has pushed some refineries to convert from HF to H2SO4 or solid-acid processes, but conversion costs are $200-500M per unit, limiting the pace of transition.

### Uranium processing (~2-3%)

The nuclear fuel conversion chain requires HF at a critical step:

UO3 → UO2 (hydrogen reduction) → UF4 (reaction with HF) → UF6 (reaction with F2)

UF6 is the gaseous form required for isotope enrichment. Each mole of UF6 produced requires HF. In the reverse direction, depleted UF6 (DUF6) conversion facilities — such as the US DOE's plants in Ohio and Kentucky — produce HF as a byproduct (6 moles HF per mole UF6 converted), which is sold commercially.

The nuclear renaissance is real: installed nuclear capacity of ~398 GWe is projected to nearly double to 746 GWe by 2040 (World Nuclear Association reference scenario), with uranium requirements more than doubling to ~150,000 Mt by 2040. The US DOE has committed $2.7B to restore domestic enrichment capacity. More enrichment means more UF6 conversion, which means more HF demand — modest in volume but structurally growing.

### What drives HF prices

- Fluorspar availability — the dominant input cost. Chinese fluorspar supply disruptions (environmental inspections, mine closures, export policy) directly hit HF prices
- Sulfuric acid cost — the second key input; H2SO4 price spikes pass through to HF
- Energy costs — kiln operation is energy-intensive; European producers especially sensitive
- Refrigerant production cycles — seasonal demand patterns from HVAC
- Semiconductor capacity additions — new fabs require electronic-grade HF supply contracts (e.g., [[Orbia]]-[[Intel]] 5-year deal, Jul 2024)
- Chinese domestic demand growth — as China consumes more HF domestically, less fluorspar and HF are available for export, tightening global supply

## The semiconductor chokepoint

Electronic-grade HF (EL-grade, UP-SSS/G5 purity) is one of the most critical wet chemicals in semiconductor fabrication. It is used for:

- Oxide etching — removal of SiO2 layers (buffered oxide etch, BOE = HF + NH4F)
- Wafer cleaning — SC-2 clean and final rinse steps use dilute HF to remove metallic contaminants
- Native oxide removal — critical pre-deposition step
- High-aspect-ratio etching — 3D NAND, gate-all-around transistors increase HF consumption per wafer

Japanese fabs alone consume >70,000 tonnes of electronic-grade HF annually. As nodes shrink and architectures go 3D, HF consumption per wafer increases — more etch steps, more cleaning cycles, higher purity requirements.

### The 2019 Japan-South Korea precedent

In July 2019, [[Japan]] imposed export controls on three semiconductor materials destined for [[South Korea]]: hydrogen fluoride, photoresist, and fluorinated polyimide. The trigger was the South Korean Supreme Court's October 2018 ruling ordering Japanese companies to compensate wartime forced laborers — though Japan framed the restrictions as a security concern about re-exports to hostile nations.

Impact:
- Japanese HF exports to South Korea fell 87.9%
- [[Japan]] controlled ~70% of global hydrogen fluoride supply and >90% of Korean imports of the restricted materials
- [[South Korea]] scrambled to diversify: sourcing from Belgium, the US, Taiwan
- [[Soulbrain]] doubled domestic HF capacity to supply ~two-thirds of Korean semiconductor demand
- South Korea filed a WTO dispute (DS590) in September 2019
- Controls were lifted in March 2023 as part of diplomatic normalization

Lesson for the investment case: electronic-grade HF is weaponizable. A handful of Japanese suppliers control the global market. Any disruption — geopolitical, natural disaster, export controls — directly threatens semiconductor production at [[TSMC]], [[Samsung Semiconductor]], [[Intel]], and every other leading-edge fab.

## Fluoropolymers and the energy transition

HF is the sole feedstock for the fluorine atoms in all fluoropolymers. The production chain runs through intermediate fluorocarbons:

HF → HCFC-22 (chlorodifluoromethane) → TFE (tetrafluoroethylene) → PTFE, FEP, PFA
HF → VDF (vinylidene fluoride) → PVDF

### PVDF: the battery material

PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride) is the dominant binder for lithium-ion battery electrodes, used in both cathodes and anodes at ~2-5% of electrode mass. It provides:
- Electrochemical stability across the full 0-5V range
- Strong adhesion between active material particles and current collector
- Chemical resistance to electrolyte solvents

[[Arkema]] (Kynar brand) and [[Syensqo]] (Solef brand) are the leading Western PVDF suppliers. Battery-grade PVDF is a higher-margin product than industrial PVDF, and capacity is being added rapidly — but each tonne of PVDF requires HF upstream, linking battery production growth to fluorspar supply.

The EU is considering PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) restrictions that could affect fluoropolymer production, though battery applications are likely to receive exemptions given their criticality to decarbonization goals.

## Refrigerant transition

### The regulatory cascade

| Phase | Protocol | Target | Timeline |
|-------|----------|--------|----------|
| CFCs | Montreal (1987) | Ozone-depleting | Eliminated by 2010 |
| HCFCs | Montreal (Copenhagen, 1992) | Ozone-depleting, high GWP | Phase-out 2020-2030 |
| HFCs | Kigali Amendment (2016) | High GWP (1,000-4,000) | Phase-down: 45% by 2024, 85% by 2036 (Group 1) |
| HFOs | Current generation | Low GWP (~1-6) | Growing; patent-protected |

Each generation still requires HF as a raw material. The transition from HFCs to HFOs does not reduce HF demand — it reshapes who captures the downstream value.

### Patent warfare

[[Honeywell]] and [[Chemours]] jointly control the key patents for HFO-1234yf, the dominant next-generation automotive refrigerant. [[Arkema]] holds competing patents for some HFO formulations. Two-thirds of all low-GWP refrigerant patent litigation globally involves these three companies. Chinese producers are challenging Western patents: Huanxin successfully invalidated [[Honeywell]]'s CN 101395108 B manufacturing patent in September 2021, potentially opening Chinese domestic production of HFO-1234yf without license fees.

## China and fluorspar: the critical mineral chokepoint

### Fluorspar supply dominance

[[China]] produces ~3.8 Mt of fluorspar annually (~40-60% of global output, depending on source and inclusion of unreported production). But the strategic picture is evolving:

| Factor | Direction | Impact on global HF supply |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Domestic fluorochemical demand | Rising fast | Less fluorspar available for export |
| Environmental inspections (2024) | Tightening supply | Production disruptions, mine closures |
| Reserve depletion | High-grade reserves declining | Shift toward lower-grade, higher-cost deposits |
| Export policy | Increasingly restrictive | Fluorspar not yet under explicit export controls, but trajectory is clear |
| Safety inspections (2024) | Country-wide | Constrained output across the industry |

### US and EU exposure

The US has zero domestic fluorspar production and is 100% import-dependent for its estimated 400,000-500,000 tonnes of annual consumption. The USGS designates fluorspar as a critical mineral. Primary import sources: Mexico, Vietnam, South Africa, China.

The EU lists fluorspar as a critical raw material under the 2024 Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA), which targets no more than 65% dependence on any single country for any critical mineral by 2030. Mexico ([[Orbia]]) provides some diversification.

### Weaponization risk

While fluorspar itself has not been specifically targeted in China's 2024-2025 export control actions (which focused on rare earths, gallium, germanium, graphite, antimony), the precedent is established. China temporarily suspended some critical mineral export controls in November 2025 as part of a trade truce, but the infrastructure for restrictions is in place. Given HF's role in semiconductors, aluminum, and nuclear fuel — all strategically sensitive — fluorspar export controls would be a logical escalation tool.

## Safety

HF is arguably the most dangerous common industrial chemical. It kills differently than other acids.

### Mechanism of injury

Unlike strong mineral acids that cause surface burns through H+ ion damage, HF's danger comes from the fluoride ion (F-), which penetrates skin, mucous membranes, and even bone. The fluoride ion:

1. Penetrates tissue rapidly — 70% HF penetrates all five epidermal layers within 1 minute and reaches the dermis in 5 minutes
2. Binds calcium and magnesium — precipitates calcium fluoride (CaF2) in tissues, causing deep necrosis
3. Causes systemic hypocalcemia — serum calcium depletion → cardiac arrhythmias → death
4. Delayed symptoms at low concentrations — exposures to dilute HF (1-50%) may not cause pain for 1-24 hours, by which time fluoride has penetrated deeply

### Lethality

- Skin exposure to as little as 2.5% of body surface area (roughly an 8-inch square) with concentrated HF can be fatal
- Deaths have occurred from exposures as small as 2% BSA with concentrated solutions
- Inhalation: NIOSH IDLH (Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health) = 30 ppm over 30 minutes
- OSHA PEL: 3 ppm (8-hour TWA)
- Lowest lethal dose (human, inhalation): 50 ppm/30 minutes

### Treatment

Calcium gluconate gel (topical) and calcium gluconate injection (intra-arterial or IV) are the primary treatments, neutralizing fluoride ion by forming insoluble CaF2. Every facility handling HF must stock calcium gluconate.

### Regulatory impact

The extreme hazard profile creates regulatory barriers to entry, specialized handling requirements, and public opposition to HF-using facilities (particularly petroleum alkylation units in urban areas). This limits new HF production capacity and creates moat-like advantages for established producers with existing permits and infrastructure.
