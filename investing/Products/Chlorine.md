# Chlorine

**Chlorine** (Cl2) is the world's most essential disinfection chemical and a foundational building block of the modern chemical industry, co-produced with [[Caustic Soda]] in a fixed stoichiometric ratio through the chlor-alkali electrolysis of brine. It is impossible to produce one without the other, making chlorine the most important example of an industrial co-product constraint.

---

## Synopsis

The global chlorine market produced approximately 75-80 million tonnes in 2024, valued at an estimated $35-40 billion. Chlorine sits at the intersection of three structural forces: PVC/construction demand (~34% of consumption, channeled through the ethylene dichloride → vinyl chloride monomer → PVC chain), water treatment (~15-20%, the inelastic backbone of global sanitation), and specialty chemicals including isocyanates for polyurethane (~10-12%), solvents, and electronic-grade HCl for semiconductor wafer cleaning.

The defining characteristic of chlorine is the co-product constraint. Every tonne of chlorine produces exactly 1.1 tonnes of [[Caustic Soda]] (NaOH) and 0.03 tonnes of hydrogen -- a 2.13-tonne package called the electrochemical unit (ECU). Producers cannot make more of one without making more of the other. But demand for the two products is driven by entirely different end markets: chlorine tracks construction and PVC cycles, while caustic soda tracks alumina refining, pulp and paper, and general manufacturing. This mismatch creates a permanent see-saw dynamic where chlorine and caustic soda prices move in inverse cycles, 180 degrees out of phase. When construction booms, chlorine tightens and caustic floods; when construction slows, chlorine is the surplus co-product and caustic firms up.

The investment thesis centers on chlorine's dual nature: it is simultaneously a cyclical commodity (tied to housing starts, PVC demand, and construction activity) and a structural necessity (water treatment, semiconductors, pharmaceuticals). The cyclical side is dominated by [[China]], which accounts for ~40% of global production and ~45% of PVC demand, with the added complexity of China's coal-based calcium carbide VCM route that introduces mercury contamination and environmental risk. The structural side is underpinned by the fact that there is no commercially viable alternative to chlorination for municipal water treatment at scale -- 98% of US water utilities use chlorine-based disinfection. This creates a floor of inelastic demand that other industrial chemicals lack.

The key risk is the co-product trap: producers cannot optimize for chlorine alone. In a weak PVC market, chlorine has negative netback value in some regions -- producers literally pay customers to take it, because shutting down the electrolysis cell would also eliminate caustic soda revenue. The ECU must be profitable in aggregate, and the split between chlorine and caustic value shifts constantly.

## Production and supply structure

### The chlor-alkali process

Chlorine is produced exclusively through the electrolysis of sodium chloride (NaCl) dissolved in water -- the chlor-alkali process. The reaction is straightforward:

2 NaCl + 2 H2O → Cl2 + 2 NaOH + H2

This always produces chlorine, [[Caustic Soda]], and hydrogen in a fixed ratio: 1.00 tonne Cl2 : 1.10 tonnes NaOH : 0.03 tonnes H2. This 2.13-tonne package is one electrochemical unit (ECU). The ratio is stoichiometric -- it cannot be changed by engineering or economics. It is set by the molecular weights of chlorine (70.9 g/mol) and sodium hydroxide (40 g/mol).

### Cell technologies

| Technology | Share (global) | Energy use (kWh/t Cl2) | Status |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|--------|
| Membrane cell | ~80%+ | ~2,400-2,600 | Standard for new plants; Nafion/Flemion membranes |
| Diaphragm cell | ~15% | ~2,800-3,100 | Legacy; still operating in US (asbestos-free) |
| Mercury cell | <1% | ~3,200-3,600 | Phased out in EU by Dec 2017; purest caustic but toxic |

The industry has almost entirely converted to membrane cell technology. Mercury cell plants, once 55% of European capacity, were mandated out by the EU Mercury Regulation. The membrane cell produces lower-purity caustic (~33% NaOH) but uses ~20% less electricity and has no mercury emissions. Electricity represents approximately 50% of total production cost, making chlor-alkali among the most energy-intensive chemical processes.

### The co-product constraint

This is the defining structural feature of the chlorine market. Chlorine production is tethered to caustic soda demand and vice versa. Neither can be produced independently at commercial scale.

The consequences:
- Surplus/deficit oscillation -- when caustic soda demand is strong (alumina refining booms, pulp demand rises), producers ramp up electrolysis. This simultaneously floods the market with chlorine, depressing Cl2 prices even if chlorine demand hasn't changed.
- Inverse price cycles -- chlorine and caustic soda prices move 180 degrees out of phase. When construction slows (reducing PVC/chlorine demand), producers cut rates, tightening caustic supply and pushing NaOH prices up.
- Negative chlorine netback -- in extreme cases of caustic-led production, chlorine has negative value; producers pay for disposal. This happened periodically in European markets during 2023-2024 when caustic demand was firm but PVC/construction was weak.
- ECU optimization -- producers think in ECU terms, not chlorine or caustic individually. The combined ECU value must cover the ~$200-300/ECU electricity cost plus other operating expenses. Which co-product carries the margin shifts cycle to cycle.

## Pricing

### ECU pricing concept

Because chlorine and caustic are co-produced, the industry prices them as a combined unit. One ECU = 1.0 t Cl2 + 1.1 t NaOH + 0.03 t H2. The base-case breakeven ECU revenue is approximately $500-600, of which electricity costs ~$200-300 (at 3.3 MWh/ECU).

The split between chlorine and caustic value within the ECU is the key variable. In a chlorine-led market (strong construction, PVC demand), chlorine captures 50-60% of ECU value. In a caustic-led market (strong alumina, paper demand), caustic captures 60-70% and chlorine may trade at or below zero netback.

The US-China price gap is enormous: US liquid chlorine at $750/Mt vs. Chinese at $34/Mt reflects fundamentally different market structures (US capacity discipline + high energy cost vs. Chinese overcapacity + coal-powered, integrated PVC operations).

### What drives price swings

- Construction/housing cycles -- PVC demand is the largest chlorine end use; US housing starts directly correlate with chlorine tightness
- Caustic soda demand -- strong caustic markets incentivize higher operating rates, flooding chlorine supply
- Energy costs -- electricity is ~50% of ECU cost; European chlor-alkali has been under severe energy cost pressure since 2022
- Producer operating rate discipline -- [[Olin]] has explicitly pursued a strategy of running below capacity to support pricing
- Seasonal water treatment demand -- summer months see higher chlorination demand (pools, higher water usage)
- Unplanned outages -- force majeures (hurricanes on Gulf Coast, plant accidents) tighten supply rapidly

## The PVC connection

PVC is the single largest chlorine derivative, consuming approximately one-third of all chlorine produced globally. The production chain is:

Ethylene + Cl2 → EDC (ethylene dichloride) → VCM (vinyl chloride monomer) → PVC

### Scale

Global PVC production reached approximately 57 million tonnes of demand in 2024, with a market valued at ~$60-65 billion. Construction accounts for 60% of PVC end use (pipes, window frames, flooring, roofing, cladding, cable insulation).

### Housing construction link

Chlorine demand is a derivative of housing starts. The chain runs: housing permits → construction activity → PVC pipe/profile/siding demand → VCM demand → EDC demand → chlorine demand. A 10% swing in US housing starts ripples through to chlor-alkali operating rates within 3-6 months.

### China's PVC overcapacity

[[China]] dominates global PVC:
- ~28 Mt capacity (end 2024), rising to ~30 Mt by end-2025 (+7.35% YoY)
- Operating rates at ~71% -- chronic overcapacity
- Nearly 45% of global PVC demand, ~40% of production
- ~75% of Chinese PVC consumed domestically in construction
- Net exports of ~900 kt/year expected, pressuring global prices

### The calcium carbide route (China-specific)

China's PVC production splits into two routes:

| Route | Share (China) | Feedstock | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Calcium carbide → acetylene → VCM | ~72-80% | Coal | Unique to China; mercury catalyst required |
| Ethylene-based (oxychlorination) | ~20-28% | Oil/gas | Global standard; mercury-free |

The calcium carbide route exists because China is "rich in coal, poor in oil and gas." Coal → calcium carbide → acetylene → VCM (using mercuric chloride catalyst) → PVC. This route requires 10,000+ tonnes of mercury-based catalyst annually (5-12% mercury content), making China's PVC industry one of the world's largest sources of anthropogenic mercury emissions.

The [[Minamata Convention]] (2013) targets mercury reduction but has not yet forced a full phase-out. [[Johnson Matthey]] developed a mercury-free gold-on-carbon catalyst, and the Asian Development Bank funded $100M in demonstration projects, but commercial adoption remains slow. The ethylene route share is rising (~28% by 2025, up from ~20% in 2021) as environmental regulation tightens.

## Water treatment

Chlorination is the backbone of global water safety and creates the most inelastic component of chlorine demand.

### Scale and importance

- ~98% of US water utilities use chlorine-based disinfection (chlorine gas, sodium hypochlorite, or chlorine dioxide)
- ~2 billion people globally still lack safely managed drinking water (WHO, 2020) -- chlorination is the primary technology for closing this gap
- Chlorination has been standard municipal practice since 1908 (Jersey City, NJ was first)
- Cost: chlorination is the cheapest disinfection method (~$0.10-0.50 per 1,000 gallons)

### Why this matters for chlorine demand

Water treatment chlorine demand is:
- Inelastic -- municipalities cannot stop disinfecting water regardless of chlorine price
- Growing -- developing world infrastructure + tightening regulations
- Non-cyclical -- uncorrelated with construction or manufacturing
- Growing at ~4.7% CAGR -- fastest-growing chlorine end-use segment

This creates a demand floor that other industrial chemicals (sulfuric acid, ethylene) lack. Even in a severe recession with construction at zero, water treatment demand persists at full volume.

## Semiconductor angle

Hydrochloric acid (HCl), derived from chlorine, is a critical chemical in semiconductor manufacturing.

### Role in wafer fabrication

In the RCA cleaning process (standard since the 1960s), Standard Clean 2 (SC-2) uses a mixture of HCl + H2O2 + deionized water to remove metallic contaminants (alkali metals, heavy metals) from silicon wafer surfaces. This step is essential before every major deposition or etching process.

### Electronic-grade HCl

Semiconductor fabrication requires ultra-high-purity (electronic-grade or "CMOS-grade") HCl with impurities measured in parts per trillion. The supply chain is specialized:

- Standard industrial HCl (~30-37% concentration) is produced as a byproduct of chlorination reactions
- Electronic-grade HCl undergoes additional purification (distillation, filtration, gas-phase synthesis)
- Key suppliers include Linde, Air Liquide, Kanto Chemical, Stella Chemifa
- Price premium: electronic-grade HCl costs 5-10x industrial-grade

### Investment relevance

Semiconductor-grade HCl is a small volume market (~1-2% of chlorine demand) but a high-value segment. As fab capacity expands globally (CHIPS Act, European Chips Act, Japanese subsidies), electronic-grade HCl demand grows proportionally. Each advanced fab consumes thousands of tonnes of process chemicals annually.

## China

[[China]] dominates both production and consumption of chlorine:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Production | ~30-32 Mt/year (~40% of global) |
| PVC capacity | ~28-30 Mt (world's largest) |
| Calcium carbide PVC share | ~72-80% of Chinese PVC |
| Chlor-alkali operating rate | ~71% (significant overcapacity) |
| Chlorine price (Sep 2025) | ~$34/Mt (vs. $752 in US) |

### Structural characteristics

China's chlor-alkali industry is structurally different from the West:

1. Coal-integrated -- the calcium carbide PVC route means Chinese chlorine economics are tied to coal prices, not natural gas or ethylene. This creates a fundamentally different cost structure.

2. Overcapacity is structural -- with PVC operating at ~71% utilization and continued capacity additions, China chronically overproduces chlorine relative to domestic demand.

3. Mercury problem -- the calcium carbide VCM route requires mercury catalysts. China's PVC industry is the world's largest industrial mercury consumer. Environmental remediation and the slow transition to mercury-free catalysts are long-term headwinds.

4. Export pressure -- Chinese chlorine is too cheap and too dangerous to export in bulk (toxic gas + low value/weight), but China exports the chlorine embedded in PVC, EDC, and other derivatives. This indirect chlorine export pressures global PVC prices.

5. Provincial fragmentation -- unlike Western markets where 3 companies control 70% of capacity, Chinese production is spread across dozens of provincial producers with limited coordination.

## Safety and regulation

### Toxicity

Chlorine is among the most dangerous industrial chemicals in routine commerce:

- LC50 (rat, 1hr): 293 ppm -- lethal at relatively low concentrations
- IDLH (immediately dangerous): 10 ppm
- Detectable by smell: 0.5-1 ppm (sharp, pungent odor)
- Heavier than air (density 3.2 g/L vs. 1.2 g/L for air) -- pools in low-lying areas
- Reacts with water in lungs to form hydrochloric and hypochlorous acid, destroying tissue

### History of chlorine as a weapon

Chlorine has a unique and dark history as the world's first chemical weapon:

- April 22, 1915 -- German forces released 168 tonnes of chlorine gas from ~6,000 canisters at the Second Battle of Ypres, Belgium. The first large-scale chemical weapons attack in history.
- Fritz Haber -- German chemist (Nobel laureate, 1918, for ammonia synthesis) who developed and personally supervised the Ypres gas attack. His wife Clara Immerwahr committed suicide one week later in protest.
- WWI total -- ~124,200 tonnes of chemical agents released; 90,000+ soldiers killed by gas exposure.
- 1925 Geneva Protocol -- banned use (but not production) of chemical and biological weapons.
- 1993 Chemical Weapons Convention -- comprehensive ban; chlorine itself is not listed as a scheduled chemical (dual-use, too widely needed for civilian purposes).
- Modern use -- chlorine barrel bombs used in Syrian civil war (2014-2018), confirmed by OPCW investigations.

### Transport regulations

- UN number: 1017
- Hazard class: 2.3 (toxic gas) with subsidiary risks 5.1 (oxidizer) and 8 (corrosive)
- Toxic Inhalation Hazard (TIH): subject to the most stringent US railroad transport regulations
- DOT specification: tank cars must meet DOT 105J600W specification (since 2008)
- Phase-out: non-normalized steel tank cars prohibited after Dec 31, 2020; full HM-246 compliance required by Dec 31, 2027
- Transport forms: 100-150 lb cylinders, 1-ton containers, 90-ton railroad tank cars, 17-ton highway trailers

The transport restrictions matter for pricing. Chlorine's toxicity and low value-to-weight ratio mean most chlorine is consumed within 100-200 miles of production. Long-distance trade is limited, creating regional market segmentation similar to (but more extreme than) [[Sulfuric Acid]].

## Cyclicality and investment relevance

### The chlorine cycle

The chlorine market follows a distinct cycle driven by the interplay of three forces:

1. Construction/PVC demand (cyclical driver) -- housing starts → PVC → chlorine demand; the primary swing factor
2. Caustic soda demand (counter-cyclical to chlorine) -- alumina, paper, chemicals; when caustic is strong, chlorine floods
3. Energy costs (structural cost driver) -- electricity at 50% of ECU cost; European producers are at structural disadvantage post-2022

### Current market position (2025-2026)

The global chlor-alkali market is in a low-profitability cycle:
- Weak construction in China and parts of Europe has depressed PVC/chlorine demand
- Caustic soda demand has been relatively firmer (alumina, manufacturing)
- This creates chlorine surplus -- the "unwanted co-product" dynamic
- [[Olin]] has responded by shutting capacity (diaphragm cells serving [[Dow]]'s Freeport operations closed Dec 2024) and deliberately running below capacity
- Chinese overcapacity continues to weigh on global derivative (PVC, EDC) prices

### Bull case

- Construction recovery (rate cuts → housing starts → PVC demand → chlorine tightens)
- Water treatment demand growth in developing world (structural, non-cyclical)
- Semiconductor fab buildout increases electronic-grade HCl demand
- [[Olin]]'s capacity discipline eventually produces pricing power
- Energy transition insulation demand (MDI/TDI for polyurethane foam)

### Bear case

- Chinese PVC overcapacity suppresses global derivative prices indefinitely
- Caustic-led production keeps chlorine in permanent surplus
- Alternative disinfection technologies (UV, ozone) erode water treatment share at the margin
- Propylene oxide shifts from chlorohydrin to HPPO process, eliminating ~3-4% of chlorine demand
- European chlor-alkali continues to structurally shrink due to energy costs

### Signals to watch

- US housing starts and permits (leading indicator for PVC/chlorine demand)
- [[Olin]] quarterly operating rates and ECU pricing commentary
- Chinese PVC operating rates and export volumes
- European chlor-alkali plant closures/restarts (energy cost dependent)
- Caustic soda pricing (inverse signal for chlorine)
- [[Minamata Convention]] enforcement milestones (pressure on China's calcium carbide route)
