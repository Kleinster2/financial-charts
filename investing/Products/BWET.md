# BWET

BWET (Breakwave Tanker Shipping ETF) is an actively managed US commodity-pool ETF launched May 3, 2023 that holds near-dated tanker freight futures (FFAs) on the two main crude oil shipping routes published by the [[Baltic Exchange]]: TD3C (Middle East Gulf → [[China]], [[VLCC]], weighted ~90%) and TD20 (West Africa → UK/Continent, Suezmax, weighted ~10%). Sub-advised by [[Breakwave Advisors]] (founded by [[John Kartsonas]]) and sponsored by [[Amplify ETFs]] since December 28, 2023. BWET is the first and only ETF providing direct public-market exposure to crude tanker shipping rates, making it the purest retail proxy for the physical economics of moving oil by sea.

During the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]], BWET became the best-performing US-listed ETF of 2026 YTD, rising from $18.73 on January 2 to $175.29 on April 9 — an 836% gain — while global oil prices swung between $80 and $166/bbl ([[Dubai crude]] all-time high, March 19).

## Holdings and structure

| Allocation | Route | Class | Notes |
|------------|-------|-------|-------|
| ~90% | TD3C (Middle East Gulf → China) | [[VLCC]] | ~6,500 nm, ~20 days laden; dominant crude flow |
| ~10% | TD20 (West Africa → UK/Continent) | Suezmax | Nigeria loadings; backup exposure |

Contract roll: BWET holds the nearest two months of TD3C FFAs and rolls them forward each month. This creates contango risk in normal markets (when forward curve is upward-sloping), but in 2026 the structure worked in reverse — spot rates kept printing new highs, the curve steepened upward, and each roll captured additional gains.

Pool structure: BWET is a commodity pool (not a '40 Act fund), allowing it to hold futures directly. Expense ratio ~3.32%, reflecting both the commodity-pool wrapper and the cost of rolling FFAs.

Sponsor history: ETF Managers Capital LLC was the original sponsor from May 2023 to December 2023, then [[Amplify ETFs]] took over on December 28, 2023 and remains the sponsor today.

Listing: NYSE Arca (BWET). AUM was in the low tens of millions even during the 2026 blowout — a tiny fund that produced outsized returns.

## The 2026 trade

Phase 1 — Escalation (Feb 28 – Mar 3): US-Israeli strikes on [[Iran]] kill Ali Khamenei; [[IRGC]] closes the [[Strait of Hormuz]] to Western shipping. TD3C explodes from ~$150,000/day in mid-February to $423,736/day on March 3 — a 20%+ daily jump and the steepest one-day plunge-precursor move in more than half a decade. BWET rises in lockstep.

Phase 2 — Paradox (Mar 4 – Mar 26): the [[Strait of Hormuz]] is effectively closed to the vessels TD3C tracks. Actual loadings at [[Ras Tanura]] collapse; Gulf producers ([[Saudi Arabia]], [[Iraq]], [[Kuwait]]) cut production or reroute via [[East-West Pipeline]] to [[Yanbu]]. [[Baltic Exchange]] panellists continue issuing TD3C assessments based on hypothetical pricing — what the rate would be if an owner were willing to transit with war-risk insurance. These become the subject of the Lloyd's List "imaginary rates" debate (see [[Imaginary freight rate]]). BWET keeps climbing because the FFA curve follows the index.

Phase 3 — Ceasefire divergence (early April 2026): on Wednesday, oil crashes 14% on news of a temporary US-Iran ceasefire. BWET rises 7% to a new all-time high the same day. The divergence reflects four dynamics: Hormuz remains effectively closed and vessels are still stranded; Asian refiners have already rerouted to [[Atlantic basin]] sources and those longer voyages are booked for weeks; the FFA curve is pricing sticky risk premia, not spot oil; and [[Nadia Martin Wiggen]]'s observation (March 23) that post-resolution port congestion — "war in [[Ukraine]] and COVID at the same time" — will keep rates elevated even after a ceasefire.

Phase 4 — Mean reversion ahead: shipping rates historically mean-revert hard. [[BDRY]] (the sibling dry bulk ETF from the same manager) rose ~390% during the 2021 dry bulk boom (January to October 2021 peak) and gave back nearly all of it by late 2022. The precedent is explicit and the Breakwave funds exist inside that cyclical logic, not outside it.

## BWET vs BDRY — the prior shipping blowout

![[bwet-vs-bdry-normalized.png]]
*BWET (blue) and BDRY (orange), normalized from BWET's May 3, 2023 inception. BDRY was the previous best-performing shipping ETF during the 2021-22 dry bulk boom; it rose 390% from January 2021 to October 2021 and then gave nearly all of it back by late 2022. BWET's 2026 move is far larger in magnitude, but the same cyclical mean-reversion dynamics apply.*

The comparison is not academic: both ETFs are managed by [[Breakwave Advisors]], use the same FFA-based structure, and exist to capture short-duration rate spikes driven by disruptions to physical trade flows. Neither is a "buy and hold" vehicle. Kartsonas has said so explicitly in media appearances during the 2026 run — see the March 2026 Lloyd's List commentary and the Zacks ETF Spotlight interview.

## Risks

| Risk | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| Mean reversion | Freight rates cycle hard; disruptions end, vessels redeploy, rates fall. BDRY lost nearly all of its 2021 gains in 12 months. |
| Contango | In normal markets the FFA curve slopes upward; rolling costs erode NAV over time. |
| Index methodology | If [[Baltic Exchange]] suspends or changes TD3C during extreme disruption, NAV pricing becomes unreliable (see [[Imaginary freight rate]] for the current debate and the TD34 alternative trial route). |
| Fund flow asymmetry | Small AUM; inflows during blowouts can be large relative to fund size, forcing the sub-advisor to buy FFAs at expensive levels. |
| Counterparty risk | FFAs are OTC-cleared instruments; counterparty default risk exists during extreme stress. |
| Expense ratio | 3.32% is high; roll costs and management fees compound over holding periods. |
