**Boeing Starliner** — [[Boeing]]'s crew capsule for [[NASA]]'s [[Commercial Crew Program|Commercial Crew]]. CST-100 platform. The 2024 crewed flight test (CFT, June 2024) was reclassified by NASA administrator [[Jared Isaacman]] on February 19, 2026 as a [[Type A mishap]] — NASA's most severe designation, alongside [[Challenger]] (1986), [[Columbia]] (2003), and [[Apollo 1]] (1967). Reasoning: spacecraft lost maneuverability approaching ISS; crew (Wilmore and Williams) had to be brought home nine months later on [[SpaceX]] [[Crew Dragon]] rather than Starliner.

## CFT Type A mishap reclassification (Feb 19, 2026)

[[Jared Isaacman]] formally reclassified the 2024 Starliner CFT as a Type A mishap on February 19, 2026. The reclassification cited:
- Loss of maneuverability as the spacecraft approached ISS
- Financial damages incurred
- Near-loss of crew (Isaacman: "We almost did have a really terrible day")

Isaacman publicly criticized the prior administration: "Concern for the Starliner program's reputation influenced that decision" not to immediately classify Type A. The reclassification places Starliner alongside the most severe NASA mishaps in agency history.

| Type A mishap precedents | Year |
|---|---|
| [[Apollo 1]] fire (3 crew lost) | 1967 |
| [[Challenger]] disaster (7 crew lost) | 1986 |
| [[Columbia]] disaster (7 crew lost) | 2003 |
| **Boeing Starliner CFT** | 2024 (reclassified Feb 2026) |

The reclassification is regulatory signaling. Boeing's certification path for crew rotation is now significantly steeper, the contractor's standing as an anchor in NASA's Commercial Crew framework is weakened, and the broader Q1 2026 contractor-renegotiation pattern (see [[NASA]]) puts Boeing on the losing side of the architecture redraw.

## Why Starliner matters (investment lens)

The reclassification is the cleanest possible regulatory signal that NASA's contracting posture has shifted under Isaacman. Boeing's defense/space segment was already running cumulative fixed-price losses on Starliner, KC-46, and SLS. Type A reclassification raises the cost of certification (more reviews, more documentation, more rework) at the exact moment the [[SpaceX]] [[Crew Dragon]] alternative is operational, profitable, and rotational. The competitive case for Starliner as an ongoing program is weakening.

For [[Boeing]] equity (NYSE: BA), the Q1 2026 read is mixed: 737 MAX commercial-aviation recovery is the dominant story (see Boeing actor note), but the Starliner reclassification + SLS core-role stripping under the new lunar architecture removes two structural growth lanes from the defense/space segment.
