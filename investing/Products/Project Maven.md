**Project Maven** — the [[Pentagon]]'s AI-powered targeting and intelligence platform, now the most significant real-world deployment of artificial intelligence in warfare. Built by [[Palantir]] as prime contractor, running on [[AWS]], with [[Claude]] integrated for intelligence processing via a $200M [[Anthropic]] agreement.

---

## Synopsis

Project Maven is the Maven Smart System, a digital mission control platform that aggregates 150+ data sources — satellite imagery, drone feeds, intercepted communications, signals intelligence — to identify, prioritize, and present strike targets to military commanders. Launched by the [[Pentagon]] in 2017, it was originally partnered with [[Google]], which withdrew in 2018 after employee protests over military AI ethics. [[Palantir]] took over as prime contractor, growing the program from a $480M contract to ~$1.3B by May 2025. In July 2025, [[Anthropic]] was awarded a two-year $200M prototype agreement to embed [[Claude]] for intelligence processing and summarization, marking a significant shift for a company that had built its brand on AI safety.

The system's defining moment came during [[Operation Epic Fury]], the US military campaign against [[Iran]] beginning March 2026. Maven presented 1,000+ strike options on day one, scanning thousands of satellite images, drone feeds, and intercepted communications. The US struck 1,000+ discrete targets in the first 24 hours — twice the firepower of the 2003 Iraq "shock and awe" campaign in a fraction of the time, reaching 5,500 strikes in 11 days. Cameron Stanley, the DoD's Chief Digital and AI Officer, called the system "revolutionary." Captain Timothy Hawkins, CENTCOM spokesperson, confirmed AI played a "critical role" in initial screening.

The paradigm cracked on the Shajareh Tayyebeh school strike, which killed 170+ people, mostly children. A US military investigation found a US munition was likely responsible, with outdated intelligence a probable cause. Democratic lawmakers demanded the [[Pentagon]] explain the strike and the role of AI in the targeting chain (NBC News, Mar 12 2026). The incident crystallized the core risk of AI-enabled warfare: automation bias, where the speed and volume of AI-generated targeting reduces human review to a formality. [[Israel]]'s Lavender AI targeting system, used in Gaza, was documented as wrong at least 10% of the time. A Nature article noted AI targeting remained "significantly untested in major conflict" even as it was being used at industrial scale. House Defense Committee leaders from both parties told NOTUS they did not know how AI was being used in the Iran strikes.

## Contract history

- 2017: [[Pentagon]] launches Project Maven. [[Google]] is original partner for AI image analysis.
- 2018: [[Google]] withdraws after employee protests over military AI ethics — thousands of employees signed internal petitions, some resigned. The episode defined the tech industry's "AI ethics" moment and created the opening for defense-native companies.
- Post-2018: [[Palantir]] takes over as prime contractor. Initial contract valued at $480M.
- May 2024: Maven expanded from hundreds to thousands of users worldwide, signaling operational maturity.
- May 2025: Contract value increased to ~$1.3B ($795M added to Palantir contract).
- July 2025: [[Anthropic]] awarded two-year $200M prototype agreement for "frontier AI capabilities advancing US national security." [[Claude]] embedded in Maven for intelligence processing and summarization.
- February 24, 2026: [[Anduril]] Lattice and [[Shield AI]] Hivemind autonomy software tested alongside Maven systems.
- March 2026: First large-scale combat deployment during [[Operation Epic Fury]].

## Controversy

### Shajareh Tayyebeh school strike

A strike on the Shajareh Tayyebeh school in [[Iran]] killed 170+ people, mostly children. The US military opened an investigation; preliminary information suggested a US munition was responsible and that outdated intelligence may have caused the error. Democratic lawmakers asked the [[Pentagon]] to explain the strike and the role of AI in the targeting chain (NBC News, Mar 12 2026).

### Automation bias

The speed and volume of AI-generated targeting options compress the time available for human review. Critics argue that human oversight in the "kill chain" risks becoming a mere formality when AI presents hundreds of targets per hour. [[Israel]]'s Lavender AI targeting system, used in Gaza, was documented as wrong at least 10% of the time — a rate that, applied to Maven's scale of thousands of strikes, would imply hundreds of erroneous targets.

A Nature article described AI targeting as *"significantly untested in major conflict"* even as it was being deployed at industrial scale. House Defense Committee leaders from both parties told NOTUS they did not know how AI was being used in the Iran strikes — a remarkable admission given congressional oversight responsibilities.
