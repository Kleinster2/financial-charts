## Rieder commentary (Apr 7, 2026)

On Bloomberg Television, Rieder described the fund's approach in the current environment:

- Running "more conservative" — building cash to weather geopolitical uncertainty (Iran deadline, oil volatility)
- Keeping duration concentrated in the front-to-belly of the curve for carry stability
- Reduced European [[High yield bonds|HY]] and IG credit — "not that interesting in terms of where the levels are"
- Using options overlays ("overwriting positions") to generate additional income against existing holdings
- Moved some capital into agency [[MBS market|mortgages]]
- Credit markets have been "amazingly impressive" despite geopolitical disruption — real buyers (insurance companies, pension funds) stepping in as yields back up

On the equity-vs-debt question for tech/AI: "For my life, my whole career, I actually think investing in technology is better in equities. The upside is greater. Recovery on debt and equity in technology become pretty equivalent numbers, but the upside potential is significantly greater."

## Investment context

BINC's growth to $16.81B in under three years makes it one of the fastest-growing active fixed income ETFs ever launched. The fund benefits from the secular shift of actively managed bond portfolios from mutual fund wrappers into ETF structures — where tax efficiency, intraday liquidity, and lower minimums attract both institutional and retail flows.

The active management element is critical: Rieder's ability to shift allocation (e.g., rotating from European IG into securitized products, or adding agency MBS as yields back up) gives BINC flexibility that passive bond index ETFs lack. In a period when [[Credit spreads|credit spreads]] are "just okay" on a valuation basis but fundamentals remain solid, that flexibility is the value proposition.
