**Goreh-Jask Pipeline** — Iran's own Hormuz bypass: a 1,000 km crude oil pipeline from Goreh (Bushehr province, southwestern Iran) to Jask on the Gulf of Oman. Design capacity: 1 mb/d. Cost: ~$2B. Iran is the only Gulf producer whose entire export portfolio would be stranded in a Hormuz closure — this pipeline was built to solve that problem. However, it remains effectively non-operational as of March 2026.

## History

Iran built the Goreh-Jask pipeline specifically to diversify its crude export routes away from Kharg Island and other Persian Gulf terminals, all of which require transit through the Strait of Hormuz. Former President Rouhani stated in 2020 that Iran "would be the only one whose oil exports would be completely cut if the Strait of Hormuz were closed" and that the pipeline would solve this.

The pipeline was inaugurated in 2021, but the Jask terminal and pipeline have struggled to become fully operational. A test load was exported in late 2024, but no further oil has been exported since (per IEA). The EIA estimates effective capacity at ~300k b/d, well below the 1 mb/d design target. Infrastructure at Jask — storage, loading berths, tanker handling — appears insufficient for full-scale operations.

## March 2026 relevance

The irony of the March 2026 conflict: the pipeline Iran built to bypass Hormuz is non-operational at the exact moment Iran needs it most. With US strikes targeting Iranian oil infrastructure (refineries, storage, loading terminals), the Goreh-Jask pipeline — even if it were functional — would likely be a military target. Karen Young (MEI) noted during the March 8 briefing that "Iranian tankers are ironically the ones still getting through" the strait, suggesting Iran has found informal workarounds rather than relying on Jask.
