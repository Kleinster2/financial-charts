# Caustic Soda

**Caustic soda** (NaOH, sodium hydroxide) is the world's most widely used industrial alkali, a commodity whose supply is structurally tethered to chlorine production through the chlor-alkali process. You cannot make one without the other. This co-product lock creates pricing dynamics unlike any other commodity: caustic soda's market is permanently hostage to chlorine demand, and vice versa.

---

## Synopsis

The global caustic soda market produced ~83 million tonnes in 2024, valued at ~$46 billion. It is the essential strong base across heavy industry -- alumina refining via the Bayer process (~20-25% of demand), pulp and paper (~12-15%), organic and inorganic chemical manufacturing (~20%), water treatment, textiles, petroleum refining, and food processing all depend on it. Unlike [[Sulfuric Acid]], whose supply is involuntarily tied to copper smelting and oil refining byproducts, caustic soda's supply constraint is the stoichiometric co-production of chlorine. Every tonne of NaOH comes with ~0.89 tonnes of Cl2 from the same electrolysis cell. Production decisions are therefore driven by whichever co-product is in shorter supply, creating a permanent balancing act that defines the commodity's economics.

The investment case revolves around the chlor-alkali balance. When chlorine demand is strong (PVC construction boom, vinyls cycle upswing), producers run plants hard and caustic floods the market, depressing prices. When chlorine demand collapses (housing downturn, PVC oversupply), producers cut operating rates to avoid chlorine disposal costs, tightening caustic supply and sending prices higher. This 180-degree phase shift between chlorine and caustic pricing is the central dynamic. As of late 2025, global PVC markets are firmly oversupplied, which means chlor-alkali operating rates are being disciplined -- supporting caustic prices. [[Olin]], the world's largest chlor-alkali producer, has permanently shut >1 Mt/year of US capacity since 2021 as part of a deliberate strategy to sell less volume at higher margins.

The structural question is whether new demand vectors -- lithium hydroxide production for batteries, sodium-ion battery cathodes, hydrogen economy byproduct monetization, tightening water treatment regulations -- can shift the long-term caustic-chlorine balance toward chronic caustic shortage. If they can, the commodity transitions from a cyclical byproduct play to a structural demand growth story. The energy intensity of chlor-alkali electrolysis (~2,200-2,400 kWh per tonne of NaOH via membrane cells) also makes it a proxy for electricity costs, particularly in Europe where energy prices have compressed margins and forced capacity closures.

## Production and supply structure

### The chlor-alkali process

Caustic soda is produced almost exclusively via electrolysis of brine (saturated NaCl solution). The reaction is:

2NaCl + 2H2O -> Cl2 + 2NaOH + H2

This produces three products in fixed stoichiometric ratios per electrochemical unit (ECU): 1.0 tonne of chlorine, 1.1 tonnes of caustic soda (dry basis), and 0.028 tonnes of hydrogen. The 2.1 tonnes of combined chlorine + caustic is one ECU. You cannot adjust the ratio -- it is dictated by chemistry.

### Cell technology

| Technology | Global share (2024) | Energy (kWh/t NaOH) | NaOH purity | Status |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------|--------|
| Membrane cell | ~60-63% | 2,200-2,400 | 33-50% (high purity) | Dominant; all new capacity |
| Diaphragm cell | ~35% | 2,500-2,700 | 10-12% (requires concentration) | Legacy; declining |
| Mercury cell | <2% | 2,700-2,900 | 50% (high purity) | Phase-out mandated by 2025 (Minamata Convention) |

Membrane cell technology dominates new builds due to lower energy consumption (~17% less than mercury), higher product purity, and no mercury or asbestos contamination. The Minamata Convention on Mercury requires complete phase-out of mercury-cell chlor-alkali by 2025. Diaphragm cells still account for a significant share of North American capacity, including legacy [[Olin]] plants, but produce lower-purity caustic that requires further concentration.

## Demand structure

### End-use breakdown (2024)

| Application | Share | Notes |
|-------------|-------|-------|
| Alumina refining (Bayer process) | ~20-25% | ~100 kg NaOH per tonne alumina; ~40-60 kg/t make-up after recycling |
| Chemical manufacturing | ~20% | Organic and inorganic chemicals, plastics intermediates, solvents |
| Pulp & paper | ~12-15% | Kraft process (chemical pulping), bleaching |
| Textiles | ~14-18% | Mercerization, dyeing, finishing |
| Water treatment | ~8-10% | pH adjustment, neutralization; 18% of NA demand |
| Soaps & detergents | ~5-7% | Saponification of fats/oils |
| Petroleum refining | ~3-5% | Caustic washing (sweetening), mercaptan removal |
| Food processing | ~2-3% | Peeling, cocoa processing, olive curing |

### Alumina refining (dominant single use)

The Bayer process dissolves aluminum oxide from bauxite ore using hot concentrated NaOH solution. Approximately 100 kg of caustic soda is consumed per tonne of alumina produced. Most caustic is recycled in a closed loop, with make-up requirements of 40-60 kg/t to replace losses from reaction with bauxite impurities (notably 0.8 tonnes NaOH lost per tonne of kaolinite). Global alumina production was ~140 Mt in 2024, implying ~6-8 Mt of make-up caustic demand. Major consumers: [[Alcoa]], [[Rio Tinto]], [[South32]], [[Norsk Hydro]], Hindalco ([[Tata Group]]). Brazil and Russia each consume >50-60% of domestic caustic soda in alumina refining.

### Pulp & paper

The kraft process uses NaOH and Na2S (white liquor) to dissolve lignin from wood chips. Global pulp production of ~190 Mt/year makes this a major demand sector. Recovery boilers recycle ~95% of cooking chemicals, but make-up caustic is still required.

### Water treatment (growth sector)

Tightening municipal water quality standards globally are driving incremental demand. North America already allocates ~18% of caustic consumption to water treatment. Federal infrastructure upgrades and PFAS remediation requirements are structural demand tailwinds.

### What drives price swings

1. Chlor-alkali operating rates -- the single most important driver. When producers cut rates to balance chlorine, caustic tightens.
2. PVC/vinyls cycle -- chlorine demand is dominated by PVC/VCM. Housing and construction booms pull chlorine, flooding caustic; busts do the opposite.
3. Energy costs -- electrolysis is energy-intensive (2,200-2,400 kWh/t). European energy crisis 2022-2023 drove plant closures and pushed EU prices far above Asia.
4. Alumina refining cycles -- aluminum demand/pricing affects the largest single caustic end-use.
5. Seasonal demand -- water treatment peaks in summer; pulp demand is relatively stable.
6. Trade flow disruptions -- caustic is less punishing to ship than sulfuric acid (not corrosive to steel in moderate concentrations), but still heavy and regionally segmented.

### The ECU pricing concept

The electrochemical unit (ECU) captures the combined value of the co-products from one electrolysis run: 1.0 tonne Cl2 + 1.1 tonnes NaOH = 2.1 tonnes. Chlor-alkali producers optimize for ECU margin, not for either product individually. When chlorine prices are strong, producers can accept lower caustic prices (and vice versa). The ECU value oscillates between "chlorine-led" and "caustic-led" regimes:

- Chlorine-led ECU (PVC boom): Chlorine commands premium; caustic is surplus. Producers run hard.
- Caustic-led ECU (PVC bust / alumina demand surge): Caustic commands premium; chlorine is surplus (can go negative -- producers pay for disposal). Producers discipline rates.

ECU cost structure (per tonne caustic soda, approximate): (2,200-2,400) x electricity price + 1.51 x raw salt price + $400-550 fixed costs. Electricity typically represents 50-60% of production cost.

## The chlor-alkali balance

This is the core investment dynamic. Understanding it is prerequisite to any position in caustic soda, chlorine, PVC, or chlor-alkali equities.

### The co-product problem

Chemistry produces NaOH and Cl2 in a fixed 1.1:1.0 ratio by weight. But market demand fluctuates independently for each product. When the demand ratio exceeds 1.1:1.0 (more caustic needed relative to chlorine), caustic tightens and prices rise. When it falls below 1.1:1.0 (more chlorine needed), caustic is oversupplied and prices fall. The two products' price cycles are permanently 180 degrees out of phase.

### What drives the swing

Chlorine demand is dominated by PVC (polyvinyl chloride), which consumes ~35-40% of global chlorine. PVC demand is highly cyclical -- tied to construction, housing, and infrastructure spending. Other chlorine derivatives (TiO2, isocyanates for polyurethane, water disinfection, solvents) add incremental demand but PVC is the swing factor.

Caustic demand is more diversified and less cyclical. Alumina refining, pulp, chemicals, and water treatment are relatively stable industrial end-uses. This asymmetry means caustic demand is stickier than chlorine demand.

### The operational response

Chlor-alkali producers typically balance to chlorine demand, not caustic demand. When chlorine demand drops (housing bust, PVC oversupply), producers cut operating rates rather than produce unwanted chlorine:

- Chlorine storage is dangerous (toxic gas, requires pressurized/refrigerated tanks)
- Chlorine disposal costs money (neutralization, conversion to HCl)
- Chlorine prices can and do go negative -- producers have paid customers to take chlorine off their hands (US merchant chlorine market briefly saw negative economics in 2024)

When producers cut rates, caustic supply falls with it, even if caustic demand hasn't changed. This is the mechanism by which a PVC bust creates a caustic soda shortage.

### The current regime (2025-2026)

As of late 2025, the global PVC market is firmly oversupplied, pressured by Chinese overcapacity and weak global construction. This has kept chlorine demand subdued. [[Olin]] has deliberately cut >1 Mt of permanent US capacity since 2021, running operations "not that far above 50%" of nameplate (CEO, July 2023). This supply discipline has supported caustic prices but compressed ECU margins for producers who prioritize chlorine revenue.

US ECU margins remain healthier than European or Asian margins, partly because scarce merchant chlorine supply has elevated US chlorine prices to record levels even as the broader chlorine market is weak. Europe faces the worst economics: high energy costs push ECU cash costs far above other regions, leaving European caustic prices the highest globally but margins the thinnest.

### Historical regime shifts

| Period | Regime | Mechanism |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| 2017-2019 | Caustic-led | Strong alumina demand (China); PVC balanced |
| 2020 | Collapse | COVID cut both; chlorine hit harder (construction) |
| 2021 | Caustic spike | Construction rebound pulled chlorine; caustic initially tight from rate cuts |
| 2022-2023 | Transition | Energy crisis crushed European production; PVC oversupply grew |
| 2024-2025 | Caustic-supported | PVC oversupply; producers disciplining rates; Olin capacity cuts |

### Excess capacity and pricing pressure

Chinese capacity additions outpaced demand growth through 2024-2025, pushing domestic prices down ~5% in H1 2025. Output from Chinese producers rose ~4% in Q1 2025, supporting intra-Asian export volumes. The low Chinese domestic price ($121/Mt for 32% lye in Q3 2025) reflects this oversupply and creates a floor for global pricing -- Chinese exports are the marginal tonne in most Asian and emerging markets.

### Downstream integration

Unlike Western producers who sell caustic and chlorine into merchant markets, many Chinese chlor-alkali plants are integrated into downstream PVC production. This means production decisions are driven by PVC economics, not caustic or chlorine markets. When PVC margins are positive, Chinese plants run regardless of caustic market conditions, exacerbating caustic oversupply.

## Energy transition and emerging demand

### Lithium hydroxide production

Caustic soda is used to convert lithium sulfate to lithium hydroxide (LiOH), a critical battery cathode precursor. As spodumene-route lithium processing scales globally, NaOH demand grows with it. The conversion: Li2SO4 + 2NaOH -> 2LiOH + Na2SO4. This is a growing demand vector but still small relative to alumina and chemicals.

### Sodium-ion batteries

[[Hanwha Solutions]] and others are exploring sodium batteries that use NaOH as a precursor rather than lithium hydroxide. If sodium-ion batteries achieve commercial scale (targeting grid storage and low-cost EVs), this would represent entirely new caustic demand at potentially large volumes. Still early-stage.

### Hydrogen byproduct monetization

The chlor-alkali process produces ~0.028 tonnes of hydrogen per ECU -- high-purity H2 (99.9%+) that is already available at the plant gate. Globally, chlor-alkali plants produce ~270 kt of hydrogen annually. Of this, 10-15% is currently vented or flared. When powered by renewable electricity, this hydrogen has a carbon footprint comparable to green hydrogen from water electrolysis, but at lower marginal cost (the hydrogen is a freebie -- the plant exists for NaOH and Cl2). INEOS Inovyn became Europe's first ISCC PLUS-certified green hydrogen producer from chlor-alkali.

### Water treatment tightening

PFAS remediation, microplastics removal, and tightening EPA/EU water quality standards are structural demand tailwinds for caustic soda in municipal and industrial water treatment. This sector already consumes ~18% of North American caustic and is growing.

## Environmental and regulatory

### Minamata Convention mercury cell phase-out

The Minamata Convention on Mercury mandates complete phase-out of mercury-cell chlor-alkali production by 2025. Mercury cells historically consumed ~2,700-2,900 kWh/t NaOH and emitted 12-18 tonnes of mercury annually from the global chlor-alkali sector. The transition to membrane cells reduces energy consumption by ~17% and eliminates mercury contamination. As of 2024, mercury cells accounted for <2% of global capacity, with Europe (Eurochlor members) completing most conversions.

### Energy intensity

Chlor-alkali electrolysis is among the most electricity-intensive chemical processes:
- Membrane cell: 2,200-2,400 kWh per tonne NaOH
- Diaphragm cell: 2,500-2,700 kWh/t
- Mercury cell: 2,700-2,900 kWh/t

Electricity typically represents 50-60% of production cost. This makes caustic soda production a direct proxy for regional electricity prices. European producers face structural disadvantage: EU industrial electricity at EUR 80-120/MWh vs. US at $40-60/MWh vs. China at $50-70/MWh creates ~$60-100/t cost gap in caustic production.

### Carbon footprint

The European chlor-alkali industry emitted ~2.0 Mt CO2 in 2022 (scope 1+2), down from ~3.5 Mt in 2010. Low-carbon caustic (certified ISCC PLUS or equivalent) commands a green premium in European markets. [[Dow]]'s Stade and Schkopau plants in Germany produce reduced-carbon membrane-grade NaOH powered by renewable electricity.

### Chlorine safety as operating constraint

Chlorine gas is extremely toxic (LC50 ~293 ppm for 1-hour exposure). Chlorine storage and transport are heavily regulated, which constrains how much surplus chlorine producers can accumulate. This safety constraint is part of why producers cut rates rather than stockpile chlorine when demand drops -- you cannot warehouse the problem.

## Cyclicality and investment signals

### Caustic soda as macro indicator

Caustic soda demand correlates with industrial breadth: alumina (metals cycle), pulp (consumer staples), chemicals (GDP), water treatment (infrastructure spending), textiles (emerging market consumption). A broad-based chemical bellwether. Unlike sulfuric acid, which is dominated by a single end-use (fertilizers at ~55%), caustic's diversified demand makes it a better proxy for overall industrial activity.

### The cycle

The chlor-alkali cycle is driven by the interaction of:

1. PVC/construction demand (chlorine pull) -- the swing factor
2. Alumina/pulp/chemicals demand (caustic pull) -- the stable base
3. Energy costs (production cost driver) -- regional differentiation
4. Capacity additions/retirements -- structural supply shifts

Bull case: PVC oversupply persists, forcing continued rate cuts; alumina demand grows with energy transition aluminum needs (EVs, solar frames, grid infrastructure); water treatment regulations tighten; lithium/sodium battery demand adds structural new caustic pull; European capacity permanently closes on energy costs.

Bear case: PVC demand recovers strongly (housing recovery, infrastructure boom), producers run hard, caustic floods market; Chinese capacity additions overwhelm; alternative alkalis (potassium hydroxide, sodium carbonate) substitute in marginal applications; energy costs decline, restoring European/Asian capacity.

### Structural signals to watch

- PVC operating rates globally (higher = more chlorine pull = lower caustic prices)
- [[Olin]] operating rate and capacity announcements (the swing producer)
- European chlor-alkali plant closures/restarts (energy cost driven)
- Chinese chlor-alkali capacity utilization and export volumes
- Alumina production growth (aluminum demand for EVs, solar, grid)
- Shintech/[[Shin-Etsu]] expansion timeline (major new NA capacity)
- Lithium hydroxide conversion capacity buildout
- US/EU water treatment regulatory actions (PFAS, microplastics)

### The long-term structural question

If PVC demand structurally peaks (substitution by other plastics, construction material shifts), the chlorine pull weakens permanently. But caustic demand from alumina, water treatment, lithium, and chemicals continues to grow. In that scenario, the chlor-alkali balance shifts toward chronic caustic shortage -- producers would need to run plants specifically for caustic, disposing of or finding new uses for surplus chlorine. That regime change would structurally reprice caustic soda upward and compress margins for chlorine-dependent industries. The timing is uncertain -- PVC demand is still growing globally at ~2-3% annually, driven by emerging markets -- but the directionality of the forces is worth monitoring.
