#product #energy #commodity #aviation

**Jet fuel** — the middle distillate that keeps the $1 trillion airline industry airborne. ~8 million barrels/day global consumption, ~$250B annual fuel bill. The single largest operating cost for airlines (25-30% of total), and the one they control least.

---

## Synopsis

Jet fuel (Jet-A1 internationally, Jet-A in the US) is a kerosene-type distillate refined from crude oil at roughly the same cut point as diesel and heating oil. That shared distillate pool means jet fuel pricing is tied to the broader middle distillate balance — not just aviation demand, but trucking, heating, and industrial use. Global consumption runs ~8 mb/d (US alone ~1.65 mb/d in 2024), and IATA's 2026 assumption is $88/bbl jet fuel vs $62 Brent — a ~$26 crack spread, elevated by tightened middle distillate balances (partly from reduced Russian refinery throughput). The structural story is [[Sustainable Aviation Fuel]] (SAF): chemically identical to Jet-A1 but made from waste oils, agricultural residues, or captured carbon. SAF is 2-4x more expensive than conventional jet fuel, and the EU's ReFuelEU mandate requires 2% SAF blending at European airports starting 2025, rising to 6% by 2030 and 70% by 2050. SAF creates a two-tier market — airlines with hedging and SAF supply contracts gain cost advantages over those scrambling on the spot market.

## Product specifications

| Grade | Freeze point | Flash point | Use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Jet-A | -40°C | 38°C min | US domestic |
| Jet-A1 | -47°C | 38°C min | International standard |
| JP-8 | -47°C | 38°C min | US military |
| TS-1 | -50°C | 28°C min | Russia/CIS |

All are kerosene-type fuels, distilled from crude at ~150-275°C. The key distinction from diesel: jet fuel has stricter freeze point and thermal stability requirements (planes fly at -50°C at altitude).

## Supply chain

### Production
Jet fuel is a co-product of refining, not a standalone production decision. A refinery optimizing for gasoline or diesel produces jet fuel as part of the distillation slate. Refiners can shift the cut point to produce more or less kerosene relative to diesel, but the flexibility is limited (~5-10% swing).

### Major refiners (US)

| Refiner | US capacity | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| [[Marathon Petroleum]] | ~3.1 mb/d total | Largest US refiner |
| [[Valero]] | ~3.2 mb/d total | Largest independent refiner, SAF via Diamond Green Diesel |

### Pricing
- Platts (S&P Global) publishes daily jet fuel assessments
- IATA Jet Fuel Price Monitor tracks global average
- Pricing = crude oil + crack spread + logistics + taxes
- Crack spread widens during supply disruptions, refinery outages, or seasonal demand peaks

Air travel demand has recovered to ~105% of pre-COVID levels. The demand trajectory is structural — global middle class growth, especially in [[India]] and [[Southeast Asia]], drives long-term aviation expansion.

## Crack spread dynamics

The jet fuel crack spread (jet fuel price minus crude oil price) is the key variable for refiner profitability and airline cost management.

| Period | Crack spread | Context |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| 2019 (pre-COVID) | ~$15/bbl | Normal |
| 2020 (COVID) | ~$5/bbl | Demand collapse |
| 2022 (post-COVID/Ukraine) | ~$40-50/bbl | Supply shock |
| 2023 | ~$25-30/bbl | Normalization |
| 2024-25 | ~$20-25/bbl | Slightly elevated |
| 2026 (IATA est.) | ~$26/bbl | Russian refinery disruptions |

Elevated cracks since 2022 reflect structural tightening: refinery closures (900k bpd globally 2020-2023), Russian supply disruptions, and slow capacity additions. [[Phillips 66]]'s Rodeo conversion from crude refining to renewables removed ~100k bpd of traditional refining capacity.

## Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF)

The biggest structural shift in jet fuel markets. SAF is chemically identical to conventional jet fuel but produced from non-petroleum feedstocks.

### Feedstocks and pathways

| Pathway | Feedstock | Maturity |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| HEFA | Used cooking oil, animal fats | Commercial |
| Fischer-Tropsch | Biomass, municipal waste | Demonstration |
| Alcohol-to-jet (AtJ) | Ethanol, isobutanol | Early commercial |
| Power-to-liquid (PtL) | Green hydrogen + captured CO₂ | Pre-commercial |
| Pyrolysis | Forestry residues | Pilot |

### Mandate landscape

| Jurisdiction | 2025 | 2030 | 2050 |
|-------------|------|------|------|
| EU (ReFuelEU) | 2% | 6% | 70% |
| UK | 2% | 10% | — |
| Singapore | 1% | 3-5% | — |
| US | Voluntary (IRA tax credits) | — | — |
| India | 1% (domestic) | 5% | — |

### Economics

SAF currently costs 2-4x conventional jet fuel. The green premium is the central challenge:
- HEFA (cheapest pathway): ~$1,200-1,800/tonne vs ~$600-700/tonne for conventional
- PtL (most expensive): ~$3,000-5,000/tonne
- EU €500/tonne incentive partially bridges the gap
- US IRA provides $1.25-1.75/gallon tax credit (SAF blender's credit)

### Key SAF producers

| Producer | Capacity | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| [[Neste]] | ~1.5 Mt/yr renewable fuel | Largest global SAF producer |
| [[Valero]]/Darling (Diamond Green Diesel) | Expanding to SAF | Port Arthur SAF project |
| [[TotalEnergies]] | Grandpuits conversion | European SAF |
| World Energy | Paramount, CA | First US dedicated SAF |

Global SAF production: ~1 Mt in 2024, vs ~300 Mt total jet fuel consumption — less than 0.5% penetration. The gap between mandates and production capacity is the defining tension.

## Investment implications

### For airlines
Fuel is the largest uncontrollable cost. Airlines manage exposure through:
- Hedging: Southwest historically most hedged; Delta shifted to refinery ownership (bought Trainer refinery 2012, later divested operations)
- Fuel surcharges: passed to passengers, partially offsetting
- Fleet renewal: A321neo, 787 Dreamliner reduce per-seat fuel burn
- SAF offtake agreements: locking in future supply at known prices

### For refiners
Elevated crack spreads favor refiners with high distillate yield. SAF mandates create a new product stream with premium pricing — but require capital investment and feedstock sourcing. [[Valero]]'s Diamond Green Diesel JV is the template.

### For energy traders
Jet fuel is less liquid than gasoline or diesel futures, creating basis risk opportunities. Seasonal patterns (summer travel, winter heating competition for distillates) drive predictable spread movements.

---

## Insights

- Jet fuel is not an isolated market — it competes for distillate barrel allocation with diesel and heating oil, so winter heating demand can spike jet fuel prices even when air travel is flat
- SAF mandates are creating a regulatory-driven premium that will structurally increase airline fuel costs in Europe before the US, giving US carriers a temporary cost advantage on transatlantic routes
- The refinery closure wave (2020-2023) permanently tightened the supply side — crack spreads are unlikely to return to pre-2020 norms
- Russia's reduced refinery throughput (drone attacks, sanctions) is an underappreciated source of middle distillate tightness globally
