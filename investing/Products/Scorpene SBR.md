**Scorpène SBR** — Brazilian Navy variant of [[Naval Group]]'s Scorpène-class diesel-electric submarine, built at the Itaguaí naval complex by [[Itaguaí Construções Navais]] (ICN) under the [[PROSUB]] program. Four submarines are being built: Riachuelo (S40, commissioned 2022), Humaitá (S41, commissioned 2024), Tonelero (S42, commissioned 2025 and entirely Brazilian-built), and Angostura (S43, under construction). The SBR variant is dimensionally larger than the baseline Scorpène to accommodate Brazilian Navy operational requirements and is the first Brazilian-built submarine class.

The November 2025 Tonelero commissioning is the structural milestone of the entire [[PROSUB]] program: the boat was built end-to-end by Brazilian industry at Itaguaí without French construction labor on the hull, validating that the tech-transfer phase has converted Brazil into a sovereign submarine builder.

## Design variation from baseline Scorpène

The SBR (S-BR) variant differs from the baseline Scorpène (operated by Chile, Malaysia, India, others) in several ways:

| Element | Baseline Scorpène | Scorpène SBR |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Length | ~66 m | ~75 m |
| Displacement | ~1,750 t | ~1,900 t |
| Crew quarters | Standard | Enlarged for Brazilian Navy patrol patterns |
| Combat system | SUBTICS standard | SUBTICS with Brazilian modifications |
| AIP option | Available (MESMA) | Not in SBR — pure diesel-electric |

The dimensional growth reflects Brazilian Navy operational preferences (longer patrols in the South Atlantic, larger weapons load, increased crew space). The decision to omit AIP from the SBR was driven by cost, schedule, and the fact that the follow-on Álvaro Alberto nuclear submarine will provide long-endurance capability without diesel-electric AIP compromises.

## Strategic significance

The Scorpène SBR is the platform that converted the [[PROSUB]] tech-transfer phase from theoretical to operational. Riachuelo and Humaitá involved meaningful French construction support; Tonelero validated that Brazil could build the boats without that support; Angostura will refine the indigenous build process. The SBR boats are also tactically valuable in their own right — modern, quiet, well-armed diesel-electric submarines suited to the South Atlantic littoral and the "Blue Amazon" continental-shelf protection mission.

The class is also a benchmark for South American submarine modernization: Brazil's SBR fleet is larger and more modern than any other Latin American submarine fleet (Argentina has a defunct fleet; Chile operates older Type 209s and Scorpènes; Venezuela has aging Type 209s). The SBR fleet structurally repositions Brazil as the dominant submarine power in the South Atlantic.
