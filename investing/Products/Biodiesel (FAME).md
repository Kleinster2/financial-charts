#product #energy #biofuel #commodity

**Biodiesel (FAME)** — the first-generation biofuel produced by transesterifying vegetable oils or animal fats with methanol, yielding fatty acid methyl esters (FAME) and glycerin as a byproduct. Global production ~48-50 billion liters/year (2023), dominated by [[Indonesia]] (~11.9 Mtoe, 26% of global output), [[Brazil]] (~6.6 Mtoe), and the EU (~9.8M tonnes). Distinct from [[Diesel fuel|renewable diesel (HVO)]] — FAME is an ester, not a hydrocarbon, and faces blending limits (B7 in Europe, B20 in US/Brazil) due to inferior cold-flow properties. Despite being structurally displaced by HVO in developed markets, FAME remains the dominant biodiesel type globally (~73% of total biodiesel production vs 27% HVO in 2023) and is growing in absolute terms, driven by mandates in [[Indonesia]], [[Brazil]], and the EU.

## Synopsis

Biodiesel is the workhorse biofuel of the developing world. While renewable diesel (HVO) captures investment dollars and headlines in the US and Europe, FAME remains the dominant biomass-based diesel globally: ~48-50 billion liters produced in 2023 vs ~12-13 billion liters for HVO. The economics are simple — transesterification is low-capex, low-tech, and scalable anywhere oilseeds grow. A plant producing 100 million gallons/year costs a fraction of an HVO hydroprocessing unit. The tradeoff is product quality: FAME has poor cold-flow properties (it gels in winter), limited shelf life, can damage engine seals at high concentrations, and is blending-limited by fuel standards (EN 590 caps it at B7 in Europe). HVO is a drop-in hydrocarbon with none of these constraints.

The structural story is a tale of two markets. In the US and Europe, FAME is being squeezed: US biodiesel consumption fell to 960 million gallons in the first 10 months of 2025 (down dramatically from prior years) as the blenders tax credit expired and the 45Z production credit shifted economics toward domestic HVO producers. Eight US biodiesel plants closed in 2024, losing ~100M gal/yr capacity. In the developing world, the opposite: [[Indonesia]]'s B40 mandate (effective Jan 2025) will consume ~15.6-16.6 million kiloliters of palm biodiesel annually, and [[Brazil]]'s Fuels of the Future Law mandates a march from B15 (Aug 2025) to B20 by 2030, requiring ~10+ million tonnes annually. These mandates are as much agricultural policy as energy policy — Indonesia props up smallholder palm farmers, Brazil supports the soybean complex.

The feedstock picture is the critical variable. Soybean oil (~37% of global feedstock, dominant in US and Brazil), palm oil (dominant in Indonesia and Malaysia), and rapeseed oil (~40% of EU feedstock) are the primary inputs. Used cooking oil (UCO) has surged to ~22-24% of EU feedstock, but supply is finite and increasingly fraudulent — Chinese UCO exports exceed credible collection estimates, and Malaysia exports 3x more UCO than it collects, suggesting palm oil laundered as waste. The EU's RED III directive, taking effect 2026, will cap UCO usage and remove double-counting for waste feedstocks, likely pushing 1 billion liters of additional demand toward rapeseed FAME.

For investors, FAME exposure is mostly indirect — through the ag commodity complex (soybean oil, palm oil, rapeseed), through refiners and blenders who handle both FAME and HVO, and through policy-driven mandates that create guaranteed demand floors. Pure-play FAME producers are rare among public companies. The key risk is structural displacement by HVO: 60% of global biofuel investment is now directed at HVO/HEFA capacity, while FAME investment has slumped. But FAME's death is greatly exaggerated — developing-world mandates ensure growing absolute volumes through at least 2030, even as its share of the biomass-based diesel mix erodes.

## FAME vs HVO — the critical distinction

| Property | Biodiesel (FAME) | Renewable diesel (HVO) |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Chemistry | Fatty acid methyl ester (contains oxygen) | Paraffinic hydrocarbon (identical to petroleum diesel) |
| Process | Transesterification (low temp, catalyst, methanol) | Hydroprocessing (high temp, high pressure, hydrogen) |
| Capex | Low (~$1-2/gal capacity) | High (~$5-10/gal capacity) |
| ASTM spec | D6751 (separate spec) | D975 (same as petroleum diesel) |
| Blend limit | B7 (EU), B20 (US), B40 (Indonesia) | No limit — drop-in replacement |
| Cold flow (CFPP) | Poor — gels at higher temps, limits winter use | Excellent — no crystallization |
| Cetane number | 50-60 | 75-95 |
| Shelf life | 6-12 months (oxidation degrades esters) | Years (same as petroleum diesel) |
| Infrastructure | Requires separate handling, tank cleaning | Uses existing diesel infrastructure |
| Byproduct | Glycerin (~10% of output by weight) | Propane, naphtha |
| Energy density | ~8% lower than petroleum diesel | Comparable to petroleum diesel |
| GHG reduction | 50-80% vs fossil diesel (lifecycle, depends on feedstock) | 50-90% vs fossil diesel |

The HVO advantage is clear on product quality. FAME's advantage is accessibility: any country with oilseed agriculture and basic chemistry can build a FAME plant. HVO requires hydroprocessing infrastructure, hydrogen supply, and significant capital — this is why [[Indonesia]] and [[Brazil]] mandate FAME, not HVO.

A critical technical nuance: blending HVO into FAME-containing diesel actually worsens cold-flow properties. A German study found that adding just 3% HVO to a B7 FAME blend seriously damaged CFPP (cold filter plugging point) performance. The two biofuels interact poorly when co-blended, complicating fuel logistics in markets that use both.

## Production process

FAME is produced via transesterification: one molecule of triglyceride (vegetable oil or animal fat) reacts with three molecules of methanol in the presence of an alkaline catalyst (NaOH or KOH) to yield three molecules of fatty acid methyl ester and one molecule of glycerin.

Mass balance: ~100 lbs of oil/fat + ~10 lbs methanol -> ~100 lbs FAME + ~10 lbs glycerin.

Steps:
1. Catalyst preparation — NaOH or KOH dissolved in methanol
2. Reaction — alcohol/catalyst mixed with oil at 60-70C for 1-2 hours
3. Separation — glycerin settles to bottom (higher density), FAME rises
4. Washing — water wash removes residual catalyst, soap, methanol
5. Drying — moisture removal to meet fuel spec

The process is sensitive to water content (produces soap rather than FAME) and free fatty acid levels in the feedstock (high FFA requires pre-treatment acid esterification). UCO and animal fats have higher FFA than virgin oils, adding a processing step.

Glycerin byproduct is sold into pharmaceutical, cosmetics, and food markets. The glycerin glut from biodiesel expansion has depressed glycerin prices, making it a marginal revenue source.

### UCO fraud — a structural problem

[[China]] is the world's leading UCO exporter, supplying ~60-70% of US UCO imports. But the volumes don't add up:
- Malaysia exports 3x more UCO than it credibly collects — suggesting palm oil laundered as "waste"
- Chinese authorities investigated fraudulent mixing of palm acid oil with UCO to claim tax rebates
- Europe has identified hundreds of suspicious UCO shipments from China
- China terminated its UCO export-tax rebate, disrupting supply chains

UCO is prized because waste-based feedstocks earn higher RIN values (lower carbon intensity scores). The fraud arbitrage is significant: virgin palm oil classified as UCO can earn double the compliance credits. This is a systemic vulnerability in the biodiesel supply chain.

### Regulatory framework — the RFS

The Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS), administered by the EPA, mandates blending volumes via Renewable Identification Numbers (RINs). Each gallon of biodiesel generates 1.5 D4 RINs.

D4 (biomass-based diesel) RVOs:

| Year | D4 RVO | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| 2023 | 2.82 billion gallons | |
| 2024 | 2.89 billion gallons | |
| 2025 | 2.95 billion gallons | |
| 2026 | ~4.75B gallons (proposed) | Major increase under EPA June 2025 NPRM |
| 2027 | ~5.0B gallons (proposed) | Approaches full capacity utilization |

D4 RIN generation: 25.22 billion RINs generated under the RFS in 2024 (up from 23.85B in 2023). Biofuel consumption exceeded RVO primarily due to growing domestic renewable diesel production.

D4 RIN prices: crashed from >$1.50 (2022 peak) to $0.41 (Feb 2024) — lowest since 2020 — as renewable diesel oversupply flooded the D4 RIN market. D4 and D6 RINs converged to near-parity for the first time, eliminating the historical D4 premium.

### The BTC-to-45Z transition

The Biodiesel Blenders Tax Credit (BTC) — $1.00/gallon for blended biodiesel — expired December 31, 2024. It was replaced by the Section 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit under the Inflation Reduction Act:

| Feature | BTC (expired) | 45Z (effective Jan 2025) |
|---------|--------------|--------------------------|
| Credit amount | $1.00/gal flat | Up to $1.00/gal (varies by carbon intensity) |
| Recipient | Blenders | Domestic producers only |
| Imports eligible | Yes | No |
| Duration | Annual renewals | Through Dec 31, 2027 |
| SAF bonus | None | $1.75/gal for SAF |

Impact — devastating for imports and FAME biodiesel specifically:
- US biodiesel + renewable diesel imports fell 90%+ in H1 2025 (from 68,000 b/d to 7,000 b/d) — lowest since 2012
- Biodiesel imports specifically: -94% (35,000 b/d to 2,000 b/d)
- Biodiesel consumption: down ~40% in H1 2025 vs H1 2024
- FAME biodiesel consumption through October 2025: 960 million gallons (dramatically below prior years)

The shift from blender credit to producer credit fundamentally disadvantages imports (including cheap Chinese UCOME) and advantages domestic HVO producers with lower carbon intensity scores. FAME producers are lobbying for reinstatement via the CROP Act (S. 3297), which would restore the BTC through May 2026.

### EPA proposed "half RIN" rule

The EPA's June 2025 NPRM proposes to reduce RIN credits for imported renewable fuel and fuel produced from foreign feedstocks, effectively cutting the subsidy value by ~$0.80/gallon. This would further shift economics toward domestic production and domestic feedstocks, benefiting the US soybean complex.

## EU market

### Regulatory framework

RED II (Renewable Energy Directive, recast 2018): mandated 14% renewable energy in transport by 2030, with a 7% cap on crop-based biofuels. UCO and animal fats counted double toward targets under the "double-counting" mechanism.

RED III (2023 revision, transposition deadline 2025): raises the transport mandate to 29% renewable energy by 2030, alongside a 14.5% GHG reduction target. Key changes for FAME:
- End of double-counting in Germany and Netherlands (2026) — waste feedstocks (Annex IX Part B: UCO, tallow Cat 1-2) will no longer count double toward mandates
- Caps on Annex IX Part B feedstocks — pushes obligated parties toward crop-based FAME
- GHG-based mandates — Netherlands shifting from volume to GHG reduction targets
- Net effect: Argus forecasts ~1 billion liters of additional rapeseed methyl ester (RME) demand in 2026 as the regulatory shift favors crop FAME over waste-based feedstocks

### Production and feedstock

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| EU FAME production (2023) | ~9.8M tonnes |
| EU HVO production (2023) | ~5.6M tonnes |
| Key feedstock: rapeseed | ~40% of EU biodiesel |
| Key feedstock: UCO | ~24% (2023); waste-based >40% total |
| Key feedstock: soybean oil | ~15% |
| Key feedstock: animal fats | ~2% |
| Palm oil | Declining; EU phasing out palm-based biofuels by 2030 |

The EU is the largest rapeseed biodiesel market globally. Germany is the largest single national market, with France, Spain, and the Netherlands following. The shift away from double-counting in 2026 will paradoxically increase demand for crop-based FAME (rapeseed, soybean) even as the policy intent was to favor waste-based fuels — because removing the 2x multiplier means obligated parties need twice as much physical volume to meet the same compliance target, exceeding available waste feedstock supply.

### Key players

[[Pertamina]] — state-owned oil company, the mandated buyer and blender. Prepared Plaju refinery (South Sumatra, 119,240 kl/month) and Kasim refinery (West Papua, 15,898 kl/month) for B40 production.

[[Wilmar International]] (SGX: F34) — world's largest palm biodiesel producer. Plants in Malaysia and Indonesia. 300,000 tonnes/yr biodiesel refinery in Singapore, plus multiple 350,000 tonnes/yr facilities in Riau, Indonesia.

Indonesia's installed capacity (~19.6M kl at 85% utilization = ~16.7M kl effective) can barely cover B40. The B50 timeline appears to be political aspiration more than industrial reality.

### Palm oil dynamics

Indonesia's biodiesel program is fundamentally about palm oil price support. The program is funded by the CPO Fund (BPDPKS), which collects a levy on palm oil exports and redistributes it as subsidies to biodiesel producers. When palm oil prices fall, the subsidy burden increases; when they rise, biodiesel becomes uncompetitive vs fossil diesel without larger subsidies. The program absorbs ~15-18M tonnes of CPO annually — roughly 25-30% of Indonesia's total CPO production — making it the single largest demand driver for Indonesian palm oil after food use.

### Margin drivers

Biodiesel margins are a function of:

1. Feedstock cost (~75-85% of production cost) — soybean oil, palm oil, rapeseed oil prices dominate economics
2. BOHO spread (Bean Oil minus Heating Oil) — the key margin proxy. When soybean oil is cheap relative to diesel, margins expand. The BOHO spread contracted to its narrowest in 4 years in Feb 2024 as soybean oil fell and diesel prices rallied.
3. D4 RIN value — the compliance credit that biodiesel generators receive. At $0.41/RIN (Feb 2024), RINs provide ~$0.62/gallon of biodiesel (1.5 RINs per gallon). At the 2022 peak of >$1.50/RIN, that was ~$2.25/gallon — the difference between profit and loss.
4. Tax credits — the expired $1.00/gal BTC was the margin of survival for many FAME producers. The 45Z credit (up to $1.00/gal for domestic producers) partially replaces it but excludes imports.
5. State LCFS credits — California's Low Carbon Fuel Standard provides additional credits based on carbon intensity. FAME from waste feedstocks (UCO, tallow) earns higher LCFS credits than virgin oil-based FAME.

### Profitability

During 2021-2024, estimated renewable diesel profit ranged from -$1.50 to +$1.00 per gallon, averaging a loss of -$0.20/gallon. FAME biodiesel margins followed a similar pattern — subsidy-dependent and volatile. Without the $1.00/gal BTC and strong RIN prices, most US FAME producers operate at a loss. This is why 8 plants closed in 2024.

## Structural trends

### FAME displacement by HVO — real but slow

The investment flow tells the story: 60% of global biofuel investment is directed at HVO/HEFA capacity, while FAME investment has collapsed. HVO capacity is on track to grow from 21 Mt (2024) to ~40 Mt by end-2026. In the US, renewable diesel feedstock usage surged 11x from 2017 to 2023 (from ~2B to ~22B lbs), while FAME biodiesel feedstock usage was flat to declining.

But FAME is not dying — it's bifurcating:
- Developed markets (US, EU): FAME in structural decline. US lost 8 plants in 2024. EU capping waste-based FAME via RED III. Policy shifting to favor HVO (drop-in, higher quality, eligible for aviation blending).
- Developing markets (Indonesia, Brazil, Argentina): FAME growing strongly, driven by agricultural mandates. Indonesia's B40 alone consumes ~15-16M kl/yr. Brazil's path to B20 by 2030 adds ~3-4M tonnes of incremental FAME demand.

### Feedstock competition intensifying

Total biomass-based diesel feedstock consumption (FAME + HVO combined) reached ~33 billion lbs in the US alone (2023). This represents 16.2% of projected global soybean oil production. As both FAME and HVO compete for the same finite feedstock pool (UCO, tallow, soybean oil), feedstock costs will structurally rise — squeezing margins for both.

The EPA's proposed "half RIN" for imported feedstock would redirect US demand toward domestic oilseeds, potentially driving a soybean crush capacity buildout but also raising domestic feedstock prices. The proposed D4 RVO increases for 2026-2027 would push US capacity utilization toward 100%, creating genuine physical tightness.

### UCO supply ceiling

UCO and animal fats are approaching maximum sustainable supply. Europe's waste-based feedstocks constitute >40% of biodiesel input but are "nearly exhausting 100% of estimated supplies." China's UCO exports — the marginal global supply source — face both fraud scrutiny and the termination of export-tax rebates. Any significant increase in biodiesel/HVO production must come from virgin vegetable oils, reconnecting biofuel demand to agricultural commodity markets.

### SAF optionality

Both FAME and HVO producers are eyeing [[Sustainable Aviation Fuel]] (SAF) as a higher-value outlet. The 45Z credit offers $1.75/gal for SAF vs $1.00 for ground transport fuels. [[Diamond Green Diesel]]'s Port Arthur plant is converting ~50% of its 470M gal/yr capacity to SAF. [[Neste]] started SAF production at Rotterdam (500,000 tonnes/yr initial). This SAF pull could reduce FAME/HVO supply available for diesel blending, tightening the ground transport market.

## Risks

- Policy risk: mandates can be delayed (Brazil's B16 already slipping), reduced (US RVOs subject to EPA discretion), or repealed (deregulatory risk under future administrations). FAME's entire demand floor is policy-created.
- Feedstock price spikes: drought, export bans, or crop failures in soybean/palm/rapeseed markets directly crush FAME margins. Feedstock is 75-85% of cost.
- HVO displacement: in any market where policy allows free competition between FAME and HVO, HVO wins on product quality. FAME survives only where mandates specifically require it or where capex barriers prevent HVO buildout.
- UCO fraud unraveling: if regulators crack down on fraudulent UCO classification, the effective feedstock supply contracts and prices spike for legitimate waste-based FAME producers.
- Food-vs-fuel: political backlash against using edible oils for fuel. Indonesia's B40-B50 program consumes 25-30% of national CPO production. Brazil's B20 path requires 4.6M additional hectares of soybeans. These are politically sensitive in a food-price-conscious world.
- Glycerin oversupply: the byproduct market is saturated. Glycerin revenues are marginal and declining, removing a secondary income source.

## Insights

- FAME biodiesel is a policy product, not a market product — without mandates and credits, it cannot compete with either petroleum diesel or HVO on price or quality
- The global FAME market is splitting into two stories: structural decline in developed markets (US plant closures, EU regulatory shift toward HVO) vs mandate-driven growth in developing markets (Indonesia B40, Brazil B15-B20)
- Feedstock is the bottleneck, not capacity — Brazil has 50% idle capacity, and global FAME production is constrained by vegetable oil supply, not reactor availability
- The UCO fraud problem is an underappreciated systemic risk — if Chinese UCO exports are partly laundered palm oil, the carbon accounting underlying compliance credits is fiction
- The BTC-to-45Z transition was an extinction event for imported FAME — US biodiesel imports fell 94% in H1 2025, fundamentally restructuring trade flows
- Investor exposure to FAME is best accessed through the feedstock complex (soybean oil, palm oil) rather than through biofuel producers — the mandate-driven demand floor for these oils is more investable than the margin-challenged producers themselves
- Indonesia's B50 aspiration is likely vaporware — the 2026 quota being set flat at 2025 levels, combined with insufficient capacity and palm supply constraints, suggests B40 is the practical ceiling for the medium term
