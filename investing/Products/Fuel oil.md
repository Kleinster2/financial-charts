# Fuel oil

Residual petroleum product from the bottom of the distillation column — the dense, sulfurous remnant after a refinery extracts gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, and naphtha. Known in the oil industry as "bottom of the barrel." Cheap and unloved in normal times, fuel oil powers the workhorses of globalization: container ships, bulk carriers, and tankers. The [[2026 Iran conflict market impact|Iran conflict]] has turned it into the most expensive and scarce petroleum product in the world.

---

## Synthesis

The fuel oil crisis is the most underreported consequence of the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure. While markets fixate on crude at ~$100/bbl, fuel oil has blown through all historical peaks — $140/bbl in [[Singapore]], ~$160 in [[Fujairah]], $175 for low-sulfur grades. The 40-60% premium over crude is structurally new: Persian Gulf refineries produce 20% of globally traded fuel oil ([[IEA]]), and the crude they process (Arab Light) yields ~50% residue vs 33% for [[Oil|WTI crude]]. Replacing Gulf crude with US or Russian barrels doesn't just replace volume — it produces less fuel oil per barrel. The world's two most important Asian bunkering ports (Singapore #1 at 54.9M metric tons, Fujairah #3 at 7.5M) are both running dry. With SPRs already tapped and refinery bypass exhausted, only demand destruction via higher prices can balance the market. If ships can't refuel, globalization's physical infrastructure halts.

The deeper structural story: fuel oil is a byproduct concentration play. Refineries don't produce fuel oil on purpose — it's whatever's left after extracting the higher-value products. Simple refineries (Nelson Complexity Index 2-3) yield ~30% fuel oil; complex US refineries (NCI 9.5 average) yield ~5%. The Middle East and Russia, with their lower-complexity refinery fleets, are the world's dominant fuel oil exporters. Cutting off the Gulf doesn't just remove crude — it removes the specific refinery slate that produces the most fuel oil per barrel. No amount of rerouting US or West African crude to Asian refineries can replicate that yield profile.

## Grades and the IMO 2020 split

[[IMO 2020]] (effective January 1, 2020) capped sulfur content in marine fuel at 0.5%, down from 3.5%. This created two parallel fuel oil markets and a permanent structural split in the shipping fuel chain.

| Grade | Sulfur content | Use case | Notes |
|-------|---------------|----------|-------|
| HSFO (high-sulfur fuel oil) | 3.5% | Ships with scrubbers | Cheaper; ~58% of heavy fuel oil market share (2024) |
| VLSFO (very low-sulfur fuel oil) | 0.5% | Most ships post-2020 | Blended from various distillates and residuals; fastest-growing segment (7.8% CAGR through 2030) |
| MGO (marine gasoil) | 0.1% | Emission control areas | Essentially a [[Diesel fuel|diesel]] substitute; most expensive option |

### Hi-5 spread (VLSFO minus HSFO)

The Hi-5 is the defining spread in post-2020 fuel oil markets. It drives scrubber economics — wider spread = faster scrubber payback.

| Period | Hi-5 spread (Singapore) | Context |
|--------|------------------------|---------|
| Jan 2020 (IMO launch) | ~$353/mt | Market priced in the transition |
| 2020-2021 | $100-200/mt | Settling in |
| Jun 2022 (record) | $538.50/mt | Russia-Ukraine war, HSFO surplus from sanctioned Russian exports |
| Jun 2024 | ~$67/mt | Narrowed — scrubber payback stretched to 3-4 years |
| Mar 2025 | ~$61/mt | Near-record lows for the sulfur cap era |
| Jan 2026 | >$300/mt | Hormuz closure — HSFO supply from Gulf cut off |

### Scrubber economics

Exhaust gas cleaning systems ("scrubbers") allow ships to burn cheaper HSFO while meeting IMO 2020's emissions limits at the stack. The investment calculus is straightforward: if the Hi-5 spread exceeds the annualized scrubber cost, scrubbers pay.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Scrubber-equipped ships | 5,200+ (~22% of commercial fleet) |
| Retrofit cost | $2-5M per vessel (VLCCs: $2.5-3.0M newbuild, $4-4.5M retrofit) |
| Container vessel (20K TEU) | ~$8M including installation |
| Payback at $200/mt spread | <1 year (large vessels) |
| Payback at $100/mt spread | 3-4 years |
| Global scrubber investment | >$16B cumulative |
| Technology mix | 80% open-loop, 17% hybrid, <2% closed-loop |
| Fleet eligible for retrofit | 38% of global merchant fleet |
| Container industry savings (2020-mid 2023) | ~$12B |

Vessel types: bulk carriers + container ships = 61% of installations. Cruise liners = 14% (adoption up 28% in 2023-2025). About half of new oceangoing orders in early 2024 included dual-fuel or scrubber-ready designs.

Major scrubber manufacturers: [[Wartsila]] (~21% market share, 2,000+ systems), [[Alfa Laval]] (~17%, 1,400+ vessels), Yara Marine Technologies, EcoSpray, Pacific Green, Fuji Electric.

## Supply dynamics

### Refinery complexity and fuel oil output

Fuel oil output is an involuntary byproduct of refining. The key variable is refinery complexity, measured by the Nelson Complexity Index (NCI). Complex refineries with coking units convert residuum into lighter products; simple refineries sell it as fuel oil.

| Refinery type | NCI range | Fuel oil yield | Example regions |
|---------------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Topping/simple | 2-3 | ~30% | Older Middle East, some African |
| Hydroskimming | 4-6 | ~25% | Much of Europe (avg NCI 6.5) |
| Cracking | 7-8 | ~15-20% | Mixed; newer Middle East |
| Deep conversion/coking | 9-10+ | ~5% | US (avg NCI 9.5), newest ME mega-refineries |

The US produces almost no fuel oil because its refinery fleet is the world's most complex (average NCI 9.5) — cokers effectively make residuum "disappear" by upgrading it into gasoline and diesel. The Middle East and Russia, with lower average complexity, are the world's structural fuel oil exporters. This is why the Hormuz closure hits fuel oil disproportionately harder than other products.

Newer Middle East mega-refineries (Kuwait Al-Zour NCI 12+, Oman Duqm) are high-complexity, but older facilities persist. The region's overall fuel oil yield is declining as simpler refineries are upgraded or retired.

### Major producers and exporters

| Country | Role | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| [[Russia]] | World's largest fuel oil exporter | ~650 kb/d fuel oil exports (Dec 2021); 80% of Russian oil now goes to China/India; shadow fleet of ~270 vessels |
| [[Saudi Arabia]] | Major producer, but consumes domestically | ~1.4 mb/d burned for power at summer peak |
| [[Kuwait]] | Producer/exporter | Simple refinery fleet yields high fuel oil share |
| [[UAE]] | Producer/exporter + bunkering hub | [[Fujairah]] FOIZ storage and bunkering |
| [[India]] | Major refiner, exporter | [[Reliance Industries]] Jamnagar = world's largest refinery (1.24M bpd); high complexity, lower fuel oil yield |
| [[China]] | Consumer (teapot refineries) | [[Chinese teapot refineries]] import fuel oil as refining feedstock; Beijing imposed 40-80% consumption tax offset since Jan 2025 |

Western price cap on Russian fuel oil: $45/bbl (set Feb 2023). Russian fuel oil has been rerouted through shadow fleet and intermediary traders to maintain supply to Asian buyers.

### The Hin Leong scandal (2020)

One of Singapore's largest independent oil traders. Founder Lim Oon Kuin ("OK Lim") hid $800M in futures trading losses. Revealed liabilities: $4.05B vs assets of $714M — a $3.34B hole. Only $141M in actual oil inventory vs $1.28B declared. OK Lim forged documents claiming 1M barrels of gasoil transferred to China Aviation Oil. Filed bankruptcy April 2020. OK Lim sentenced to 17.5 years (November 2024). The collapse reshuffled Singapore's bunkering market — MPA awarded new licenses to Minerva ([[Mercuria Energy Group|Mercuria]]) and TFG Marine ([[Trafigura]]) immediately after.

## Crude yield dynamics

Not all crude produces the same amount of fuel oil. This is the compounding factor behind the Hormuz crisis:

| Crude grade | Origin | Residue yield (fuel oil feedstock) |
|-------------|--------|-----------------------------------|
| Arab Light | [[Saudi Arabia]] | ~50% |
| Arab Heavy | [[Saudi Arabia]] | ~55%+ |
| Kuwait Export | [[Kuwait]] | ~45-50% |
| WTI | US (Permian/Midland) | ~33% |
| Brent | North Sea | ~35% |

When Asian refiners substitute lost Gulf crude with American or Russian barrels, they get structurally less fuel oil per barrel processed. The volume loss from Hormuz is therefore worse for fuel oil than for lighter products like gasoline or naphtha. Even if total crude supply is fully replaced (it isn't — there's a 1.5 mb/d unfilled gap per [[Kpler]]), the fuel oil shortfall persists because of yield mismatch.

## 2026 crisis — Hormuz closure impact

![[fuel-oil-singapore-price-history.png]]
*Singapore benchmark fuel oil has surpassed the 2008 commodities super-cycle and 2022 Russia-Ukraine peaks. The Hormuz closure has produced an unprecedented price spike (Bloomberg, Mar 2026).*

The [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure cut off refineries in [[Saudi Arabia]], [[Kuwait]], and the [[UAE]] that together produce 20% of globally traded fuel oil ([[IEA]]). The problem is compounded because:

1. Gulf crude naturally yields more fuel oil (~50% residue for Arab Light) than alternatives
2. [[Fujairah]], the #3 bunkering port and the strategic bypass for Hormuz, is itself under Iranian attack risk — tankers won't dock
3. SPRs have already been tapped and refinery bypass capacity already used — no more buffers
4. Only demand destruction via higher prices remains as a balancing mechanism

[[Maersk]] CEO [[Vincent Clerc]] (to Le Monde, Mar 2026): *"If we do nothing, we risk ending with dry supply points in Asia."*

Clerc: *"We are starting to organize ourselves to transfer stocks from one continent to another and continue operating."*

Traders are quoting prices over the phone valid for only a few minutes, on a take-it-now-or-miss-out basis — indicating genuine scarcity, not just a speculative premium.

### Shipping implications

If bunkering ports run dry, ships halt. This would cascade through global supply chains: container vessels carrying manufactured goods, bulk carriers moving grain and commodities, tankers carrying the crude that produces the fuel oil itself (a reflexive loop). The shipping industry, typically conservative in public pronouncements, has broken that pattern to sound the alarm.

## Investment implications

### For shipping companies
Fuel oil is typically 40-60% of a vessel's voyage cost. Record bunker prices compress margins for container lines ([[Maersk]], [[COSCO]], [[CMA CGM]]), bulk carriers, and any operator without long-term fuel hedges. Scrubber-equipped ships gain a cost advantage if HSFO remains cheaper than VLSFO (Hi-5 spread positive), but the current crisis is hitting both grades. Tanker operators ([[Frontline]], DHT, INSW) benefit from rate spikes but face the same bunkering cost pressure.

### For refiners
Low-complexity refiners that produce high fuel oil yields are indirect beneficiaries — their residual output commands record prices. High-complexity US refiners produce almost no fuel oil, so the fuel oil premium doesn't accrue to them. The crack spread between fuel oil and crude has blown out to historically unprecedented levels.

### For power generators
Countries burning fuel oil for electricity ([[Saudi Arabia]], Caribbean nations, [[Bangladesh]]) face sharply higher generation costs. Saudi Arabia's ~1.4 mb/d summer burn at $140+/bbl is a massive fiscal hit. This accelerates the case for gas-to-power and renewables investment.

### For commodity traders
The geographic dislocation (Asia short, Europe/Americas adequate) creates huge arb opportunities. Moving fuel oil from Rotterdam to Singapore is suddenly the most profitable trade in the physical oil market. Trading houses with logistics networks (Vitol, Trafigura, Mercuria) and physical infrastructure at both ends of the arb are positioned to capture the dislocation.

## Insights

- Fuel oil is the petroleum product most sensitive to refinery complexity and crude slate — cutting off Gulf crude doesn't just remove volume, it removes the specific barrel type that produces the most residual per unit processed
- The [[IMO 2020]] split created a two-tier market where scrubber economics are driven entirely by the Hi-5 spread; the current crisis has re-widened this spread, accelerating scrubber payback to <1 year and likely triggering a new wave of retrofit orders
- Singapore's 54.9M metric ton bunkering dominance (5.6x #2 Rotterdam) means a supply crisis there is a supply crisis for global shipping — there is no alternative port of equivalent scale in Asia
- The Hin Leong collapse (2020) consolidated Singapore's bunkering market in the hands of major commodity traders, reducing counterparty risk but also concentrating supply decisions
- Saudi Arabia's seasonal fuel oil burn for power generation (~1.4 mb/d at peak) is a structural demand driver that competes with export volumes — the kingdom's Vision 2030 gas/renewables push could eventually free up fuel oil for export, but that's years away
- Russia's role as the world's largest fuel oil exporter adds a sanctions/shadow fleet layer — the $45/bbl price cap interacts with the current crisis, as Russian HSFO flowing via shadow tankers becomes relatively more attractive to Asian buyers at current spot prices
