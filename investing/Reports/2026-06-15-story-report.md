## The commodity-equity beta law — family completed, granularity proven

The story is that the cluster campaign completed the commodity-equity beta family — the set of "a metal's listed miners cohere into one tradeable factor" cohorts — and in doing so nailed down two laws: every commodity-miner cohort is real but reproduces its own sector ETF, and the cohort's distance from the underlying metal scales with how many of its members are pre-production developers.

What changed: four cohorts were validated or stress-tested in one session. [[Lithium equity beta]] (the lithium miners [[Albemarle]], [[SQM]], [[Lithium Americas]], [[Sigma Lithium]]) validated as real but the loosest in the family (weekly intra-correlation 0.624, equal to the LIT ETF), with the pre-revenue Thacker Pass developer [[Lithium Americas]] (118% annualized volatility) splitting off as a lithium option, not lithium beta. [[Uranium equity beta]] (the fuel producers [[Cameco]], [[Uranium Energy Corp]], [[Denison Mines]], [[NexGen Energy]], [[Energy Fuels]], Ur-Energy) validated tight and durable (intra 0.765, stable holdout 1.01, equal to the URNM/URA miner ETFs), carrying the family's biggest miners-vs-metal wedge: +0.180 against physical uranium (the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust) because half the cohort is Athabasca developers ([[NexGen Energy]], [[Denison Mines]]) pricing the supply deficit — the equities returned +45% to +156% this year while the metal returned about +8.5%. [[Oil and gas equity beta]] (the supermajors and large E&Ps [[ExxonMobil]], [[Chevron]], [[ConocoPhillips]], [[EOG Resources]]) validated as the purest case (intra 0.802, first principal component 85.2%) but only +0.04 distinct from XLE — the ETF literally sits inside the cohort. And [[Steel and aluminum equity beta]] is the granularity proof: a "metals producers" label is not one factor but two, with aluminum ([[Alcoa]] plus [[Century Aluminum]], 0.686, robust) and steel ([[Nucor]], [[Steel Dynamics]], [[Cleveland-Cliffs]], 0.640) merging only at distance 0.645.

Why it matters: the family now reads as one law with two dials. Cohesion is set by how clean the price regime is — gold (0.86) and uranium (0.77) tight in sustained bulls, copper (0.71) and lithium (0.62) loose in chop — and the chart above is the proof, the uranium cohort visibly breathing with the metal's trend. Distance-from-metal is set by developer content: copper +0.033 (mature majors), gold +0.070, uranium +0.180 (half developers). And the granularity is the individual commodity, not the asset class: steel and aluminum split because they share neither inputs (scrap and hot-rolled coil vs alumina and power) nor demand (auto and construction vs packaging and grid), so a "metals" basket has no single factor while each metal does.

The tension: every one of these cohorts is ETF-replicable — the point of validating them is to discover they are their own sector ETF, which means the cohesion the permutation tests certify is co-movement, not a distinct tradeable edge. The structural exception is lithium: it has no investable spot instrument (no equivalent of GLD or CPER), so for lithium the equity complex and the LIT ETF are the only lithium exposure that exists — there is nothing to decompose against.

Watch: a sideways chop in any of the four metals should visibly loosen its cohort (the family's cleanest forward test), and a wave of uranium developers reaching production should pull that +0.180 wedge back toward spot.

## The depositary-receipt taxonomy — a hub built, the ADR orphan filled

Touched: [[Depositary receipts]], [[American Depositary Receipts]], [[Global Depositary Receipts]], [[Chinese Depositary Receipts]], [[Hong Kong Depositary Receipts]], [[Indian Depositary Receipts]], [[Japanese Depositary Receipts]], [[European Depositary Receipts]], [[Foreign private issuer]], [[Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act]], [[Executive Order 13959]]

The story is that the vault now models depositary receipts — the certificates that let a foreign company's shares trade on a domestic exchange in local currency — as a proper instrument-class hub with national children, closing a long-standing gap where the single most-referenced variant, the American Depositary Receipt, was cited in about 150 notes but never actually defined.

What changed: a depositary-receipt layer was built top to bottom. [[Depositary receipts]] is the new umbrella hub — the depositary-bank mechanism, the universal sponsored-versus-unsponsored and listing-level axes, and a national-variants table. [[American Depositary Receipts]] is the new US-variant concept (Level I/II/III plus Rule 144A; sponsored versus unsponsored; the China-ADR delisting cluster) that fills the orphan anchoring those ~150 vault notes. Six national variants were fleshed into their own notes, each sized to what it warrants: [[Global Depositary Receipts]] (the main international DR, London and Luxembourg, Rule 144A plus Regulation S), [[Chinese Depositary Receipts]] (the STAR-Market homecoming vehicle, State Council 2018, Ninebot the first actual issue in 2020 at ¥18.94 rising to ¥38.50), [[Hong Kong Depositary Receipts]] (Hong Kong Exchange 2008, [[Vale]] the pioneer, underused), [[Indian Depositary Receipts]] (SEBI 2009, [[Standard Chartered]] the only issuer, defunct since 2020 — the family's clearest failure case), [[Japanese Depositary Receipts]] (a trust-beneficiary-certificate structure, niche), and [[European Depositary Receipts]] (Euronext, euro-denominated, largely historical). [[American Depositary Receipts]] was then forward-wired into the notes where the ADR is structurally central.

Why it matters: the tree now reads [[Depositary receipts]] into ADR, GDR, BDR, CDR, HDR, IDR, JDR, and EDR, so any future cross-border-listing question resolves to a defined node instead of a bare acronym. The forward-wiring was deliberately by intent, not volume: the China-ADR delisting cluster — [[Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act]] (the audit-track threat to the whole ADR complex) and [[Executive Order 13959]] (the ownership-track delistings that took out the three Chinese telecom ADRs) — plus the standalone foreign-issuer ADRs ([[Petrobras]], [[Santander Brasil]], [[Embraer]], [[NIO]]) got the link; the roughly 150 incidental "cross-region ADRs" mentions did not, because a cluster note's price-unit aside does not earn a concept link. The connective tissue across the whole layer is [[Foreign private issuer]], the SEC reporting regime (20-F, 6-K, IFRS) that DR issuers fall under on US markets — the same regime that explains why foreign-issuer financials like [[Equinox Gold]]'s and [[Nubank]]'s do not reconcile cleanly.

The tension: the family is now complete but lopsided — the ADR is a ~150-note anchor while the IDR is a defunct one-issuer footnote, and the build deliberately sized each note to that reality rather than giving every variant equal weight. The forward-wiring also stopped at the substantive subset, leaving the incidental mentions un-wired by design — a judgment call that trades exhaustiveness for signal.

Watch: whether the [[Chinese Depositary Receipts]] homecoming vehicle gains real issuance the next time US-listed China ADRs face delisting pressure (the format's relevance rises and falls with that cycle) — the one national variant whose weight could still change.
