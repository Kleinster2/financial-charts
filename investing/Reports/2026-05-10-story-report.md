## Training-to-inference cluster rotation as priced framework

Touched: [[Training-to-inference cluster rotation]], [[Inference economics]], [[Blackwell]]

The story is that Mirae Asset Securities' two-part research note (May 8) named a transaction pattern that has now cleared as a publicly priced deal at scale for the first time: an AI lab rotates a heterogeneous cluster (built originally for training but architecturally suboptimal for it) over to a counterparty that needs inference capacity, and the cluster prices as a different category of business depending on whose workload runs on it. Colossus 1 (the Memphis cluster — ~150K H100 + ~50K H200 + ~20K GB200, 11% Model FLOPS Utilization — MFU — during training per [[The Information]]) is the first cluster where that rotation has a public price tag attached.

What changed: a new concept note [[Training-to-inference cluster rotation]] documents the framework — the mechanical asymmetry that makes the same physical cluster a training laggard and an inference winner (NCCL ring-topology limits where a single straggler GPU bottlenecks the whole synchronous training step, vs inference where heterogeneous nodes can be scheduled independently); the $2.60/GPU-hour blended pricing the Mirae math implies vs $1.80-2.20/hr commodity neocloud comps; the structural distinction from [[Neocloud financing]] (which prices homogeneous fleets via debt) and from cross-border arbitrage models (Foulis); the IPO-defense application for [[SpaceX IPO 2026]]; and three failure modes — single-tenant scarcity (one customer holds the lease), hardware obsolescence (H100s age out faster as GB300 ramps), counterparty concentration (Anthropic compute-mix exposure to xAI). [[Inference economics]] adds a "Heterogeneous cluster asymmetry (May 2026)" section laying out the mechanical reason training and inference impose opposite cluster-architecture constraints, and using the [[Lambda Labs]] fleet-utilization vs MFU distinction to sharpen what the 11% xAI MFU figure actually means (training-step efficiency, not power-on time).

Why it matters: when a transaction pattern has a public price, comparable assets re-rate against it. Neoclouds with heterogeneous fleets ([[CoreWeave]], [[Crusoe]], [[Lambda Labs]] historically) and any hyperscaler with retired-from-training H100/H200 inventory now have a benchmark for what "rotated to inference" is worth as recurring revenue. The same logic applies in reverse: the framework defines why Colossus 2 (homogeneous Blackwell, retained for xAI training) and Colossus 1 (heterogeneous, leased for Anthropic inference) belong on opposite sides of a balance sheet even though they sit in the same campus.

The tension: Mirae's lease-economics math, the New Street range, and the implied per-GPU-hour price are not yet reconcilable, and the failure modes are real — if Anthropic's own multi-year TPU and Trainium ramps come online in 2027 as committed, single-tenant lease revenue from Colossus 1 may compress before H100 obsolescence forces it down. The framework is priced for the first time but not yet stress-tested across a second comparable.

Watch: any second public rotation transaction (a hyperscaler offloading training-retired capacity to an inference-only lab, or another lab leasing in heterogeneous capacity) and the next disclosure from Anthropic or xAI on Colossus 1 economics. The [[Blackwell]] note also adds GB200 power smoothing as a Deployment-challenges factor — Hopper-era software stacks can damage GB200 silicon (Patel reporting on physical chip destruction at xAI), which is a related but separate hardware-side risk.
