## The paradox: best year ever and worst exodus ever, in the same week

Touched: [[Wachtell Lipton]], [[Big Law transformation]], [[Andrew Nussbaum]]

The story is that [[Wachtell Lipton]] — short for Wachtell, Lipton, Rosen & Katz, the New York corporate law firm founded by four NYU Law School Jewish graduates in 1965 on a handshake deal that the firm has never replaced with a formal partnership agreement — is simultaneously having the strongest financial year in its history and the worst lateral-exodus year in its history. The firm advised on 9 of the 20 largest US M&A transactions of 2025 (per LSEG data) and posted profits per equity partner around $12 million, the highest of any major US firm. In the same window it has lost 8 partners since the start of 2025, where for most of its prior history the count was effectively zero.

What changed: the FT (May 2026) reporting put exact numbers on the duality. In late February 2026, [[Andrew Nussbaum]] — Wachtell's executive committee co-chair since 2023, an M&A specialist in his sixties — emailed the firm to note Wachtell had advised on transactions worth $230bn announced in the preceding five days and won a landmark corporate-governance case at Delaware's Supreme Court. The same week, two partners left. The 2025 partner exodus broke down as: [[Josh Feltman]] (former corporate restructuring head) to [[Kirkland & Ellis]] on a reported $80 million / three-year package, four mid-career M&A "younger stars" to [[Latham & Watkins]], and three other partners to undisclosed destinations.

Why it matters: the data fits the [[Big Law transformation]] frame — that elite NY corporate law is shifting from lockstep partnership (pay tied to seniority) to investment-bank-style firms (pay tied to revenue origination), with the FT and academic commentators (John Morley, Yale Law School) labelling the shift "Kirklandisation". Wachtell has been the most resistant lockstep holdout. Its 2025 P&L proves the model still produces extraordinary economics; its 2025 lateral-exodus count proves the model can no longer hold mid-career stars against $80 million guaranteed cash packages. The two facts are not in tension at the firm-economics level — the same partners who delivered the 2025 league table are also the ones receiving outside offers — but they are in tension at the franchise-continuity level.

The tension: which side compounds. If 2025 PEP keeps the senior partnership profitable enough to fund retention top-ups for outside-targeted partners, the model survives in modified form. If the exodus accelerates and the partners the firm loses are disproportionately the high-revenue mid-career deal lawyers, the cross-practice integration that makes lockstep economics work erodes.

Watch: the 2026 partner exodus rate. If 8 was the unprecedented baseline for 2025, the 2026 number tells whether this was a one-off catch-up after years of structural underpayment or the new run rate. The next disclosed move from the firm is the data point.

## The defection mechanic: $30 million-plus packages convert structural underpayment to immediate cash

Touched: [[Josh Feltman]], [[Kirkland & Ellis]], [[Latham & Watkins]], [[Big Law transformation]]

The story is the arithmetic that explains why Wachtell partners keep leaving despite the firm's record profits: a high-revenue mid-career lockstep partner is structurally underpaid relative to peer market rates because lockstep transfers compensation upward with tenure and laterally across non-deal practices (litigation, antitrust, tax) that cannot match a top originator's revenue, and a guaranteed multi-year cash package at a scale-plus-pay rival converts that lockstep tax into immediate cash.

What changed: [[Josh Feltman]] — the former head of [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s corporate restructuring practice — moved to [[Kirkland & Ellis]] on a reported $80 million / three-year package per the FT (May 2026). That is the largest publicly disclosed Wachtell-to-rival defection on record and concretizes the mechanic. Lockstep would deliver Feltman roughly $5-6 million annually at his Wachtell tenure tier. The Kirkland package implies $25-30 million annual run-rate plus a guarantee that removes the career-long retention risk the lockstep firm cannot offer.

Why it matters: the FT reports that "a handful of lawyers" at Kirkland and similar firms now command annual pay above $30 million. This means the lockstep ceiling at Wachtell — the all-in median for senior partners — sits below the floor for top-tier originators at scale-plus-pay competitors. That is a price-discrimination opening that a rational mid-career star can take only once: the receiving firm gets the partner permanently, the lockstep firm cannot bid back without breaking the model that funds its overall median compensation.

The tension: Wachtell's 2025-26 lockstep modifications (lower first-year partner equity, more discretion to reward deal originators, some redistribution from litigators to deal lawyers) attempt to defend at the margin. They do not change the basic arithmetic for a mid-career originator who can clear $25-30 million / year at Kirkland or $20-25 million / year at [[Latham & Watkins]]. The four partners Latham hired from Wachtell in 2025 are the second data point: when one rival opens the bidding, others follow on smaller but still market-rate packages.

Watch: any disclosure that names a Wachtell mid-tier deal partner alongside a $50 million-plus reported package would mark the next visible defection in the same arithmetic frame.

## The succession problem at Wachtell: Lipton 94, Herlihy 79, and the next-generation question

Touched: [[Marty Lipton]], [[Edward Herlihy]], [[Jacob Kling]], [[Kevin Schwartz]]

The story is that [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s economic model depends on multi-decade institutional knowledge concentrated in a small number of senior partners, and the two most concentrated points in that distribution — co-founder [[Marty Lipton]] (94, in office almost daily) and his protégé [[Edward Herlihy]] (79, the firm's largest individual revenue generator across three decades of bank-sector dealmaking) — are at the top of an aging curve that the [[Big Law transformation]] argues scale-plus-pay rivals cannot replicate but Wachtell cannot indefinitely sustain either.

What changed: per the FT (May 2026), Wachtell promoted two younger lawyers to keep the bank-sector M&A franchise inside the firm. [[Jacob Kling]] — 40, an M&A specialist and Herlihy's protégé — was named co-head of the M&A practice with David Lam (50) and added to the executive committee. [[Kevin Schwartz]] — Lipton's son-in-law — was also added to the executive committee. The promotions sit alongside the older 2023 elevation of [[Andrew Nussbaum]] and [[William Savitt]] (both in their sixties) to executive committee co-chair roles. The pattern is an explicit two-tier succession: Nussbaum / Savitt for the immediate near-term, Kling / Schwartz / Lam for the post-Herlihy phase.

Why it matters: Lipton invented the [[Poison pill]] in 1982 — formally a shareholder rights plan, the defensive corporate structure that gave boards the ability to dilute hostile acquirers and effectively block hostile tender offers without shareholder vote. The 1982 invention reshaped US M&A defence and is the canonical claim to Wachtell's institutional standing. Herlihy has advised on virtually every major US bank-sector transaction over three decades, including the 2008 [[Bank of America]] / [[Merrill Lynch]] acquisition. Both men hold relationships and judgment-product that scale-plus-pay rivals — which retire partners by the mid-60s to make room for younger talent — structurally cannot replicate. The succession promotions are the firm's bet that the two-decade compounding of those relationships can transfer to the next generation before the originating partners exit.

The tension: succession from a 79-year-old senior rainmaker to a 40-year-old protégé is not a guaranteed transfer. The bank-sector M&A franchise is built on CEO-level relationships that take 10-15 years to mature. If Kling stays — and is not himself recruited by [[Kirkland & Ellis]] or [[Latham & Watkins]] in the next two years — the transfer can plausibly work. If Kling's promotion is read across the lateral market as a retention signal, it becomes a recruiting target marker in the same way Feltman's restructuring leadership was.

Watch: any external lateral approach disclosed against Kling, Lam, or Schwartz in the next 12 months would invalidate the succession premise. The firm's own bank-sector deal flow over 2026-27 is the slower-moving validation: if Wachtell loses one or two large bank-sector mandates that historically went to Herlihy, the relationship-transfer assumption is failing.

## Kirklandisation: the rival model that pays $30 million for the same lawyer

Touched: [[Kirkland & Ellis]], [[Latham & Watkins]], [[Big Law transformation]], [[Liability management exercises]]

The story is that [[Kirkland & Ellis]] — a Chicago-founded firm now over 4,000 lawyers globally — has spent the 2010s and 2020s building the rival model to lockstep. Scale-plus-pay: top originators command $30 million-plus annual compensation, mid-tier underperformers are routinely de-equitised, senior partners are pushed toward retirement by their mid-60s, and the firm runs aggressive lateral recruiting from the elite holdouts. The 2025 result: Kirkland became the first US law firm to clear $10 billion in annual revenue, with profits per equity partner around $11.5 million — closing within $0.5 million of [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s record figure despite a roughly 14× larger lawyer count.

What changed: the FT (May 2026) made the convergence explicit. Kirkland's 2025 revenue was approximately $10.5 billion; in 2020 it was approximately $4.8 billion. That is a more-than-doubling in five years, almost entirely organic, in an industry where prior decade-long doubling was rare. [[Latham & Watkins]] — second by revenue at approximately $8.5 billion in 2025 — followed the same recipe and was the largest single beneficiary of the 2025 Wachtell exodus, recruiting four mid-career M&A partners. The economic engine of both firms is concentrated in private-equity-driven M&A and [[Liability management exercises]] (out-of-court restructuring of corporate debt — drop-down financings, uptiering amendments, exchange offers — the dominant pre-bankruptcy distressed-credit playbook of the late 2010s and 2020s, generating large M&A-adjacent fees from a continuous deal flow).

Why it matters: Kirklandisation reframes elite Big Law from "small partnership of senior generalists" to "scale platform with star-anchored practice groups". The economic premise is that recurring PE-backed transactional volume rewards firms willing to staff at scale and pay individual originators investment-bank-style compensation, even at the cost of partnership cohesion. The model is now mature enough — Kirkland is over 30 years post-pivot — that it has produced its own institutional culture, with academic commentators (John Morley, Yale Law School) treating Kirklandisation as the post-2016 standard rather than an aberration.

The tension: PE-side dominance creates structural conflicts in distressed situations. The 2025 [[Altice]] uptiering case forced Kirkland to withdraw because its three named defendants (Apollo, Ares Management, BlackRock) were all major Kirkland clients. That is the same cross-client structural problem that lockstep partnerships were originally designed to avoid through firm-wide conflict screens. The model that wins on volume can lose specific high-stakes mandates that a smaller, less-conflicted firm can still take.

Watch: the next high-stakes distressed restructuring where Kirkland faces a conflict similar to Altice. If a Wachtell- or Cravath-level firm picks up that mandate at premium fees, the Kirklandisation tradeoff is visible at the deal level.

## Lockstep modifications: defending the model without abandoning it

Touched: [[Wachtell Lipton]], [[Big Law transformation]], [[Andrew Nussbaum]]

The story is that [[Wachtell Lipton]] has explicitly broken with several of [[Marty Lipton]]'s 1990 "35 principles" — the foundational document, archived at the University of Pennsylvania, that codified the firm's lockstep partnership, one-partner-one-vote governance, and one-NY-office model — while preserving the core architecture. The modifications are designed to widen the senior committee's discretion to top up retention packages for outside-targeted partners without breaking the firm-wide median compensation that funds the model overall.

What changed: per the FT (May 2026), Wachtell has implemented four specific changes since 2025. First, partner compensation is no longer fully transparent within the firm; partners can no longer fully see what other partners earn, ending the historical visibility that made lockstep self-policing. Second, the equity-points system has been tilted away from litigators toward partners who originate deal-fee revenue. Third, first-year partners now receive roughly half the equity allocation they would have received under the prior system, conserving capacity for senior-tier discretion. Fourth, executive committee membership has been expanded to younger lawyers (Kling, Schwartz) alongside the 2023 senior co-chair structure under [[Andrew Nussbaum]] and [[William Savitt]].

Why it matters: the changes preserve the public-facing identity of lockstep — partners are still nominally on a tenure-based system, the firm still has 80 partners and one office, the cross-practice fee architecture still pools revenue across the firm — while quietly enabling negotiated retention. This is the same trajectory [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]] followed after the 2016 [[Scott Barshay]] departure: selective modifications without formal abandonment. The economic question is whether the modifications are large enough to retain the next generation of mid-career stars without being so large that the firm-wide median erodes the senior-partner economics that fund the structure.

The tension: insiders quoted in the FT split. Some associates were unsettled by the cultural shift, reading it as the beginning of full Kirklandisation by stages. Other partners noted that the litigation specialists who lost equity points are themselves now the next defection candidates — the same arithmetic that pulled Feltman out applied to anyone whose equity was reduced to fund deal-side retention. The modifications solve the deal-partner retention problem by creating a litigator retention problem.

Watch: any disclosed Wachtell litigation-partner departure to Paul, Weiss, [[Latham & Watkins]], or another scale firm would mark the modifications producing the predicted second-order exodus.

## The 2016 Cravath/Barshay precedent: the original tear in lockstep

Touched: [[Scott Barshay]], [[Paul, Weiss]], [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]]

The story is that the [[Big Law transformation]] cycle has a specific date and a specific person: in 2016, M&A partner [[Scott Barshay]] left [[Cravath, Swaine & Moore]] — the 19th-century-founded firm whose "Cravath system" of lockstep partnership, hire-from-within, and one-firm-one-vote governance had defined elite NY corporate law for the entire 20th century — for [[Paul, Weiss]], on a package the FT (May 2026) describes as "multiples" of what Cravath's lockstep system could pay. That move proved a top corporate-M&A originator at the most resistant lockstep firm could be moved with money. Every subsequent move — including 2025's [[Josh Feltman]] $80 million / three-year package — sits on the precedent Barshay set.

What changed: today's vault treatment named the moment explicitly. Cravath's 2025 economics — approximately $1.4 billion revenue, approximately $7 million profits per equity partner, materially below [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s ~$12 million PEP — quantify what selective post-Barshay lockstep modification produced. Cravath competes for the same prestige assignments as the scale-plus-pay rivals without matching the lateral-recruiting budget of [[Kirkland & Ellis]] or [[Latham & Watkins]], and the gap shows up in the per-partner profit number.

Why it matters: the precedent matters because it demonstrates that lockstep modification, once initiated under defensive pressure, does not stabilise into a new equilibrium — it slides toward the scale-plus-pay model over time. Cravath nine years later sits closer in compensation structure to Paul, Weiss than to Wachtell. That is the trajectory question Wachtell's 2025-26 modifications now face.

The tension: the Cravath case suggests modifications without scale do not produce the same economics as Kirkland's full transformation. Cravath's per-partner economics are below both lockstep elite (Wachtell) and scale-plus-pay leaders (Kirkland, Latham). The intermediate path — partial transformation, no scale — is the worst-of-both outcome the Wachtell modifications risk.

Watch: Cravath's 2026-27 lateral hires and partner departures. If Cravath itself begins losing mid-career partners to Kirkland or Latham at Wachtell-comparable rates, the worst-of-both reading is being confirmed.

## The Wachtell representation portfolio: bet-the-company anchor, litigation anchor, success-fee anchor

Touched: [[Wachtell Lipton]], [[William Savitt]], [[Twitter]], [[Poison pill]], [[M&A tracker]], [[Paramount Skydance]], [[Norfolk Southern]], [[Cox Communications]]

The story is that [[Wachtell Lipton]]'s 2025 league-table dominance — advising on 6 of the 10 largest US M&A transactions of the year and 9 of the top 20 — illustrates how the firm's three economic engines work in combination: bet-the-company defensive M&A, high-stakes trial litigation, and project-based success fees that compound with deal size rather than billable hours.

What changed: today's vault recorded the 2025 deal table for the first time. The six Wachtell-counsel deals among the top 10 by value are: [[Paramount Skydance]] / [[Warner Bros Discovery]] $109.9 billion (largest US deal of the year, the David Ellison-led combined entity acquiring Warner Bros Discovery); [[Union Pacific]] / [[Norfolk Southern]] $87.7 billion (eastern Class I rail merging into the western Class I network, creating the first transcontinental US rail operator); [[Saudi PIF|PIF]]-led consortium / [[Electronic Arts]] $48.1 billion (the Saudi sovereign wealth fund's gaming take-private); [[Charter Communications]] / [[Cox Communications]] $35.2 billion (cable consolidation); [[SoftBank]]-led $30 billion investment in [[OpenAI]]; and [[Palo Alto Networks]] / [[CyberArk]] $24.7 billion (cybersecurity vertical integration). Each carried litigation, governance, hostile-bid, or sovereign-buyer risk that is the structural reason a board picks Wachtell.

Why it matters: the success-fee model is concentrated in single deals. The 2022 [[Twitter]] / [[Elon Musk]] transaction generated a $90 million Wachtell fee against time sheets that suggested under $30 million; the gap is the model. After Musk completed the takeover, X (the renamed entity) sued Wachtell to recover the difference, then abandoned the case. Today, [[William Savitt]] — Wachtell's executive committee co-chair since 2023 and the firm's senior trial lawyer — is lead trial counsel for [[OpenAI]] in its dispute with Musk, and cross-examined Musk in California in early May 2026 (the same week the FT published its profile). The litigation franchise sits alongside the M&A franchise as a co-equal economic anchor and is the public expression of the cross-practice integration that scale-plus-pay rivals cannot replicate.

The tension: project-based success fees are episodic. A year with one $90 million Twitter-style fee is structurally different from a year of steady billable-hour throughput, and the volatility is real. The 2025 league table looks like fortune as much as franchise — six of ten top deals is an extraordinary share that is unlikely to repeat. If 2026 produces a more typical Wachtell year (still elite, but not topping the league table), the financial gap to Kirkland will reopen and the lockstep modifications will face funding pressure earlier than expected.

Watch: 2026 H1 league-table position. Wachtell finishing meaningfully behind its 2025 share would be the first signal that the success-fee model's good year is mean-reverting.
