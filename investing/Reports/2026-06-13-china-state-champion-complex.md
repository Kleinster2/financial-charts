# China state-champion equity complex — the bond market China's savers can't buy

The cleanest way to read China's state-owned-enterprise re-rating is that it is not an equity story at all. [[China special valuation]] — Beijing's "valuation system with Chinese characteristics," the campaign to lift the multiples of state champions — is usually narrated as policy: the CSRC decided its banks, telecoms, and oil majors were too cheap and willed them higher. But the engine underneath is the bond market. With the 10-year [[China government bond yields|Chinese government bond yield]] down near 1.6–1.8% and the property sector dead as a store of value, income-starved domestic capital has nowhere to go for yield except the high-dividend SOEs — state banks at 5–7%, [[China Mobile|telecoms]] and [[PetroChina|energy majors]] at 4–7%, all with an implicit state backstop on the payout. The policy supplied permission and catalysts; the yield gap supplied the bid. That distinction is the whole report: a re-rating built on a yield gap lives or dies on the yield gap, not on a slogan.

The vault's cluster work turns that claim from assertion into structure. Run the ten lead SOE H-shares — four banks, three energy majors, the three telecoms — through a hierarchical clustering and 中特估 does not behave like one factor: PC1 explains just 46% of variance, and the three sectors form three clean sub-blocs that only merge with each other at correlation distances above the 0.5 threshold. Banks move with banks (intra-correlation 0.72–0.89, the tightest), telecoms with telecoms, energy with energy; across sectors the links are weak-but-positive (banks↔telecoms 0.35, energy↔telecoms 0.19). The one thread that ties the blocs together is duration: the strongest cross-sector pair is [[China Mobile]] ↔ [[ICBC]] at 0.50 — the two purest bond-proxy names, lowest volatility and highest payout. 中特估 trades less like a sector and more like a synthetic perpetual bond, and the names that behave most like a high-grade perpetual co-move most. This is an old instrument in new clothing: when the risk-free yield collapsed in 19th-century Britain, rentiers reached for perpetual income through [History: British Consols](obsidian://open?vault=history&file=04%20-%20Early%20Modern%2FBritish%20Consols); China's savers, denied yield by financial repression, are manufacturing a Consol out of dividend equities.

The constraint is that this complex exists in its current, domestically-bid form largely because of decoupling. [[Executive Order 13959]] (the 2020 ban on US persons holding "Communist Chinese military companies," which delisted the three telecom ADRs) and the audit-driven [[Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act|HFCAA]] were not just a few delistings — together they repatriated the primary listings home, sending [[China Telecom]] to Shanghai in 2021 and [[China Mobile]] in 2022, and pushing the broader ADR complex toward Hong Kong. In the geopolitics vault these read as [Weaponized Interdependence](obsidian://open?vault=geopolitics&file=Events%2FExecutive%20Order%2013959) — capital-markets access as the financial analogue of semiconductor export controls. The market irony is that walling these firms out of US capital pools concentrated them in the HK/mainland pool where the domestic yield bid operates. The thing that looked like the loss (US listing access) set up the thing that drove the gains (the domestic dividend re-rating). The same state that the US is decoupling from is the state whose savers, herded by policy and a broken property market, became the marginal buyer.

The counter-example — the reason this is a tension and not a thesis — is the growth story the complex is trying to tell and the market is refusing to pay for. [[China Telecom]] runs [[Tianyi Cloud]], by its own reckoning the #2 public cloud in China at over RMB120bn of revenue, and the largest of the state-operator clouds now contesting the government segment in the [[China AI cloud war]]; [[China Unicom]] was the first firm in the country to put [[Data as an asset|data on its balance sheet]] under the 2024 Ministry of Finance rule, the marquee proof-of-concept for treating data as a [Data as a Factor of Production](obsidian://open?vault=geopolitics&file=Concepts%2FData%20as%20a%20Factor%20of%20Production) — a frontier the [Technologies: Data as an Asset](obsidian://open?vault=technologies&file=Data%20as%20an%20Asset) note frames as the feedstock layer of AI. None of it is in the tape. The cluster test puts China Telecom's correlation to the cloud complex ([[Alibaba]]/[[Tencent]]/[[Baidu]]) at 0.253 against 0.635 inside the telecom bloc; the data-asset filing was RMB204mn against RMB671bn of total assets. The state champions are being valued as bond substitutes at the exact moment they are pitching themselves as AI-infrastructure plays — and the market is paying for the coupon, not the compute. There is precedent for that too: the chartered monopoly champions of the early-modern era ([History: Chartered Company Model](obsidian://open?vault=history&file=04%20-%20Early%20Modern%2FChartered%20Company%20Model)) were priced on dividend flow and the durability of their state relationship, with the charter both capping the upside and backstopping the income — the same double-edge that state ownership gives the SOEs today.

The one-line: China's state champions are trading as the bond market the country's savers are no longer allowed to buy, and the decoupling that severed them from American capital is what penned them into the domestic pool where that trade works — which makes the bull case (these become AI-infrastructure growth names) a bet against the only force currently bidding them.
