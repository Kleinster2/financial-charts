## DOJ closes the Powell investigation and the Warsh path clears

Touched: [[Jerome Powell]], [[Kevin Warsh]], [[Tom Tillis]], [[Federal Reserve]], [[Jeanine Pirro]], [[Scott Alvarez]], [[French Hill]], [[Standing Repo Facility]], [[Ira Jersey]], [[Bloomberg Intelligence]], [[Ian Lyngen]], [[BMO]], [[Polymarket]]

Background. [[Jerome Powell]] is the current Chair of the [[Federal Reserve]], the US central bank. His four-year term as Chair ends May 15, 2026, after which he can remain a Board Governor through 2028 if he chooses. President Trump nominated [[Kevin Warsh]] (a former Fed Governor 2006-2011 and now a partner at the [[Stanley Druckenmiller|Druckenmiller]] family office Duquesne) to replace Powell on Jan 30, 2026. Through early 2026 the confirmation was held up by a US Department of Justice (DOJ) grand-jury investigation into Powell over alleged irregularities in the multi-billion-dollar renovation of the Fed's Eccles Building headquarters in Washington, DC — a probe widely read in markets as politically motivated. Senator [[Tom Tillis]] (Republican of North Carolina, member of the Senate Banking Committee) had publicly stated since January 2026 that he would not vote to confirm any Fed nominee until the Powell investigation was "fully resolved" — making him the swing vote because Republicans hold the Banking Committee by a one-seat margin.

The story is that the Fed Chair transition has stopped being a litigation contest and started being a balance-sheet contest. On Apr 24 2026, [[Jeanine Pirro]] — the US Attorney for the District of Columbia who had been overseeing the Powell probe — announced via social media that her office was closing the investigation. She added a clause reserving the right to revisit "if facts warrant," but otherwise framed the closure as final. [[Scott Alvarez]] — the former Fed General Counsel from 2004 to 2017 and the institutional memory of how the Fed handles legal challenges — read the closure as procedurally final on the [[Bloomberg]] Real Yield broadcast that afternoon, telling host Sonali Basak that the Pirro statement "smells of transparency and finality up to the last sentence." Within hours, [[Polymarket]] (the prediction-market platform whose Fed Chair odds are widely tracked by rates traders) saw odds of Warsh being confirmed by May 15 jump from the high-50s to 82%.

What changed in the vault: the [[Jerome Powell]] note now carries an Apr 24 timeline entry and a "DOJ closes investigation" subsection capturing Pirro's reservation language and the scenario in which Powell stays on as a Board Governor through 2028 even after handing over the Chair seat to Warsh. The [[Kevin Warsh]] note carries the parallel Apr 24 row with the Polymarket spike, plus two named-analyst frames worth their own notes. [[Ira Jersey]] — chief US rates strategist at [[Bloomberg Intelligence]], the in-house research arm of Bloomberg LP — argued on the broadcast that even a Warsh-led Fed cannot shrink its balance sheet below roughly $2.7 trillion without rewriting the post-2008 reserve-and-liquidity rules that govern how much cash banks must hold at the Fed; Jersey's framing is that the $2.7T is a structural floor, not a target. [[Ian Lyngen]] — head of US rates strategy at [[BMO]] (the Canadian bank Bank of Montreal, whose Capital Markets division runs a major US Treasuries desk) — gave a tradeable level: 4.25% on the 10-year Treasury yield is where, in his view, real-money buyers should add duration into any Warsh-driven sell-off. The [[Tom Tillis]] note now flags that he was still publicly silent as of the broadcast that evening; [[Bloomberg]] Fed correspondent Mike McKee read the silence as tacit consent — DOJ would not have moved without first checking with Tillis's office, McKee argued, so the working assumption is Tillis is satisfied. New actor stubs were created for Pirro, Alvarez, [[French Hill]] (the House Financial Services Committee Chair, who issued a one-sentence statement welcoming the closure), Jersey, [[Bloomberg Intelligence]], Lyngen, and BMO. A new concept note for the [[Standing Repo Facility]] was created — this is the Fed's permanent overnight lending program, made permanent in July 2021 after the September 2019 repo squeeze (when overnight funding rates briefly spiked from ~2.2% to over 10% because banks ran short of reserves). The Standing Repo Facility lets primary-dealer banks borrow cash from the Fed against Treasury collateral at a fixed rate, capping how high overnight funding can spike — it is the engineered backstop that, on paper, lets the Fed run a smaller balance sheet without re-running 2019.

Why it matters: with the legal overhang gone, the entire question of what a Warsh Fed will do moves from "will he be confirmed" to "what does he do with the balance sheet." Warsh has been on the record for years preferring a much smaller Fed balance sheet (current size ~$6.6T, down from a 2022 peak of ~$8.9T). Jersey's $2.7T floor framing is the constraint: it says the structural bottom is roughly $4 trillion above where Warsh would ideally take it, and trying to push lower would risk re-running the September 2019 repo squeeze. That turns the Warsh confirmation into a duration-supply question — how much Treasury paper the private sector has to absorb as the Fed lets bonds roll off — rather than a rate-cut question. Lyngen's 4.25% 10Y level is the operational price tag: if duration sells off into Warsh's confirmation hearings (currently scheduled for late April 2026 in the Senate Banking Committee), 4.25% is where the BMO desk says the market should start scaling in. The Powell-as-governor-through-2028 scenario matters because it means the seat changing hands does not mean Powell leaves the building — he can keep voting on the FOMC for two more years, which dampens any sharp Warsh-era policy pivot.

The tension. Pirro's closure language reserved the right to revisit the file — that keeps a tail risk on it rather than zeroing it. Tillis's silence is the next political tell; McKee's reading that DOJ would have checked with the Senate Banking ranking member's office before announcing is plausible inference, not confirmed fact. The Standing Repo Facility is a cushion but not an eliminator of the structural reserve floor — the September 2019 episode is the historical proof that aggressive quantitative tightening (QT, the gradual unwinding of the Fed's bond holdings) runs into front-end plumbing constraints, and a single quarter-end repo squeeze under a new Chair would do more political damage to Warsh's mandate than any specific rate-path call.

Watch: a public Tillis statement on the Warsh nomination (silence past the Banking Committee hearing date is itself a signal); whether DOJ exercises its "right to revisit" clause in the next 90 days; the Senate Banking schedule and the timing of any committee floor vote relative to the May 15 expiration of Powell's Chair term; concrete Warsh comments on the balance-sheet target — does he name a number above or below Jersey's $2.7T floor; the 10-year Treasury yield's behavior around the 4.25% Lyngen level on confirmation news; Standing Repo Facility daily usage as a stress vs. cushion signal (sustained large draws would be the early warning that QT is biting).

## A credit-fund principal calls private credit "Lehman-style" on tape

Touched: [[Jonathan Lewinsohn]], [[Diameter Capital]], [[Marc Pinto]], [[Moody's]], [[Private Credit]], [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]], [[Blackstone]], [[Distressed assets]]

Background. Private credit is the $1.8-$2T market of non-bank loans made directly to corporate borrowers by funds rather than banks; it has roughly tripled since 2020 and is now ~30% of US sub-investment-grade corporate debt. The retail-investor share — money raised from individuals through non-traded business development companies (BDCs, regulated investment vehicles that hold private loans and offer quarterly redemptions, typically capped at 5% of assets per quarter) — has grown to a Moody's-estimated $200-$400B over the last five years. In late 2025 and early 2026, redemption requests at the largest non-traded BDCs — [[Blackstone]]'s BCRED, [[Blue Owl]]'s OBDC II — began exceeding the quarterly cap; OBDC II became the first to "gate" redemptions (suspend them above the cap) in February 2026. This is the [[Private credit retail liquidity crisis 2026]], the active vault thread tracking whether the retail leg of the private-credit boom unwinds disorderly.

The story is that the unfolding stress now has a named "Lehman-style" framing on broadcast tape and a quantified asset-quality bifurcation between BDC structures that maps the next 12-24 months. [[Jonathan Lewinsohn]] — co-founder and managing partner of [[Diameter Capital]], a New York credit hedge fund of roughly $20B in assets that runs both liquid (publicly traded leveraged loans and high-yield bonds) and illiquid credit strategies — appeared on Bloomberg the week of Apr 20 2026 and compared the redemption pressure directly to the bank-run dynamics of the 2008 Lehman Brothers collapse. His exact quote, replayed on Bloomberg Real Yield Apr 24: "There is a reckoning threatening… If you look at what has gone on in the last three or four months as people have tried to get money out of private credit funds — private credit doesn't have a liability mismatch. We have the opportunity to buy loans being sold." The framing matters because Lewinsohn is positioning Diameter as a buyer of forced sales — the liquid-credit side of the trade where gated BDCs and stressed insurance balance sheets have to liquidate at distressed prices.

[[Marc Pinto]] — senior credit analyst at [[Moody's]], the credit-rating agency, covering BDCs and alternative-asset managers — appeared in the same broadcast and put numbers on the bifurcation. Non-traded perpetual BDCs (open-ended vehicles like BCRED that take new investor money continuously and offer quarterly redemptions) are running a 0.5% troubled-borrower share — defined as borrowers more than 90 days delinquent or on Moody's internal watchlist. Traded BDCs (closed-end vehicles whose shares trade like stocks on the NYSE — for example [[Ares Management|Ares]]'s ARCC) are running 2.0%, down from 2.5% earlier in the cycle. The 2028-29 software-loan maturity wall — the calendar bunching of refinancings as roughly 25% of average BDC loan portfolios are concentrated in software borrowers, most of whom refinanced in 2023-24 with three-to-five-year tenors — is the calendar-driven trigger that Pinto says turns "asset-quality good today" into a Q3-Q4 2026 stress window.

What changed in the vault. [[Jonathan Lewinsohn]] is a new actor stub carrying the Lehman framing quote and the liquid-side-buyer positioning. [[Diameter Capital]] gets Lewinsohn added alongside the original co-founder Scott Goodwin, plus an Apr 2026 "buying forced sales" section. [[Marc Pinto]] is a new actor stub with the BDC bifurcation table (0.5% perpetual / 2.0% traded), the 25% software concentration call, the Q3-Q4 2026 stress window, and a notable Moody's conference exchange in which Pinto pulled [[Blackstone]] President and COO Jon Gray back from his upbeat private-credit pitch with the asset-quality deterioration angle (Gray acknowledged the data but maintained the long-run return thesis). The [[Moody's]] note carries the Pinto Apr 24 BDC asset-quality section. The [[Private Credit]] sector hub gets an Apr 24 escalation paragraph anchoring the Lewinsohn quote and Pinto's numbers, and Pinto is added to the data/research analyst row. Pinto's $200-$400B retail-money-into-private-credit estimate is now the quantified anchor for what is at risk on the retail leg.

Why it matters: a credit-fund principal calling the retail-BDC redemption pressure a Lehman analogy on Bloomberg is the first time that label has been put on it publicly. Through 2024 and 2025, retail BDC investors had been treated by the alt-management industry as the marginal buyer of the next leg of growth — the "democratization of private markets" pitch. Lewinsohn's framing reverses that: retail is now the marginal seller. Pinto's bifurcation table is the first quantified read that the asset-quality story is about structure, not just sector — non-traded perpetual BDCs may be running cleaner books either because they genuinely select better borrowers or because they have not yet marked positions to where traded BDCs already have. The 25% software concentration plus the 2028-29 maturity wall means the trigger is calendar-driven (loans coming due) rather than sentiment-driven (a market shock), which makes the timing more predictable than a typical credit cycle. Pinto's framing — that the credit cycle plays out slower in fixed income than equities have already priced — is the call distressed-debt funds (Diameter, Marblegate Asset Management, Strategic Value Partners, Davidson Kempner) are positioning around: equity multiples already discount stress, debt instruments do not yet.

The tension: Pinto's own read on the absolute numbers is that today's data is "asset-quality good" and likely to normalize over time rather than spike — a much milder framing than Lewinsohn's "reckoning threatening." Lewinsohn's commercial interest is in being the buyer of forced sales, so the rhetoric serves the strategy — distressed credit funds always talk their book. Jon Gray of Blackstone, with the largest non-traded BDC in the market (BCRED, ~$80B), maintained the long-run private-credit return thesis at the Moody's conference even as Pinto pulled him back on the asset-quality data. The 0.5% perpetual / 2.0% traded bifurcation cuts both ways: it can be read as perpetual-fund structures genuinely protecting asset quality through stricter underwriting, or as perpetual funds simply not yet marking what traded funds have already marked.

Watch: BDC redemption gates expanding beyond OBDC II's February 2026 first gate (BCRED is the largest test case); software-borrower billings and customer-retention numbers — Pinto flagged these as his leading indicator before defaults show up; the 2028-29 maturity-wall refinancing fork starting to show up in early-2027 rate-locks (refinance the credits where the thesis still holds, exit the rest); troubled-borrower share in non-traded perpetual BDCs (currently 0.5%) migrating toward traded BDC levels (currently 2.0%) — that convergence would validate the "they just haven't marked yet" reading; Diameter and other liquid-credit funds disclosing forced-sale acquisitions in Q2/Q3 2026 marks; whether BCRED redemption trends bend back toward the perpetual-structure baseline or worsen.

## Mamdani's millionaire surtax becomes the first NYC capital-mobility test

Touched: [[Wealth tax]], [[Zohran Mamdani]], [[Citadel]], [[Kenneth Griffin]], [[Capital flight]], [[New York City budget]], [[Bloomberg]]

Background. [[Zohran Mamdani]] is the current mayor of New York City, elected in November 2025 on an affordability platform that promised aggressive city spending paid for by progressive tax increases. His [[New York City budget|FY2026 NYC budget]] proposal is roughly $127 billion — almost double Mike Bloomberg's last city budget of ~$70 billion in FY2014 — and the funding mechanism is a combination of a millionaire-bracket income surtax (an additional bracket on annual income above $1M), a corporate tax increase, or a ~10% property-tax hike. New York City cannot impose income taxes unilaterally — Albany (the state legislature) controls income-tax authority, so any surtax requires passage at the state level, not just city-council approval.

The story is that Mamdani's surtax announcement turned a national tax-policy debate — true wealth tax versus millionaire surtax — into a concrete NYC repricing event, and Citadel founder [[Kenneth Griffin]] became the visible counter-party signaling that the high-end response is mobility, not absorption. The vault now distinguishes a true wealth tax (an annual mark-to-market levy on net assets — the structurally hardest version, currently being studied as legislative proposals in California and Minnesota) from a millionaire surtax (an additional income-tax bracket on annual flow above a threshold — easier to legislate, currently active in Massachusetts at 4% above $1M, Washington at 9.9% above $1M despite no general state income tax, plus new top-bracket surtaxes in Maine 2026, and proposed in NY under Mamdani). The two are structurally different — one taxes the stock of assets each year, the other taxes the income flow once. Most US "wealth tax" headlines describe the latter, not the former.

The historical precedent the vault is anchored on: Norway raised its true wealth tax from 0.85% to 1.1% in 2022 and saw $54B in personal wealth physically leave the country, including 500-plus wealthy residents who emigrated in 2022-2023. The Norwegian Treasury had projected +$146M in incremental revenue from the rate hike; the actual outturn was -$448M because the emigrating taxpayers took their full tax base with them. Sweden abolished its wealth tax entirely in 2007 after decades of capital flight, including IKEA founder Ingvar Kamprad relocating to Switzerland in 1973 and the Tetra Pak family leaving in the 1980s. France replaced its broad wealth tax (the ISF, abolished in 2017 under President Macron) with a real-estate-only version (the IFI), exempting financial assets specifically because financial wealth is the most mobile.

What changed in the vault. The [[Wealth tax]] concept note now carries a "US state-level — wealth tax vs. millionaire surtax" section with the Massachusetts / Maine / Washington / California / Minnesota / New York breakdown, sourced to Michael Bologna, the senior tax correspondent at Bloomberg Industry Group (the legal-and-tax research arm of Bloomberg LP). The [[Zohran Mamdani]] note carries the Apr 2026 surtax announcement section and the Citadel pushback. Bloomberg flagged a media-framing detail in the Citadel response: the social-media video Citadel posted to react to the Mamdani announcement was filmed against the backdrop of what Bloomberg identified as a $38M Manhattan apartment, a visual choice that shifted the public optics of who the surtax is hitting from "millionaires" to "billionaires." The [[Citadel]] note carries a 2025-2026 update row covering the NYC pushback and Citadel's stated halt to its NYC investment plans. The investment-implication framing — state-level surtaxes are positive for in-state municipal-bond demand (because residents face higher state taxes and value the federal tax-exemption on munis more) but pressure local property markets at the very high end (because top-bracket residents have the strongest incentive to move) — is now embedded in the [[Wealth tax]] note.

Why it matters: this is the first NYC-specific public repricing event for high-end capital under the Mamdani administration. [[Citadel]] is the multi-strategy hedge fund founded by [[Kenneth Griffin]] (one of the wealthiest hedge-fund managers in the US, with a personal net worth of roughly $40B); Griffin already moved Citadel's headquarters from Chicago to Miami in 2022, citing Illinois tax and policy concerns. That HQ move is the canonical interstate-mobility precedent — a top-five hedge fund actually relocating an entire C-suite and trading floor to Florida (which has no state income tax). When Griffin publicly states that Citadel is reconsidering its NYC investment plans in response to Mamdani's surtax announcement, it is a low-cost / high-signal move: it does not commit Citadel to leave New York but it puts a price on the surtax even before the legislation moves through Albany. The Bloomberg-flagged $38M-apartment background also matters as media framing — it reframes the public argument from "millionaire" to "billionaire," which changes the political calibration of who the surtax actually targets and whether voters perceive the policy as fair. The structural lesson from the Norway / Sweden / France precedents — that financial wealth is more mobile than real estate or labor income — applies a fortiori at sub-national level, because moving from New York to Florida is far cheaper logistically than moving from Norway to Switzerland.

The tension: Mamdani's surtax has been announced, not legislated. Whether it actually passes the New York State Assembly (the lower chamber in Albany), survives state-level constitutional review, and survives federal challenge under the dormant Commerce Clause are all open questions. The political backdrop of the $127B FY2026 budget — nearly double Bloomberg's last budget — means there is real pressure to find the revenue, but the capital-flight math from the Norwegian case (-$448M actual versus +$146M projected) says even modest emigration at the very top can swing the fiscal arithmetic the wrong way. The vault is also still tracking whether any true wealth-tax proposals (mark-to-market annual levies on net assets, currently legislative drafts in California and Minnesota) actually pass — those are the structural test cases for whether the US replicates the European wealth-tax experiment at sub-national level, and they would face far harder constitutional challenges than a simple income-tax surtax. The Mamdani case is the surtax variant; the harder constitutional question still sits in California and Minnesota.

Watch: New York State Assembly action on the Mamdani surtax (Albany is the legislative gate, not the City Council); concrete Citadel positioning changes — does the rhetoric translate into specific relocations, M&A activity in New York, or hiring shifts away from the New York office; high-end NYC residential transaction volumes for Q2 and Q3 2026 (any significant volume drop above the $20M-per-unit price tier would corroborate the mobility signal); muni-bond demand and pricing for in-state New York tax-exempt issuance (a tightening of NY muni spreads versus the national index would corroborate the surtax's positive effect on in-state muni demand); the California and Minnesota true-wealth-tax legislative timelines as the next-tier test of whether the US gets a real mark-to-market wealth tax anywhere; Florida domicile-shift filings from New York high-AGI residents tracked through the IRS Statistics of Income series; whether other Democratic mayors of major cities (Chicago, Boston, Los Angeles) match Mamdani's surtax framing or back away from it.
