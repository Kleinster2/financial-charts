## Refining slate rigidity — joint-product mechanism breaks the airline-cut thesis

Touched: [[Refining slate rigidity]], [[Crack spread]], [[Jet fuel]], [[Hormuz Permanent Risk Premium]], [[Iran conflict airline disruption]], [[Deutsche Lufthansa]], [[Cathay Pacific]], [[Delta Air Lines]], [[Ryanair]], [[Qantas]]

What changed today: a new concept note ([[Refining slate rigidity]]) was created from the FT structural column "Why flying less won't do much to ease the oil crisis" (Apr 27, 2026) and the embedded Argus Media "Going crackers" chart (NW Europe jet/diesel/gasoline + Brent front-month, Jan-Apr 2026). The concept covers the joint-product refining mechanism, cross-product demand-destruction sequencing (jet → diesel/gasoline → naphtha/LPG → fuel oil as price signals propagate down the slate), April 2026 case (NW Europe jet ~$200 vs Brent ~$100 from the Argus chart — a ~$100 crack spread on jet vs ~$20 on gasoline), and historical examples (1973 oil shock with parallel jet-fuel separation, 2022 post-invasion European diesel shortage, 2026 current case).

The cut wave specifics layered onto airline notes: [[Ryanair]] — Bernstein 35% fuel/revenue 2025 estimate as Quick stats row + first-mover demand-destruction logic; [[Deutsche Lufthansa]] — direction reversal from March schedule-share opportunity (adding flights as competitors retreated) to April cut wave; [[Cathay Pacific]] — basis-risk note on the ~30% crude hedge as crack spread blew out (the hedge is on Brent, not jet, so widening cracks make the hedge ineffective at the product level); [[Delta Air Lines]] — Apr 27 cut wave section after Q1 earnings, with Monroe refinery's $300M Q2 contribution as partial insulation (Delta is the only major US carrier that owns refining capacity); [[Qantas]] — closes a dead wikilink in [[Iran conflict airline disruption]]; March Perth-London reroute via Singapore + April 27 cut. CEO Bastian's "plan for pricier flights" narrative hardened into actual capacity reduction.

Why it matters: this is the demand-side third leg of the 2026 oil regime alongside the shale-supply-discipline supply-side leg and the Hormuz-permanent-risk-premium chokepoint-side leg. The refining-slate constraint means the conventional "high oil → demand destruction → price relief" feedback runs slower than usual, because the most price-sensitive product (jet, the discretionary travel cut) does not propagate back to crude until the slate's other products also weaken. Refiners keep buying $100 crude as long as the aggregate slate clears, even when one cut weakens. This makes oil prices stickier-up than the old reaction function suggested.

The tension: the FT article makes the joint-product / fixed-yield argument cleanly, but the slate is not perfectly fixed (the 5-10% swing matters at the margin). If the airline cut wave persists into Q3 2026 and crack spreads stay wide, refiners will eventually shift slate composition (more diesel, less jet), but the elasticity is slow and lagged. The cleanest signal would come from European refinery turnaround scheduling adjustments — refiners optimize unit configurations during planned maintenance, and a shift in turnaround targets would indicate which products are clearing and which are not.
