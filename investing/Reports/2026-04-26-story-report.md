## Pentagon procurement reform stops being rhetoric

Touched: [[Dan Driscoll]], [[Defense procurement reform]], [[Pentagon]], [[Pete Hegseth]], [[General Motors]], [[Ford]], [[Defense Primes]], [[Defense supply chain]], [[Trump defense budget]], [[Lockheed Martin]], [[RTX]], [[Anduril]], [[Defense]]

The story is that the [[Defense procurement reform]] campaign has moved from speeches into commercial-manufacturer outreach. The April 15 FT/WSJ report on Pentagon talks with [[General Motors]] and [[Ford]] CEOs is not a one-off; it is the operational extension of [[Dan Driscoll]]'s 12-month campaign — May 2025 "kill a prime in two years," Nov 2025 "primes conned the country," Feb 2026 "adapt and change or die." [[Pete Hegseth]] codified the doctrine at cabinet level; [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s Jan 8, 2026 EO ties capital-return restrictions to production-capacity investment. The structural target is a 90/10 inversion of the Army's purchasing mix.

What changed: the reform now has a named architect, a written timeline, an investment-implications matrix, and a documented commercial-recruit list. [[Ford]] is the more interesting structural signal — no defense subsidiary, so its inclusion is pure dual-use industrial-base reconstitution. [[Trump defense budget]] now reads as the enforcement layer of a 12-month campaign, not a one-day market reaction.

Why it matters: every Driscoll public statement is now a candidate prime-stock catalyst; every contract award outside the prime ecosystem is a data point along the inversion trajectory. [[Lockheed Martin]] and [[RTX]] carry concentrated political-attribution risk.

The tension: the government does not own equity in the primes, has no board seats, and the EO has no statutory backing for the capital restrictions. Legal challenge risk is real, and even at the inversion target the primes still hold the program-of-record contracts and qualified-supplier ecosystems.

Watch: any defense contract award routed through [[General Motors]], [[Ford]], or the [[Anduril]]/[[Palantir]] insurgent stack, and the [[Defense Autonomous Warfare Group]] (~$55B FY27) funding line as the routing vehicle.

## Brazil fintech is what fintech looks like after payments become public infrastructure

Touched: [[C6 Bank]], [[Brazil fintech]], [[Marcelo Kalim]], [[Neobanks]], [[Fintech]], [[Pix]], [[UPI]]

The story is that [[Brazil fintech]] is not a list of fast-growing apps; it is a market architecture built around [[Banco Central do Brasil]] and [[Pix]]. Payments are now a public utility, so the durable moat moved upstack to underwriting, funding, product density, and trust. [[C6 Bank]] is the proof point because it is private — the only large Brazilian digital bank you cannot read in a quarterly print — and it carries a 46% [[JPMorgan Chase]] strategic stake.

What changed: C6 reported R$2.5B 2025 net income, R$9.2B revenue, 45% ROE, R$89.3B expanded credit book — and 80% of credit is secured (payroll loans, auto finance). The vault now has a concept-level [[Brazil fintech]] note that frames why the country leads (high incumbent spreads, [[Pix]] rail, central-bank activism, mobile-first behavior, 200M+ market) and a competitive map across [[Nubank]], [[Banco Inter|Inter]], [[StoneCo]], [[PagSeguro]], [[Mercado Libre]], and the incumbent oligopoly.

Why it matters: C6 is a read-through on what global-bank capital wants from Brazilian digital banking — not user growth, but secured credit, affluent products, business banking, and governance strong enough to scale. JPMorgan's stake increase from 40% to 46% in 2023 (after C6 went from 8M to 25M clients) is the disclosure-light signal that the partnership is a scale-and-governance overlay, not an integration play.

The tension: the 2025 profitability print is credit-led. Credit quality, [[Selic]] path, and [[Brazilian real]] funding conditions decide whether the inflection holds.

Watch: digital-bank NPLs across [[Nubank]] / [[Banco Inter|Inter]] / C6, secured-vs-unsecured mix, and Pix P2B transaction share.
