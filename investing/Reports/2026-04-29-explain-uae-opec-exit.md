# The UAE leaving OPEC — what's going on, in plain terms

On April 28, 2026, the [[UAE]] (United Arab Emirates, a federation of seven Gulf emirates of which Abu Dhabi sits on most of the country's oil) announced that it will leave [[OPEC]] (the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries, a cartel of major oil-producing states founded in 1960 to coordinate production quotas and manage global oil prices). The exit takes effect May 1 and ends roughly six decades of UAE membership that began with Abu Dhabi joining the cartel in 1967. The UAE will also leave OPEC+, the broader producer alliance that adds Russia and ten other non-OPEC states to OPEC's eleven members. It is the cartel's largest defection in its history.

## Who's involved

[[Suhail Al Mazrouei]], the UAE's Minister of Energy and Infrastructure, is the public face of the announcement. He has framed the move as a "policy decision" — telling Reuters by telephone that it followed "a careful look at current and future policies related to level of production" — and explicitly denied any political dispute with [[Saudi Arabia]], OPEC's largest member and the cartel's de facto leader. The production ramp the exit makes possible runs through [[ADNOC]], the Abu Dhabi National Oil Company, which has been spending $150 billion through 2027 to lift Emirati capacity from roughly 4.8 million barrels per day today toward a 5 million barrel target.

[[Anwar Gargash]], the UAE's senior diplomatic adviser, gave a separately-noteworthy comment the same day, calling the wartime political and military cohesion of the [[GCC]] (Gulf Cooperation Council, the six-state Gulf alliance of Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, and Oman) "the weakest historically." That admission, paired with the OPEC exit, is unusual for an Emirati official.

[[Jorge Leon]] of [[Rystad Energy]], a Norwegian energy consultancy, supplied the most-quoted analyst frame: the UAE has historically been OPEC's "shock absorber" — the member willing to absorb above-quota cuts to support Saudi-led price discipline — and Saudi Arabia must now do "more of the heavy lifting on price stability."

## Why it matters

Three numbers carry the weight. First, the UAE produces roughly 3.5 million barrels per day under its OPEC+ quota; its actual capacity is closer to 4.8 million. The 1.3 million barrel gap is what the quota system has been keeping out of the market. Second, the Baker Institute (a Houston-based energy think tank) estimates the unconstrained-production revenue upside at "upward of $50 billion in additional annual revenues." Third, the UAE is OPEC's third-largest producer after Saudi Arabia and Iraq — large enough that its departure materially shifts the cartel's share of global supply.

Beyond the immediate barrels, the exit is the third major OPEC departure in seven years (Qatar 2019, Angola 2024, UAE 2026), and the first time a major exporter has left at the **peak** of its capacity arc rather than the trough. Indonesia and Ecuador left as their production declined; Qatar refocused on natural gas; Angola left because its quota was below its actual output. The UAE leaves because its capacity is years ahead of any quota OPEC would assign — a different signal entirely.

## The context

The timing is the load-bearing question, and it sits inside three larger threads.

**First, the war.** The exit comes two months into the [[2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis]] — the closure of the Persian Gulf's main oil chokepoint, which began in late February 2026 when Iran mined the strait following Israeli strikes. With roughly a fifth of global seaborne oil traffic stopped and Brent crude trading above $108 a barrel (up nine straight days through the announcement), the market is already short of supply. Mazrouei's argument is that this is precisely *why* now is the right moment to exit: one more producer being formally outside the quota system doesn't change today's barrels, so the cartel cost of leaving is minimal. The contestable question is what happens *after* Hormuz reopens — whether the UAE then ramps unconstrained, and whether Saudi Arabia and the rest of OPEC+ can still discipline prices without their largest cooperative partner inside the tent.

**Second, the Saudi-UAE rift.** The Gulf has been visibly fracturing for months. In December 2025, Saudi airstrikes hit what Riyadh called Iranian weapons shipments to UAE-backed [[Southern Transitional Council]] separatists in southern Yemen — separatists with whom the Saudis had previously been coalition partners against the Houthis. The two governments had also been competing over Red Sea ports and post-war architecture for [[Iran]]. A March 2026 *Foreign Policy* essay by political scientist [[Marc Lynch]] documented Riyadh's pre-war push to build a Turkey-Qatar-Egypt-Pakistan alliance partially against the UAE-Israel axis. Iran's strikes on every Gulf state in late February briefly reunified them under existential threat, but the underlying disagreements never resolved. The OPEC exit is the first unilateral structural decision the UAE has taken since that wartime pause — the answer to whether the rift was repaired by shared crisis is, apparently, no.

**Third, the parallel financial architecture.** Five days before the OPEC announcement, [[Scott Bessent]], the US Treasury Secretary, defended a $20 billion currency swap line for the UAE before a Senate appropriations subcommittee, declining to name the country directly. NEC Director [[Kevin Hassett]] called the UAE "an incredibly valuable ally" in supporting language. The swap line had been raised earlier in the month by UAE Central Bank Governor [[Khaled Mohamed Balama]] with Bessent and Federal Reserve officials at the IMF/World Bank spring meetings. The sequence — UAE Treasury access in mid-April, public US support around April 23, OPEC exit on April 28 — gives the announcement a financial-architecture backstop that an apolitical "policy change" framing would not require. The interpretation in the press wrap (Bloomberg, Reuters, Al Jazeera, Washington Post, Fortune) is that the UAE is reorienting from multilateral GCC and OPEC architecture toward bilateral US anchoring.

## What's contested and what to watch

The factual question — does this tighten or loosen oil supply? — is unsettled. In the current closed-Hormuz regime the exit changes nothing immediately. But once the strait reopens and inventories rebuild, an unconstrained UAE producing toward 5 million barrels a day at the same time as a rebuilt OPEC+ output would be the cleanest test of cartel cohesion since Saudi Arabia's 2014-2016 price war. Three scenarios are open: coordinated rebuild (UAE ramps gradually, Saudi-UAE align informally), quiet acceleration (UAE captures the $50 billion revenue upside without provoking Saudi retaliation), or market-share reset (everyone produces flat-out, prices crash). Mazrouei's commitment to "not shock the market" is reassuring on tone but provides no enforcement mechanism.

The diagnostic markers a reader can track: any explicit Saudi response (silence so far is itself a signal), an Iraqi or Kuwaiti follow-on exit announcement (which would signal cartel cascade), the pace at which Hormuz reopens, whether the UAE extends the [[Chinese yuan]] settlement experiments it has been quietly running on some oil cargos (which would erode the [[petrodollar]] — the post-1973 convention of pricing oil in US dollars that has anchored global dollar demand for half a century), the next OPEC+ ministerial meeting (typically in June), and the ADNOC quarterly production figures, which will reveal whether the ramp is gradual or aggressive.
