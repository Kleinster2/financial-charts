# AI capex arms race — funded by faith, then by debt, now by each other

The vault's [[AI capex arms race]] thesis has tracked the buildout through seven refinements — GPU scarcity to systems scarcity to the external-balance problem to the earnings-test to capex-vs-commodity to platform-vs-supplier to the free-cash-flow trough to the market-window. The Jun 23 tape adds the eighth and least comfortable: the buildout is now substantially financed by its own customers. The datum the thesis note does not yet carry comes from the cold sweep — the four largest US cloud providers hold roughly $2.1T of revenue backlog, but about half of it, ~$1.05T, is owed by [[OpenAI]] and [[Anthropic]], two labs that themselves burn cash and are funded in part by the same hyperscalers and by [[NVIDIA]] whose revenue that backlog underwrites. The capex is real. The open question is whether the demand beneath it is independent or circular.

The constraint that bound today is the one the thesis note treats as benign: rates. Refinement III asserted that "a 25bp move does not change the buildout calculus" because AI capex is funded by corporate cash flow plus investment-grade debt. That held while the Fed was easing. Refinement VI then established the cash-flow trough — Big 4 capex near $725B in 2026 against roughly $4B combined Q3 free cash flow, with [[Amazon]]'s trailing FCF down ~95% to $1.2B and hyperscalers having raised $108B of debt in 2025 against ~$1.5T projected. Refinement VII established the market-window fragility on Jun 5. Jun 23 is where all three bind: the marginal AI dollar is increasingly debt-and-lease financed exactly as the discount rate on its terminal payoff rises under a [[Kevin Warsh]] Fed that has dropped the easing bias (see the [[Rate expectations]] companion). [[Mike Wilson]] named the mechanism on the eve of the tape — "a renewed slowdown in AI capex as markets demand clearer payback from debt-funded spending." That is the eighth refinement in a sentence, and the proximate trigger ([[Broadcom]]'s cautious AI-chip-demand commentary) is the supply chain saying the same thing from the inside.

The vault's instinct is to ask whether this is 1999, and the history vault carries a cleaner analogue than the dot-com equity mania. The [Telecommunications Deregulation](obsidian://open?vault=history&file=Telecommunications%20Deregulation) note documents the late-1990s fiber overbuild: deregulation pulled a flood of capital into infrastructure on demand projections that were right in direction but wrong in magnitude and timing. [[Nortel]] peaked near a $300B market cap in 2000 and was bankrupt by 2009; the dark fiber got built, the traffic took a decade to arrive, and the builders died before the demand validated them. The AI parallel is not that demand is fake — it is that the lag between when the capex is spent and when the payback lands is exactly where over-levered builders fail. The disanalogy [[Jensen Huang]] himself draws, cited in that same history note, is that chips sit under a [[CUDA]]-class software moat that telecom equipment never had, so the AI profit pool may be defensible in a way fiber's never was. Both can hold at once: the infrastructure is more defensible than fiber, and the financing structure is more circular than fiber ever was — see the SPV mechanics in [[AI infrastructure financing]].

The arms race was always a bet that demand would arrive before the financing strained. Jun 23 is the first session that repriced both halves of that bet at once, and [[Micron]]'s print tomorrow is the first hard read on whether the demand half is still winning.
