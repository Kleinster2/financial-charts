# Lithium — unilateral supply discipline and the Brazilian undercut

The tape says deficit. [[Albemarle|ALB]] ran +16.3% today on +4.9σ beta-adjusted, [[SQM]] ran +8.8% on +2.6σ, and Chinese lithium carbonate spot at ~167,500 CNY/T (~$23,000/t) is now up 134% year-over-year — the highest print since mid-2023. Fastmarkets (the industry pricing agency) pegs the 2026 balance at a 1,500t deficit. [[UBS]] raised the [[Albemarle|ALB]] target to $230 on April 9. But the structural driver is not a single demand pull. It is four uncoordinated national producers all tightening on the same tape: China has cancelled 27 lithium-mining permits in Yichun under its "anti-involution" campaign — Beijing's explicit program to curb overcapacity-driven price collapses in strategic industries; Zimbabwe suspended lithium-concentrate exports in April 2026 to force domestic refining; [[CATL]]'s Jiangxi mine went down in August 2025 on permit expiry (~4% of global supply per [[Morgan Stanley]]); and [[Albemarle]]'s own Kemerton suspension removed 50,000+ tonnes of announced capacity. None of this is coordinated. There is no OPEC of lithium. The Apr 16 rally is the market pricing four producers acting unilaterally with no mechanism to align on price or volume — exactly the dynamic the [[Lithium Triangle]] documents is structurally impossible to negotiate into existence.

The [[Lithium Triangle]] — the resource basin shared by [[Chile]], [[Argentina]], and [[Bolivia]] around the Puna Plateau salt flats — demonstrates the incoherence in concentrated form. [[Bolivia]] holds 21% of global reserves, the largest share of any country, but produced only a few hundred tonnes in 2024; a $1B Chinese-investor deal and a parallel Russian agreement both stalled in July 2025 when Bolivian congress descended into shouting matches over foreign dependency. [[Chile]] runs a partial-state-control model: the December 2025 [[Codelco]]-[[SQM]] **Nova Andino Litio** JV secures the Salar de Atacama brine operation through 2060 but captures 70% of operating margin for the state through 2030, rising to 85% from 2031. [[Argentina]] went the opposite direction under Milei — installed capacity tripled from 35,500 to 186,000 tonnes per year of lithium carbonate equivalent (LCE, the standard industry unit) through market-open concessions. And then in March 2026 [[José Antonio Kast]] took power in Chile pledging to make lithium "concessionable" — a framework that would unwind the state-anchored National Lithium Strategy Boric built in 2023, though Nova Andino's 2060 contract has protection. The regional regime moved state-led → state-JV → potentially-market-led in under three years. The commodity cycle crashed and rallied on the same timeline. They are connected: resource-nationalism peaks when prices crater and unwinds when the price signal returns.

The Nova Andino structure is analytically underweighted. [[SQM]] accepting a 70% state-margin capture through 2030 and 85% from 2031 is not a royalty hike — it is a multi-decade rent allocation in which the state monetizes the concession value itself while the private partner keeps operational control through the intermediate phase. The haircut explains today's dispersion: [[SQM]] ran +8.8% against [[Albemarle|ALB]] at +16.3% despite identical commodity exposure because roughly half of ALB's move is the shareholder equity value that — at [[SQM]] — now accrues to the Chilean state. This is the priced-in cost of political legibility. [[SQM]] has 2060 concession certainty, the kind of decades-visibility ALB's Australian and Nevada operations don't carry, but at the price of giving up most of the upside. Kast's "concessionable" policy, if it materializes, would apply to *new* Chilean lithium entrants — an asymmetric two-tier regime in which Nova Andino operates under 85% state capture while new market-terms entrants compete for the same Atacama watershed. The structural bull case is capacity coming online outside Nova Andino within a Kast term.

Brazil is the answer the market is not yet fully pricing. [[Sigma Lithium]]'s Grota do Cirilo in Minas Gerais runs at $440/t CIF China and is ramping from 270 ktpa toward 520 through 2026. [[Atlas Lithium]]'s Neves project has cleared a 145% IRR at $489/t production cost — among the lowest anywhere — and was named in the April 2026 Japan-US Critical Minerals Joint Fact Sheet as the only Brazil-based lithium project eligible for allied-government financing, with Mitsui already committing $30M against off-take. [[AMG]]'s Mibra mine has been running since 1945 and is building the only scaled European LiOH (lithium hydroxide) conversion plant at Bitterfeld-Wolfen, bypassing Chinese refining entirely. [[Brazil]] produced 4,900 MT in 2023 and is forecast at 57,500 MT LCE in 2025 — a 10x ramp in five years, at cost structures that undercut most of the incumbent curve. And yet Brasília is still at the concentrate-export stage — the [Brazil: Mining Sector](obsidian://open?vault=Brazil%20News%20%26%20Analysis&file=Analysis%2FMining%20Sector) analysis documents this as a pattern across minerals, not just lithium, and [Brazil: Critical Minerals](obsidian://open?vault=Brazil%20News%20%26%20Analysis&file=Analysis%2FCritical%20Minerals) names it as the "same commodity trap" the country faces on iron ore and soy. The [Geopolitics: Critical Minerals](obsidian://open?vault=geopolitics&file=Concepts%2FCritical%20Minerals) note layers the sequencing: Roberto Goulart Menezes at the University of Brasília frames the February 2026 [[India]]-Brazil MOU as "a pilot before negotiating with a country with which Brazil has an asymmetrical relationship" — Brasília testing its terms with a peer before sitting down with the US or EU. Brazil has the cost curve to dominate. It does not yet have the diplomatic sequencing — or the downstream processing — to capture the margin those costs deserve.

The synthesis sits in the tension between those four paragraphs. The Apr 16 rally is four uncoordinated supply regimes converging. The next wave of tightening comes from Chile's two-tier regime under Kast, Nova Andino ramping at 85%-state-margin from 2031, and the diplomatic window closing on Brazil's peer-pilot framework. The next wave of *loosening* comes from Argentine capacity expansion, Brazilian cost-curve disruption at $440-489/t, and the Smackover [[Direct lithium extraction|DLE]] pipeline eventually reaching commercial scale. At $23,000/t spot, both waves are being funded simultaneously. That is what today's tape is pricing — and what the [[Sigma Lithium]], [[Atlas Lithium]], [[AMG]] cost curves make concrete.
